Schemes, visually

An interactive tour of Grothendieck's geometry: every commutative ring is a space, and every space is made of rings glued together.

1. Why schemes?

Classical algebraic geometry studies the zero sets of polynomials. The circle is $V(x^2+y^2-1)\subset \mathbb{R}^2$. A variety over $\mathbb{C}$ is a subset of $\mathbb{C}^n$ cut out by polynomial equations.

This is great but throws away too much. Three problems nag at you:

problem 1 The rings $\mathbb{R}[x]/(x)$ and $\mathbb{R}[x]/(x^2)$ have the same "zero set" — a single point — yet they are different rings. The second one remembers a tangent direction. Classical geometry can't see it.

problem 2 Over $\mathbb{R}$, the polynomial $x^2+1$ has no zeros at all, so its zero set is empty. But the ring $\mathbb{R}[x]/(x^2+1)\cong\mathbb{C}$ is interesting; it should correspond to a geometric object.

problem 3 The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ feel a lot like a ring of functions on something. On what?

Grothendieck's answer reverses the usual arrow:

Ring ⟶ Space. Every commutative ring $R$ becomes a topological space $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$, whose points are the prime ideals of $R$. Elements of $R$ become "functions" on this space. A scheme is a space built by gluing spaces of the form $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$.

Why prime ideals? Three clean reasons:

(i) If $\phi\colon R\to k$ is a homomorphism to a field, $\ker\phi$ is prime. So "$R$-valued points in fields" correspond to prime ideals — maximal ideals give closed points, smaller primes give "generic" points.

(ii) The set $V(I)=\{\mathfrak p : I\subseteq \mathfrak p\}$ behaves like a Zariski-closed set, so primes carry a natural topology.

(iii) For an element $f\in R$, evaluating $f$ at a prime $\mathfrak p$ means reducing: look at the image of $f$ in the residue field $\kappa(\mathfrak p) = \operatorname{Frac}(R/\mathfrak p)$. The "value" of $f$ at different points may live in different fields — that's fine.

The rest of this page turns that paragraph into pictures.

2. The simplest scheme: Spec ℤ

Take $R=\mathbb{Z}$. Its prime ideals are $(0)$ and $(p)$ for each prime $p$. So $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{Z}$ is a countable set of "closed" points $2,3,5,7,11,\ldots$ together with one extra point, $(0)$, the generic point.

An integer $n\in\mathbb{Z}$ is a "function" on this space. Its value at the prime $p$ is $n \bmod p$, an element of the residue field $\mathbb{F}_p$. Its value at the generic point $(0)$ is $n$ itself inside $\mathbb{Q}$.

So $n$ "vanishes" at exactly the primes dividing $n$, with multiplicity equal to the exponent — we see factorization as the zero locus of a function.

Reading a scheme. A scheme has more information than a topological space. At each point $\mathfrak p$ there's a residue field $\kappa(\mathfrak p)$ and functions take values in different fields at different points. For $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{Z}$: $\kappa((p))=\mathbb{F}_p$ and $\kappa((0))=\mathbb{Q}$.

3. The affine line Spec k[x]

Let $k$ be a field. The primes of $k[x]$ are $(0)$ and $(f)$ where $f$ is irreducible over $k$. So $\operatorname{Spec}k[x]$ has one generic point, plus one closed point for each monic irreducible polynomial.

When $k=\mathbb{C}$ every irreducible is linear $x-\alpha$, and the closed points are exactly the complex numbers — this is the "usual" affine line. When $k=\mathbb{R}$ irreducibles are either linear $x-a$ or quadratic $x^2+bx+c$ with negative discriminant; a single closed point of $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{R}[x]$ can gather a conjugate pair of complex numbers. When $k=\mathbb{F}_p$ there are finitely many irreducibles of each degree, and a closed point may have residue field $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$.

4. Dimension two: Spec k[x,y]

One more dimension up. For $R=k[x,y]$ the primes come in three flavors:

dim 0

maximal ideals $(x-a,y-b)$ — the closed points, one for each $(a,b)$.

dim 1

height-one primes $(f)$ with $f$ irreducible — irreducible curves. Each curve is itself a "point" of $\operatorname{Spec}k[x,y]$ whose closure is the curve and its closed points.

dim 2

the zero ideal $(0)$ — the single generic point of the plane. Its closure is everything.

So $\operatorname{Spec}k[x,y]$ has three tiers: 2-dimensional generic point at the top, 1-dimensional generic points of curves in the middle, 0-dimensional closed points at the bottom. Specializations go downward.

The generic point is not a dot. Every nonempty open set of $\operatorname{Spec}k[x,y]$ contains the generic point $(0)$. It "lives everywhere" — it isn't drawable as a location. Instead think of it as a perspective: statements true at the generic point hold on a dense open set.

5. Zariski topology & specialization

The closed sets of $\operatorname{Spec}R$ are the vanishing loci $V(I)=\{\mathfrak p\supseteq I\}$. The closure of a single point $\mathfrak p$ is $V(\mathfrak p)$ — the set of primes containing it. We say $\mathfrak p$ specializes to $\mathfrak q$, written $\mathfrak p \leadsto \mathfrak q$, if $\mathfrak p\subseteq \mathfrak q$, i.e. $\mathfrak q$ is in the closure of $\{\mathfrak p\}$.

The resulting poset is a clean way to see dimension. Below: three curves in $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{C}[x,y]$ and their closed intersection points. Click any node to see everything that specializes from it (i.e., what it "contains" in closure).

Heuristic: smaller ideal ⇒ bigger point. The empty ideal $(0)$ is the "biggest" (most generic) point; maximal ideals are the "smallest" (most specialized). Dimension of a scheme = length of the longest specialization chain.

6. Nilpotents: fat points

Here's the payoff for the problem 1 we raised at the start. Consider the rings $\mathbb{C}[x]/(x)$ and $\mathbb{C}[x]/(x^n)$ for $n\geq 2$. Their sets of primes are both $\{(x)\}$ — a single point. But as schemes they differ: the structure ring remembers up to $(n-1)$-th order derivatives.

Call $D_n := \operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{C}[x]/(x^n)$ the $n$-th order infinitesimal neighborhood of the origin or the $n$-th jet. A morphism $D_n \to X$ into a scheme $X$ is the same as a point of $X$ together with an $(n-1)$-jet of a curve through it.

Nilpotents are the glue that makes schemes more expressive than classical varieties. Tangent vectors, higher jets, divisors with multiplicity, and the whole theory of deformation live here.

Two variables: $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{C}[x,y]/(x^2,xy,y^2)$ is a "fat point" with the full 2-dim tangent space. $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{C}[x,y]/(x^2,y)$ is a length-2 scheme pointing in the $x$-direction — an infinitesimal arrow.

7. Gluing: building ℙ¹

A general scheme is not of the form $\operatorname{Spec}R$. It's built by gluing affines along open subsets. The simplest nontrivial example is the projective line $\mathbb{P}^1$.

Take two copies of the affine line: $U = \operatorname{Spec}k[x]$ and $V = \operatorname{Spec}k[y]$. On each, remove the origin: the open set $x\ne 0$ of $U$ is $\operatorname{Spec}k[x,x^{-1}]$, and $y\ne 0$ of $V$ is $\operatorname{Spec}k[y,y^{-1}]$. These two are canonically isomorphic via $y=1/x$. Glue along this isomorphism.

The result covers every point of $\mathbb{P}^1$: a point in $U$ has a well-defined $x$, a point in $V$ has a well-defined $y$, and points in the overlap have both with $y=1/x$. The extreme points are the origin of $U$ (has $x=0$, $y$ undefined — would be "$\infty$") and the origin of $V$ (has $y=0$, $x$ undefined).

ℙ¹ is a scheme that locally looks like affine space but globally isn't affine. Every projective variety, scheme of curves, moduli space of configurations, etc., is built this way: a patchwork of affines with compatible transition maps. That is the main reason Grothendieck's definition is so powerful — it gives a single language for all of algebraic geometry, equally comfortable with $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{Z}$, the complex projective plane, or moduli of elliptic curves.

Where next? Sheaves (local-to-global functions), morphisms of schemes, base change, fiber products, projective schemes, coherent sheaves, and cohomology. From the scheme foundation, category theory (the language of functors, adjunctions, Yoneda) takes over, and stacks extend schemes to handle moduli problems where "points have automorphisms" — another page for another day.

8. Generic points and specialization

Classical geometry only sees closed points — maximal ideals. Scheme theory adds one extra point for every non-maximal prime, and those extra points are surprisingly useful. A prime $\mathfrak p\subsetneq R$ that is not maximal is called a generic point. Its closure in $\operatorname{Spec}R$ is exactly $V(\mathfrak p) = \{\mathfrak q : \mathfrak p\subseteq \mathfrak q\}$, an entire irreducible subvariety — not just a point.

So in $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{Z}[x]$ the prime $(x)$ is one point that, when you take its closure, spreads out to cover the whole "$x=0$" axis. The zero ideal $(0)$ is an even more generic point: its closure is all of $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{Z}[x]$.

The partial order "$\mathfrak p\leadsto \mathfrak q$ iff $\mathfrak p\subseteq \mathfrak q$" is specialization: the smaller (more generic) prime specializes to the larger (more special) one. Think of it as taking a limit — a generic point "flows into" every closed point of its closure. Properties that hold at a generic point (being smooth, being reduced, being irreducible) typically hold on an open dense neighborhood; properties at closed points are local tests of what happens "at one spot".

This is what lets algebraic geometers say things like "the generic fiber of a family is smooth, and only finitely many special fibers degenerate" — the generic fiber is literally the fiber over the generic point of the base.

9. Residue fields: evaluating at a point

A function on a classical variety over $k$ takes values in $k$. On $\operatorname{Spec}R$ the ring $R$ is not a priori an algebra over any one field, so "evaluating $f\in R$ at a point $\mathfrak p$" needs care. The answer is the residue field $$\kappa(\mathfrak p) \;=\; \operatorname{Frac}(R/\mathfrak p) \;=\; R_{\mathfrak p}/\mathfrak p R_{\mathfrak p},$$ the fraction field of the integral domain $R/\mathfrak p$, equivalently the residue of the local ring $R_{\mathfrak p}$.

An element $f\in R$ has a well-defined image $f(\mathfrak p)\in \kappa(\mathfrak p)$ — but the target field depends on the point. A single $f\in\mathbb{Z}[x]$ takes values in $\mathbb{F}_p$ at the maximal ideal $(p,x-a)$, in $\mathbb{Q}(x)$ at the generic point $(0)$, and in $\mathbb{F}_p(x)$ at the height-one prime $(p)$. "Vanishing at $\mathfrak p$" just means $f\in \mathfrak p$, i.e. $f(\mathfrak p)=0$ in $\kappa(\mathfrak p)$.

The residue field is exactly what makes the slogan "elements of $R$ are functions on $\operatorname{Spec}R$" honest. A $k$-point of an $R$-scheme — a homomorphism $R\to k$ — factors uniquely through the residue field of its kernel, so geometric points over an algebraically closed field correspond to maximal ideals with residue field $k$.

Concretely, at the closed point $\mathfrak m=(x-a)\subset \mathbb{C}[x]$ we get $\kappa(\mathfrak m)=\mathbb{C}$ and $f\mapsto f(a)$ — the classical evaluation. Everything schemes do to enrich this picture is built on top.

10. Locally ringed spaces

A ringed space is a topological space $X$ together with a sheaf of rings $\mathcal O_X$. A ringed space is locally ringed if for every point $x\in X$ the stalk $\mathcal O_{X,x}$ is a local ring — a ring with exactly one maximal ideal $\mathfrak m_x$. Its residue field is $\kappa(x) = \mathcal O_{X,x}/\mathfrak m_x$.

Every scheme is a locally ringed space: on $\operatorname{Spec}R$ the stalk at the prime $\mathfrak p$ is the localization $R_{\mathfrak p}$, which is local by construction, with maximal ideal $\mathfrak p R_{\mathfrak p}$. Conversely, a scheme is defined as a locally ringed space that is locally isomorphic to $\operatorname{Spec}$ of a ring — this is the invariant definition that doesn't depend on a choice of open cover.

The "locally ringed" part is doing real work. A morphism of ringed spaces $f\colon (X,\mathcal O_X)\to (Y,\mathcal O_Y)$ is a continuous map plus a pullback $f^\sharp\colon \mathcal O_Y \to f_*\mathcal O_X$. A morphism of locally ringed spaces also requires that the induced map on stalks $\mathcal O_{Y,f(x)}\to \mathcal O_{X,x}$ is local: it sends the maximal ideal into the maximal ideal. Without that condition, maps between schemes would not correspond to ring homomorphisms; with it, $\operatorname{Spec}$ becomes a fully faithful contravariant functor from commutative rings to locally ringed spaces.

In short: "sheaf of rings whose stalks detect a unique point of vanishing" is precisely the structure needed to glue $\operatorname{Spec}$'s into a category of honest geometric objects.

11. Projective schemes and Proj

The gluing of $\mathbb{P}^1$ in §7 was ad hoc — two copies of $\mathbb A^1$ along the overlap $y=1/x$. For higher-dimensional and more intricate projective geometry the Proj construction gives the uniform recipe. Start with a graded ring $S = \bigoplus_{d\geq 0} S_d$ — for example $S = k[x_0,\dots,x_n]$ with the usual grading by total degree.

Define $\operatorname{Proj}S$ as the set of homogeneous prime ideals $\mathfrak p\subset S$ not containing the irrelevant ideal $S_+ = \bigoplus_{d>0} S_d$. The topology is the Zariski topology inherited from homogeneous ideals, and the structure sheaf is built from localizations at homogeneous elements: for each homogeneous $f\in S_d$ with $d>0$, the basic open $D_+(f)$ is canonically identified with $\operatorname{Spec}(S_f)_0$, the degree-zero part of the localization.

For $S=k[x_0,\dots,x_n]$ this reproduces classical $\mathbb P^n_k$: the opens $D_+(x_i)\cong \operatorname{Spec}k[x_0/x_i,\dots,x_n/x_i]\cong \mathbb A^n_k$ patch together exactly by "divide by $x_i$" transitions. For $S=k[x_0,\dots,x_n]/I$ with $I$ a homogeneous ideal, $\operatorname{Proj}S$ is the projective subscheme cut out by $I$ — hypersurfaces, complete intersections, projective varieties, and more.

The payoff: every projective scheme is of the form $\operatorname{Proj}S$ for some graded ring, and conversely any finitely generated graded algebra over a field produces a projective scheme. This converts projective geometry into the algebra of graded rings, the same way $\operatorname{Spec}$ converted affine geometry into the algebra of commutative rings.

12. Closed subschemes, reducedness, and irreducibility

A Zariski-closed subset of $\operatorname{Spec}R$ is $V(I)$ for some ideal $I$. But as a scheme, "the closed set $V(I)$" has extra structure: it is $\operatorname{Spec}(R/I)$, and different ideals with the same radical give different subschemes. So a closed subscheme of $\operatorname{Spec}R$ is a choice of ideal $I\subseteq R$; the underlying space is $V(I)=V(\sqrt I)$ but the ring of functions remembers $I$ exactly.

Two complementary simplifications are often useful:

reduced Replace $I$ by its radical $\sqrt I=\{f\in R : f^n\in I\text{ some }n\}$. The subscheme $\operatorname{Spec}(R/\sqrt I)$ is called the reduced subscheme underlying $V(I)$: it has no nilpotents, matching the classical vanishing locus on the nose.

irreducible $\operatorname{Spec}R$ is irreducible iff its nilradical $\operatorname{nil}(R)$ is prime, equivalently iff $R$ has a unique minimal prime. In general, the minimal primes $\mathfrak p_1,\dots,\mathfrak p_r$ of $R$ are in bijection with the irreducible components: each $V(\mathfrak p_i)$ is a maximal irreducible closed subset, and $\operatorname{Spec}R = \bigcup_i V(\mathfrak p_i)$.

For $R=k[x,y]/(xy)$ the minimal primes are $(x)$ and $(y)$, so the two coordinate axes are the two irreducible components, meeting at the closed point $(x,y)$. For $R=k[x,y]/(x^2,y)$ the radical is $(x,y)$: a single reduced point, but $R$ itself is a length-2 non-reduced ring — the "length-2 fat point in the $x$-direction" seen in §6.

13. Dimension of a scheme

The specialization picture from §5 already hints at a natural notion of dimension: chains of primes $\mathfrak p_0\subsetneq \mathfrak p_1\subsetneq\cdots\subsetneq \mathfrak p_d$ in $R$ correspond to chains of specializations $\mathfrak p_0\leadsto \mathfrak p_1\leadsto\cdots\leadsto \mathfrak p_d$ in $\operatorname{Spec}R$. The dimension of $\operatorname{Spec}R$ is the length of the longest such chain — by definition the Krull dimension of $R$.

For a general scheme $X$, $\dim X$ is the supremum of chain lengths of irreducible closed subsets, and it is a local property: $\dim X = \sup_{x\in X}\dim \mathcal O_{X,x}$ when $X$ is finite-dimensional. On each affine chart $\operatorname{Spec}R\subseteq X$, this recovers the Krull dimension of $R$.

Some examples that are good to keep in your head:

  • $\dim \operatorname{Spec} k = 0$ (only the zero ideal).
  • $\dim \operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z = 1$: chain $(0)\subsetneq (p)$.
  • $\dim \operatorname{Spec} k[x_1,\dots,x_n] = n$: chain $(0)\subsetneq (x_1)\subsetneq (x_1,x_2)\subsetneq\cdots\subsetneq (x_1,\dots,x_n)$.
  • $\dim \operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z[x_1,\dots,x_n] = n+1$: one extra step for the $\mathbb Z$ direction.
  • $\dim \mathbb P^n_k = n$ — gluing together $n+1$ copies of $\mathbb A^n$ preserves dimension.

Dimension is the scheme-theoretic version of "how many independent coordinates do I need". Height-one primes cut out codimension-one subschemes (divisors); height-$n$ primes in a regular local ring of dimension $n$ are closed points. The Krull dimension of the local ring $\mathcal O_{X,x}$ — an invariant of the point — is the local dimension of $X$ at $x$.

Dimension can behave badly for pathological rings, but for finite-type schemes over a field (or $\mathbb Z$) it is well-behaved and matches the geometric intuition. A variety is equidimensional if all irreducible components have the same dimension.

14. Fibers of a morphism over a point

A morphism of schemes $f\colon X\to Y$ breaks $X$ into fibers, one over each point $y\in Y$. Just as a morphism of varieties over $\mathbb{C}$ has fibers $f^{-1}(y)$, a scheme morphism has a scheme-theoretic fiber.

The fiber over $y\in Y$ is the scheme $$X_y \;=\; X\times_Y \operatorname{Spec}\kappa(y),$$ the fiber product with the residue field at $y$. Concretely, if $X=\operatorname{Spec}B$ and $Y=\operatorname{Spec}A$ with $f$ induced by $A\to B$, then $X_y = \operatorname{Spec}(B\otimes_A \kappa(y))$. The underlying set of $X_y$ is exactly the preimage $f^{-1}(y)\subseteq X$, with scheme structure that records multiplicities and residue-field extensions.

The most arresting example is the structural morphism $f\colon \operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z[x]\to \operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z$. Its fibers depend on which point of the base we sit over:

over $(p)$ the fiber is $\operatorname{Spec}(\mathbb Z[x]\otimes_{\mathbb Z}\mathbb F_p)=\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb F_p[x]$ — the affine line over $\mathbb F_p$. One for each prime $p$.

over $(0)$ the generic fiber is $\operatorname{Spec}(\mathbb Z[x]\otimes_{\mathbb Z}\mathbb Q)=\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Q[x]$ — the affine line over $\mathbb Q$.

So $\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z[x]$ is a "family of affine lines parametrized by primes". This is the single picture that explains why number theorists and geometers can share a vocabulary: a Diophantine equation is a scheme over $\mathbb Z$, and reducing mod $p$ is just "looking at the fiber over $p$".

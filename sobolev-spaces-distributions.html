Sobolev spaces & distributions

A duality lifts differentiation off functions and onto $C_c^\infty$ via integration by parts. The dual classes — distributions, then $W^{k,p}$ — are the natural workshop for PDE: they make $\delta$, the derivative of a step, the Laplace fundamental solution, and the variational $-\Delta u = f$ all one calculation away.

1. Test functions and the space of distributions

Fix an open set $\Omega\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n$. The test-function space is

$$\mathcal{D}(\Omega) \;=\; C_c^\infty(\Omega) \;=\; \{\phi\in C^\infty(\Omega) : \operatorname{supp}\phi \text{ compact in } \Omega\}.$$

A sequence $\phi_k\to\phi$ in $\mathcal{D}$ when (i) all $\operatorname{supp}\phi_k$ live in a single compact $K\subset\Omega$ and (ii) $\partial^\alpha\phi_k\to\partial^\alpha\phi$ uniformly for every multi-index $\alpha$. Bump functions exist (e.g. mollifying $\mathbf{1}_{B_1}$ with the model bump $e^{-1/(1-|x|^2)}\mathbf{1}_{|x|<1}$), so $\mathcal{D}$ is non-trivial — surprisingly so given the strength of "smooth, compactly supported".

The space of distributions on $\Omega$ is the topological dual

$$\mathcal{D}'(\Omega) \;=\; \{T\colon\mathcal{D}(\Omega)\to\mathbb{R}\;\text{linear and continuous}\}.$$

Every $f\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(\Omega)$ embeds as the regular distribution

$$\langle T_f,\phi\rangle \;=\; \int_\Omega f(x)\,\phi(x)\,dx.$$

The map $f\mapsto T_f$ is injective up to a.e. equality, so functions sit inside $\mathcal{D}'$ as a proper subspace.

2. Distributional and weak derivatives

Differentiation is defined on every distribution by moving the derivative onto the test function. For $T\in\mathcal{D}'(\Omega)$ and a multi-index $\alpha$, set

$$\langle \partial^\alpha T, \phi\rangle \;=\; (-1)^{|\alpha|}\,\langle T, \partial^\alpha \phi\rangle, \qquad \phi\in\mathcal{D}.$$

The sign comes from formal integration by parts: when $T=T_f$ is regular and $f\in C^{|\alpha|}$, the boundary terms vanish because $\phi$ has compact support, recovering the classical $\partial^\alpha f$. So distributional differentiation is consistent with — and strictly extends — the classical operator.

Two consequences are immediate:

  • Every distribution is $C^\infty$. There is no analogue of "$T$ is once-differentiable but not twice" — every order exists, automatically.
  • Differentiation is continuous on $\mathcal{D}'$: if $T_k\to T$ as distributions, then $\partial^\alpha T_k\to\partial^\alpha T$. Term-by-term differentiation of distributional series is free.

Three calculations every analyst keeps on hand:

(a) Heaviside derivative. Let $H(x)=\mathbf{1}_{x>0}$. For any $\phi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})$,

$$\langle H',\phi\rangle = -\langle H,\phi'\rangle = -\int_0^\infty \phi'(x)\,dx = \phi(0) = \langle\delta_0,\phi\rangle,$$

so $H' = \delta_0$ in $\mathcal{D}'(\mathbb{R})$. Pointwise the classical derivative is $0$ on $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$ and undefined at $0$ — the distributional answer captures the jump.

(b) Log derivative. $(\log|x|)' = \mathrm{p.v.}\,\tfrac{1}{x}$ in $\mathcal{D}'(\mathbb{R})$. The logarithm is locally integrable; differentiating it classically away from $0$ gives $1/x$, which is not locally integrable, so it must be regularised — and the principal value is exactly the regularisation that respects integration by parts.

(c) Weak derivative on intervals. A function $u\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(a,b)$ has weak derivative $v\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(a,b)$ if $\int u\,\phi' = -\int v\,\phi$ for every $\phi\in C_c^\infty(a,b)$. When this $v$ exists in $L^p$, $u$ has a Sobolev-class derivative — the bridge from §1 to §3.

3. Sobolev spaces $W^{k,p}$ and $H^s$

With distributional derivatives in hand, define

$$W^{k,p}(\Omega) \;=\; \{u\in L^p(\Omega) : \partial^\alpha u\in L^p(\Omega)\text{ for every } |\alpha|\le k\},$$

where $\partial^\alpha u$ is the distributional derivative. Equip it with

$$\|u\|_{W^{k,p}} \;=\; \Bigl(\sum_{|\alpha|\le k}\|\partial^\alpha u\|_{L^p}^p\Bigr)^{1/p}.$$

$W^{k,p}(\Omega)$ is a Banach space; for $p=2$ it is a Hilbert space, and we write $H^k(\Omega) = W^{k,2}(\Omega)$ with inner product $(u,v)_{H^k}=\sum_{|\alpha|\le k}\int \partial^\alpha u\,\overline{\partial^\alpha v}$.

The point of the table is that $W^{1,p}$ allows kinks but not too-violent unboundedness in the derivative. The kink in $|x-1/2|$ is fine; the spike in $x^{1/2}$ is fine for small $p$ only; the slow spiral of $\log\log(2/x)$ is too bad in any $L^p$.

Fractional and Hilbert-scale Sobolev spaces $H^s$

For $s\in\mathbb{R}$, the fractional space $H^s(\mathbb{R}^n)$ is defined via the Fourier transform on tempered distributions:

$$H^s(\mathbb{R}^n) \;=\; \bigl\{u\in\mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R}^n) : (1+|\xi|^2)^{s/2}\hat u(\xi)\in L^2(\mathbb{R}^n)\bigr\}.$$

For non-negative integer $s=k$ this agrees with $H^k$ (Plancherel + the multiplier $|\xi|^{|\alpha|}$ corresponds to $\partial^\alpha$). The family $\{H^s\}_{s\in\mathbb{R}}$ forms a Hilbert scale: $H^t\hookrightarrow H^s$ for $t>s$, and the operator $\Lambda^s u = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\bigl[(1+|\xi|^2)^{s/2}\hat u\bigr]$ is an isometric isomorphism $H^s\to L^2$.

4. Sobolev embeddings: Sobolev–Morrey–Rellich

The defining bound on $\|\partial^\alpha u\|_{L^p}$ controls $u$ itself in better norms — that is the content of the embedding theorems. Throughout, $\Omega\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n$ is bounded with sufficiently smooth boundary (Lipschitz suffices); the regularity assumption matters mostly for the first inclusion.

5. The trace theorem

A function in $L^p(\Omega)$ has no boundary values: the boundary $\partial\Omega$ has measure zero, so $u$ is defined only up to a.e. equivalence and any pointwise restriction is meaningless. Sobolev regularity changes that: $W^{1,p}$ functions on a Lipschitz domain admit a well-defined boundary trace, and the trace operator is continuous.

For $\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ bounded with Lipschitz boundary and $1\le p<\infty$, define $\operatorname{Tr}\colon C^\infty(\overline\Omega)\to L^p(\partial\Omega)$ as ordinary restriction $u\mapsto u|_{\partial\Omega}$. Then:

Trace theorem. $\operatorname{Tr}$ extends uniquely to a bounded operator

$$\operatorname{Tr}\colon W^{1,p}(\Omega) \;\to\; W^{1-\tfrac1p,\,p}(\partial\Omega) \;\hookrightarrow\; L^p(\partial\Omega),$$

and the image is exactly $W^{1-1/p,p}(\partial\Omega)$. For $p=2$ this reads $\operatorname{Tr}\colon H^1(\Omega)\to H^{1/2}(\partial\Omega)$.

Two consequences worth memorising:

  • Loss of half a derivative. The boundary inherits half a derivative less than the bulk. Trying to push to $H^1$ on the boundary fails — unboundedly so; one really does drop to $H^{1/2}$.
  • $H^1_0(\Omega)$ kernel. The closure of $C_c^\infty(\Omega)$ in $H^1(\Omega)$ is exactly $\ker\operatorname{Tr}$. This is the right space for Dirichlet boundary conditions: "$u=0$ on $\partial\Omega$" means $u\in H^1_0$, with no pointwise interpretation needed.

Why half a derivative?

The cleanest hint comes from the half-space $\Omega = \mathbb{R}^n_+ = \{x_n>0\}$. By Fourier transform in the tangential variables $x'\in\mathbb{R}^{n-1}$, an $H^1$ function on $\mathbb{R}^n_+$ has $|\xi'|\,\hat u\in L^2$ in the tangential directions and $\partial_n u\in L^2$ in the normal direction. Restricting to the boundary $\{x_n=0\}$ averages the normal direction and leaves the tangential $|\xi'|$ paired with $1/2$ a normal derivative — hence the symbol $|\xi'|^{1/2}$ shows up on the boundary trace, defining $H^{1/2}(\partial\mathbb{R}^n_+)$.

6. Fundamental solutions: $\Delta E = \delta$

A fundamental solution for a constant-coefficient differential operator $L$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a distribution $E\in\mathcal{D}'(\mathbb{R}^n)$ satisfying

$$L E \;=\; \delta_0 \quad \text{in } \mathcal{D}'(\mathbb{R}^n).$$

Once you have one, you can solve $L u = f$ for nice $f$ by convolution: $u = E * f$ formally satisfies $L(E*f) = (LE)*f = \delta * f = f$. Distribution theory is the framework where this calculation is rigorous.

The Newtonian / Coulomb potential

For the Laplacian $\Delta = \sum\partial_i^2$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ with $n\ge 3$, the fundamental solution is

$$E_n(x) \;=\; -\,\frac{1}{(n-2)\,\omega_{n-1}}\;\frac{1}{|x|^{n-2}},$$

where $\omega_{n-1}$ is the surface area of the unit sphere in $\mathbb{R}^n$ ($\omega_2 = 4\pi$). For $n=3$ this is the famous

$$E_3(x) \;=\; -\,\frac{1}{4\pi\,|x|}, \qquad \Delta E_3 = \delta_0,$$

with the sign convention $\Delta = \partial_1^2+\partial_2^2+\partial_3^2$. (For $n=2$ the answer is $\tfrac{1}{2\pi}\log|x|$, which lives outside $L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}$ at infinity but is locally integrable; for $n=1$ it is $\tfrac12 |x|$.)

The verification has two ingredients: away from the origin, $\Delta(|x|^{2-n}) = 0$ by direct computation, so $E_3$ is harmonic on $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{0\}$. The delta concentration at the origin is detected by integration against a test function $\phi$:

$$\langle \Delta E_3, \phi\rangle = \langle E_3, \Delta\phi\rangle = \int_{\mathbb{R}^3} -\frac{\Delta\phi(x)}{4\pi|x|}\,dx \;=\; \phi(0) \;=\; \langle\delta_0,\phi\rangle,$$

where the punchline integral evaluation uses Green's identity on $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus B_\varepsilon$ and lets $\varepsilon\to 0$. The boundary term on $\partial B_\varepsilon$ contributes $\phi(0)$ as the surface area $4\pi\varepsilon^2$ exactly cancels the $1/(4\pi\varepsilon^2)$ from differentiating $1/|x|$ in the radial direction.

How to remember the dimension. The constant in $E_n$ is set so that the radial flux of $\nabla E_n$ across any small sphere around $0$ equals $1$ — Gauss's flux law for a unit point source. In $n=3$ the gradient has size $\tfrac{1}{4\pi r^2}$, the sphere has area $4\pi r^2$, and the product is $1$.

7. Variational $-\Delta u = f$ via Lax–Milgram

The Sobolev/distributional machinery turns the Dirichlet problem

$$\begin{cases} -\Delta u = f & \text{in } \Omega, \\ u = 0 & \text{on } \partial\Omega \end{cases}$$

on a bounded $\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ with $f\in L^2(\Omega)$ into a Hilbert-space optimisation. Multiply by a test function $v\in C_c^\infty(\Omega)$, integrate by parts, and use the boundary condition to drop the boundary term:

$$\int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx \;=\; \int_\Omega f\,v\,dx \quad \forall v\in C_c^\infty(\Omega).$$

Density of $C_c^\infty$ in $H^1_0$ (the trace-zero space from §5) extends the test class to $v\in H^1_0(\Omega)$. The weak formulation is: find $u\in H^1_0(\Omega)$ such that

$$a(u,v) \;=\; L(v) \quad \forall v\in H^1_0(\Omega), \qquad a(u,v)=\int\nabla u\cdot\nabla v, \;\; L(v)=\int f\,v.$$

Lax–Milgram applied

The bilinear form $a$ is continuous on $H^1_0\times H^1_0$ and coercive:

$$a(u,u) = \|\nabla u\|_{L^2}^2 \;\ge\; \tfrac{1}{1+C^2}\,\|u\|_{H^1}^2,$$

where $C$ is the Poincaré constant ($\|u\|_{L^2}\le C\|\nabla u\|_{L^2}$ on $H^1_0$ for bounded $\Omega$). $L$ is bounded by Cauchy–Schwarz: $|L(v)|\le \|f\|_{L^2}\|v\|_{L^2}\le \|f\|_{L^2}\|v\|_{H^1}$.

Lax–Milgram (a generalisation of the Riesz representation theorem to non-symmetric coercive $a$) then gives a unique $u\in H^1_0$ with $a(u,\cdot)=L$. When $a$ is symmetric (as here), this $u$ is the unique minimiser of

$$J(v) \;=\; \tfrac12\,a(v,v) - L(v) \;=\; \tfrac12\int|\nabla v|^2 - \int f\,v,$$

recovering the classical Dirichlet principle "the equilibrium minimises energy". Higher regularity (Schauder, $L^p$, or $H^s$ estimates) then upgrades the abstract weak solution back to a classical one when $f$ and $\partial\Omega$ are sufficiently smooth — but the existence step alone needed only Hilbert duality on $H^1_0$.

The chain in summary. $-\Delta u = f$ in distributions ⟺ $a(u,v)=L(v)$ for all $v\in H^1_0$ ⟸ Lax–Milgram on the coercive form ⟸ Riesz representation on $H^1_0$. Each arrow uses a different ingredient from §§1–5: distributional formulation, the trace theorem to define $H^1_0$, the Sobolev/Poincaré bound to make $a$ coercive, Hilbert structure to invoke Riesz.

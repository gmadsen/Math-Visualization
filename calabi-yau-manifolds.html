Calabi–Yau manifolds

Ricci-flat Kähler manifolds, the Hodge diamond, mirror symmetry, and what string theorists do at night.

1. Definition: Ricci-flat Kähler manifolds

A Calabi–Yau manifold is a compact complex Kähler manifold $X$ of complex dimension $n$ satisfying any (hence all) of the following equivalent conditions:

  • The first Chern class vanishes: $c_1(X) = 0 \in H^2(X, \mathbb{R})$.
  • The canonical bundle is trivial: $K_X = \Omega^n_X \cong \mathcal{O}_X$.
  • There is a nowhere-vanishing global holomorphic $n$-form $\Omega \in H^0(X, \Omega^n_X)$.
  • The holonomy of the Kähler metric is contained in $\mathrm{SU}(n)$ (rather than the larger $\mathrm{U}(n)$).

Some authors strengthen the definition by also requiring $h^{i,0}(X) = 0$ for $0 < i < n$, which excludes complex tori (so that ‘CY 3-fold’ in the strong sense means simply-connected with $\pi_1 = 0$). We use the broad definition unless noted.

The names attached to this object are not interchangeable. Calabi's conjecture (1954) asked whether every Kähler class on a manifold with $c_1 = 0$ contains a Ricci-flat representative. Yau's theorem (1976) proved it: there is a unique Ricci-flat Kähler metric in each Kähler cohomology class. The bridge from algebra ($c_1 = 0$, a topological cohomological condition) to geometry (an actual Einstein metric, $\mathrm{Ric} = 0$) is the central fact of the subject. Note: Ricci-flat is not flat — a K3 surface has nonzero Riemann curvature, only its trace vanishes.

2. The Hodge diamond

The cohomology of any compact Kähler manifold decomposes (Hodge decomposition) as $H^k(X, \mathbb{C}) = \bigoplus_{p+q=k} H^{p,q}(X)$, with $H^{p,q}(X) = H^q(X, \Omega^p_X)$. The numbers $h^{p,q} = \dim H^{p,q}$ form the Hodge diamond, displayed as a square rotated $45°$ with $h^{p,q}$ at position $(p, q)$. Two general symmetries hold for any Kähler manifold:

  • Hodge symmetry: $h^{p,q} = h^{q,p}$ (complex conjugation swaps the two summands).
  • Serre duality: $h^{p,q} = h^{n-p, n-q}$ (pair via $\wedge \Omega$ on a CY, or via $K_X$ in general).

For a CY $n$-fold, triviality of $K_X$ collapses Serre duality to the symmetry of the diamond about its centre. In addition, $h^{n,0} = h^{0,0} = 1$ (the holomorphic volume form $\Omega$ generates $H^0(K_X)$, and connectedness gives $h^{0,0} = 1$). Compactness plus connectedness force the corners. 3. Examples: elliptic curves, K3, the quintic

The Calabi–Yau condition is restrictive enough that the early examples are isolated landmarks rather than a continuum.

Dimension 1: elliptic curves

A smooth projective curve has trivial canonical bundle iff $\deg K_C = 2g-2 = 0$, i.e. $g = 1$. Over $\mathbb{C}$ every such curve is a complex torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ — the simplest CY. There is a 1-parameter family parametrised by $\tau \in \mathbb{H}/\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$, the moduli space of complex structures. See elliptic curves.

Dimension 2: K3 surfaces

The name is Kummer–Kähler–Kodaira (with a nod to the mountain). A K3 surface is a simply-connected compact complex surface with $K_X \cong \mathcal{O}_X$. The standard projective example is a smooth quartic in $\mathbb{P}^3$ — adjunction gives $K_X = \mathcal{O}(4-3-1) = \mathcal{O}$. All K3 surfaces are diffeomorphic; the Hodge diamond is fixed:

          0   0

            1

and $\chi = 24$, $b_2 = 22$ (with intersection form $E_8(-1)^2 \oplus U^3$, the famous K3 lattice). Unlike elliptic curves, K3s come in a 20-dimensional moduli space of complex structures.

Dimension 3: the quintic and its zoo

The quintic 3-fold $\{f_5(x_0,\ldots,x_4) = 0\} \subset \mathbb{P}^4$ is the prototypical CY 3-fold, with Hodge numbers $(h^{1,1}, h^{2,1}) = (1, 101)$, hence $\chi = -200$. The 101-dimensional space of complex structures on the quintic is parameterised, modulo $\mathrm{PGL}_5$ action on the equation, by a $101$-dimensional variety. Other complete-intersection CY 3-folds in products of projective spaces give thousands of distinct Hodge-number pairs; even more come from hypersurfaces in toric Fano 4-folds (Batyrev–Borisov), whose enumeration produced the famous Kreuzer–Skarke list of $\sim$$473$ million reflexive 4D polytopes.

The pattern continues: in every dimension, hypersurfaces of degree $n+1$ in $\mathbb{P}^n$ give CY $(n-1)$-folds, and complete intersections of multidegrees $(d_1, \ldots, d_k)$ in $\mathbb{P}^{n}$ are CY iff $\sum d_i = n+1$. Toric methods extend this further. What is striking — and largely conjectural — is whether the list of distinct CY 3-folds is finite (Yau's finiteness conjecture). At present several hundred million reflexive polytope examples are known with no obvious upper bound.

4. Periods and Picard–Fuchs

Fix a CY $n$-fold $X_0$ and deform it through a family $\pi: \mathcal{X} \to S$ over a base $S$ (a disc $\Delta$ for now). Each fibre $X_t$ has a holomorphic $n$-form $\Omega_t$, well-defined up to scale, depending holomorphically on $t$. Pick a basis $\{\gamma_i\}$ of $H_n(X_0, \mathbb{Z})$ and parallel-transport it (using the Gauss–Manin connection on the local system $R^n\pi_* \mathbb{Z}$). The periods

$\Pi_i(t) = \displaystyle\int_{\gamma_i(t)} \Omega_t$

are multivalued holomorphic functions on $S \setminus (\text{discriminant})$. Differentiating $\Omega_t$ enough times against the connection produces a linear relation:

$\mathcal{L} \Pi(t) = 0, \qquad \mathcal{L} = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^N a_k(t) \partial_t^k$.

This is the Picard–Fuchs equation. For the mirror quintic, written in a coordinate $z = (5\psi)^{-5}$, it is the order-$4$ hypergeometric operator

$\mathcal{L} = \theta^4 - 5z(5\theta + 1)(5\theta + 2)(5\theta + 3)(5\theta + 4), \qquad \theta = z\partial_z$,

and its four solutions encode the prepotential of the mirror $A$-model — the genus-$0$ Gromov–Witten generating function of the original quintic.

5. Mirror symmetry: swapping the diamond

Mirror symmetry is the conjecture (now theorem in many cases) that CY 3-folds come in pairs $(X, Y)$ such that

$h^{1,1}(X) = h^{2,1}(Y), \qquad h^{2,1}(X) = h^{1,1}(Y)$,

and physical / mathematical structures on $X$ are interchanged with structures on $Y$:

$A$-model on $X$$\leftrightarrow$$B$-model on $Y$
Kähler moduli ($h^{1,1}$ many)=complex-structure moduli ($h^{2,1}$ many)
Symplectic structure=Complex structure
Gromov–Witten invariants (curve counts)=Periods of $\Omega$
Quantum cohomology=Variation of Hodge structure
Type IIA superstring on $X$=Type IIB superstring on $Y$

Historically: in 1991 Candelas, de la Ossa, Green, and Parkes computed the prepotential of the mirror quintic from periods of the hypergeometric Picard–Fuchs equation, expanded the answer near the large-volume limit, and read off predictions for the number $n_d$ of rational curves of each degree on a generic quintic 3-fold:

$n_1 = 2{,}875, \quad n_2 = 609{,}250, \quad n_3 = 317{,}206{,}375, \quad n_4 = 242{,}467{,}530{,}000, \ldots$

The case $n_1 = 2{,}875$ matched a 19th-century enumerative calculation of Schubert. The case $n_2 = 609{,}250$ corrected an algebraic-geometry computation that had stood since the 1980s. Higher $n_d$ were unprovable by any then-existing method. Givental and Lian–Liu–Yau later proved the predictions rigorously.

Kontsevich's homological mirror symmetry proposal upgrades the Hodge-number swap to an equivalence of categories: $D^b \mathrm{Coh}(X) \simeq D^\pi \mathrm{Fuk}(Y)$, the bounded derived category of coherent sheaves on $X$ matches the (split-closed) derived Fukaya category on $Y$. This has been proved for many examples (Seidel for the genus-2 surface and quartic surface; Polishchuk–Zaslow for elliptic curves; Sheridan for Fano hypersurfaces and CY hypersurfaces in projective space).

6. String compactifications, DT, SYZ

String theory

The original motivation: superstring theory wants a 10-dimensional spacetime, but only 4 are observed. The compactification $\mathbb{R}^{1,3} \times X^6$ preserves $\mathcal{N} = 1$ supersymmetry in the effective 4D theory iff the Killing spinor equation $\nabla \xi = 0$ admits a covariantly constant spinor on $X$ — equivalently, $X$ has special holonomy contained in $\mathrm{SU}(3) \subset \mathrm{SO}(6)$. That is exactly the CY condition. Moduli of the CY (its $h^{1,1} + h^{2,1}$ shape parameters) become massless scalar fields ("moduli fields") in 4D, and engineering models of particle physics from CY compactification is a major industry — the string landscape.

Donaldson–Thomas invariants

For a CY 3-fold $X$, the moduli space of stable ideal sheaves $\mathcal{I}$ with fixed Chern character carries a perfect obstruction theory of virtual dimension 0; integrating $1$ against the virtual fundamental class gives an integer, the Donaldson–Thomas invariant $\mathrm{DT}(X, \beta, n)$. The MNOP conjecture (Maulik–Nekrasov–Okounkov–Pandharipande, partly proved) says these are equivalent to Gromov–Witten invariants of $X$ via an explicit generating-function identity, exhibiting a stable-pairs / curve-counting correspondence. Under mirror symmetry, DT on $X$ corresponds to GW on $Y$.

Strominger–Yau–Zaslow

The geometric explanation for why mirror pairs exist at all: SYZ (1996) conjectures that a CY 3-fold $X$ near a maximally-unipotent boundary point of moduli admits a special-Lagrangian $T^3$-fibration $X \to B$ over a 3-base with codimension-2 discriminant locus. The mirror $Y$ is obtained by dualising the torus fibres ($T$-duality on each $T^3$). Any local fibrewise calculation on $X$ can be transported to a calculation on $Y$ — the swap of $h^{1,1}$ and $h^{2,1}$ comes from swapping the cohomology of the base with the cohomology of the fibre (via the Leray spectral sequence). SYZ has been established near the large-complex-structure limit by Gross–Wilson and developed rigorously in the toric/tropical setting by Gross–Siebert.

Connections

Calabi–Yau geometry has been central to algebraic and differential geometry since Yau's 1976 proof, and central to physics since the 1985 paper of Candelas–Horowitz–Strominger–Witten on string compactification. The open frontiers are still vast: classification of CY 3-folds (Yau's finiteness conjecture remains open), homological mirror symmetry beyond the Fano / hypersurface cases, the SYZ programme over a generic base, and the role of CY $n$-folds for $n \geq 4$ in F-theory.

  • Differential forms — Kähler differentials — the holomorphic $n$-form $\Omega$ that defines a CY lives in $H^0(X, \Omega^n_X)$.
  • Algebraic de Rham — Hodge numbers — the Hodge diamond of §2 and Serre duality are special cases of the Hodge / Lefschetz package.
  • Elliptic curves — the dimension-1 case, where the period $\tau$ already exhibits the modular features that generalise to higher-dimensional CY.
  • Moduli spaces — the $h^{1,1}$ Kähler moduli and $h^{2,1}$ complex-structure moduli are tangent directions to families of CY, and their global geometry is largely uncharted.
  • Mirror symmetry — the dedicated topic that picks up where §5 leaves off, including homological mirror symmetry and the SYZ programme.

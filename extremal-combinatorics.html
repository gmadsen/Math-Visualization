Extremal combinatorics

How dense can a graph be before it must contain a triangle? How many edges force a $K_{2,2}$? How big an antichain fits in the Boolean lattice? Extremal combinatorics asks "what is the maximum size of a structure avoiding a forbidden pattern?" — and answers in sharp asymptotics. The five landmark theorems on this page (Turán, Kővári–Sós–Turán, Erdős–Stone–Simonovits, Ramsey, Sperner) plus the removal-lemma machinery sit at the centre of the field; together they exhibit a recurring move where a counting inequality compresses local restrictions into a global density bound.

1. Turán's theorem

The founding result of extremal graph theory. Fix integers $r \ge 1$ and $n \ge 1$. The Turán number $\mathrm{ex}(n, K_{r+1})$ is the maximum number of edges in a simple graph on $n$ vertices that contains no copy of the complete graph $K_{r+1}$. Turán (1941) determined it exactly.

Theorem (Turán, 1941). For all $n, r \ge 1$, $$\mathrm{ex}(n, K_{r+1}) \;=\; \Big(1 - \tfrac{1}{r}\Big)\,\frac{n^2}{2} \;-\; O(1),$$ and the unique extremal graph is the Turán graph $T(n, r)$ — the complete $r$-partite graph on $n$ vertices with parts of sizes as equal as possible.

The Turán graph $T(n, r)$

Partition $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ into $r$ parts $V_1, \ldots, V_r$ with $|V_i| \in \{\lfloor n/r \rfloor, \lceil n/r \rceil\}$. Connect $u$ to $v$ iff they lie in different parts. So $T(n, r)$ has no independent set of size $r+1$ inside any single part — actually it has no $K_{r+1}$ at all, because $K_{r+1}$ would require $r+1$ vertices pairwise adjacent, hence in $r+1$ distinct parts, contradiction. The number of edges is $$e(T(n, r)) \;=\; \binom{n}{2} - \sum_{i=1}^{r} \binom{|V_i|}{2}.$$ When $r \mid n$ this simplifies to $\frac{r-1}{r} \cdot \frac{n^2}{2}$, the limiting density.

Proof idea — vertex-shifting / weight-pushing

Among all $K_{r+1}$-free graphs on $n$ vertices with the maximum number of edges, take one whose largest independent neighbourhood is as large as possible. A short argument shows that for non-adjacent $u, v$ with $\deg(u) \ge \deg(v)$, replacing $v$ by a copy of $u$ does not decrease the edge count and preserves $K_{r+1}$-freeness. Iterating produces a complete multi-partite graph; balancing the parts maximizes edges by convexity. This is sometimes called Zykov symmetrization.

The "right" parameter is density. Dividing both sides by $\binom{n}{2}$, Turán's theorem reads $\mathrm{ex}(n, K_{r+1}) / \binom{n}{2} \to 1 - 1/r$ as $n \to \infty$. The number $1 - 1/r$ is the Turán density $\pi(K_{r+1})$. It depends only on the chromatic number of the forbidden graph — a foreshadowing of Erdős–Stone–Simonovits in § 3.

2. Kővári–Sós–Turán & the Zarankiewicz problem

Turán's theorem handles forbidden complete graphs $K_{r+1}$, all of which have chromatic number $\ge 3$. The bipartite case ($\chi = 2$) is fundamentally different: forbidding $K_{2,2}$ allows only $O(n^{3/2})$ edges, not $\Theta(n^2)$. The first general bound is Kővári–Sós–Turán (1954).

Theorem (KST). For $2 \le s \le t$, $$\mathrm{ex}(n, K_{s,t}) \;\le\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,(t-1)^{1/s}\,(n - s + 1)\,n^{1 - 1/s} + \tfrac{s-1}{2}\,n.$$ In particular, $\mathrm{ex}(n, K_{s,t}) = O(n^{2 - 1/s})$.

Proof — double counting cherries

Let $G$ be a $K_{s,t}$-free graph on $n$ vertices with $m$ edges. Count pairs $(v, S)$ where $v \in V(G)$, $S \subseteq N(v)$, and $|S| = s$. On one side this equals $\sum_v \binom{\deg(v)}{s}$; by convexity (Jensen) this is at least $n \binom{2m/n}{s}$. On the other side, each $s$-subset $S$ of vertices is the neighbourhood of at most $t-1$ vertices (else $S$ has $t$ common neighbours, forming $K_{s,t}$). So $\sum_v \binom{\deg(v)}{s} \le (t-1) \binom{n}{s}$. Solving for $m$ gives the stated bound.

The Zarankiewicz problem

The bipartite version: $z(m, n; s, t)$ is the maximum number of $1$s in an $m \times n$ $0/1$-matrix with no $s \times t$ all-ones sub-matrix. Equivalently, maximum edges in a bipartite graph $G \subseteq K_{m, n}$ with no $K_{s, t}$. KST gives $z(n, n; 2, 2) = O(n^{3/2})$; the constant is sharp via the Erdős–Rényi polarity construction: vertices are points and lines of a projective plane $\mathrm{PG}(2, q)$, edges given by incidence. This achieves $\Theta(n^{3/2})$, matching KST up to constants.

3. Erdős–Stone–Simonovits

What if we forbid an arbitrary graph $H$? The answer involves only one number: the chromatic number $\chi(H)$.

Theorem (Erdős–Stone, 1946; Erdős–Simonovits, 1966). For any graph $H$ with at least one edge, $$\mathrm{ex}(n, H) \;=\; \Big(1 - \tfrac{1}{\chi(H) - 1}\Big)\,\frac{n^2}{2} \;+\; o(n^2).$$ Equivalently, $\pi(H) := \lim_{n \to \infty} \mathrm{ex}(n, H) / \binom{n}{2} = 1 - 1/(\chi(H) - 1)$.

What this says

The leading-order Turán density depends only on the chromatic number — not on the structure, not even on the number of vertices of $H$. Forbidding $K_4$ gives the same density as forbidding the Petersen graph (both have $\chi = 4$), namely $2/3$. Two consequences:

  • Bipartite case ($\chi(H) = 2$). The leading term is $0 \cdot n^2/2 = 0$, so $\mathrm{ex}(n, H) = o(n^2)$. The whole game becomes determining the lower-order rate — KST exponents, $C_{2k}$ exponents, and the celebrated Sidorenko conjecture ($\mathrm{ex}(n, H) \le \mathrm{ex}(n, \text{star})$ for bipartite $H$) live here.
  • Non-bipartite case. The Turán graph $T(n, \chi(H) - 1)$ is asymptotically extremal: it has chromatic number $\chi(H) - 1$, so it is $H$-free, and it has the right edge count.

Proof sketch — embedding via supersaturation

The lower bound is exhibited by $T(n, \chi(H) - 1)$. The upper bound: if $G$ has more than $(1 - 1/(\chi(H)-1))\,n^2/2 + \varepsilon n^2$ edges, then $G$ contains many copies of $K_{\chi(H), \chi(H), \ldots, \chi(H)}$ (the balanced complete multipartite with one part per colour class of $H$), into which $H$ embeds. The supersaturation step — "many edges $\Rightarrow$ many copies, not just one" — is the workhorse.

4. Ramsey numbers

Turán-type theorems forbid one specific subgraph globally; Ramsey-type theorems insist that any partition produces a forbidden monochromatic copy. The slogan: complete disorder is impossible.

Ramsey's theorem (graph form). For every $k, \ell \ge 2$ there exists a smallest integer $R(k, \ell)$ such that every $2$-edge-coloring of $K_{R(k,\ell)}$ contains a monochromatic $K_k$ in colour $1$ or a monochromatic $K_\ell$ in colour $2$.

The numbers $R(k, \ell)$ — and their diagonal versions $R(k) := R(k, k)$ — are notoriously hard to compute. Only a handful of exact values are known.

$R(3, 3) = 6$

The lower bound $R(3, 3) > 5$ is exhibited by the $5$-cycle $C_5$ together with its complement (also $C_5$) — neither contains a triangle. The upper bound $R(3, 3) \le 6$ is the pigeonhole gem: in any $2$-coloring of $K_6$, fix a vertex $v$; among its $5$ incident edges, three share a colour (say red); those three endpoints either form a red triangle (with $v$) or include a blue edge, which closes a blue triangle.

The probabilistic lower bound

Erdős (1947) proved $R(k, k) \ge (1 + o(1))\frac{k}{e \sqrt{2}} 2^{k/2}$ by colouring $K_n$ uniformly at random. The expected number of monochromatic $K_k$s is $\binom{n}{k} \cdot 2^{1 - \binom{k}{2}}$; if this is less than $1$ then with positive probability no monochromatic clique exists, so $R(k,k) > n$. This was the founding example of the probabilistic method: an existence proof using only an inequality on a random structure.

Schur's theorem & sum-free sets

An arithmetic Ramsey relative: for every $r$, there is $S(r)$ such that any $r$-colouring of $\{1, \ldots, S(r)\}$ contains a monochromatic solution to $x + y = z$. Equivalently, $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ cannot be partitioned into $r$ sum-free sets (those containing no $x + y = z$) once $n \ge S(r)$. Proof: apply graph Ramsey to the colouring of edges of $K_n$ by $c(\{i, j\}) := $ colour-of$(|i - j|)$; a monochromatic $K_3$ on $\{a < b < c\}$ gives the equation $(c-b) + (b-a) = (c-a)$ with the same colour on all three differences.

The exponential gap. The diagonal Ramsey bounds $\sqrt{2}^k \le R(k,k) \le 4^k$ have only been improved by lower-order factors since 1947 — until 2023, when Campos, Griffiths, Morris, and Sahasrabudhe proved $R(k,k) \le (4 - \varepsilon)^k$ for an explicit $\varepsilon > 0$. The lower bound is still essentially Erdős's. Bridging this gap is one of the great unsolved problems.

5. Sperner's theorem & the LYM inequality

Switch from graphs to set systems. The Boolean lattice $2^{[n]}$ has $2^n$ subsets of $[n] = \{1, \ldots, n\}$, ordered by inclusion. An antichain is a family $\mathcal{F} \subseteq 2^{[n]}$ no member of which contains another. How big can an antichain be?

Theorem (Sperner, 1928). For every $n \ge 1$, $$\max\{|\mathcal{F}| : \mathcal{F} \subseteq 2^{[n]}\ \text{is an antichain}\} \;=\; \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}.$$ The extremal antichains are exactly the middle layer $\binom{[n]}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$ (and, for odd $n$, the upper-middle layer).

The LYM inequality

Sperner is a corollary of the LYM inequality (Lubell, Yamamoto, Meshalkin, Bollobás), which is itself a counting argument over maximal chains in the Boolean lattice. For an antichain $\mathcal{F}$ in $2^{[n]}$, $$\sum_{F \in \mathcal{F}} \frac{1}{\binom{n}{|F|}} \;\le\; 1.$$

Proof. A maximal chain $\emptyset = C_0 \subset C_1 \subset \cdots \subset C_n = [n]$ is determined by a permutation of $[n]$: there are $n!$ of them. A given $F$ with $|F| = k$ lies on exactly $k!\,(n-k)! = n! / \binom{n}{k}$ chains. Since $\mathcal{F}$ is an antichain, no chain contains two elements of $\mathcal{F}$, so summing over $F \in \mathcal{F}$, $$\sum_{F \in \mathcal{F}} \frac{n!}{\binom{n}{|F|}} \;\le\; n!,$$ giving LYM. Sperner now follows: each summand is at least $1 / \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$, so $|\mathcal{F}| \le \binom{n}{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$.

BLYM and Bollobás's set-pair

The "B" in BLYM is Bollobás's strengthening: for any antichain $\mathcal{F}$ with $a_k = |\{F \in \mathcal{F} : |F| = k\}|$, one has $\sum_k a_k / \binom{n}{k} \le 1$. The same chain-counting works with weights, and yields the more general two-family theorem: if $A_1, \ldots, A_m$ and $B_1, \ldots, B_m$ are sets with $A_i \cap B_i = \emptyset$ but $A_i \cap B_j \ne \emptyset$ for $i \ne j$, then $\sum_i 1/\binom{|A_i|+|B_i|}{|A_i|} \le 1$. This bookkeeping argument fuels much of probabilistic combinatorics.

6. The triangle removal lemma & Szemerédi regularity

The deepest tool of modern extremal graph theory. The triangle removal lemma says: a graph that is "almost" triangle-free is in fact "almost" a triangle-free graph after deleting a small number of edges.

Theorem (Ruzsa–Szemerédi triangle removal lemma). For every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta = \delta(\varepsilon) > 0$ such that every graph $G$ on $n$ vertices containing fewer than $\delta n^3$ triangles can be made triangle-free by deleting at most $\varepsilon n^2$ edges.

Why the contrapositive matters

The reformulation is potent: if $G$ contains at least one triangle and is "robust" (you must delete $\ge \varepsilon n^2$ edges to destroy all triangles), then it contains $\ge \delta n^3$ triangles — so triangles come in supersaturated packs. This is the recurring pattern in extremal combinatorics: a single forbidden copy compresses to a quadratic-density-many copies as a counting threshold.

Application: Roth's theorem on $3$-term progressions

The triangle removal lemma yields Roth's theorem (1953): every set $A \subseteq \{1, \ldots, N\}$ free of $3$-term arithmetic progressions has size $|A| = o(N)$. Sketch: build a tripartite graph on three copies of $\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z}$ with edges $(x, y)$ if $y - x \in A$, $(y, z)$ if $z - y \in A$, $(x, z)$ if $(z - x)/2 \in A$. Triangles correspond to $3$-APs in $A$; trivial triangles ($x=y=z$) number exactly $|A| \cdot N$, are edge-disjoint, and force the graph to be supersaturated — so by removal there is a non-trivial $3$-AP unless $|A|/N \to 0$.

Szemerédi regularity (the engine room)

Regularity Lemma (Szemerédi, 1975). For every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $K = K(\varepsilon)$ such that every graph $G$ on $n$ vertices admits a partition $V = V_0 \cup V_1 \cup \cdots \cup V_k$ with $k \le K$, $|V_0| \le \varepsilon n$, $|V_i| = |V_j|$ for $i, j \ge 1$, and all but $\varepsilon k^2$ pairs $(V_i, V_j)$ are $\varepsilon$-regular (their bipartite density is robust to taking large sub-pairs).

Translation: every dense graph looks, when viewed at the right resolution, like a quasi-random graph between roughly equal blobs. The bound $K(\varepsilon)$ is a tower of twos of height $\varepsilon^{-O(1)}$ — Gowers (1997) showed this is sharp. Counterintuitively, it is the partition, not the blocks, that does the work: a counting lemma converts the regularity decomposition into approximate copies of any fixed pattern.

The whole package. Regularity + Counting + Removal is the standard argumentative loop. Regularity decomposes $G$; the counting lemma estimates copies of a fixed $H$ in regular pairs; if $H$-copies are scarce in $G$, all "rich" pairs can be cleaned out, removing few edges and leaving an $H$-free graph. Roth, Szemerédi's theorem on long APs, the Green–Tao theorem on primes — all run on this loop.

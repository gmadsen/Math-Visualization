Enumerative combinatorics

How many things are there? From $\binom{n}{k}$ and stars-and-bars through inclusion–exclusion, generating functions, and permutation statistics, the engine of counting is bijection: turn a hard count into an easy one by mapping onto something you already know.

1. Binomials, multinomials, and stars-and-bars

The most fundamental count is the binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{k}$ — the number of $k$-element subsets of $\{1,\ldots,n\}$. Two equivalent definitions:

$\displaystyle \binom{n}{k} \;=\; \frac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!} \;=\; \frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)}{k!}.$

The first version is symmetric: $\binom{n}{k}=\binom{n}{n-k}$, mirroring the bijection "subset $\leftrightarrow$ complement". The second is the recipe: arrange $k$ distinguishable choices in $n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)$ ways, then quotient by $k!$ to forget the order. The two together yield Pascal's recurrence:

$\displaystyle \binom{n}{k} \;=\; \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k},$

by partitioning $k$-subsets of $[n]$ according to whether they contain the element $n$ or not.

Multinomials

If you want to split $n$ distinguishable objects into ordered bins of sizes $k_1, \ldots, k_r$ (with $k_1+\cdots+k_r = n$), the count is the multinomial coefficient:

$\displaystyle \binom{n}{k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_r} \;=\; \frac{n!}{k_1!\,k_2!\cdots k_r!}.$

It is symmetric in the $k_i$'s. Setting $r=2$ recovers the binomial. The classic application: the number of distinct anagrams of MISSISSIPPI is $\binom{11}{1,4,4,2} = 11!/(1!\,4!\,4!\,2!) = 34650$.

Stars-and-bars

How many ways are there to write $n$ as an ordered sum $x_1 + \cdots + x_r$ with each $x_i \ge 0$? The bijection is the stars-and-bars trick: line up $n$ stars, then drop $r-1$ bars among them to mark the boundaries between bins. Each bin can be empty, and the bars can sit anywhere among the $n+r-1$ slots:

$\displaystyle \#\{\text{weak compositions of }n\text{ into }r\text{ parts}\} \;=\; \binom{n+r-1}{r-1}.$

Example: $n=4, r=3$ gives $\binom{6}{2}=15$ solutions to $x_1+x_2+x_3=4$. Counts of multisets, monomials of fixed degree in fixed variables, and lattice points in simplices all reduce to stars-and-bars.

2. Inclusion–exclusion and derangements

How big is a union? For two sets, $|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|$. The general formula corrects for over-counting at every level of intersection:

$\displaystyle \left| \bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i \right| \;=\; \sum_{\emptyset \ne S \subseteq [n]} (-1)^{|S|+1} \left| \bigcap_{i \in S} A_i \right|.$

Equivalently — and often more useful — the count of elements in none of the $A_i$ is

$\displaystyle \left| \overline{A_1} \cap \cdots \cap \overline{A_n} \right| \;=\; \sum_{S \subseteq [n]} (-1)^{|S|} \left| \bigcap_{i \in S} A_i \right|.$

The proof is one line: each element $x$ in the universe contributes $(1 - 1)^{|T(x)|} = 0$ if it lies in any $A_i$ (where $T(x) = \{i : x \in A_i\}$) and $1$ if it lies in none.

The derangement count

A derangement of $[n]$ is a permutation $\sigma$ with $\sigma(i) \ne i$ for every $i$. Let $A_i$ be the set of permutations fixing $i$. Then $|A_i| = (n-1)!$, $|A_i \cap A_j| = (n-2)!$, and in general $|\bigcap_{i \in S} A_i| = (n-|S|)!$. Inclusion–exclusion:

$\displaystyle D_n \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} (n-k)! \;=\; n!\sum_{k=0}^n \frac{(-1)^k}{k!}.$

So $D_n / n! \to e^{-1} \approx 0.3679$: the probability that a uniformly random permutation has no fixed points stabilises to $1/e$. The first few values are $D_1=0, D_2=1, D_3=2, D_4=9, D_5=44, D_6=265, \ldots$.

3. Recurrences and generating functions

A sequence $a_0, a_1, a_2, \ldots$ becomes a single object once you encode it as a power series. Two encodings are standard:

$\displaystyle A(x) \;=\; \sum_{n\ge 0} a_n x^n \quad (\text{ordinary}), \qquad \hat A(x) \;=\; \sum_{n\ge 0} a_n \frac{x^n}{n!} \quad (\text{exponential}).$

Operations on sequences become operations on series: shifting, convolution, scaling. A linear recurrence becomes a rational function; a convolution recurrence becomes a polynomial equation in $A(x)$.

The Catalan numbers

The Catalan numbers $C_0, C_1, C_2, \ldots = 1, 1, 2, 5, 14, 42, \ldots$ count many things: Dyck paths, triangulations, binary trees, non-crossing partitions. The fundamental recurrence is

$\displaystyle C_{n+1} \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{n} C_k\,C_{n-k}.$

(A non-empty Dyck path of $2(n+1)$ steps starts with an up-step, returns to the axis at some point, splitting into a Dyck path of length $2k$ and one of length $2(n-k)$.) Multiplying by $x^{n+1}$ and summing,

$\displaystyle C(x) - 1 \;=\; x\,C(x)^2.$

This is a quadratic in $C(x)$, with the branch chosen so $C(0)=1$:

$\displaystyle C(x) \;=\; \frac{1 - \sqrt{1 - 4x}}{2x}.$

Expanding $\sqrt{1 - 4x}$ via the generalised binomial theorem and simplifying yields the closed form:

$\displaystyle C_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n+1}\binom{2n}{n}.$

4. Permutation statistics

$|S_n| = n!$ is the trivial count. The interesting questions are refined: how many permutations have $k$ inversions, $k$ descents, $k$ cycles? Each statistic gives a polynomial whose specialisation at $x=1$ is $n!$.

Inversions

An inversion of $\sigma \in S_n$ is a pair $i < j$ with $\sigma(i) > \sigma(j)$. The total inversion count $\mathrm{inv}(\sigma)$ measures how far $\sigma$ is from the identity. The generating polynomial

$\displaystyle \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} q^{\mathrm{inv}(\sigma)} \;=\; [n]_q! \;=\; \prod_{k=1}^n \frac{1 - q^k}{1 - q}$

is the $q$-factorial. At $q=1$ it specialises to $n!$; at $q=0$ it gives $1$ (only the identity has zero inversions).

Descents and Eulerian numbers

A descent of $\sigma$ is a position $i$ with $\sigma(i) > \sigma(i+1)$. The Eulerian number $\langle {n\atop k}\rangle$ counts permutations with exactly $k$ descents. Worpitzky's identity packages them: $x^n = \sum_k \langle {n\atop k}\rangle \binom{x+k}{n}$.

Cycles and Stirling numbers

The (signless) Stirling number of the first kind $c(n, k)$ counts permutations of $[n]$ with exactly $k$ cycles. Their generating identity is the rising factorial:

$\displaystyle x(x+1)(x+2)\cdots(x+n-1) \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{n} c(n,k)\,x^k.$

At $x=1$ this is $n!$, recovering $\sum_k c(n,k) = n!$. The cycle-index polynomial of $S_n$ encodes the full cycle-type distribution and is the engine behind Pólya enumeration of colourings under group actions.

5. Integer partitions and Euler's product

A partition of $n$ is a way to write $n = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_r$ with $\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_r \ge 1$. The function $p(n)$ counts these. The first few values:

$\displaystyle p(0) = 1,\; p(1) = 1,\; p(2) = 2,\; p(3) = 3,\; p(4) = 5,\; p(5) = 7,\; p(6) = 11,\; p(7) = 15,\; \ldots$

There is no closed form, but Hardy–Ramanujan's asymptotic formula $p(n) \sim \frac{1}{4n\sqrt{3}}\,e^{\pi\sqrt{2n/3}}$ is one of the gems of analytic combinatorics. The starting point is Euler's infinite product:

$\displaystyle \sum_{n\ge 0} p(n)\,q^n \;=\; \prod_{k\ge 1} \frac{1}{1 - q^k}.$

The factor $1/(1-q^k) = 1 + q^k + q^{2k} + \cdots$ chooses how many parts of size $k$ to use; multiplying across all $k$ enumerates partitions exactly.

Young diagrams

A partition $\lambda$ is drawn as a left-justified array of boxes, the Young diagram. Conjugation — reflecting across the main diagonal — gives a bijection $\lambda \mapsto \lambda'$ between partitions of $n$ that preserves the count but swaps "number of parts" with "largest part".

Rogers–Ramanujan

The deepest classical partition identities are due to Rogers and Ramanujan. They assert the surprising equinumeration:

Rogers–Ramanujan (first identity). For each $n$, the partitions of $n$ in which any two parts differ by at least $2$ equal the partitions of $n$ into parts congruent to $\pm 1 \pmod 5$.

Equivalently, as power-series identities,

$\displaystyle \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{q^{n^2}}{(1-q)(1-q^2)\cdots(1-q^n)} \;=\; \prod_{n\ge 0}\frac{1}{(1-q^{5n+1})(1-q^{5n+4})}.$

Both sides equal $\sum_{n} a_n q^n$ where $a_n$ is the common count. There is no easy combinatorial bijection between the two families — the original proofs are analytic, and a bijective proof (Garsia–Milne) is genuinely intricate.

6. Bijective proofs

Worked example: Catalan bijection.

The most satisfying proof of $|A| = |B|$ is a bijection $\varphi : A \to B$ — an explicit invertible map. Such a proof gives more than the count: it transports structure from one side to the other. The Catalan numbers are the prime example, with at least 200 known Catalan-counted families and a forest of bijections among them.

Dyck paths and triangulations

A Dyck path of length $2n$ is a lattice path from $(0,0)$ to $(2n,0)$ using steps $(+1,+1)$ (up) and $(+1,-1)$ (down) that never dips below the $x$-axis. There are $C_n$ of them.

A triangulation of a convex $(n+2)$-gon is a maximal set of non-crossing diagonals; equivalently, a way to cut the polygon into $n$ triangles. There are also $C_n$ of these.

The bijection: fix a base edge of the polygon. Walk the triangulation tree rooted at that edge in pre-order, recording an "up" step whenever you enter a child triangle and a "down" step whenever you back out. The resulting word of length $2n$ is a Dyck path.

RSK and Young tableaux

The Robinson–Schensted–Knuth correspondence is a bijection between non-negative integer matrices and pairs $(P, Q)$ of semistandard Young tableaux of the same shape. Restricted to permutation matrices it bijects $S_n$ with pairs of standard tableaux of the same shape, yielding the spectacular identity

$\displaystyle n! \;=\; \sum_{\lambda \vdash n} f^\lambda \cdot f^\lambda \;=\; \sum_{\lambda \vdash n} (f^\lambda)^2,$

where $f^\lambda$ is the number of standard Young tableaux of shape $\lambda$. The tableau pair carries an enormous amount of structure: the shape determines the longest increasing subsequence (Schensted's theorem), and RSK underlies the entire combinatorics of symmetric functions, Schur polynomials, and representations of $S_n$.

7. Connections

The generating-function / bijective-proof / cycle-index trinity is the workhorse of combinatorial counting — a small toolkit that surfaces almost everywhere a counting question arises.

Algebra and representations

Symmetric functions and the cycle index polynomial connect counting to representation theory; the RSK correspondence and Schur–Weyl duality are the bridge to character theory of $S_n$ and $\mathrm{GL}_n$.

Probability and probabilistic method

Generating-function methods (Wilf's "snake oil", saturation) come up in probability for moment problems; probabilistic existence proofs are often the only way to count an extremal object.

Algebraic combinatorics and posets

The Möbius function on posets generalises inclusion-exclusion; matroid theory, lattice theory, and Stanley's "Combinatorics and Commutative Algebra" all rest on this.

Analytic combinatorics

Singularity analysis of generating functions (Flajolet–Sedgewick) gives asymptotics of combinatorial sequences from the analytic structure of their OGFs/EGFs — the bridge to complex analysis via residues.

Open frontiers: cluster algebras (Fomin–Zelevinsky); flag algebras (Razborov, computer-assisted extremal proofs); positivity questions (Schubert calculus, Macdonald polynomials); algebraic combinatorics via Hopf algebras and quasi-symmetric functions.

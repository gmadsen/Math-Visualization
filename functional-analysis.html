Functional analysis: Banach spaces, Hilbert spaces, and the spectral theorem

Infinite-dimensional linear algebra done with an eye on convergence. The story: pick a norm, ask for completeness, trade geometry for topology, and watch operators decompose along their spectrum.

1. Normed, Banach, Hilbert

Functional analysis begins with a confession: infinite-dimensional vector spaces are not $\mathbb{R}^n$. Different norms on the same space can give genuinely different topologies; linear functionals can be discontinuous; a bounded sequence may have no convergent subsequence at all. The fix is to work with norms and topological compatibility: that is, Banach and Hilbert spaces.

A normed space is a vector space $X$ (over $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$) equipped with a map $\|\cdot\|\colon X\to[0,\infty)$ satisfying

  • $\|x\|=0\iff x=0$ (definiteness),
  • $\|\alpha x\|=|\alpha|\,\|x\|$ (absolute homogeneity),
  • $\|x+y\|\le\|x\|+\|y\|$ (triangle inequality).

A norm makes $X$ a metric space via $d(x,y)=\|x-y\|$. A Banach space is a normed space that is complete in this metric: every Cauchy sequence converges. Completeness is what lets us write limits, series, and fixed points with a straight face — it is the switch that turns pure linear algebra into analysis.

The canonical examples

Not every complete inner-product-less normed space is a Banach space you will care about, but it is worth naming them: $(\mathbb{R},|\cdot|)$, $(\mathbb{C}^n,\|\cdot\|_p)$ for every $p\in[1,\infty]$, and finite-dimensional spaces in general are trivially Banach. The drama starts infinite-dimensionally.

Fix $1\le p\le\infty$. The sequence spaces

$$\ell^p = \Big\{(x_n)_{n\ge1}\in\mathbb{C}^{\mathbb{N}} : \|x\|_p := \big(\sum_{n}|x_n|^p\big)^{1/p} < \infty\Big\}\qquad(1\le p<\infty),$$ $$\ell^\infty = \big\{(x_n) : \|x\|_\infty := \sup_n|x_n|<\infty\big\}$$

are all Banach spaces. The key inequality behind completeness for $\ell^p$ is Minkowski's inequality $\|x+y\|_p\le\|x\|_p+\|y\|_p$, itself a consequence of Hölder's inequality

$$\sum|x_n y_n|\le\|x\|_p\|y\|_q,\qquad \tfrac1p+\tfrac1q = 1.$$

So are their continuous cousins: for a measure space $(\Omega,\mu)$,

$$L^p(\Omega,\mu) = \Big\{f : \|f\|_p = \big(\textstyle\int_\Omega |f|^p\,d\mu\big)^{1/p} <\infty\Big\}\quad(\text{modulo }\mu\text{-null functions}).$$

The proof of completeness is the Riesz–Fischer theorem; the key move is extracting a fast-Cauchy subsequence $(f_{n_k})$ with $\|f_{n_{k+1}}-f_{n_k}\|_p\le 2^{-k}$, showing pointwise a.e. convergence of $\sum(f_{n_{k+1}}-f_{n_k})$ to some $f$, then applying Fatou to $|f_{n_k}-f|^p$ to verify $\|f_{n_k}-f\|_p\to 0$. For continuous functions on a compact interval,

$$C[a,b] \text{ with } \|f\|_\infty = \sup_{t\in[a,b]}|f(t)|$$

is a Banach space — uniform limits of continuous functions are continuous. In contrast $C[a,b]$ with the $L^2$ norm is not complete; its completion is $L^2[a,b]$.

Hilbert spaces: when the norm comes from an inner product

An inner product space has a sesquilinear (or bilinear, over $\mathbb{R}$) form $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle\colon X\times X\to\mathbb{C}$ with $\langle x,x\rangle\ge 0$, zero only at $x=0$, and $\langle x,y\rangle=\overline{\langle y,x\rangle}$. Then $\|x\|:=\sqrt{\langle x,x\rangle}$ is a norm (Cauchy–Schwarz). A Hilbert space is a complete inner product space. Distinguished examples:

$$\ell^2,\qquad L^2(\Omega,\mu),\qquad\text{Sobolev }H^s(\mathbb{R}^n).$$
A norm comes from an inner product iff the parallelogram law holds: $$\|x+y\|^2+\|x-y\|^2 = 2\|x\|^2+2\|y\|^2.$$ The law fails already in $\ell^p$ for $p\ne 2$ — Hilbert structure is a very restrictive bonus on a Banach space.

Quick arithmetic on $\ell^p$: take $x=(1,0,0,\ldots)$ and $y=(0,1,0,\ldots)$. Then

$$\|x+y\|_p^2+\|x-y\|_p^2 = 2\cdot 2^{2/p},\qquad 2\|x\|_p^2+2\|y\|_p^2 = 4.$$

These agree only at $p=2$. The Jordan–von Neumann theorem promotes "parallelogram law holds" to "inner product exists, via polarization", so the logic is tight.

Equivalence of norms in finite dimensions

On $\mathbb{R}^n$ any two norms are equivalent: there are $c,C>0$ with $c\|x\|_\alpha\le\|x\|_\beta\le C\|x\|_\alpha$. In particular all finite-dimensional normed spaces are Banach, and every linear map between them is bounded. None of this survives infinite dimensions: on $C[0,1]$, $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ and $\|\cdot\|_{L^2}$ are not equivalent — uniform convergence is strictly stronger than mean-square convergence.

Separability, density, and the subspace of polynomials

A Banach space is separable if it has a countable dense subset. $\ell^p$ and $L^p[a,b]$ are separable for $1\le p<\infty$; $\ell^\infty$ is not. The Weierstrass approximation theorem (polynomials are dense in $C[a,b]$ with sup norm) and the Stone–Weierstrass theorem (any subalgebra of $C(X)$ separating points and containing constants is dense) are the first tools that populate Banach spaces with explicit bases.

Cautionary example. Not every Banach space has a Schauder basis. Enflo (1973) constructed a separable Banach space with no Schauder basis, settling a forty-year open problem; the approximation property is genuinely subtle outside the Hilbert setting.

2. Hilbert-space geometry

Hilbert space feels like $\mathbb{R}^n$ with a countable basis — angles, perpendicularity, Pythagoras, projection onto a subspace, all work. The miracle is that these survive infinite dimensions when the space is complete.

The polarization identities say the inner product is determined by the norm (when the parallelogram law holds). You get the geometry back from the metric.

Orthogonal projection

Let $H$ be a Hilbert space and $M\subset H$ a closed subspace. For every $x\in H$ there is a unique $Px\in M$ minimizing $\|x-m\|$ over $m\in M$, and it is characterized by

$$x - Px \perp M,\qquad\text{i.e. }\langle x-Px, m\rangle = 0\ \forall m\in M.$$

The map $P\colon H\to M$ is linear, idempotent ($P^2=P$), self-adjoint ($P^*=P$), and satisfies the Pythagorean identity

$$\|x\|^2 = \|Px\|^2 + \|x-Px\|^2.$$

Equivalently, $H = M\oplus M^\perp$ for every closed $M$. Non-closed subspaces do not split this way, and this is the first place "complete" buys geometry.

As always, the uniqueness proof is free: if $m_1,m_2\in M$ both minimize, $m=(m_1+m_2)/2\in M$, and the parallelogram law on $x-m_1, x-m_2$ gives $\|m_1-m_2\|^2=0$. The existence proof needs a minimizing sequence $(m_n)$ and the parallelogram identity to make it Cauchy — completeness of $M$ does the rest.

Orthonormal bases and Gram–Schmidt

A family $\{e_i\}_{i\in I}\subset H$ is orthonormal if $\langle e_i,e_j\rangle=\delta_{ij}$. It is an orthonormal basis when the closed linear span is all of $H$. Then every $x\in H$ has a Fourier expansion

$$x = \sum_{i\in I}\langle x,e_i\rangle e_i,\qquad \|x\|^2 = \sum_i|\langle x,e_i\rangle|^2\quad(\text{Parseval}).$$

Before that: for any orthonormal set, Bessel's inequality $\sum|\langle x,e_i\rangle|^2\le\|x\|^2$ holds. Equality (for every $x$) is equivalent to the set being an orthonormal basis (Parseval's theorem); equivalently, the only vector orthogonal to all $e_i$ is $0$. A separable Hilbert space has a countable orthonormal basis and is unitarily isomorphic to $\ell^2$ — all separable Hilbert spaces are, up to isomorphism, the Hilbert space.

3. Bounded operators and the operator norm

Before specializing to Hilbert space, take a moment with the operator norm itself. Given normed spaces $X,Y$ and a linear map $T\colon X\to Y$, the quantity

$$\|T\| = \sup_{\|x\|\le 1}\|Tx\|_Y = \sup_{x\ne 0}\frac{\|Tx\|_Y}{\|x\|_X} = \inf\{C\ge 0 : \|Tx\|\le C\|x\|\ \forall x\}$$

is three definitions of the same thing. The finiteness of any one of them is what we mean by bounded. For linear maps, bounded $\iff$ continuous $\iff$ Lipschitz with constant $\|T\|$ — so one number does all the work of measuring $T$'s stretching, $T$'s continuity, and $T$'s Lipschitz constant simultaneously.

Writing $B(X,Y)=\{T\colon X\to Y \text{ bounded linear}\}$, the operator norm makes it a normed space. A foundational fact, often taken for granted: $B(X,Y)$ is complete whenever $Y$ is, regardless of completeness of $X$. Completeness transfers from the target, not the source. The proof goes: given Cauchy $(T_n)$, for each $x$ the sequence $(T_n x)$ is Cauchy in $Y$ so it converges to some $Tx$; linearity transfers; then uniformity in $\|x\|\le 1$ upgrades pointwise convergence to operator-norm convergence.

Computing operator norms of $2\times 2$ matrices

On $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the Euclidean norm, the operator norm of a matrix is its largest singular value: $\|T\|=\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(T^\top T)}$. For symmetric $T$, $T^\top T = T^2$ and the singular values are $|\lambda_i|$, so $\|T\|=\max_i|\lambda_i|$. For a general matrix, diagonal entries can drastically under- or overestimate the true operator norm — stretching can be bigger than any single entry (and smaller than the largest singular value when there is cancellation).

4. Bounded linear operators

Let $X,Y$ be normed spaces. A linear map $T\colon X\to Y$ is bounded when

$$\|T\| := \sup_{\|x\|\le 1}\|Tx\|_Y < \infty.$$

For linear maps, bounded $\iff$ continuous $\iff$ continuous at $0$. The collection

$$B(X,Y) = \{T\colon X\to Y\text{ bounded linear}\}$$

is itself a normed space under the operator norm, and complete whenever $Y$ is — a lovely "completeness is contagious" result. When $Y=X$, $B(X)=B(X,X)$ is a Banach algebra under composition.

The dual space

The dual $X^* := B(X,\mathbb{K})$ is always a Banach space (the scalars are complete). A vector can be probed with linear functionals — this is the engine of weak convergence, duality, and optimization.

An isometry satisfies $\|Tx\|=\|x\|$ for all $x$; it is always injective, norm-preserving, not necessarily surjective (as the shift shows). A surjective isometry between Banach spaces is an isometric isomorphism.

Why "bounded" is the right word. A linear map takes $0\to 0$, so continuity at any point is equivalent to continuity at $0$. Continuity at $0$ means $Tx\to 0$ whenever $x\to 0$; that is exactly $\|Tx\|\le C\|x\|$ for some $C$. So bounded = continuous = Lipschitz with constant $\|T\|$.

Adjoints

On a Hilbert space, every $T\in B(H)$ has a unique adjoint $T^*\in B(H)$ defined by

$$\langle Tx,y\rangle = \langle x,T^*y\rangle,\qquad\forall x,y\in H.$$

Then $\|T^*\|=\|T\|$ and $\|T^*T\|=\|T\|^2$ (the $C^*$-identity). We call $T$ self-adjoint if $T^*=T$, normal if $T^*T=TT^*$, unitary if $T^*T=TT^*=I$. Self-adjoint operators have real spectrum; unitaries have spectrum on the unit circle; normal operators are the class for which the spectral theorem holds in its cleanest form.

On a Banach space, the adjoint $T^*\colon Y^*\to X^*$ lives between different spaces, defined by $(T^*\phi)(x)=\phi(Tx)$. Norms still match, $\|T^*\|=\|T\|$, but the $C^*$-identity fails without a compatible inner product.

A tiny operator algebra sampler

On the two-dimensional Hilbert space $\mathbb{C}^2$, the Pauli matrices

$$\sigma_x=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},\quad \sigma_y=\begin{pmatrix}0&-i\\i&0\end{pmatrix},\quad \sigma_z=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$$

are self-adjoint, unitary, and traceless. Each has spectrum $\{-1,+1\}$ and the two spectral projections are $(I\pm\sigma_k)/2$. This is the simplest nontrivial illustration of the spectral theorem in finite dimensions: each $\sigma_k = (+1)\cdot P_+^{(k)} + (-1)\cdot P_-^{(k)}$, and the three bases are mutually unbiased.

5. The adjoint operator

On a Hilbert space, the adjoint $T^*$ of an operator $T\in B(H)$ is the unique operator satisfying

$$\langle Tx,y\rangle = \langle x, T^*y\rangle,\qquad \forall x,y\in H.$$

Existence is a one-line application of the Riesz representation theorem: for fixed $y$, the map $x\mapsto\overline{\langle Tx,y\rangle}$ is a bounded conjugate-linear functional, so there is a unique $T^*y$ representing it.

The adjoint sorts operators into four natural classes by how $T$ and $T^*$ commute or coincide:

classdefining identityspectrum
self-adjoint (Hermitian)$T^*=T$$\sigma(T)\subset\mathbb{R}$
skew-adjoint (anti-Hermitian)$T^*=-T$$\sigma(T)\subset i\mathbb{R}$
unitary$T^*T=TT^*=I$$\sigma(T)\subset\{|z|=1\}$
normal$T^*T=TT^*$general $\subset\mathbb{C}$

Normal is the weakest: self-adjoint, skew-adjoint, and unitary operators are all normal. A normal operator on a finite-dimensional Hilbert space admits an orthonormal eigenbasis — that is the finite-dimensional spectral theorem. The operator algebraic slogan: normal $\iff$ spectral theorem applies $\iff$ unitarily diagonalizable in a generalized sense.

Polar decomposition and the Cayley transform

Every $T\in B(H)$ factors as $T=U|T|$ where $|T|=\sqrt{T^*T}\ge 0$ is self-adjoint and $U$ is a partial isometry — the polar decomposition. This is the operator-theoretic analogue of $z=e^{i\theta}|z|$ for complex numbers. The Cayley transform $U=(T-i)(T+i)^{-1}$ bijects (densely defined) self-adjoint operators with unitaries avoiding $1$; this is the device for passing from real-spectrum (self-adjoint) problems to bounded (unitary) ones.

6. The big four theorems

Four theorems, all ultimately powered by the Baire category theorem (a complete metric space is not a countable union of nowhere-dense sets) or by Zorn's lemma, organize Banach-space analysis.

Hahn–Banach (extension form)

Theorem (Hahn–Banach). Let $X$ be a real vector space, $p\colon X\to\mathbb{R}$ a sublinear functional ($p(x+y)\le p(x)+p(y),\ p(\alpha x)=\alpha p(x)$ for $\alpha\ge 0$), and $\ell\colon M\to\mathbb{R}$ a linear functional on a subspace $M$ with $\ell\le p$ on $M$. Then $\ell$ extends to $\tilde\ell\colon X\to\mathbb{R}$ linear with $\tilde\ell\le p$ on all of $X$.

Corollary: on a normed space, every bounded linear functional on a subspace extends to $X$ with the same norm. Consequence: $X^*$ separates points. This is what gives duality traction — the existence of "enough" linear functionals is not automatic and requires choice.

Hahn–Banach (separation form)

Theorem. If $A, B$ are disjoint convex subsets of a normed space, $A$ open, then there is a continuous linear functional $\ell$ and $c\in\mathbb{R}$ with $\ell(a)strictly: $\sup_A\ell<\inf_B\ell$.

Applications: existence of supporting hyperplanes, convex optimization duality, Mazur's theorem (weakly closed convex = strongly closed convex).

Open Mapping Theorem

Theorem (Banach). A surjective bounded linear map $T\colon X\to Y$ between Banach spaces is open: $T(U)$ is open for every open $U\subset X$.

Corollary (Inverse Mapping Theorem): a bijective bounded linear map between Banach spaces has a bounded inverse. So "continuous and invertible" automatically gives "continuously invertible" — no extra work. Application: an ODE operator that is bijective from $C^2[0,1]\cap$(boundary conditions) to $C[0,1]$ automatically has bounded inverse (Green's function).

Closed Graph Theorem

Theorem. Let $T\colon X\to Y$ be linear, $X,Y$ Banach. If the graph $\{(x,Tx):x\in X\}$ is closed in $X\times Y$, then $T$ is bounded.

The practical use is backwards from what one might guess: checking continuity of $T$ reduces to checking that $x_n\to x$ and $Tx_n\to y$ already forces $y=Tx$. You get the convergence $Tx_n\to Tx$ for free from closedness.

Uniform boundedness (Banach–Steinhaus)

Theorem. Let $(T_\alpha)_{\alpha\in A}\subset B(X,Y)$ with $X$ Banach. If $\sup_\alpha\|T_\alpha x\|<\infty$ for every fixed $x\in X$, then $\sup_\alpha\|T_\alpha\|<\infty$.

"Pointwise bounded ⇒ uniformly bounded" in the Banach setting. This is the tool behind pointwise limits of operators being bounded, the convergence of Fourier series at "enough" points, and the Nikishin–Stein theory.

Proof sketch — all four from Baire. Pick the set $E_n=\{x:\sup_\alpha\|T_\alpha x\|\le n\}$; each $E_n$ is closed by continuity of $T_\alpha$; their union is $X$ by hypothesis. Baire gives some $E_{n_0}$ with non-empty interior; a translation + dilation argument delivers the uniform bound. The Open Mapping and Closed Graph proofs run along parallel lines.

Where the big four actually get used

  • Hahn–Banach → existence of Green's functions, Lagrange multipliers in infinite dimensions, continuous extension of densely-defined functionals, and the entire theory of weak topologies.
  • Open Mapping → boundedness of $T^{-1}$ without computing it; automatic continuity of inverse operators in PDE; Banach isomorphism theorem.
  • Closed Graph → if an operator factors through a Banach space and each factor has closed graph, so does the composition. Used repeatedly to promote a-priori estimates into continuity.
  • Banach–Steinhaus → divergence of Fourier series at a dense $G_\delta$ of points (du Bois-Reymond), existence of pathological operators, resonance theorems.

7. Compact operators and the spectrum

A bounded operator $T\colon X\to Y$ is compact if $T(B_X)$ is relatively compact in $Y$, where $B_X$ is the closed unit ball. Equivalently, $T$ sends bounded sequences to sequences with norm-convergent subsequences. Compact operators are the infinite-dimensional analogue of "finite-rank-ish" — they are a closed two-sided ideal in $B(X)$ and the norm-closure of finite-rank operators on a Hilbert space (and on many Banach spaces with the approximation property).

Classical examples of compact operators

  • Finite-rank operators are always compact. On a Hilbert space (and any Banach space with the approximation property), the closure of finite-rank operators in operator norm equals the compacts.
  • Integral operators with continuous kernels $(Tf)(x)=\int_0^1 k(x,y)f(y)\,dy$ on $C[0,1]$ or $L^2[0,1]$ are compact when $k\in C([0,1]^2)$ or $k\in L^2([0,1]^2)$ (Arzelà–Ascoli / Hilbert–Schmidt).
  • Inclusion $H^1[0,1]\hookrightarrow L^2[0,1]$ is compact (Rellich). This is the reason compactness appears in PDE: a bound in a stronger norm gives you a convergent subsequence in a weaker norm.
  • Inverses of elliptic differential operators on bounded domains are compact by Rellich plus elliptic regularity, which explains why Laplacian eigenvalues form an unbounded discrete sequence $0<\lambda_1<\lambda_2\le\cdots\to\infty$ (apply Riesz–Schauder to $(-\Delta)^{-1}$).

Spectrum

For $T\in B(X)$, the resolvent set is $\rho(T)=\{\lambda\in\mathbb{C}:T-\lambda I\text{ invertible in }B(X)\}$; the spectrum is $\sigma(T)=\mathbb{C}\setminus\rho(T)$. It is always a compact non-empty subset of $\{|\lambda|\le\|T\|\}$.

The spectrum subdivides into:

  • Point spectrum $\sigma_p(T)$: $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue, $T-\lambda I$ not injective.
  • Continuous spectrum: $T-\lambda I$ injective, range dense, not closed.
  • Residual spectrum: $T-\lambda I$ injective, range not dense.

Riesz–Schauder

Theorem. If $T\in B(X)$ is compact on an infinite-dimensional Banach space, then:
  • $0\in\sigma(T)$;
  • every $\lambda\in\sigma(T)\setminus\{0\}$ is an eigenvalue of finite geometric multiplicity;
  • $\sigma(T)$ is at most countable, with $0$ as the only possible accumulation point.

So compact operators are almost diagonalizable: outside $0$, the spectrum is a sequence tending to $0$, and each non-zero eigenvalue has a finite-dimensional generalized eigenspace.

Fredholm alternative

Theorem. If $T$ is compact and $\lambda\ne 0$, then $T-\lambda I$ is Fredholm of index $0$: $\dim\ker(T-\lambda I) = \dim\mathrm{coker}(T-\lambda I) < \infty$, and exactly one of the following holds:
  • $T-\lambda I$ is a bijection, so $(T-\lambda I)x = y$ has a unique solution for every $y$;
  • $\ker(T-\lambda I)\ne\{0\}$, and $(T-\lambda I)x = y$ is solvable iff $y\perp\ker(T^*-\bar\lambda I)$.

This is the abstract version of the existence/uniqueness dichotomy for integral equations: either the homogeneous equation has only zero, or the inhomogeneous equation has solutions exactly when the right-hand side satisfies finitely many orthogonality conditions.

Trace-class and Hilbert–Schmidt

Two special ideals of compact operators sit inside $B(H)$:

  • Hilbert–Schmidt operators: $\|T\|_{HS}^2=\sum_n\|Te_n\|^2=\sum_{m,n}|\langle Te_m,e_n\rangle|^2<\infty$. Form a Hilbert space themselves; integral operators with $L^2$ kernels are the standard examples.
  • Trace-class operators: $\|T\|_1 = \sum s_n(T)<\infty$ where $s_n$ are singular values. Then $\mathrm{tr}(T)=\sum\langle Te_n,e_n\rangle$ is basis-independent.

Inclusions: (finite rank) $\subset$ trace-class $\subset$ Hilbert–Schmidt $\subset$ compact $\subset$ bounded, all proper on an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space.

8. The spectral theorem

The spectral theorem is the centerpiece: it says a self-adjoint (or more generally normal) operator can be decomposed along its spectrum, in a way that reduces the study of $T$ to the study of scalar multiplication. Four tiers:

Rayleigh quotients and the variational principle

For self-adjoint $T$ on a Hilbert space, the Rayleigh quotient is $R_T(x)=\langle Tx,x\rangle/\|x\|^2$. Critical points of $R_T$ on the unit sphere are eigenvectors; the critical values are the eigenvalues. The Courant–Fischer min-max principle for compact self-adjoint operators on Hilbert space says the ordered eigenvalues $\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\cdots$ (by absolute value, with multiplicity) satisfy

$$\lambda_k = \max_{\dim V=k}\min_{x\in V,\|x\|=1}\langle Tx,x\rangle.$$

This is how you prove existence of the eigenvectors in the first place: iterate "maximize $R_T$ on the orthogonal complement of the previous eigenvectors".

Finite-dimensional: normal matrices diagonalize

Theorem. A matrix $A\in\mathbb{C}^{n\times n}$ is normal ($AA^*=A^*A$) $\iff$ there is a unitary $U$ and a diagonal $D$ with $A=UDU^*$. Equivalently, $\mathbb{C}^n$ has an orthonormal eigenbasis of $A$. In the self-adjoint case ($A=A^*$), the eigenvalues are real.
$$A = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i v_i v_i^*$$

is the spectral decomposition: a sum of rank-one orthogonal projections weighted by eigenvalues. The widget below computes this for a $3\times 3$ symmetric matrix.

Spectral radius and the Gelfand formula

For $T\in B(X)$, the spectral radius $r(T)=\sup\{|\lambda|:\lambda\in\sigma(T)\}$ satisfies

$$r(T) = \lim_{n\to\infty}\|T^n\|^{1/n}\le\|T\|.$$

This "Gelfand's formula" is a genuine surprise: the spectrum — a complex-analytic, algebra-theoretic object — is computed from the norm of iterates. For normal operators on Hilbert space, equality holds: $r(T)=\|T\|$. For self-adjoint $T$, $\|T\|=\sup_{\|x\|=1}|\langle Tx,x\rangle|$.

Compact self-adjoint operators

Theorem (Hilbert–Schmidt). Let $T$ be a compact self-adjoint operator on a Hilbert space $H$. Then $H$ has an orthonormal basis $(e_n)$ of eigenvectors with real eigenvalues $\lambda_n\to 0$, and $$Tx=\sum_n\lambda_n\langle x,e_n\rangle e_n.$$

This is the infinite-dimensional diagonalization theorem, and it is proven by the Courant–Fischer variational principle: the top eigenvalue is $\max_{\|x\|=1}\langle Tx,x\rangle$, then induct on orthogonal complements.

A key corollary: the Laplacian on a bounded smooth domain $\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ with Dirichlet boundary conditions has an orthonormal basis of eigenfunctions $\{\phi_k\}\subset H^1_0(\Omega)$ with $-\Delta\phi_k=\lambda_k\phi_k$ and $\lambda_k\uparrow\infty$. This is the spectral theorem applied to $(-\Delta)^{-1}$, which is compact self-adjoint on $L^2$ by Rellich. It is the bedrock for separation-of-variables, heat kernel expansions, and the mathematics of vibrating drums.

Bounded self-adjoint operators: spectral measures

For a general bounded self-adjoint $T$ on a Hilbert space $H$, there may be no eigenvalues at all (think of $M_x$ on $L^2[0,1]$: $\sigma(M_x)=[0,1]$, $\sigma_p=\emptyset$). But there is a projection-valued measure $E\colon\mathrm{Borel}(\mathbb{R})\to B(H)$ supported on $\sigma(T)\subset\mathbb{R}$ such that

$$T = \int_{\sigma(T)} \lambda\,dE(\lambda),\qquad f(T) = \int f(\lambda)\,dE(\lambda)\ \text{for}\ f\in C(\sigma(T)).$$

Formally $E(B)$ is the orthogonal projection onto the closed subspace "of vectors whose spectrum lies in $B$". Concretely, for the multiplication operator $M_g$ on $L^2(X,\mu)$, $E(B) = M_{\mathbf{1}_{g^{-1}(B)}}$: the projection onto functions supported where $g$ lands in $B$. Every self-adjoint operator is cohomologous, in this precise sense, to a multiplication operator — see the statement below.

The projections $E(B)$ satisfy $E(B_1\cap B_2)=E(B_1)E(B_2)$; the finite-sum spectral decomposition $T=\sum\lambda_i P_i$ is the special case where $E$ is atomic. The functional calculus $f\mapsto f(T)$ extends continuously from polynomials to all bounded Borel functions on $\sigma(T)$.

Multiplication-operator model

Spectral theorem (multiplication form). Every bounded self-adjoint $T$ on a separable Hilbert space $H$ is unitarily equivalent to a multiplication operator $M_g$ on $L^2(X,\mu)$ for some $\sigma$-finite measure space and $g\in L^\infty(X,\mu)$ real-valued: $$UTU^{-1}=M_g,\qquad U\colon H\xrightarrow{\sim} L^2(X,\mu).$$

This is the statement mathematicians usually reach for. It says: every self-adjoint operator "is" multiplication by a real function, after a change of basis. Quantum mechanics lives off this theorem — position, momentum, and Hamiltonian all become multiplication operators in the right picture.

Continuous functional calculus

Once we have spectral measures, we get a homomorphism of Banach algebras

$$C(\sigma(T))\hookrightarrow B(H),\qquad f\mapsto f(T)=\int_{\sigma(T)} f(\lambda)\,dE(\lambda).$$

It satisfies $\overline{f}(T) = f(T)^*$ and $\|f(T)\|=\|f\|_{\sup,\sigma(T)}$. So for self-adjoint $T$ with $\sigma(T)\subset[0,\infty)$, $\sqrt{T}$ is unambiguously defined; for the Laplacian on a compact domain, $e^{-tT}$ is the heat semigroup; for the Hamiltonian, $e^{-itH}$ is unitary evolution. The calculus extends (via bounded Borel functions) to indicator functions $\mathbf{1}_B(T)=E(B)$, giving direct access to the spectral projections.

Unbounded self-adjoint operators

Most interesting operators (differential operators on $L^2$) are unbounded: they are defined only on a dense domain $D(T)\subset H$, and bookkeeping about domains becomes essential. The right notion is that $T$ is self-adjoint if $T=T^*$ as unbounded operators (domains included). The spectral theorem, Stone's theorem on one-parameter unitary groups, and the Schrödinger equation all live in this setting — but the price is that "self-adjoint" is genuinely stronger than "symmetric" ($\langle Tx,y\rangle=\langle x,Ty\rangle$ on $D(T)$), and von Neumann's deficiency-index theory is the tool for deciding when a symmetric operator extends to a self-adjoint one.

9. Classifying the spectrum: point, continuous, residual

The spectrum $\sigma(T)$ tells you where $T-\lambda I$ fails to be invertible in $B(X)$. There are three distinct ways this can fail, and they organize the spectrum into disjoint pieces:

partcondition on $T-\lambda I$name
$\sigma_p(T)$not injective (i.e. $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue, $\exists v\ne 0, Tv=\lambda v$)point spectrum
$\sigma_c(T)$injective, range dense but not closed (so no bounded inverse)continuous spectrum
$\sigma_r(T)$injective, range not denseresidual spectrum
$\rho(T)$bijection with bounded inverseresolvent set

These four sets partition $\mathbb{C}$. The spectrum $\sigma(T)=\sigma_p\sqcup\sigma_c\sqcup\sigma_r$ is always closed and bounded by $\|T\|$; the resolvent $\rho(T)$ is the open complement. For self-adjoint operators, $\sigma_r=\emptyset$ (proof: $\mathrm{ran}(T-\lambda)^\perp=\ker(T-\lambda)$ uses $T=T^*$ and reality of $\lambda$). For compact operators the spectrum is $\{0\}\cup\sigma_p$ — continuous and residual parts vanish outside $\{0\}$.

Canonical examples

  • Multiplication $M_x$ on $L^2[0,1]$: $\sigma(M_x)=[0,1]$, all continuous spectrum. No eigenvalues ($\{x=\lambda\}$ has measure zero), range dense but not closed for $\lambda\in[0,1]$.
  • Right shift $S$ on $\ell^2$: $\sigma(S)=\overline{D(0,1)}$ (closed unit disk). Point spectrum empty, continuous spectrum = unit circle, residual spectrum = open unit disk.
  • Left shift $S^*$ on $\ell^2$: $\sigma(S^*)=\overline{D(0,1)}$. Point spectrum = open unit disk (many eigenvectors), continuous spectrum = unit circle, residual spectrum empty.
  • Self-adjoint (e.g., $\sigma_z$ on $\mathbb{C}^2$): $\sigma=\{-1,+1\}$, all point spectrum; residual empty by the general theorem.

The shift and its adjoint provide a textbook demonstration that residual spectrum of $T$ is dual to point spectrum of $T^*$: $\sigma_r(S)\subseteq\overline{\sigma_p(S^*)}$, with the conjugation becoming identity in the real case.

10. Duality and the Riesz representation

Every Banach space $X$ has a dual $X^* = B(X,\mathbb{K})$. Understanding $X^*$ concretely is often half the job; it tells you which "tests" probe points of $X$.

The edge cases are interesting: $(L^1)^*=L^\infty$ on a $\sigma$-finite space, but $(L^\infty)^*\supsetneq L^1$ — the dual of $L^\infty$ is the space of finitely-additive measures (so $L^\infty$ is not reflexive). For $1

Riesz representation on Hilbert space

Theorem (Riesz). Let $H$ be a Hilbert space. The map $$\Phi\colon H\to H^*,\qquad (\Phi y)(x) = \langle x,y\rangle,$$ is a conjugate-linear isometric bijection. Thus $H\cong H^*$ canonically (up to complex conjugation).

Proof sketch: given $\ell\in H^*$, let $M=\ker\ell$. If $\ell\ne 0$ then $M^\perp$ is 1-dimensional, pick a unit $z\in M^\perp$ and set $y=\overline{\ell(z)}z$. Then $\ell(x)=\langle x,y\rangle$ by checking the two sides on $M$ and on $z$.

This identification is what makes the adjoint $T^*$ of an operator on a Hilbert space live in the same space — on a Banach space, $T^*$ naturally lives in $B(Y^*,X^*)$.

Riesz–Markov–Kakutani

Theorem. For a locally compact Hausdorff space $X$, $C_0(X)^*$ is isometrically the space $M(X)$ of finite complex regular Borel measures with the total-variation norm. Every continuous linear functional on $C_0(X)$ is integration against a measure.

This is the bridge between the "function" and "measure" worldviews: the dual of functions is measures, and vice versa under suitable hypotheses. It underlies weak-* convergence of probability measures, Young measures in PDE, and much of geometric measure theory.

Hölder and duality at the source

The $(L^p)^*=L^q$ identification is a theorem, but the inequality that drives it is elementary:

$$\Big|\int fg\,d\mu\Big|\le\|f\|_p\|g\|_q\qquad\text{(Hölder)}.$$

Hölder says the pairing is well-defined and bounded; the content of the theorem is that every bounded functional on $L^p$ is of this pairing form. The proof passes through the Radon–Nikodym theorem on finite measure spaces, then bootstraps to $\sigma$-finite ones — measure theory inside functional analysis inside measure theory.

Weak and weak-* topologies

On $X$, the weak topology is the coarsest making every $\ell\in X^*$ continuous: $x_n\rightharpoonup x$ iff $\ell(x_n)\to\ell(x)$ for all $\ell\in X^*$. On $X^*$, the weak-* topology is the coarsest making evaluation-at-$x$ continuous for every $x\in X$. The Banach–Alaoglu theorem says the closed unit ball of $X^*$ is weak-* compact — an infinite-dimensional substitute for the Heine–Borel theorem, absolutely essential for existence-via-compactness arguments (minimizers, weak solutions to PDE, invariant measures).

Reflexivity

The natural map $J\colon X\to X^{**}$, $Jx(\ell)=\ell(x)$, is always an isometric embedding. $X$ is reflexive when $J$ is surjective. Hilbert spaces are reflexive (Riesz gives $H\cong H^*\cong H^{**}$); $L^p$ is reflexive for $1not. Reflexivity is a compactness property: the unit ball of a reflexive space is weakly sequentially compact (Eberlein–Šmulian), which is why variational problems love reflexive spaces.

11. The Riesz representation theorem

The Riesz representation theorem is the statement that makes a Hilbert space into its own dual. Given a Hilbert space $H$, every bounded linear functional $\ell\in H^*$ is of the form $\ell(x)=\langle x,y\rangle$ for a unique $y\in H$, and the correspondence $y\leftrightarrow\ell$ is an isometric (conjugate-linear) bijection $H\cong H^*$. Concretely: to probe a Hilbert vector with a continuous linear test, you take an inner product with another Hilbert vector — nothing else is available.

Two extensions of the Hilbert case are indispensable in analysis. On $L^p$-spaces with $1Riesz–Markov–Kakutani theorem identifies $C_0(X)^*$ with the space $M(X)$ of finite regular Borel measures via $\mu\mapsto\int\cdot\,d\mu$. The single slogan is: every continuous linear functional is integration against something. Which "something" — a vector, a function, a measure — depends on the space.

Why completeness matters. The representation $\ell(x)=\langle x,y\rangle$ is constructed via $M=\ker\ell$ and the 1-dimensional orthogonal complement $M^\perp$. Without the splitting $H=M\oplus M^\perp$ — which requires $M$ closed and $H$ complete — no such $y$ need exist. On an incomplete inner-product space the theorem fails.

12. Reflexivity

Every Banach space $X$ has a canonical map into its double dual:

$$J\colon X\to X^{**},\qquad (Jx)(\ell) = \ell(x)\ \text{for } \ell\in X^*.$$

Hahn–Banach guarantees this is always an isometric embedding: $\|Jx\|=\sup_{\|\ell\|\le 1}|\ell(x)|=\|x\|$. The space $X$ is reflexive when $J$ is also surjective — i.e., every functional on $X^*$ is evaluation at a point of $X$.

Reflexivity is a compactness property in disguise. The deep equivalence:

Theorem (Kakutani). $X$ is reflexive $\iff$ the closed unit ball $B_X$ is weakly compact. Equivalently (Eberlein–Šmulian on $X$ metrizable-weakly), every bounded sequence has a weakly convergent subsequence.

This is why reflexive spaces are the natural arena for the direct method in the calculus of variations: a minimizing sequence $(x_n)$ is bounded (so has a weak limit $x^*$), and weak lower semicontinuity of a convex functional gives $F(x^*)\le\liminf F(x_n)$ — a minimizer exists without any explicit construction. Non-reflexive spaces can fail this: minimizing sequences may escape to infinity at infinity.

13. Weak and weak-* topologies

In infinite dimensions, norm-compactness is rare — the closed unit ball of an infinite-dimensional Banach space is never compact (Riesz's lemma). The fix is to weaken the topology. The weak topology on a Banach space $X$ is the coarsest topology making every $\ell\in X^*$ continuous: $x_n\rightharpoonup x$ means $\ell(x_n)\to\ell(x)$ for every $\ell\in X^*$. The weak-* topology on the dual $X^*$ is the coarsest making evaluation-at-$x$ continuous for every $x\in X$: $\ell_n\xrightarrow{w*}\ell$ means $\ell_n(x)\to\ell(x)$ pointwise in $x$.

The miracle is Banach–Alaoglu: the closed unit ball $B_{X^*}=\{\ell\in X^*:\|\ell\|\le 1\}$ is weak-* compact. The proof embeds $B_{X^*}$ inside $\prod_{x\in X}\overline{D(0,\|x\|)}\subset\mathbb{C}^X$ and quotes Tychonoff. Combined with the Eberlein–Šmulian theorem (on reflexive spaces weak sequential compactness holds), this is the compactness tool behind existence of minimizers for convex variational problems, weak solutions to PDE, and invariant measures for dynamical systems. In short: give up on strong compactness and you inherit weak compactness for free on the dual ball.

14. Distributions and test functions

Many objects in analysis and PDE — the delta "function," the derivative of a step, the Fourier transform of a polynomial — are not functions. They are distributions: continuous linear functionals on a chosen space of very-nice test functions.

Fix $\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ open. The space of test functions is

$$\mathcal{D}(\Omega) = C_c^\infty(\Omega) = \{\phi\in C^\infty(\Omega) : \mathrm{supp}(\phi)\text{ compact in }\Omega\}.$$

Equipped with the natural inductive-limit topology (convergence in $\mathcal{D}$ means: supports stay in a common compact set, and all derivatives converge uniformly), the dual

$$\mathcal{D}'(\Omega) = \{T\colon\mathcal{D}(\Omega)\to\mathbb{C} \text{ continuous linear}\}$$

is the space of distributions on $\Omega$. Every $f\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(\Omega)$ defines one by $\langle T_f,\phi\rangle=\int f\phi$, but $\mathcal{D}'$ contains more:

  • Dirac delta: $\langle\delta_0,\phi\rangle=\phi(0)$. Not given by any function — the defining "integral" is a point evaluation.
  • Principal value: $\langle\mathrm{p.v.}\tfrac{1}{x},\phi\rangle=\lim_{\varepsilon\to 0^+}\int_{|x|>\varepsilon}\phi(x)/x\,dx$, a regularized integral.
  • Derivatives of distributions: for any $T\in\mathcal{D}'$, define $\langle T',\phi\rangle=-\langle T,\phi'\rangle$. Everything in $\mathcal{D}'$ is infinitely differentiable. For instance, $(\log|x|)'=\mathrm{p.v.}\tfrac{1}{x}$, and the derivative of the Heaviside step is $\delta_0$.
  • Tempered distributions $\mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R}^n)$ — dual of Schwartz space $\mathcal{S}$, the smooth rapidly-decreasing functions. The Fourier transform acts continuously on $\mathcal{S}$, so by duality it extends to $\mathcal{S}'$. This is how $\hat{\delta}=1$, $\hat 1=(2\pi)^{n/2}\delta$, and the Fourier transform of a polynomial make sense.

A sequence $(f_n)$ of $L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}$ functions converges to $T\in\mathcal{D}'$ in the distributional sense if $\int f_n\phi\to\langle T,\phi\rangle$ for every $\phi\in C_c^\infty$. The delta is the weak limit of any bump with unit mass shrinking to the origin — a classic example being $n\cdot\mathbf{1}_{[0,1/n]}$, or a Gaussian $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma}e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)}$ as $\sigma\to 0$.

15. Connections

Functional analysis is the grammar of modern mathematical analysis. Some of the neighbors:

  • Real analysis — $L^p$, convergence theorems, and the completeness proofs are the anchor; this page assumes basic measure theory, and the companion page will dig into it.
  • Representation theory — unitary representations of a locally compact group $G$ live in $B(H)$ for a Hilbert space $H$; Peter–Weyl and Gelfand–Naimark–Segal rest directly on the spectral theorem and $C^*$-algebra theory.
  • Partial differential equations — Sobolev spaces $H^s$ are Hilbert, Lax–Milgram is a Hilbert-space fixed-point theorem, and weak solutions live in reflexive Banach spaces so that Banach–Alaoglu delivers compactness. Elliptic regularity and semigroup theory sit on top of spectral theory.
  • Quantum mechanics — states are rays in a Hilbert space, observables are self-adjoint operators, the spectral theorem gives probability distributions for measurement, and time evolution is a unitary group $e^{-itH/\hbar}$ by Stone's theorem. Every computation with observables is secretly a computation with spectral measures.
  • $C^*$-algebras and noncommutative geometry — Gelfand: commutative $C^*$-algebras with unit are exactly $C(X)$ for compact Hausdorff $X$. Dropping commutativity gives operator algebras, the "noncommutative" side of the bridge.
  • Optimization and economics — Hahn–Banach separation is the linear-programming duality theorem in disguise; Minkowski and Farkas are its finite-dimensional shadows.
  • Signal processing and machine learning — reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, kernel methods, and the representer theorem all live in a Hilbert framework; the SVD of a compact operator is the Mercer expansion.
  • Ergodic theory — the mean ergodic theorem is a Hilbert-space statement about the projection onto the invariant subspace of a unitary operator (Stone–von Neumann).
  • Number theory — modular forms live in $L^2$ of a fundamental domain; the Selberg trace formula relates spectral data of Laplacians to geometric data of geodesics.
  • Category theory (companion page) — $B(H)$, viewed as a dagger category with adjoint as the dagger, is the testbed for categorical approaches to quantum mechanics and compositional semantics.

The spectral theorem is the destination: it says that on a Hilbert space, self-adjoint operators are, up to a change of basis, just multiplication. Everything else on this page — completeness, the big four theorems, compactness, duality — is infrastructure to make that statement precise and to extend it as far as the hypotheses will carry.

A short reading route

  1. Start at the norm. Learn $\ell^p$ and $L^p$ well enough to prove completeness, duality, and density of simple functions. These are the non-Hilbert Banach spaces you will actually use.
  2. Get comfortable in Hilbert. Projections, orthonormal bases, Fourier series, the Riesz representation theorem, and the adjoint. Do the exercises with shift operators.
  3. Internalize the big four. Understand each as a statement about maps or functionals, not as a proof. Use them as licenses.
  4. Compact, then spectral. Spectral theory on compact operators is "infinite-dimensional linear algebra". Only then move to bounded and unbounded self-adjoint.
  5. Pick a partner field. PDE (Sobolev and semigroups), quantum mechanics (Stone's theorem, unbounded Hamiltonians), or harmonic analysis (Fourier multipliers). Each one sharpens different theorems on this page.

Singular cubics and reduction mod $p$

When the discriminant vanishes, an elliptic curve degenerates into a node or a cusp ŌĆö and its "group law" collapses from the exotic group $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ down to either the multiplicative group $\mathbb{G}_m$ or the additive group $\mathbb{G}_a$. Reducing an integral Weierstrass equation modulo a prime turns this from accident into a systematic tool: good primes, bad primes, and the first glimpse of a local $L$-factor.

1. Smooth elliptic curves, briefly

An elliptic curve over a field $k$ of characteristic not $2$ or $3$ is a smooth projective cubic $$E : y^2 = x^3 + a x + b, \qquad a,b \in k,$$ together with its point at infinity $O=[0:1:0]$. Smoothness is encoded by the discriminant $$\Delta(E) \;=\; -16\,(4 a^3 + 27 b^2).$$ The curve is smooth precisely when $\Delta \ne 0$; equivalently, the cubic $f(x) = x^3 + a x + b$ has three distinct roots over $\bar k$.

When $\Delta \ne 0$ the set $E(k)$ forms an abelian group under the chord-and-tangent construction, and the resulting group scheme $E/k$ is a one-dimensional abelian variety. This page asks: what happens when $\Delta = 0$? The cubic is still a perfectly good plane curve ŌĆö it just fails to be smooth. Its singular point either pinches two branches together (a node) or collapses them into a single cuspidal tangent. The smooth complement still carries a group law, but it is no longer the elliptic one: it is one of two much simpler groups.

2. Two singular cubic types: node and cusp

Up to a change of coordinates, every singular Weierstrass cubic over an algebraically closed field is isomorphic to exactly one of the following two curves:

The nodal cubic

$$C_{\mathrm{node}} : y^2 = x^3 + x^2.$$ The singular point is the origin $(0,0)$. Writing $y^2 - x^3 - x^2 = 0$ and expanding the degree-$2$ part at the origin gives $y^2 - x^2 = (y-x)(y+x)$: two transverse linear factors. So at $(0,0)$ the curve has two distinct tangent directions, namely $y = x$ and $y = -x$. The point looks like two smooth branches crossing at an angle ŌĆö a node (also called an ordinary double point or $A_1$-singularity).

The cuspidal cubic

$$C_{\mathrm{cusp}} : y^2 = x^3.$$ Again the origin is singular. The degree-$2$ part at the origin is just $y^2$: a single linear factor $y$ repeated, giving one tangent direction $y = 0$ with multiplicity $2$. But the curve itself does not split into two branches; the unique tangent absorbs the singularity in a qualitatively different way. This is a cusp ($A_2$-singularity), parametrizable by $(x,y) = (t^2, t^3)$.

Node vs. cusp side-by-sidetangent lines shown in yellow
Left: $y^2=x^3+x^2$ (node). Right: $y^2=x^3$ (cusp). Pink dot = singular point.
Algebraic test. A singular point $(x_0,y_0)$ of the affine curve $F(x,y)=0$ is a node iff the Hessian $F_{xx} F_{yy} - F_{xy}^2$ is nonzero there (two distinct tangent lines), and a cusp iff it vanishes (tangent lines collide). For $F = y^2 - x^3 - a x - b$ at a singular point, this reduces cleanly to whether $a \ne 0$ (node) or $a = 0$ (cusp) after translating the double root of $f(x)=x^3+ax+b$ to the origin.

These two cases are exclusive and exhaustive for plane cubics: any singular Weierstrass cubic is, up to affine change of coordinates, either $y^2=x^3+x^2$ or $y^2=x^3$. Their global resolutions are both the projective line $\mathbb{P}^1$ ŌĆö each singular cubic is a nodal or cuspidal degeneration of genus $1$ down to genus $0$.

3. The group law on the smooth part

Remove the singular point from a nodal or cuspidal cubic, and what remains ŌĆö the smooth locus $C^{\mathrm{sm}}$ ŌĆö is an honest variety of dimension $1$. Remarkably, the chord-and-tangent construction still produces a well-defined group law on $C^{\mathrm{sm}}$. But the resulting group is no longer a mysterious elliptic thing; it is one of two very familiar commutative algebraic groups.

Nodal: smooth locus is the multiplicative group

Let $C : y^2 = x^3 + x^2$. The tangent directions at the node are $y = \pm x$. Parametrize the smooth locus by $$t \longmapsto \bigl(t^2-1,\; t(t^2-1)\bigr),\qquad t\in \mathbb{G}_m = \mathbb{A}^1 \setminus \{0\},$$ and observe that $t = \pm 1$ both hit the node $(0,0)$ from the two branches, while $t = 0$ and $t = \infty$ give the two "ends" ŌĆö these are glued into a single node on the projective completion. After removing the node, one gets a rational map $\mathbb{G}_m \to C^{\mathrm{sm}}$ that identifies $t$ with $1/t$, except that a cleaner parametrization (using the two tangent slopes as a dual coordinate) gives an isomorphism $$C^{\mathrm{sm}} \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; \mathbb{G}_m,$$ and under this map the chord-and-tangent law becomes ordinary multiplication.

Statement. On a nodal cubic, $C^{\mathrm{sm}}(\bar k) \cong \mathbb{G}_m(\bar k) = \bar k^{\times}$ as algebraic groups.

Cuspidal: smooth locus is the additive group

Let $C : y^2 = x^3$. The unique tangent at the cusp is $y = 0$ with multiplicity $2$, and the curve is parametrized by $$t \longmapsto (t^2, t^3), \qquad t \in \mathbb{A}^1.$$ The cusp itself is the image of $t = 0$. Removing it identifies the smooth locus with $\mathbb{A}^1 \setminus \{0\}$ ŌĆö but as a group, the law is inherited from the parameter $t$, and the chord-and-tangent construction on $C$ translates to ordinary addition in $t$ (the parameter $1/t$ makes the identification with $\mathbb{G}_a$ direct after removing infinity).

Statement. On a cuspidal cubic, $C^{\mathrm{sm}}(\bar k) \cong \mathbb{G}_a(\bar k) = \bar k$ as algebraic groups.

These isomorphisms explain the entire structure. Over a field $k$, the smooth locus of a nodal cubic is $k^\times$ (the "multiplicative" group law), while the smooth locus of a cuspidal cubic is $k$ itself (the "additive" group law). All the subtlety of an elliptic curve vanishes at the boundary; we are back in the territory of plain old algebra.

Why "multiplicative" / "additive" reduction. The names come precisely from this: if the curve becomes nodal, its smooth locus looks like $\mathbb{G}_m$ (multiplicative); if cuspidal, like $\mathbb{G}_a$ (additive). The vocabulary is rigorously justified by the N├®ron model, but the mnemonic is already visible at the level of affine parametrizations.

4. Reduction modulo $p$

Now let $E : y^2 = x^3 + a x + b$ with $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$ ŌĆö an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ with an integral Weierstrass model. For each prime $p$, reducing coefficients modulo $p$ gives a cubic over the finite field $\mathbb{F}_p$: $$\tilde E_p \;:\; y^2 \equiv x^3 + \bar a\, x + \bar b \pmod{p}, \qquad \bar a, \bar b \in \mathbb{F}_p.$$ This reduced curve $\tilde E_p / \mathbb{F}_p$ is sometimes an elliptic curve and sometimes a singular cubic. Which it is depends entirely on the discriminant modulo $p$.

A single arithmetic object, many faces

The family $\tilde E_p$ as $p$ varies through all primes is not just a disconnected collection ŌĆö it is the fiber family of a single scheme $\mathcal{E} \to \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Z}$, the scheme-theoretic model of $E$ over the integers. The fiber over the closed point $(p) \in \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Z}$ is exactly $\tilde E_p$; the fiber over the generic point $(0)$ is $E/\mathbb{Q}$. Singular fibers correspond to the primes of "bad reduction."

Caveat: minimal models. The reduction $\tilde E_p$ depends on the chosen Weierstrass equation, not just on the $\mathbb{Q}$-isomorphism class of $E$. For a given $E/\mathbb{Q}$ there is a minimal Weierstrass equation at each $p$ (minimizing the $p$-adic valuation of $\Delta$), and the "official" reduction type is read off the minimal model. On this page we take the chosen equation at face value.

The basic dichotomy at a prime $p$ is:

  • Good reduction at $p$: $\tilde E_p$ is smooth, i.e.\ an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{F}_p$. This happens iff $\Delta \not\equiv 0 \pmod p$.
  • Bad reduction at $p$: $\tilde E_p$ is singular ŌĆö a nodal or cuspidal cubic. This happens iff $p \mid \Delta$.

Because $\Delta$ is an integer, only finitely many primes divide it. Almost every prime is a prime of good reduction; the finitely many primes of bad reduction are distinguished arithmetic features of $E$ and collectively form its conductor.

5. Good reduction vs. bad reduction, numerically

The table below shows reduction types for several small examples. Scan for the pattern: good primes are the ones where $\Delta$ remains a unit in $\mathbb{F}_p$; bad primes are the ones where $p$ divides $\Delta$.

Discriminant-mod-$p$ tablepick a curve; primes up to 19

The "node vs. cusp" determination in the table uses a coarse rule: at a prime $p \mid \Delta$, the reduction is additive (cusp) iff $\bar a \equiv 0$ in $\mathbb{F}_p$, and otherwise it is multiplicative (node). This is correct when the singular point has been translated to the origin and no further vertical shift is needed ŌĆö which holds, after a change of coordinates, for the examples above ŌĆö but the precise distinction at small primes is subtle and is refined in the next section.

Conductor, briefly. The conductor $N$ of an elliptic curve records which primes are bad and how bad: the $p$-adic valuation of $N$ is $1$ for multiplicative reduction and $\ge 2$ for additive reduction (with $=2$ outside of $p=2,3$). This page stops short of defining $N$ precisely but everything here is a prerequisite.

How to tell node from cusp from the tangent cone

Translate the singular point to the origin and look at the lowest-order terms of the equation. After the translation, the equation becomes, schematically, $$y^2 = (x - \alpha)^2 (x - \beta) \;\;\leadsto\;\; y^2 - (\alpha-\beta)\, x^2 + \text{(higher)}.$$ At the singular point, the quadratic part is $y^2 - (\alpha-\beta) x^2$. This factors as $$(y - s x)(y + s x), \qquad s = \sqrt{\alpha-\beta},$$ whenever $\alpha \ne \beta$ ŌĆö giving two tangent lines and hence a node. When $\alpha = \beta$, the quadratic part degenerates to $y^2$, giving the single tangent of a cusp.

For a node over $\mathbb{F}_p$, the further question is whether the tangent slope $s = \sqrt{\alpha - \beta}$ lives in $\mathbb{F}_p$ or only in its quadratic extension $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$. This is precisely the question of whether $\alpha - \beta$ is a square in $\mathbb{F}_p^{\times}$ ŌĆö a Legendre symbol computation.

The non-split case is the subtlest. Over $\mathbb{F}_p$ the two tangent lines are defined only after passing to $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$, where they get swapped by the Frobenius $\mathrm{Frob}_p$. The smooth locus is then a non-split torus ŌĆö the group of elements of norm $1$ in $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}^{\times}$, which has order $p+1$. Counting points of this non-split torus is what gives the characteristic "$+1$" (rather than "$-1$") behaviour in the $L$-factor at non-split primes.

Local $L$-factors (preview). For an elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ and a prime $p$, the local factor of the HasseŌĆōWeil $L$-function is $$L_p(E,s) \;=\; \begin{cases} (1 - a_p p^{-s} + p^{1-2s})^{-1} & p \text{ good} \\ (1 - p^{-s})^{-1} & p \text{ split multiplicative} \\ (1 + p^{-s})^{-1} & p \text{ non-split multiplicative} \\ 1 & p \text{ additive} \end{cases}$$ with $a_p = p + 1 - |\tilde E(\mathbb{F}_p)|$ in the good case. Read top to bottom: good primes contribute an order-$2$ Euler factor; bad primes degrade that factor to order $1$ (multiplicative) or kill it entirely (additive).

7. Counting points and the Hasse bound

The point count $|\tilde E(\mathbb{F}_p)|$ depends cleanly on the reduction type. Let us assemble the four cases.

In each bad case the smooth locus contributes its known group order and the singular point adds $1$. In the good case there is no singular point and the genuinely deep bound is

Hasse's theorem. For $E/\mathbb{F}_p$ smooth, $$\bigl|\,|E(\mathbb{F}_p)| - (p+1)\,\bigr| \;\le\; 2 \sqrt{p}.$$ Equivalently, $|a_p| \le 2\sqrt p$. This is a Riemann Hypothesis for $E$ over the finite field, proved by Hasse in 1933 and generalised to all varieties by Deligne.

A few calibration remarks. At a good prime, the group $\tilde E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is a finite abelian group whose order lies in the Hasse interval $[p+1-2\sqrt p,\, p+1+2\sqrt p]$; the integer $a_p$ is the single crucial invariant. At a multiplicative prime, $a_p \in \{+1,-1\}$ encodes the split/non-split distinction and nothing else ŌĆö the entire quadratic character over $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}/\mathbb{F}_p$ reduces to a sign. At additive primes there is no $a_p$ to speak of, and the Euler factor is trivial.

What this sets up

This page is the bridge between the global object $E/\mathbb{Q}$ and its local behavior at a single prime. Each prime $p$ gives a local Euler factor $L_p(E,s)$; assembled over all primes they produce the global Dirichlet series $$L(E, s) \;=\; \prod_p L_p(E, s)^{-1},$$ whose analytic properties sit at the heart of modern number theory (modularity, BirchŌĆōSwinnerton-Dyer, and so on). The story of modular forms enters because the coefficients $a_p$ of a suitable weight-$2$ modular form coincide with those of an elliptic curve ŌĆö and the matching of $a_p$'s across good, split, non-split, and additive primes is exactly the compatibility one must verify.

Two nearby pages deepen this picture from complementary directions. Reduction mod $p$ is the "residue-field" operation on the arithmetic scheme $\mathcal{E} \to \operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{Z}$; the general machinery of closed fibers and generic fibers is the subject of schemes and fiber products of morphisms. On the other side, the local field $\mathbb{Q}_p$ of $p$-adic numbers provides the right universe to study reduction as a specialization: the minimal Weierstrass model over $\mathbb{Z}_p$, the N├®ron model, and the Tate uniformization of multiplicative-reduction curves $E(\mathbb{Q}_p) = \mathbb{Q}_p^{\times}/q^{\mathbb{Z}}$ all live there.

Forward reference. The classification above is the local piece of a much larger picture. Globally, the modularity theorem of WilesŌĆōBreuilŌĆōConradŌĆōDiamondŌĆōTaylor says that for every elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ there is a weight-$2$ cuspidal eigenform $f$ whose local Hecke eigenvalues $a_p(f)$ match the $a_p$'s computed here, prime by prime, including at bad primes. The four-case table of this section is exactly the local compatibility that modularity has to respect.

8. Minimal Weierstrass models and the conductor

The reduction story so far was told over $\mathbb{F}_p$, but for a global elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ we first have to pick which integer Weierstrass equation to reduce. Different models give different discriminants: replacing $(x,y) \mapsto (u^2 x, u^3 y)$ scales $\Delta$ by $u^{12}$, so an equation with $\Delta = 2^{12}\cdot 11$ describes the same curve as one with $\Delta = 11$. The first is non-minimal; it has a spurious bad prime at $2$ that can be cleared.

Minimal Weierstrass model. Over a discrete valuation ring $R$ with maximal ideal $\mathfrak p$ (e.g. $R = \mathbb{Z}_p$), a Weierstrass equation for $E$ with integer coefficients is minimal at $\mathfrak p$ if $v_{\mathfrak p}(\Delta)$ is as small as possible among integer models. Over $\mathbb{Z}$, a global minimal model is one that is simultaneously minimal at every prime. For $E/\mathbb{Q}$ such a global minimal model always exists (N├®ron), and its discriminant $\Delta_{\min}(E)$ is an invariant of the curve.

Tate's algorithm is the explicit procedure: given any Weierstrass equation over $\mathbb{Z}_p$, it either outputs a minimal model directly or detects a change of variables that reduces $v_p(\Delta)$ by $12$. Iterate and you converge. Once you have the minimal model, the reduction type at $p$ (good, split/non-split multiplicative, or additive) is an invariant of the curve ŌĆö not of the chosen equation.

Attached to each bad prime is a non-negative integer $f_p$, the conductor exponent, defined so that

$N(E) \;=\; \prod_{p \text{ bad}} p^{f_p}$

is the conductor of $E$. The exponent encodes how bad the reduction is, and is bounded by:

Conductor exponents.
  • $f_p = 1$ if the reduction is multiplicative (split or non-split) ŌĆö a node.
  • $f_p = 2$ if the reduction is additive and $p \ne 2, 3$ ŌĆö a cusp.
  • $f_p \le 2 + \delta_p$ at $p = 2, 3$, where $\delta_p$ is the additional "wild" part coming from ramification in the $\ell$-adic Galois representation. Tate's algorithm computes $f_p$ case by case.
This is Ogg's formula and the classification on N├®ron's list. The conductor $N(E)$ is the data that appears in every $L$-function formula for $E$.

Worked example: take $E\colon y^2 = x^3 + 1$ with (minimal) discriminant $\Delta = -432 = -2^4\cdot 3^3$. The curve has $j$-invariant $0$, and an analysis at each bad prime shows:

  • At $p = 2$: additive reduction, with $f_2 = 4$ (wild ramification contributes extra).
  • At $p = 3$: additive reduction, with $f_3 = 3$ (wild at $3$).

So the conductor is $N(E) = 2^4 \cdot 3^3 = 432$, which coincides (coincidentally, in this easy case) with $|\Delta|$. A general smooth curve with multiplicative reduction at $p = 11$ and good reduction elsewhere has $N = 11$, matching the famous "smallest-conductor" curve $y^2 + y = x^3 - x^2$ of Cremona label 11a1.

The conductor is the single most important arithmetic invariant of $E/\mathbb{Q}$ after its $L$-function ŌĆö modular forms of weight $2$ and level $N$ are (by modularity) in bijection with elliptic curves of conductor $N$, so the minimal model and its conductor are exactly the bridge between the geometric page above and the analytic picture on modular-forms.html.

Hamiltonians and classical mechanics

Phase space is a symplectic manifold and physics is its geometry — Hamilton's equations, Noether's theorem, action-angle coordinates, and KAM, viewed through the cotangent bundle.

1. Phase space and the cotangent bundle

Newtonian mechanics is second-order in position: $m\ddot q = F(q)$. Re-expressed as a first-order system, it lives on a doubled space — positions paired with momenta. The natural geometric home is the cotangent bundle $T^*Q$ of configuration space $Q$.

$T^*Q$ comes equipped, with no extra choices, with a 1-form: the tautological (or Liouville) form $\theta$. In any chart $(q^i, p_i)$ on $T^*Q$,

$$\theta = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i\,dq^i.$$

Its negative exterior derivative is the canonical symplectic form

$$\omega = -d\theta = \sum_{i=1}^n dq^i\wedge dp_i.$$

Each fibre $T^*_q Q$ is an $n$-dimensional vector space; together $\dim T^*Q = 2n$. The form $\omega$ is closed (since $\omega = d(-\theta)$) and non-degenerate (the matrix $\begin{pmatrix}0 & I\\ -I & 0\end{pmatrix}$ in canonical coordinates is invertible). Phase space is a symplectic manifold by birthright — and the natural coordinates $(q,p)$ are already Darboux.

Why $T^*Q$ and not $TQ$? Velocities $\dot q$ live in the tangent bundle; momenta $p_i = \partial L/\partial \dot q^i$ are linear functionals on velocities, hence cotangent vectors. The Legendre transform $TQ\to T^*Q$ sends a Lagrangian $L(q,\dot q)$ to a Hamiltonian $H(q,p)$ — when invertible, the two formulations are equivalent. Symplectic structure lives naturally on $T^*Q$ because $\theta$ is built without choosing a metric.

2. Hamilton's equations and the Hamiltonian flow

A smooth function $H\colon T^*Q\to\mathbb{R}$ — the Hamiltonian, usually total energy — generates a vector field $X_H$ defined by the symplectic equation

$$\iota_{X_H}\omega \;=\; dH.$$

Non-degeneracy of $\omega$ makes $X_H$ unique. In canonical coordinates, working out both sides gives the celebrated

$$\dot q^i \;=\; \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i},\qquad \dot p_i \;=\; -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i}.$$

These are Hamilton's equations: a system of $2n$ first-order ODEs replacing the $n$ second-order Lagrangian equations.

Two structural facts fall out of the defining identity, almost for free:

Energy conservation. Along the flow, $\dot H = X_H(H) = dH(X_H) = \omega(X_H, X_H) = 0$ since $\omega$ is alternating. Energy is conserved because $\omega$ is antisymmetric — geometry, not a separate axiom.

Liouville's theorem. By Cartan's magic formula,

$$\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\omega = d(\iota_{X_H}\omega) + \iota_{X_H}\,d\omega = d(dH) + 0 = 0.$$

So $X_H$ preserves $\omega$ and therefore its top exterior power $\omega^n/n!$ — the phase-space volume form. Hamiltonian flows are volume-preserving.

3. Canonical transformations and Hamilton–Jacobi

A change of coordinates $\varphi\colon T^*Q\to T^*Q$ is canonical (or symplectic) when it preserves the symplectic form: $\varphi^*\omega = \omega$. Such transformations move Hamilton's equations to Hamilton's equations, possibly with a different Hamiltonian — but they always look like Hamilton's equations.

The cleanest way to manufacture a canonical transformation is via a generating function. Pick $S(q, Q)$ depending on the old position $q$ and the new position $Q$. Set

$$p_i = \frac{\partial S}{\partial q^i},\qquad P_i = -\frac{\partial S}{\partial Q^i}.$$

The graph $\{(q, p, Q, P) : p = \partial_q S,\ P = -\partial_Q S\}$ is a Lagrangian submanifold of $(T^*Q\times T^*Q,\,\omega\ominus\omega)$, and the resulting map $(q,p)\mapsto(Q,P)$ is canonical for free.

The Hamilton–Jacobi equation asks: can we choose $S(q, t)$ so that the transformed Hamiltonian vanishes? If so, the new coordinates are constants of motion and we've integrated the dynamics. The condition is

$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial t} + H\!\left(q,\frac{\partial S}{\partial q},t\right) = 0.$$

A non-linear first-order PDE for one function $S$ — sometimes solvable by separation of variables, sometimes not.

4. Symmetries and Noether's theorem

The Poisson bracket on $C^\infty(T^*Q)$ is

$$\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g) = \sum_i \frac{\partial f}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q^i}.$$

It makes $C^\infty(T^*Q)$ into a Lie algebra, and along the Hamiltonian flow,

$$\dot f \;=\; X_H(f) \;=\; \{f, H\}.$$

The slogan is short and exact: $f$ is conserved iff $\{f, H\} = 0$.

Each conserved $f$ generates its own flow $X_f$, and $\{f, H\} = 0$ means that flow is a symmetry of $H$. So Noether's theorem in Hamiltonian dress reads:

Continuous symmetries of $H$ ↔ functions Poisson-commuting with $H$ ↔ conserved quantities.

5. Integrable systems, action-angle coordinates, and KAM

A Hamiltonian system on a $2n$-dimensional symplectic manifold is Liouville integrable if it admits $n$ functionally independent first integrals $f_1 = H, f_2, \dots, f_n$ that pairwise Poisson-commute: $\{f_i, f_j\} = 0$. The maximum possible. Their joint flows define a (locally free) $\mathbb{R}^n$-action.

Liouville–Arnold theorem. If a regular common level set $\{f = c\}$ is compact and connected, it is diffeomorphic to a torus $T^n$, and there exist action-angle coordinates $(I_1,\dots,I_n,\theta_1,\dots,\theta_n)$ on a neighbourhood in which

$$\omega = \sum_i dI_i\wedge d\theta_i,\qquad H = H(I)\ \text{(independent of $\theta$)}.$$

Hamilton's equations become trivially soluble: $\dot I = 0$, $\dot\theta_i = \partial H/\partial I_i \equiv \omega_i(I)$. Each torus carries a quasi-periodic linear flow with frequency vector $\omega(I)$.

The KAM theorem. The generic Hamiltonian is not integrable. But integrable systems are not isolated curiosities: under a small perturbation $H_\varepsilon = H_0(I) + \varepsilon H_1(I, \theta)$, the theorem of Kolmogorov, Arnold, and Moser says that most invariant tori survive — specifically, those whose frequency vectors satisfy a Diophantine condition

$$|k\cdot\omega(I)| \;\geq\; \frac{\gamma}{|k|^\tau}\quad\text{for all $0\ne k\in\mathbb{Z}^n$.}$$

The perturbed tori form a Cantor-like set of positive measure. Resonant tori (with rationally related frequencies) shatter into thin Birkhoff zones with chaotic layers — the seeds of chaos in nearly-integrable systems.

What KAM is famous for. KAM was the first rigorous answer to a long-standing question: do small perturbations of the solar system (an integrable Kepler problem at zeroth order) preserve stability? Answer: most planetary tori survive small perturbation. Modern long-time numerical simulations show the inner solar system is in fact in a weakly chaotic regime — KAM applies locally, not over astronomical timescales.

6. Worked examples

The canon of classical mechanics is short, and almost every named system is integrable. Each one teaches a different lesson.

The harmonic oscillator

$H = \tfrac{p^2}{2m} + \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 q^2$. Action-angle coordinates: $I = E/\omega$, $\theta = \omega t + \theta_0$. Phase portrait: concentric ellipses, all traversed at the same period $2\pi/\omega$ — the only system besides Kepler with all bounded orbits closed (Bertrand's theorem).

The Kepler problem

$H = \tfrac{|p|^2}{2m} - \tfrac{k}{|q|}$. Conserved: energy $H$, angular momentum $L = q\times p$, and the Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector

$$A = p\times L - mk\,\hat q.$$

Together $(H, L, A)$ give more independent integrals than the dimension allows generically — Kepler is super-integrable. The hidden symmetry is $SO(4)$ (for bound states), and it forces orbits to close: ellipses, parabolas, or hyperbolae, never precessing rosettes.

Geodesic flow

On a Riemannian $(M, g)$, take $H(q, p) = \tfrac{1}{2}g^{ij}(q)\,p_i p_j$. Hamilton's equations on $T^*M$ project to geodesics on $M$. Curvature, conjugate points, the Jacobi equation — the geometry of $M$ is the geometry of this flow on phase space.

Convex billiards

Free flight inside a convex region $\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^2$ with elastic reflection at $\partial\Omega$. The Birkhoff billiard map on $\partial\Omega\times[0,\pi]$ is a 2D area-preserving map, an excellent KAM testbed: caustics are surviving invariant curves, glancing trajectories live near the singular boundary $\sin\alpha = 0$.

What's left out

The three-body problem is famously not integrable (Poincaré, 1890): the failure of the Birkhoff series to converge near a homoclinic point opens the door to chaos. Most named "complicated" systems — the double pendulum, the Hénon–Heiles potential, gravitational $N$-body — sit on the non-integrable side.

Connections

Classical mechanics is the source of symplectic geometry, but the import runs both ways: every modern advance in symplectic topology — Gromov's non-squeezing, Floer homology, mirror symmetry — was conceived to detect rigidity invisible to the Hamiltonian flow itself. Quantisation maps $\{f, g\}\rightsquigarrow [\hat f, \hat g]/(i\hbar)$, but no fully natural functor from symplectic manifolds to Hilbert spaces exists; geometric quantisation, deformation quantisation, and the Fukaya category are partial answers.

  • Symplectic manifolds — Hamiltonian flow — the abstract setup we specialised here to $T^*Q$.
  • Symplectic manifolds — Poisson bracket — the algebraic shadow of the symplectic form, classical limit of the commutator.
  • Dynamical systems — Conservative flows — Liouville's theorem from the dynamicist's vantage.
  • Differential forms — wedge product — the multilinear algebra underwriting $\omega$.
  • Riemannian geometry — geodesic flow is the cleanest non-trivial Hamiltonian system after the oscillator.

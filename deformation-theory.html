Deformation theory

First-order deformations live in $H^1(T)$, obstructions in $H^2(T)$ — the cotangent complex unifies them.

1. First-order deformations

Let $k$ be a field and $X_0$ a scheme over $k$ — for now think of an affine variety, but the definition is local and globalises with no friction. Write $D = k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$ for the ring of dual numbers; geometrically $\mathrm{Spec}\,D$ is a "fattened point", a single closed point with a one-dimensional tangent direction. A first-order deformation of $X_0$ is a flat $D$-scheme $X$ together with a Cartesian square

$$\begin{array}{ccc} X_0 & \hookrightarrow & X \\ \downarrow & & \downarrow \\ \mathrm{Spec}\,k & \hookrightarrow & \mathrm{Spec}\,D \end{array}$$

identifying the special fibre $X \otimes_D k$ with $X_0$. Flatness is the key word: it is what stops $X$ from being just a disjoint pair of fibres or an arbitrary thickening, and it is the algebraic incarnation of "the family deforms continuously". Two first-order deformations $X, X'$ are equivalent if there is a $D$-isomorphism $X \to X'$ restricting to the identity on $X_0$. The set of equivalence classes is denoted $\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}(D)$, or simply $T^1_{X_0}$.

Why is the dual-number axis the right "tangent direction"? Functorially, a $D$-point of any moduli space $\mathcal{M}$ is a pair (a $k$-point $[X_0]$, plus a tangent vector at $[X_0]$). So $\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}(D)$ literally is the tangent space to the moduli problem at $X_0$ — provided we have a moduli space at all. (Hartshorne, Sernesi, and Illusie all open with this observation; it is the gateway to every later structure theorem.)

The simplest non-example shows what flatness does. Take $X_0 = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[x,y]/(xy)$, the node. The trivial deformation is $X_0 \times_k \mathrm{Spec}\,D = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[x,y,\epsilon]/(xy, \epsilon^2)$ — a node with a fattened point glued to it but no actual smoothing. Replacing $xy$ by $xy - \epsilon$ gives a different deformation $\mathrm{Spec}\,k[x,y,\epsilon]/(xy - \epsilon, \epsilon^2)$, which is again flat over $D$ but now not equivalent to the trivial one — it is the first-order trace of smoothing the node to a hyperbola $xy = t$. Cohomology will see this as a non-zero class in $H^1(X_0, T_{X_0})$; the proof-scrubber below is the bridge.

Worked example: for $X_0 = \mathrm{Spec}\,A_0$ affine smooth over $k$, every first-order deformation is trivial — equivalently, $T^1_{X_0} = H^1(X_0, T_{X_0})$ vanishes for $X_0$ affine. The Čech computation behind this is exactly the obstruction-free patching the scrubber walks through. The interesting examples are global (projective) or singular.

2. Tangent space of moduli is $H^1(X_0, T_{X_0})$

Section 1 ended on the punchline that $\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}(D) \cong H^1(X_0, T_{X_0})$ for $X_0$ smooth (or, more generally, locally complete intersection with controlled singularities). This section unpacks why this is the tangent space to moduli: the construction is the standard "patching" pattern, and exhibiting the bijection on a small example is the right way to internalise it.

The setup. Choose an affine open cover $\{U_i\}$ of $X_0$ with $X_0$ smooth. Flatness of any $D$-deformation $X$ is a local condition; on each $U_i$, smoothness gives a trivialisation $\varphi_i\colon X|_{U_i} \xrightarrow{\sim} U_i \times_k \mathrm{Spec}\,D$ (Hartshorne III.9.9). On $U_i \cap U_j$ we have two such trivialisations, and their difference $\varphi_j \circ \varphi_i^{-1}$ is an automorphism of $(U_i \cap U_j) \times \mathrm{Spec}\,D$ that restricts to the identity modulo $\epsilon$. Such an automorphism is precisely $\mathrm{id} + \epsilon \cdot \theta_{ij}$ for a unique derivation $\theta_{ij} \in T_{X_0}(U_i \cap U_j)$. The cocycle condition $\theta_{ij} + \theta_{jk} = \theta_{ik}$ on triples is forced; coboundaries arise from changes of trivialisation. Hence equivalence classes of first-order deformations $\leftrightarrow \check H^1(\{U_i\}, T_{X_0}) = H^1(X_0, T_{X_0})$.

The geometric content: a class in $H^1(X_0, T_{X_0})$ encodes the obstruction to gluing the locally trivial pieces back into a globally trivial deformation. For $X_0 = \mathbb{P}^1$, $T_{\mathbb{P}^1} = \mathcal{O}(2)$ and $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1, \mathcal{O}(2)) = 0$ — every first-order deformation of $\mathbb{P}^1$ is trivial, i.e. $\mathbb{P}^1$ is infinitesimally rigid. The same vanishing $H^1(\mathbb{P}^n, T_{\mathbb{P}^n}) = 0$ holds for all $n \ge 1$ via the Euler sequence; $\mathbb{P}^n$ has no moduli. By contrast for a smooth curve $C$ of genus $g \ge 2$, Riemann–Roch and Serre duality give $\dim H^1(C, T_C) = 3g - 3$, the classical dimension of $\mathcal{M}_g$.

Worked example. For an elliptic curve $E$ over $k = \bar k$, $T_E \cong \mathcal{O}_E$ (the tangent bundle of an abelian variety is trivial), so $H^1(E, T_E) \cong H^1(E, \mathcal{O}_E)$ has dimension $1$ — recovering the familiar fact that $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ is one-dimensional, parametrised by the $j$-invariant.

3. Obstructions live in $H^2(X_0, T_{X_0})$

First-order deformations are the linear shadow of moduli; to "integrate" them — to extend a first-order lift over $D = k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$ to a second-order lift over $k[t]/(t^3)$, then $k[t]/(t^4)$, and onwards — generally requires solving a non-linear cocycle equation, and the obstruction to doing so lives one degree higher: in $H^2(X_0, T_{X_0})$.

The mechanism. Start with a flat lift $X_n$ over $A_n = k[t]/(t^{n+1})$. Try to extend it to a lift $X_{n+1}$ over $A_{n+1} = k[t]/(t^{n+2})$. Locally — on each affine $U_i$ in your cover — extensions exist freely; the local automorphism groups are torsors over $T_{X_0}(U_i)$, exactly as before. Compatible local extensions glue iff a 2-cocycle $\mathrm{ob}(X_n) \in C^2(\{U_i\}, T_{X_0})$ vanishes; its cohomology class $[\mathrm{ob}(X_n)] \in H^2(X_0, T_{X_0})$ is the obstruction. If $[\mathrm{ob}(X_n)] = 0$ the obstruction can be killed by re-choosing the local extensions, and a global lift $X_{n+1}$ exists. If not, no $X_{n+1}$ exists; the deformation is obstructed at order $n$.

Two consequences worth memorising. (a) Unobstructed implies smooth moduli. If $H^2(X_0, T_{X_0}) = 0$ then every obstruction class is forced to vanish and every first-order deformation extends to all orders, giving a formal smoothness statement: $\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}$ is pro-represented by a power-series ring $k[\![t_1, \ldots, t_d]\!]$ with $d = \dim H^1(X_0, T_{X_0})$. (b) Curves are special. For a smooth projective curve $C$ the dimension constraint $\dim C = 1$ forces $H^2(C, T_C) = 0$ (any sheaf on a 1-dimensional space has no $H^{\ge 2}$); this is why $\mathcal{M}_g$ is smooth and why curves "have no obstructions" — the deeper reason behind the dimension count $3g-3$.

Counter-classical examples. Calabi–Yau threefolds typically have non-zero $H^2(X, T_X)$ but a miraculous cancellation (Bogomolov–Tian–Todorov, Ran's $T^1$-lifting): the obstruction map vanishes nonetheless, and the deformation theory is unobstructed despite having non-trivial $H^2$. By contrast, some threefolds have genuinely obstructed deformations — Mukai-style classifying spaces with non-reduced moduli.

Worked example. Consider $X_0 = \mathbb{P}^n$. The Euler sequence $0 \to \mathcal{O} \to \mathcal{O}(1)^{n+1} \to T_{\mathbb{P}^n} \to 0$ shows $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n, T_{\mathbb{P}^n}) = 0$ for all $i \ge 1$. Hence $\mathbb{P}^n$ is rigid (no first-order deformations) and unobstructed (no obstructions to chase). The "moduli space" of $\mathbb{P}^n$'s is a single point.

4. Deformation functors and Schlessinger's criteria

The two preceding sections gave a hands-on Čech construction. To talk about moduli abstractly — even when no actual moduli scheme exists — Grothendieck and Schlessinger formalised the data into a functor.

Let $\mathrm{Art}_k$ denote the category of Artin local $k$-algebras with residue field $k$ (so $A$ is finite-dimensional over $k$ and has a unique maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_A$ with $A/\mathfrak{m}_A = k$). The deformation functor of $X_0$ is

$\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}\colon \mathrm{Art}_k \to \mathrm{Set}, \quad A \;\longmapsto\; \{\text{flat lifts $X/\mathrm{Spec}\,A$ of $X_0$}\}/\!\sim$

where $\sim$ is $A$-isomorphism fixing the special fibre $X_0$. The dual numbers $D = k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$ are an Artin local ring, so $\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}(D) \cong H^1(X_0, T_{X_0})$ from §1–2 fits cleanly into this language.

The functor $\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}$ is rarely representable in the strict sense (it would require a fine moduli scheme; deformations have automorphisms which obstruct this). Schlessinger's 1968 paper Functors of Artin rings identifies four conditions, traditionally numbered (H1)–(H4), characterising when $\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}$ has a hull:

  • (H1) For surjections $A' \twoheadrightarrow A$ in $\mathrm{Art}_k$ and any $A'' \to A$, the natural map $\mathrm{Def}(A' \times_A A'') \to \mathrm{Def}(A') \times_{\mathrm{Def}(A)} \mathrm{Def}(A'')$ is surjective.
  • (H2) When $A = k$ and $A' \twoheadrightarrow k$ is a small extension, the map in (H1) is bijective.
  • (H3) $\dim_k \mathrm{Def}(D) < \infty$ — the tangent space of the functor is finite-dimensional.
  • (H4) The map in (H1) is bijective for arbitrary $A$ (this strengthens (H1)+(H2)). When (H4) holds along with (H1)–(H3), $\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}$ is pro-representable by a complete local Noetherian $k$-algebra $R$.

A hull is a complete local $k$-algebra $R$ together with a smooth, surjective natural transformation $h_R \to \mathrm{Def}_{X_0}$ inducing an isomorphism on tangent spaces. Schlessinger's theorem: $\mathrm{Def}_{X_0}$ has a hull iff (H1), (H2), and (H3) hold; it is pro-representable iff (H1)–(H4) all hold. For $X_0$ smooth projective, all four hold, and the hull lives at the heart of every deformation-space construction (Hilbert schemes, $\mathrm{Quot}$-schemes, moduli of stable bundles).

Worked example. The functor $h_R \colon \mathrm{Art}_k \to \mathrm{Set}$, $A \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_k\text{-alg}(R, A)$, of any complete local $k$-algebra $R$ trivially satisfies (H1)–(H4); so a representable functor is its own hull. The interesting examples are deformation functors of singular schemes (a node, a cusp), where (H4) can genuinely fail and only a hull exists. The bridge to actual moduli stacks is then through Artin's algebraisation criteria.

5. The cotangent complex $L_{X/Y}$

The Čech-cohomology pictures of §1–3 see $T_{X_0} = \mathcal{H}om(\Omega^1_{X_0/k}, \mathcal{O}_{X_0})$ on the nose, but for $X_0$ singular the sheaf $\Omega^1_{X_0/k}$ doesn't carry enough information — squashing higher derived structure into a single sheaf loses obstruction data. Illusie (and, independently, Quillen) discovered the correct replacement: a complex $L_{X/Y}$ in the derived category $D^{\le 0}(\mathcal{O}_X)$ whose Ext groups recover the deformation invariants in a uniform way.

The construction starts from a general fact about commutative algebras. Given a morphism $f\colon X \to Y$, choose a "free resolution" of $\mathcal{O}_X$ as an $\mathcal{O}_Y$-algebra by polynomial algebras $P_\bullet$ (a simplicial $\mathcal{O}_Y$-algebra resolution). The cotangent complex

$L_{X/Y} \;:=\; \Omega^1_{P_\bullet/\mathcal{O}_Y} \otimes_{P_\bullet} \mathcal{O}_X$

is an object in $D^{\le 0}(\mathcal{O}_X)$, well-defined up to quasi-isomorphism. The construction is the algebraic analogue of "take a CW model and compute its tangent bundle" — the resolution $P_\bullet$ is a "smooth approximation" of $X \to Y$, even when $f$ itself is singular.

The structural facts that make this useful:

  • $f$ smooth $\Rightarrow$ $L_{X/Y}$ is concentrated in degree $0$ and equals $\Omega^1_{X/Y}[0]$. No new information beyond the relative differentials.
  • $f$ a regular embedding (l.c.i.) of codimension $r$ $\Rightarrow$ $L_{X/Y}$ has cohomology in degrees $0$ and $-1$: $\mathcal{H}^0 = \Omega^1_{X/Y}$, $\mathcal{H}^{-1} = \mathcal{I}/\mathcal{I}^2$ (the conormal sheaf).
  • Transitivity triangle. For $X \to Y \to Z$ there is a distinguished triangle $f^* L_{Y/Z} \to L_{X/Z} \to L_{X/Y} \to f^* L_{Y/Z}[1]$ — the derived analogue of the conormal exact sequence.
  • Deformation invariants. $\mathrm{Def}_{X/Y}(D) = \mathrm{Ext}^1_{\mathcal{O}_X}(L_{X/Y}, \mathcal{O}_X)$, the obstruction lives in $\mathrm{Ext}^2_{\mathcal{O}_X}(L_{X/Y}, \mathcal{O}_X)$. For $X/Y$ smooth this recovers $H^1(X, T_{X/Y})$ and $H^2(X, T_{X/Y})$ — sections 1–3 are the smooth case of the Ext interpretation.

The point is uniformity: even when $X_0$ is singular and $T_{X_0} = \mathcal{H}om(\Omega^1_{X_0}, \mathcal{O}_{X_0})$ is the "wrong" object, $\mathrm{Ext}^i(L_{X_0/k}, \mathcal{O}_{X_0})$ is always the right one. Read Illusie's two-volume Complexe cotangent et déformations (SLN 239 + 283) once and the obstruction-theoretic fog clears.

Worked example. For $X_0 = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[x]/(x^2)$ (a fat point), $L_{X_0/k}$ has $\mathcal{H}^0 = \Omega^1 = k \cdot dx /(2x\,dx) \cong k$ and $\mathcal{H}^{-1} = (x^2)/(x^4) \cong k \cdot \overline{x^2}$ — both 1-dimensional. The deformation problem has $\mathrm{Ext}^1(L, k) = k$ (one direction: $x^2 = t$, smoothing the fat point) and $\mathrm{Ext}^2 = 0$ (no obstruction), correctly capturing $\mathrm{Def}(\text{fat point}) \cong \mathrm{Spec}\,k[\![t]\!]$.

6. Worked example: deformations of a smooth curve

The cleanest concrete instance of the §1–5 machinery: a smooth projective curve $C$ over $k = \bar k$, of genus $g$. Two facts conspire to give a uniformly clean answer:

  • $\dim C = 1 \Rightarrow H^i(C, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for any $i \ge 2$ and any coherent $\mathcal{F}$. In particular $H^2(C, T_C) = 0$, so curves are unobstructed — every first-order deformation extends to all orders.
  • Riemann–Roch + Serre duality compute $H^1(C, T_C)$ explicitly. $T_C$ is a line bundle of degree $2 - 2g$ (it is the dual of the canonical bundle $\Omega^1_C$, which has degree $2g - 2$). For $g \ge 2$, $\deg T_C = 2 - 2g < 0$, hence $H^0(C, T_C) = 0$ (no infinitesimal automorphisms). Riemann–Roch then gives

$\chi(T_C) = \deg T_C - g + 1 = (2 - 2g) - g + 1 = 3 - 3g$,

so $\dim H^1(C, T_C) = -\chi(T_C) = 3g - 3$. Equivalently, by Serre duality $H^1(C, T_C) \cong H^0(C, \Omega^1_C \otimes \Omega^1_C)^\vee = H^0(C, \Omega^{\otimes 2}_C)^\vee$ — the dual of the space of quadratic differentials, which has dimension $3g - 3$ by Riemann–Roch applied to $\Omega^{\otimes 2}_C$ (degree $4g - 4$).

So for $g \ge 2$ the moduli space $\mathcal{M}_g$ is smooth of dimension $3g - 3$ at every point. The boundary cases:

  • $g = 0$: $C = \mathbb{P}^1$, $T = \mathcal{O}(2)$, $H^1 = 0$. Rigid: $\mathcal{M}_0$ is a point.
  • $g = 1$: $C$ elliptic, $T_C = \mathcal{O}_C$, $H^1(C, \mathcal{O}_C) \cong k$ has dimension $1$. $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ is one-dimensional (the $j$-line). Note $h^0(T_C) = 1$ as well — non-trivial automorphisms appear, foreshadowing why $\mathcal{M}_g$ for $g \le 1$ is best treated as a stack.
  • $g \ge 2$: $\dim \mathcal{M}_g = 3g - 3$, automorphism groups are finite, and $\mathcal{M}_g$ is a quasi-projective variety (Deligne–Mumford).

Worked example. For $g = 4$: $\dim \mathcal{M}_4 = 3(4) - 3 = 9$. A canonical curve of genus $4$ embeds via $\Omega^1$ into $\mathbb{P}^3$ as a degree-$6$ curve, the complete intersection of a quadric and a cubic. The "quadric or cubic" choice (whether the quadric is smooth or a cone) gives a stratification of $\mathcal{M}_4$ by trigonal versus general curves — the kind of geometric feature only made possible by knowing $\mathcal{M}_g$ is $9$-dimensional and smooth here.

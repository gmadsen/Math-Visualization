Expanders

Sparse graphs that are nevertheless impossible to cut: every small set of vertices touches many new neighbours, the second eigenvalue stays bounded away from the trivial one, and a random walk forgets where it started in $O(\log n)$ steps. Expanders are the workhorse of pseudorandomness — codes, derandomisation, and Reingold's $\mathrm{LOGSPACE}$ algorithm for connectivity all run on them.

1. Combinatorial expansion

Fix a $d$-regular graph $G=(V,E)$ on $n=|V|$ vertices. For a vertex set $S\subseteq V$ write $N(S)$ for the set of vertices outside $S$ adjacent to something in $S$. We say $G$ is an $(n,d,\varepsilon)$-expander when

$|N(S)| \;\ge\; (1+\varepsilon)\,|S| \qquad \text{for every } S \text{ with } |S|\le n/2.$

Small sets always touch lots of new vertices. The restriction $|S|\le n/2$ is unavoidable: $N(S)\subseteq V\setminus S$ has at most $n-|S|$ elements, so any inequality $|N(S)|\ge(1+\varepsilon)|S|$ forces $|S|\le n/(2+\varepsilon)$. The complement side is symmetric, so this is no real loss.

The slogan: $G$ has no "narrow waist". A balanced cut $V=S\sqcup\bar S$ would have $|N(S)|\le n-|S|=|S|$, which the expansion condition rules out; that is why expanders cannot be cleanly bisected.

2. Spectral expansion

Now fix a $d$-regular graph and look at the normalized adjacency $\widehat A = A/d$. Because $G$ is regular, $\widehat A$ has $\mathbf{1}$ as a top eigenvector with eigenvalue $1$; the rest of its spectrum lives in $[-1,1]$. Order the eigenvalues

$1 = \lambda_1 \;\ge\; \lambda_2 \;\ge\; \cdots \;\ge\; \lambda_n \;\ge\; -1.$

The graph is a spectral expander when there is a uniform spectral gap $1-\lambda_2 \ge \delta > 0$ across the family. The largest non-trivial eigenvalue $\lambda_2$ measures how close $G$ is to disconnecting (which would make $\lambda_2 = 1$).

Here is the easy direction of the equivalence with edge expansion. Given $S\subseteq V$ with $|S|\le n/2$, write $\mathbf{1}_S = (|S|/n)\mathbf{1} + v$ with $v\perp\mathbf{1}$. Then

$\langle \mathbf{1}_S, \widehat A \mathbf{1}_S\rangle \;=\; \frac{|S|^2}{n} + \langle v, \widehat A v\rangle \;\le\; \frac{|S|^2}{n} + \lambda_2\,\|v\|^2,$

and the left side counts edges inside $S$ divided by $d$. A short calculation turns this into $|E(S,\bar S)| \ge \tfrac{d}{2}(1-\lambda_2)\,\tfrac{|S|\,|\bar S|}{n}$, so a spectral gap forces edge expansion. (Vertex expansion follows up to constants.)

3. The Cheeger inequality

Define the edge expansion (or Cheeger constant) of $G$ by

$\phi(G) \;=\; \min_{|S|\le n/2} \frac{|E(S, \bar S)|}{d\,|S|}.$

This is the discrete analogue of the isoperimetric ratio $\operatorname{vol}(\partial \Omega)/\operatorname{vol}(\Omega)$ on a Riemannian manifold. Cheeger's classical 1970 theorem bounds the smallest positive Laplace eigenvalue from below by $h(M)^2/4$. The graph version, due to Alon–Milman and Dodziuk, says

$\frac{1}{2}\,\lambda_2(\mathcal{L}) \;\le\; \phi(G) \;\le\; \sqrt{2\,\lambda_2(\mathcal{L})},$

where $\lambda_2(\mathcal{L}) = 1 - \lambda_2(\widehat A)$ is the second eigenvalue of the normalized Laplacian $\mathcal{L} = I - \widehat A$. The lower bound is straightforward; the upper bound is the deeper direction, proved by the spectral sweep: threshold the Fiedler eigenvector $u_2$ at every value, pick the best resulting cut, and that cut achieves the right ratio.

4. Explicit constructions

A non-trivial existence proof: a random $d$-regular graph is almost surely an expander (Pinsker, 1973). But pseudorandomness applications need explicit constructions — a deterministic recipe one can write down. There are three pillars.

Margulis–Gabber–Galil (1973–81)

Vertices are $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^2$. Edges are given by the action of eight maps from $SL_2(\mathbb{Z})$:

$T_1\colon (x,y)\mapsto(x,x+y),\quad T_2\colon (x,y)\mapsto(x+y,y),\quad T_3\colon (x,y)\mapsto(x,x+y+1),\quad T_4\colon (x,y)\mapsto(x+y+1,y),$

and their inverses. The spectral gap is uniform in $n$, with the lower bound coming from the Kazhdan property (T) of $SL_3(\mathbb{Z})$. This was the first explicit family; the gap is sub-optimal but everything is elementary group theory.

Lubotzky–Phillips–Sarnak (1988)

Fix primes $p\ne q$ with $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$. Vertices of the LPS graph $X^{p,q}$ are elements of $\mathrm{PGL}_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$ (or $\mathrm{PSL}_2$, depending on whether $p$ is a square mod $q$). Edges connect $g$ to $\alpha_i g$ where the $\alpha_i$ are the $p+1$ integer quaternions of norm $p$, projected into $\mathrm{PGL}_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$. The result is a $(p+1)$-regular Cayley graph with the Ramanujan property $|\lambda| \le 2\sqrt p$ — saturating Alon–Boppana.

Zigzag product (Reingold–Vadhan–Wigderson, 2002)

Given a "big" graph $G$ on $N$ vertices with degree $D$ and a "small" graph $H$ on $D$ vertices with degree $d$, the zigzag product $G \,\textcircled{z}\, H$ has $ND$ vertices, degree $d^2$, and a spectral gap that is essentially the product of the two parent gaps. Iterating this gives constant-degree expanders of arbitrary size from a single small "seed" graph — and the analysis is purely combinatorial. This is the construction behind Reingold's $\mathrm{LOGSPACE}$ algorithm for connectivity.

5. Applications

Why does anyone care? Because expanders are the cheapest source of pseudorandomness in computer science. A short tour of where they show up:

Derandomisation: walk on an expander

Suppose a randomised algorithm uses $t$ independent random samples and you want to amplify success probability. Instead of $t \log n$ random bits, take a single random vertex of an expander and walk for $t$ steps — only $\log n + O(t)$ random bits, and the expander Chernoff bound says the empirical mean concentrates as well as for independent samples. This is seed extension.

Error-correcting codes (Sipser–Spielman, 1996)

Take a bipartite expander with $n$ left vertices (bits) and $cn$ right vertices (parity checks); each right vertex requires the bits in its neighbourhood to XOR to zero. Expansion forces low-weight error patterns to fail many checks, so an iterative bit-flipping decoder runs in linear time and corrects a constant fraction of errors. These were the first asymptotically good codes with linear-time encoding and decoding.

MCMC sampling

The mixing time of a reversible Markov chain on $G$ is $O((\log n)/\gamma)$ where $\gamma=1-\lambda_2$ is the spectral gap. Designing chains as random walks on expanders gives $O(\log n)$ mixing and turns hard counting/integration problems into rapid sampling.

Reingold's $\mathrm{LOGSPACE}$ connectivity (2004)

Given an undirected graph $G$ and vertices $s,t$, decide whether they are in the same component. Reingold turns $G$ into an expander by zigzag iteration in logarithmic space, then walks $\Theta(\log n)$ steps from $s$. If $t$ is reachable, the walk visits it w.h.p. The construction proves $\mathrm{SL}=\mathrm{L}$, settling a major question in space complexity.

6. Ramanujan graphs and Alon–Boppana

How small can $\lambda_2$ be in a $d$-regular graph family? The honest answer is given by the spectral measure of the $d$-regular infinite tree $T_d$ — the universal cover of every $d$-regular graph. Its spectrum is the interval $[-2\sqrt{d-1},\,2\sqrt{d-1}]$ (Kesten 1959). Lifting to finite quotients:

Alon–Boppana lower bound. For any infinite family of $d$-regular graphs $G_n$ with $|V(G_n)|\to\infty$,

$\liminf_{n\to\infty} \lambda_2(A_{G_n}) \;\ge\; 2\sqrt{d-1}.$

The proof: the universal cover is $T_d$, so the spectrum of $G_n$ converges to that of $T_d$, whose top is $2\sqrt{d-1}$.

Definition. A $d$-regular graph $G$ is Ramanujan when every nontrivial eigenvalue $\lambda$ of its adjacency matrix satisfies $|\lambda| \le 2\sqrt{d-1}$ — i.e. $G$ saturates Alon–Boppana.

The LPS construction proves that infinite families of Ramanujan graphs exist at every degree $p+1$ where $p$ is prime. The depth of the result: it reduces the eigenvalue bound to Deligne's proof of the Ramanujan–Petersson conjecture for weight-$2$ modular forms. Each adjacency eigenvalue is a Hecke eigenvalue $a_p$ of a cusp form, and Deligne's $|a_p| \le 2\sqrt p$ (a corollary of his Weil-conjectures proof) is exactly the Ramanujan bound for the graph. The number-theoretic name is no accident.

The strip is $[-d, d]$; the shaded yellow band is the Ramanujan window $[-2\sqrt{d-1}, 2\sqrt{d-1}]$. A graph is Ramanujan iff every dot except the trivial $\pm d$ falls inside the band. The cycle isn't really an expander, so its $\lambda_2 \to d$ as $n\to\infty$ and it overflows the band.

Connections

Optimal expanders at every degree (not just $p+1$) became reality only in 2013–15: Marcus–Spielman–Srivastava produced bipartite Ramanujan graphs of every degree via interlacing polynomials, and the non-bipartite case at all $d\ge 3$ remains open. High-dimensional analogues (simplicial complexes with spectral expansion in every dimension) are an active arena bridging combinatorics and geometry.

  • Representation theory — Characters — the LPS bound on Cayley graph eigenvalues factors through irreducible representations of $\mathrm{PGL}_2$.

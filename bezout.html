Bézout's theorem: counting plane-curve intersections

Two plane curves of degrees $d$ and $e$ should meet in $de$ points. Getting this to be literally true takes three conceptual moves: closing the field, projectivizing, and counting with multiplicity. Once you make those moves, the chord–tangent construction on a cubic becomes lawful and you can do geometry with it.

1. The naive count vs. the real count

Pick two conics in the real affine plane $\mathbb{R}^2$. Generically they meet in four points — and that already matches the naive expectation of $2\cdot 2=4$. But push them around and the count collapses: ellipses that barely kiss yield two points, tangent pairs yield just one, and parallel parabolas can miss each other entirely. Any honest theorem on intersection numbers has to explain why the count seems to fluctuate and which count is the invariant one.

Here is the toy case: the unit circle $x^2+y^2=1$ and a horizontal line $y=t$. Slide $t$ through the interval $(-1,1)$ and you get two intersection points; at $t=\pm 1$ they collide; outside you see none at all. Naively the count is "two, one, or zero." The right count is always two, but only after we agree to work over $\mathbb{C}$ and count the collision as a double point.

Two behaviours drive everything that follows:

  • Points escape into $\mathbb{C}$. When $|t|>1$ the two intersection points are still there — they are just complex conjugate. Counting over $\mathbb{R}$ undercounts; counting over $\mathbb{C}$ (or any algebraically closed field) is invariant.
  • Points escape to infinity. Two parallel lines in $\mathbb{R}^2$ meet nowhere, yet we want $1\cdot 1=1$ intersection. The missing point lives "at infinity" in the direction the lines share. The cure is to work in the projective plane $\mathbb{P}^2$.

And a third, subtler issue appears already above: when the line is tangent to the circle, two distinct points have collapsed into one fat point. The count should record fatness. That is the intersection multiplicity.

2. Three refinements

To turn "$de$ intersections" into a theorem we make three conceptual moves, each one closing a way the naive count can fail.

(a) Algebraically closed ground field

Real plane curves miss the non-real common zeros of their defining polynomials. The fix: do the algebra over $\overline{k}$, the algebraic closure. For $k=\mathbb{R}$ that is $\mathbb{C}$. Counting intersections means counting solutions in $\overline{k}^{\,2}$ (or rather, in $\mathbb{P}^2_{\overline{k}}$). See Galois theory for what "algebraic closure" means as a field extension.

(b) Projective completion

An affine curve $C\subset\mathbb{A}^2$ cut out by $f(x,y)=0$ has a natural projective closure $\overline{C}\subset\mathbb{P}^2$ cut out by the homogenization $F(X,Y,Z)=Z^{\deg f}\,f(X/Z,Y/Z)$. Points at infinity are the zeros of $F(X,Y,0)$ — the "leading form" of $f$. Two lines meet once projectively, even if they are parallel affinely.

(c) Counting with multiplicity

Even over $\mathbb{C}$ and in $\mathbb{P}^2$, two curves that share a tangent line at a point still meet there "more than once." A useful count must record the higher-order contact. This is the intersection multiplicity $I_P(f,g)$, defined algebraically in §3.

Slogan. The three refinements are not ad hoc — they are the three directions in which a commutative algebra student should instinctively complete the picture: close the field (so every polynomial factors), projectivize (so the structure sheaf has no "missing charts"), and replace elements by their local ring (so multiplicities appear as $\dim_k$).

3. Intersection multiplicity

Let $P\in\mathbb{A}^2$ (or more generally in $\mathbb{P}^2$) and let $f,g\in k[x,y]$ be polynomials both vanishing at $P$. The intersection multiplicity of $f$ and $g$ at $P$ is

$$I_P(f,g) \;=\; \dim_k \bigl(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{A}^2,P} \,/\, (f,g)\bigr).$$

Here $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{A}^2,P}=k[x,y]_{\mathfrak{m}_P}$ is the local ring at $P$ — the ring of rational functions regular at $P$ — and $(f,g)$ is the ideal they generate inside it. The quotient is a finite-dimensional $k$-vector space iff $P$ is an isolated common zero; its dimension is the multiplicity.

Three concrete properties determine it uniquely, and together give a hands-on recipe:

  • Transversality. If $f(x,y)=x$ and $g(x,y)=y$ (the coordinate axes crossing at the origin), then $I_P(f,g)=1$.
  • Bilinearity under products. $I_P(f,gh)=I_P(f,g)+I_P(f,h)$.
  • Invariance under row-reduction. $I_P(f,g)=I_P(f,g+hf)$ for any polynomial $h$.

Together they let you compute $I_P(f,g)$ by elementary manipulations; the widget below does that for three canonical examples.

Worked sample: tangent vs. transverse

Take $f=y$, $g=y-x^2$ at $P=(0,0)$. In the local ring, $(f,g)=(y,\,y-x^2)=(y,\,x^2)$ — the second generator is $g-f$, which already eliminates the $y$ term. The quotient $\mathcal{O}_{P}/(y,x^2)$ is spanned by $\{1,x\}$ as a $k$-vector space, so $I_P(f,g)=2$. That matches the geometric picture: the $x$-axis is tangent to the parabola at the origin.

Take instead $f=y$, $g=y^2-x^3$. Now $(f,g)=(y,x^3)$, the quotient has $k$-basis $\{1,x,x^2\}$, and $I_P=3$. The $x$-axis is an inflectional tangent of the cuspidal cubic — triple contact.

Why this is the right definition. The local ring $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2,P}$ is exactly the ring you need in order to talk about the behaviour of $f$ and $g$ "near $P$" without contamination from any other common zero. Quotienting by $(f,g)$ says: identify polynomials that differ by something in the ideal of the common vanishing locus. The dimension of this quotient is a finite, additive, local invariant — exactly the three axioms it ought to satisfy.

4. Resultants — a computational tool

Intersection multiplicities via local rings are beautiful but not always easy to compute. The resultant is a determinantal gadget that, for two polynomials in one variable, packages "do they share a root?" as a single number.

Given $f(Y) = a_d Y^d + \cdots + a_0$ and $g(Y) = b_e Y^e + \cdots + b_0$ with $a_d,b_e\neq 0$, the Sylvester resultant is the determinant of the $(d+e)\times(d+e)$ matrix

$$\mathrm{Res}(f,g) \;=\; \det\!\begin{pmatrix} a_d & a_{d-1} & \cdots & a_0 & & \\ & a_d & a_{d-1} & \cdots & a_0 & \\ & & \ddots & & & \\ b_e & b_{e-1} & \cdots & b_0 & & \\ & b_e & b_{e-1} & \cdots & b_0 & \\ & & \ddots & & & \end{pmatrix}$$

($e$ rows of $a$-coefficients and $d$ rows of $b$-coefficients; zeros fill the rest.) Its key property:

Theorem (resultant = common-root detector). $\mathrm{Res}(f,g)=0$ if and only if $f$ and $g$ share a root in $\overline{k}$.

For bivariate $f(x,y), g(x,y)$, fix $y$ as a parameter: form $\mathrm{Res}_x(f,g)$, a polynomial in $y$ alone. Its roots are exactly the $y$-coordinates of the common zeros of $f$ and $g$ (up to points at infinity or $y$-axis-direction common zeros, which one handles by going projective). Moreover, the multiplicity of a root $y_0$ in $\mathrm{Res}_x(f,g)$ equals the total intersection multiplicity of $f=0,g=0$ along the line $y=y_0$. This connects the algebraic count to the analytic one.

There is a clean bridge: if $C=V(f), D=V(g)\subset\mathbb{P}^2$ are curves with no common component, then

$$\deg\bigl(\mathrm{Res}_Y(F,G)(X,Z)\bigr) \;=\; \deg f\cdot \deg g \;=\; de.$$

The resultant is a form of degree $de$ in $(X,Z)$, hence has $de$ zeros in $\mathbb{P}^1$ counted with multiplicity — and each zero corresponds (via its fibre over $Y$) to a set of intersection points whose combined $I_P$'s add up to the resultant's multiplicity. That gives the resultant-based proof of Bézout.

Parallels with commutative algebra. The resultant lives inside the eliminant ideal $(f,g)\cap k[x]$; computing it is the classical form of elimination theory, which in modern language becomes Gröbner bases or pushforward along projection — see commutative algebra and schemes.

5. Statement of Bézout's theorem

Theorem (Bézout). Let $k$ be an algebraically closed field and let $C, D\subset\mathbb{P}^2_k$ be projective plane curves of degrees $d,e\ge 1$ with no common irreducible component. Then $$\sum_{P\in C\cap D} I_P(C,D) \;=\; d\,e,$$ where $I_P(C,D)$ is the intersection multiplicity defined in §3. In particular, $C$ and $D$ meet in at most $de$ points, and in exactly $de$ points when counted with multiplicity.

Three hypotheses cover the three refinements of §2:

  1. $k$ algebraically closed — so all common zeros are accounted for (refinement (a)).
  2. Curves in $\mathbb{P}^2$ — so no points escape to infinity (refinement (b)).
  3. $I_P$ in the sum — so tangencies and singular intersections are counted correctly (refinement (c)).

The "no common component" hypothesis is essential: if $C$ and $D$ share a curve component, they meet in infinitely many points and the sum is meaningless. Dropping any other hypothesis yields strict inequality.

Sketch of the proof via resultants

Pick coordinates so that $[0:1:0]\notin C\cup D$. Form the resultant $R(X,Z)=\mathrm{Res}_Y(F,G)$ where $F,G$ are the defining forms. Three facts — each a computation:

  • $R$ is a form of degree $de$ in $(X,Z)$, so it has $de$ roots in $\mathbb{P}^1$ (with multiplicity).
  • A root $[X_0:Z_0]$ of $R$ corresponds to a line through $[0:1:0]$ that cuts both $C$ and $D$ in at least one common point — i.e. to a set of intersection points of $C$ and $D$ projecting to $[X_0:Z_0]$.
  • The $Y$-multiplicity of that root equals the sum of $I_P(C,D)$ over the points $P$ above it.

Totalling: $\sum_P I_P = \deg R = de$. Done.

6. Worked examples

Line meets conic: $1\cdot 2 = 2$

A line $L$ of degree 1 and a smooth conic $Q$ of degree 2 meet in $1\cdot 2 = 2$ points. Over $\mathbb{C}$, in $\mathbb{P}^2$, these two are always there: possibly two transverse real points, possibly a tangency (one real point of multiplicity 2), possibly two complex conjugate points.

Two conics: $2\cdot 2 = 4$

The "four intersection points of two conics" is the first non-trivial case. Over $\mathbb{C}$ with no common component, the count is always 4, though it may include tangential or imaginary points.

Two conics in $\mathbb{P}^2$
dial parameters; intersection count is always 4

Pascal's theorem: six conic points, three collinear lines

Pascal (1640, age 16) observed: if you inscribe a hexagon in any conic and extend its three pairs of opposite sides, the three intersection points are collinear. The line is called the Pascal line of the hexagon. This is a pure Bézout consequence: the six vertices are the $2\cdot 3=6$ intersection points of the conic with a certain reducible cubic (a union of three lines), and Bézout forces the three "extra" intersections to lie on a common line.

Cubic meets line: $3\cdot 1 = 3$ and the chord–tangent law

A smooth cubic $C\subset\mathbb{P}^2$ meets a line $L$ in exactly $3$ points counted with multiplicity. This is the Bézout fact that makes the chord–tangent law on an elliptic curve lawful: given two points $P,Q\in C(k)$, the line through them meets $C$ in a uniquely determined third point $R$, and the group operation is defined by reflecting $R$ about the identity.

Cubic meets cubic: $3\cdot 3 = 9$ — and the nine-point miracle

Two cubics meet in nine points. This count is the engine behind a classical theorem of Chasles/Cayley–Bacharach:

Cayley–Bacharach (special case). If two cubics $C_1, C_2\subset\mathbb{P}^2$ meet in nine distinct points, and if a third cubic $C_3$ passes through eight of those nine, then $C_3$ passes through the ninth.

Applied to a single smooth cubic $E$ (an elliptic curve), this gives the associativity of the chord–tangent group law: $(P+Q)+R = P+(Q+R)$ is a statement about three cubics (each a union of three lines) sharing nine points. The proof of associativity is an elegant Bézout argument, and nothing less would work.

7. Cayley–Bacharach and the associativity miracle

The cubic-meets-cubic count of §6 hides a surprisingly rigid combinatorial phenomenon: given any nine points that appear as the common zeros of two cubics, almost every cubic that contains eight of them automatically contains the ninth. Made precise, this is the Cayley–Bacharach theorem, and it is the engine behind associativity of the elliptic-curve group law.

Cayley–Bacharach (classical form). Let $C_1, C_2\subset \mathbb{P}^2$ be plane cubics meeting transversely in nine distinct points $\{P_1,\dots,P_9\}$. Then every cubic $C_3$ passing through $P_1,\dots,P_8$ also passes through $P_9$.

The dimension count that proves this is itself a Bézout bookkeeping exercise. The space of cubics in $\mathbb{P}^2$ has projective dimension $\binom{3+2}{2}-1=9$. Passing through one prescribed point is one linear condition, so eight points impose eight linear conditions — yielding at least a $\mathbb{P}^1$-family of cubics. The pencil $\lambda C_1 + \mu C_2$ (with $[\lambda:\mu]\in\mathbb{P}^1$) sits inside that family. Any ninth linear condition on the pencil is either identically satisfied or cuts out a unique cubic; Bézout's $3\cdot 3=9$ count ensures that $P_9$ is already in the base locus, so the condition is tautological.

The miracle pays off on any smooth cubic $E\subset \mathbb{P}^2$. Define the chord–tangent addition by choosing a point $O\in E$ and setting $$P + Q \;=\; O \ast (P\ast Q),$$ where $X\ast Y$ is the "third intersection of line $\overline{XY}$ with $E$." Commutativity is obvious and the existence of identities and inverses is a short chase. Associativity — which in any other construction would be a nightmare — pops out of Cayley–Bacharach by applying it to three line-triples assembled from $P,Q,R$ and their partial sums. Nine points, eight of which force the ninth: the associativity identity $(P+Q)+R = P+(Q+R)$ falls out as "the ninth point."

A less geometric but equally useful corollary: if a plane cubic passes through eight of the nine flexes of a smooth cubic, it passes through the ninth. The nine flex points form a $(\mathbb{Z}/3)^2$-subgroup of $E$, and Cayley–Bacharach is the statement that this subgroup is determined by any eight of its nine points — a rigidity one does not expect from the linear algebra alone.

Generalizations. Cayley–Bacharach extends to curves of degrees $d, d$ meeting in $d^2$ points: any curve of degree $2d-3$ through $d^2-1$ of them passes through the last one. It is the first instance of a much larger pattern — "residual intersection" in modern intersection theory — and one of the reasons $de$ is not just a count but a robust algebraic constraint.

8. Bézout in higher dimensions

Bézout's $\mathbb{P}^2$ theorem is the shadow of a more general intersection-theoretic statement in $\mathbb{P}^n$. The cleanest formulation:

Theorem (Bézout in $\mathbb{P}^n$). Let $H_1,\dots,H_n\subset \mathbb{P}^n_k$ be hypersurfaces of degrees $d_1,\dots,d_n$, defined over an algebraically closed field $k$, meeting in a zero-dimensional set $Z$. Then $$\sum_{P\in Z} \mathrm{length}_{\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n,P}}\!\bigl(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n,P}/(f_1,\dots,f_n)\bigr) \;=\; d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n.$$

The $\mathbb{P}^2$ theorem is the case $n=2$, where "length" collapses to $\dim_k$. For $n=3$, three surfaces of degrees $d_1,d_2,d_3$ meet in $d_1 d_2 d_3$ points counted with multiplicity — e.g., three generic quadrics in $\mathbb{P}^3$ meet in $2\cdot 2\cdot 2=8$ points. The key hypotheses are the same as in $\mathbb{P}^2$: algebraically closed field, projective ambient, and count with multiplicity; and the same hypothesis needs stating anew — the intersection must be zero-dimensional, i.e. the $H_i$ must meet properly. If any two hypersurfaces share a positive-dimensional component, the total count is infinite.

The modern formulation recasts this as a computation in the Chow ring $A^{\ast}(\mathbb{P}^n) = \mathbb{Z}[H]/(H^{n+1})$, where $H$ is the hyperplane class. A hypersurface of degree $d_i$ has class $d_i H$, so $$[H_1]\cdot [H_2]\cdots [H_n] \;=\; d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n \cdot H^n \;=\; d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n\cdot [\text{pt}].$$ The intersection pairing on Chow classes reproduces Bézout without mentioning polynomials.

Two variations are useful. First, if the subvarieties have complementary codimensions summing to $n$ but are not cut out by single equations, the Chow-theoretic count still works via degrees of the subvarieties. Second, for positive-dimensional intersections, the right generalization is Fulton–MacPherson intersection theory, which attaches a well-defined excess intersection class supported on the intersection.

Why the passage to $\mathbb{P}^n$ is gentle. None of the three refinements from §2 change meaning: projectivize, take $\bar k$, count length. What gets genuinely more subtle is the proper intersection hypothesis — in higher dimensions it is much easier for two subvarieties to share an excess component, and the naïve sum of local lengths can be infinite even when the degrees are small. A typical fix is to perturb (move one of the cycles to a rationally equivalent one in general position), which is what the Chow ring formalizes.

9. Applications and forward references

Why Bézout enables elliptic-curve arithmetic

The three numerical miracles Bézout makes possible:

  • Third-point determination. A line meets a cubic in three points counted with multiplicity — so given any two of those points, the third is determined. This is the entire basis of the chord–tangent construction.
  • Associativity. A $9$-point Bézout count combined with Cayley–Bacharach converts the pictorial "rotate and reflect" into a genuine abelian-group law.
  • Finiteness of torsion over number fields. The $n$-torsion of $E$ is cut out inside $E\times E$ by polynomial equations whose combined Bézout degree bounds its size — you end up with $|E[n]|=n^2$ over $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$.

Degeneracy scales the count, never invalidates it

If one of the curves degenerates (becomes singular, reducible, or gains a common component), the intersection numbers redistribute but the total is preserved by continuity in the space of curves of a given degree. This is the first glimpse of deformation invariance of intersection numbers — a cornerstone of modern scheme-theoretic intersection theory.

Local-to-global: where $I_P$ really comes from

The equation $$I_P(C,D) = \dim_k\bigl(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2,P}/(f,g)\bigr)$$ is the local length of an intersection, and the Bézout theorem says the global intersection "length" is a topological–algebraic constant $de$. This pattern — local lengths summing to a degree — generalizes in two directions: to higher-dimensional intersections in $\mathbb{P}^n$ (with Chow groups and the intersection pairing from homological algebra) and to the theory of schemes, where the fibre of $V(f)\cap V(g)$ over the base becomes a structured sheaf whose stalk-by-stalk length is the local multiplicity; see sheaves and sheaf cohomology.

The view from algebraic geometry

  • Bézout computes $\int_{\mathbb{P}^2}[C]\cdot[D]$ in the Chow ring $A^*(\mathbb{P}^2)=\mathbb{Z}[H]/(H^3)$, with $[C]=d\,H$ and $[D]=e\,H$, so $[C]\cdot[D]=de\,H^2=de\cdot[\text{pt}]$.
  • In scheme-theoretic language the count is $\dim_k H^0(\mathbb{P}^2,\mathcal{O}/(F,G))$, a sheaf cohomology calculation.
  • For smooth curves in higher $\mathbb{P}^n$ one uses degree of the ideal sheaf instead of degree of a polynomial — Bézout is the $\mathbb{P}^2$ shadow of Hilbert–Samuel multiplicity.

What's next

Bézout is the gateway: once intersection numbers are well defined on $\mathbb{P}^2$ you can build the group law on cubics (elliptic curves) and study degenerations (singular cubics and reduction). In number-theoretic direction, the chord–tangent law on a cubic over $\mathbb{Q}_p$ is the entry to $p$-adic arithmetic of elliptic curves, and the analytic avatar is the theory of modular forms and Dirichlet series attached to $E/\mathbb{Q}$. For the projective setup underpinning all of this, see the foundational projective plane page; for the commutative-algebra engine behind intersection multiplicity, see commutative algebra.

Takeaway. Bézout's theorem is not a curiosity about conics. It is the first theorem that says geometry in $\mathbb{P}^2$ behaves like the algebra of $k[x,y,z]$ — and it converts the intuitive "count of intersections" into a robust invariant that is at the heart of everything from elliptic curves to Frobenius reciprocity in number theory.

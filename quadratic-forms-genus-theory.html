Quadratic forms & genus theory

Gauss's Disquisitiones Arithmeticae (1801) classifies binary quadratic forms $ax^2+bxy+cy^2$ by discriminant and equivalence. The resulting form class group, Gauss composition, and genus theory constitute both a computational masterpiece and the historical seed of modern algebraic number theory — every prime represented by a given form is governed by a congruence condition that traces directly back to Legendre symbols and, ultimately, to ideal class groups of imaginary quadratic fields.

1. Binary quadratic forms

A binary quadratic form is a homogeneous degree-2 polynomial in two variables: $$ f(x,y) = ax^2 + bxy + cy^2, \qquad a,b,c \in \mathbb{Z}. $$ The single most important invariant is the discriminant $$ D = b^2 - 4ac. $$ The sign of $D$ determines the character of $f$: if $D < 0$ and $a > 0$ then $f(x,y) > 0$ for all $(x,y)\ne(0,0)$ (positive-definite); if $D > 0$ then $f$ takes both positive and negative values (indefinite).

Two forms $f$ and $g$ are properly equivalent if $g(x,y) = f(px+qy,\,rx+sy)$ for some matrix $\begin{pmatrix}p&q\\r&s\end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{GL}_2^+(\mathbb{Z})$ (determinant $+1$). Equivalence preserves $D$ and the set of integers represented by $f$. A form is primitive if $\gcd(a,b,c)=1$; primitivity is also preserved.

Why discriminants? Completing the square gives $4af(x,y) = (2ax+by)^2 - Dy^2$. So for $D < 0$ the form is positive-definite (for $a>0$), and the root structure of the associated quadratic $at^2+bt+c$ has $D$ as the classical discriminant. The same $D$ governs which number field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{D})$ the form "lives over."

What integers does a form represent?

We say $f$ represents an integer $n$ if $f(x,y)=n$ for some $(x,y)\in\mathbb{Z}^2$. Two equivalent forms represent exactly the same integers. The key question of genus theory is: for which primes $p$ does a given form represent $p$? The answer will depend only on $p$ modulo $|D|$, a fact equivalent to quadratic reciprocity.

2. Reduction theory

For positive-definite forms ($D < 0$, $a > 0$), Gauss showed every equivalence class contains a unique reduced form satisfying: $$ |b| \le a \le c, \quad \text{and} \quad b \ge 0 \text{ if } |b| = a \text{ or } a = c. $$ This turns the set of equivalence classes into a finite, computable list. The number of such classes, the class number $h(D)$, depends only on $D$. For example, $h(-4)=1$, $h(-23)=3$, $h(-163)=1$ (a deep fact connected to Heegner numbers).

The reduction algorithm

Starting from any primitive positive-definite form $(a,b,c)$ with $D = b^2-4ac$:

  1. If $a > c$, swap $a \leftrightarrow c$ and negate $b$ (which replaces $f(x,y)$ by $f(y,x)$).
  2. If $|b| > a$, replace $b$ by $b - 2ak$ where $k = \lfloor (b+a)/(2a) \rfloor$; update $c$ via $c \leftarrow (b'^2 - D)/(4a)$ using new $b' = b-2ak$.
  3. Repeat until the inequalities hold.

This algorithm terminates because at each step $a$ strictly decreases or the pair $(a,|b|)$ improves lexicographically.

3. The form class group

The set $\mathrm{Cl}(D)$ of proper equivalence classes of primitive forms of discriminant $D$ is more than a set — it carries a group structure discovered by Gauss (via composition of forms) and elegantly repackaged by Dirichlet. The Dirichlet composition of two primitive forms $f_1,f_2$ of discriminant $D$ is the unique primitive form $f_1 * f_2$ of discriminant $D$ such that: $$ f_1(x_1,y_1)\cdot f_2(x_2,y_2) = (f_1*f_2)\bigl(B(x_1,x_2,y_1,y_2),\, C(x_1,x_2,y_1,y_2)\bigr) $$ for explicit bilinear forms $B$ and $C$. This composition descends to an abelian group law on $\mathrm{Cl}(D)$.

The identity element is the class of the principal form: $$ f_0 = \begin{cases} x^2 - \tfrac{D}{4}y^2 & \text{if } 4\mid D, \\ x^2 + xy - \tfrac{D-1}{4}y^2 & \text{if } D \equiv 1\pmod{4}. \end{cases} $$ The inverse of the class of $(a,b,c)$ is the class of $(a,-b,c)$ — simply negate the middle coefficient.

A finite abelian group. $|\mathrm{Cl}(D)| = h(D)$, the class number. By Minkowski's bound, every class contains a form with $a \le \sqrt{|D|/3}$, so $h(D)$ is finite. Its growth is predicted by the Brauer–Siegel theorem: roughly $h(D) \sim \sqrt{|D|}$ on average.

4. Genus theory

Gauss observed that the form class group could be further partitioned into genera: classes that are indistinguishable by congruence conditions. A genus character for a prime $p \mid D$ is a homomorphism $$ \chi_p : \mathrm{Cl}(D) \to \{\pm 1\}, \qquad \chi_p([f]) = \left(\frac{m}{p}\right) $$ where $m$ is any integer represented by $f$ with $\gcd(m,D)=1$. The value is well-defined on the class (independent of which $m$ we pick) by the theory of the Legendre symbol.

The collection of all genus characters combines into a single homomorphism $$ \Phi : \mathrm{Cl}(D) \longrightarrow (\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^{t-1}, $$ where $t$ is the number of prime discriminant divisors of $D$ (odd primes $p \mid D$ plus corrections for the factor of $4$ or $8$ at $2$). The image of $\Phi$ is all of $(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^{t-1}$, so the number of genera is exactly $2^{t-1}$, and each genus has the same number of classes.

The principal genus. $\ker\Phi$ is the principal genus: it equals the subgroup of squares $\mathrm{Cl}(D)^2 = \{[f]^2 : [f] \in \mathrm{Cl}(D)\}$. The quotient $\mathrm{Cl}(D)/\mathrm{Cl}(D)^2 \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^{t-1}$ is the group of genera.

Ambiguous classes

A class $[f]$ is ambiguous if $[f] = [f]^{-1}$, i.e., if $[f]$ has order $\le 2$ in $\mathrm{Cl}(D)$. The reduced representative $(a,b,c)$ of an ambiguous class satisfies $b=0$, $b=a$, or $a=c$. The number of ambiguous classes is $2^{t-1}$ — exactly the number of genera. This is no coincidence: every genus contains exactly one ambiguous class.

5. Forms and ideal classes

The deepest connection in this theory is the identification of the form class group with the ideal class group of an imaginary quadratic field. Fix a fundamental discriminant $D < 0$ and let $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{D})$. The ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$ is $\mathbb{Z}[\tfrac{1+\sqrt{D}}{2}]$ if $D \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, and $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$ otherwise.

The bijection. To a primitive form $(a,b,c)$ of discriminant $D$, associate the $\mathcal{O}_K$-ideal $$ \mathfrak{a} = a\mathbb{Z} + \frac{-b+\sqrt{D}}{2}\mathbb{Z}. $$ This construction sends proper equivalence classes of forms to ideal classes, and the Dirichlet composition corresponds exactly to ideal multiplication. The result is a canonical group isomorphism: $$ \mathrm{Cl}(D) \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} \mathrm{Cl}(K). $$

Under this isomorphism, the principal genus corresponds to the principal genus of ideals — ideals in the principal genus are exactly those whose ideal class lies in the kernel of all genus characters, which equals $\mathrm{Cl}(K)^2$, the subgroup of squares.

Primes represented by the principal form

An odd prime $p$ not dividing $D$ is represented by the principal form of discriminant $D$ if and only if the prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$ above $p$ in $\mathcal{O}_K$ is a principal ideal. By class field theory, this happens if and only if $p$ splits completely in the Hilbert class field $H/K$ — the maximal unramified abelian extension of $K$, which has degree $[H:K] = h(D)$. The primes represented by the principal form are exactly those in a specific set of congruence classes modulo $|D|$, determined by a product of Legendre symbols. This is the final link back to quadratic residues:

A prime $p$ (with $p \nmid D$) is represented by the principal form $\Leftrightarrow$ $\left(\frac{D}{p}\right) = 1$ (splits in $K$) and $p$'s Frobenius in $\mathrm{Gal}(H/K)$ is trivial — equivalently, $p$ splits completely in the Hilbert class field.

Class field theory in miniature

The isomorphism $\mathrm{Cl}(D) \cong \mathrm{Gal}(H/K)$ (Artin isomorphism restricted to $K$) makes the Hilbert class field $H$ the "universal" field where every ideal class becomes principal. Gauss's genus theory then says: there is an intermediate field $K \subset K^{\mathrm{gen}} \subset H$ such that $$ \mathrm{Gal}(K^{\mathrm{gen}}/K) \cong \mathrm{Cl}(D)/\mathrm{Cl}(D)^2 \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^{t-1}. $$ This field $K^{\mathrm{gen}}$ is the genus field, an explicitly constructible abelian extension of $K$. Gauss's genus theory is thus a concrete preview of the main theorem of class field theory, proved over a century later.

The full class field theory proof of $\mathrm{Cl}(D) \cong \mathrm{Cl}(K)$ uses the fact that every fractional ideal of $\mathcal{O}_K$ can be written in the form above, plus that two such ideals are in the same class iff the corresponding forms are properly equivalent. The key identity linking norms of ideals to values of forms is $N(\mathfrak{a}) = a$, where $a$ is the leading coefficient.

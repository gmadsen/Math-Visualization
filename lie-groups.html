Lie groups, Lie algebras, and the exponential map

Smooth manifolds that are also groups, and their tangent spaces at the identity. The exponential map turns infinitesimal data — a Lie algebra — into one-parameter subgroups, and the bracket records the failure of two flows to commute. We end with a glimpse of root systems, the combinatorics that classify the simple Lie algebras.

1. Lie groups: smooth manifolds with a compatible group law

A Lie group $G$ is a smooth manifold equipped with a group structure such that multiplication $\mu : G\times G \to G$ and inversion $\iota : G\to G$ are smooth maps. The geometry constrains the algebra: any smooth homomorphism between Lie groups is, up to its derivative at the identity, controlled by linear data. Conversely, the algebra constrains the geometry: most of what one wants to know about $G$ near $e$ is encoded in $T_eG$ and a single bilinear bracket on it.

The classical examples are matrix Lie groups — closed subgroups of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$ or $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$. A theorem of von Neumann and Cartan asserts that every closed subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_n$ is automatically a smooth submanifold, hence a Lie group. So our zoo is large.

Two facts to internalise: $R^\top R = I$ holds at every parameter setting (orthogonality is a closed condition), and $\det R = +1$ is automatic — flipping sign would take you out of $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ into the other component of $\mathrm{O}(3)$.

Local vs global. Lie groups can have intricate global topology — $\mathrm{SO}(3)\cong\mathbb{RP}^3$ has fundamental group $\mathbb{Z}/2$, $\mathrm{SU}(n)$ is simply connected, $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$ has two components. But locally near $e$, every Lie group looks like its Lie algebra, and the bracket determines the local group law completely (Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff).

Why these specific groups?

The classical groups are not an arbitrary collection. Each one is the isometry group of a particular bilinear form on a vector space:

  • $\mathrm{O}(n)$ preserves the standard inner product $\langle v,w\rangle = \sum v_iw_i$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$.
  • $\mathrm{U}(n)$ preserves the Hermitian form $\langle v,w\rangle = \sum \bar v_iw_i$ on $\mathbb{C}^n$.
  • $\mathrm{Sp}(2n,\mathbb{R})$ preserves a non-degenerate skew-symmetric form $\omega$ on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$.
  • $\mathrm{SL}_n$ preserves the top exterior form $e_1\wedge\cdots\wedge e_n$ (i.e., volume).

The $S$-prefixes isolate the identity component by imposing $\det = 1$. On the physics side, each group governs a different kind of symmetry: rotations ($\mathrm{SO}(3)$), phases ($\mathrm{U}(1)$), internal spin ($\mathrm{SU}(2)$), the Standard Model gauge group ($\mathrm{U}(1)\times\mathrm{SU}(2)\times\mathrm{SU}(3)$), and symplectic evolution in classical mechanics ($\mathrm{Sp}(2n)$).

2. The Lie algebra: tangent space at the identity

The Lie algebra of a Lie group $G$ is

$$\mathfrak{g} = T_e G,$$

the tangent space at the identity element. Equivalently, it is the space of left-invariant vector fields on $G$. The bracket $[\,,\,]$ on $\mathfrak{g}$ is the commutator of vector fields, restricted to $T_eG$; for matrix groups it is just $[X,Y] = XY-YX$.

To compute $\mathfrak{g}$ for a matrix group, differentiate the defining equation along a smooth curve $A(t)$ with $A(0) = I$, $A'(0) = X$:

For $\mathrm{O}(n)$: differentiate $A(t)^\top A(t) = I$ at $t=0$ to get $X^\top + X = 0$. So $\mathfrak{so}(n) = \mathfrak{o}(n)$ is the space of $n\times n$ skew-symmetric matrices, with the bracket $[X,Y] = XY - YX$ (one checks the result is again skew). Note that $\mathfrak{o}(n) = \mathfrak{so}(n)$ even though $\mathrm{O}(n)\ne\mathrm{SO}(n)$: the disconnected piece carries no extra infinitesimal information.

For $\mathrm{SU}(n)$: the determinant condition $\det A(t) = 1$ linearises (using $\det e^{tX} = e^{t\operatorname{tr}X}$) to $\operatorname{tr} X = 0$. So $\mathfrak{su}(n)$ consists of $n\times n$ traceless skew-Hermitian matrices, and $\dim_{\mathbb R}\mathfrak{su}(n) = n^2-1$. In particular $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ is 3-dimensional with basis

$$\frac{1}{2i}\sigma_1,\ \frac{1}{2i}\sigma_2,\ \frac{1}{2i}\sigma_3,$$

where $\sigma_k$ are the Pauli matrices.

Two definitions, one object. Abstractly, a Lie algebra is a vector space $\mathfrak{g}$ with a bilinear, antisymmetric bracket satisfying the Jacobi identity $[X,[Y,Z]] + [Y,[Z,X]] + [Z,[X,Y]] = 0$. Lie's three theorems say: every finite-dimensional real Lie algebra arises as $T_eG$ for some Lie group $G$, and the simply-connected $G$ realising a given $\mathfrak{g}$ is unique. So Lie algebras and simply-connected Lie groups are essentially the same data.

The bracket on $\mathfrak{so}(3)$

A skew-symmetric $3\times3$ matrix has the form

$$X = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -c & b \\ c & 0 & -a \\ -b & a & 0 \end{pmatrix},$$

and the map $X\leftrightarrow(a,b,c)$ identifies $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ with $\mathbb{R}^3$. Under this identification one computes $[X,Y]\leftrightarrow X\cdot \vec v\times \vec w$, i.e., the Lie bracket on $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ is the cross product on $\mathbb{R}^3$. This is the prototype for everything in §6 — the adjoint action of a Lie group on its Lie algebra.

Why the left-invariant picture

There is a choice to make: a tangent vector at $e$ extends uniquely to a left-invariant vector field by $X^L_g = (dL_g)_e(X)$, where $L_g : h\mapsto gh$ is left translation. This construction turns $\mathfrak{g}$ into a Lie algebra of vector fields on $G$ — all invariant under left translation — and the bracket of vector fields restricts correctly to the tangent space at $e$.

The slightly subtle sign convention: some texts use right-invariant fields, which produces the same $\mathfrak{g}$ as a vector space but flips the bracket ($[X,Y]_{\text{right}} = -[X,Y]_{\text{left}}$). For matrix groups the matrix commutator corresponds to the left-invariant convention. Either choice is fine as long as you are consistent.

Sub-algebras, ideals, quotients

Just as for groups, $\mathfrak{g}$ has a natural notion of sub-structure. A subalgebra is a vector subspace closed under $[\,,\,]$. An ideal $\mathfrak{i}\subset\mathfrak{g}$ satisfies $[\mathfrak{g},\mathfrak{i}]\subset\mathfrak{i}$; the quotient $\mathfrak{g}/\mathfrak{i}$ inherits a Lie bracket. Ideals correspond to normal Lie subgroups by Lie's correspondence, and simple Lie algebras (no nontrivial ideals, $\dim > 1$) correspond to simple Lie groups.

Two structural ideals deserve names. The centre $Z(\mathfrak{g}) = \{X : [X,Y] = 0\ \forall Y\}$ is the kernel of $\operatorname{ad}$. The derived algebra $[\mathfrak{g},\mathfrak{g}]$ is the ideal generated by all brackets. A Lie algebra is abelian iff $[\mathfrak{g},\mathfrak{g}] = 0$, perfect iff $[\mathfrak{g},\mathfrak{g}] = \mathfrak{g}$ (e.g., $\mathfrak{sl}_n$ for $n\ge 2$), and semisimple iff it has no nonzero abelian ideals.

3. The exponential map

The bridge from algebra back to group is the exponential map

$$\exp : \mathfrak{g} \to G.$$

For a matrix Lie group it is given by the absolutely-convergent power series

$$\exp(X) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{X^k}{k!} = I + X + \tfrac{1}{2}X^2 + \tfrac{1}{6}X^3 + \cdots.$$

Three structural facts make $\exp$ the right notion:

  • $\exp(0) = e$ and $d\exp_0 = \mathrm{id} : \mathfrak{g}\to\mathfrak{g}$, so $\exp$ is a local diffeomorphism near $0$.
  • For each $X\in\mathfrak{g}$, the curve $\gamma_X(t) = \exp(tX)$ is the unique one-parameter subgroup with $\gamma_X'(0) = X$. That is, $\gamma_X : \mathbb{R}\to G$ is a smooth homomorphism from the additive group $\mathbb{R}$.
  • If $G$ is connected and compact, $\exp$ is surjective — every group element is the exponential of some Lie algebra element. In general $\exp$ surjects onto the identity component, but only after taking products of exponentials.

The crucial caveat: $\exp(X+Y) \ne \exp(X)\exp(Y)$ unless $X$ and $Y$ commute. The defect is exactly the bracket, as we will see in §4.

$\exp$ on $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ via Rodrigues

For a skew-symmetric $X = \theta K$ with $K$ unit (i.e., $K = \hat n^\wedge$ for some unit axis $\hat n$ and $\theta = \|X\|$), the series telescopes because $K^3 = -K$. The result is Rodrigues's formula:

$$\exp(\theta K) = I + \sin\theta\,K + (1-\cos\theta)\,K^2.$$

One-parameter subgroup on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$

Let us repeat the construction for $\mathrm{SU}(2)$. The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ consists of $2\times2$ traceless skew-Hermitian matrices, with basis

$$T_k = -\tfrac{i}{2}\sigma_k, \qquad \sigma_1=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},\ \sigma_2=\begin{pmatrix}0&-i\\i&0\end{pmatrix},\ \sigma_3=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}.$$

An element is $X = -\tfrac{i}{2}(a\sigma_1 + b\sigma_2 + c\sigma_3)$, and a short computation shows $\exp(tX) = \cos(t\rho/2)\,I - i\sin(t\rho/2)\,\hat n\cdot\vec\sigma$, where $\rho = \sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}$ and $\hat n = (a,b,c)/\rho$. The image is a great circle on $S^3 = \mathrm{SU}(2)$.

Two things stand out. First, the trajectory is closed: $\exp(tX)$ comes back to $I$ at $t = 4\pi/\rho$ (not $2\pi/\rho$ — this is the "spinor sign" that distinguishes $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ from $\mathrm{SO}(3)$). 4. The bracket captures commutators

The bracket $[X,Y] = XY-YX$ on $\mathfrak{g}$ measures how badly $\exp(X)$ and $\exp(Y)$ fail to commute. To lowest order this failure is exactly second-order:

$$\exp(tX)\exp(tY)\exp(-tX)\exp(-tY) = \exp\!\big(t^2[X,Y] + O(t^3)\big).$$

Take logarithm and divide by $t^2$ on both sides; the limit as $t\to 0$ is $[X,Y]$. The full statement is the Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff formula

$$\log(\exp X \exp Y) = X + Y + \tfrac12[X,Y] + \tfrac{1}{12}\!\big([X,[X,Y]] + [Y,[Y,X]]\big) + \cdots,$$

an iterated bracket series. The remarkable point: it expresses the group product entirely in terms of the bracket. Up to convergence issues, the group is reconstructible from the algebra.

The bracket also satisfies the Jacobi identity:

$$[X,[Y,Z]] + [Y,[Z,X]] + [Z,[X,Y]] = 0.$$

For matrices this is a one-line check. Conceptually it says that $\operatorname{ad}_X = [X,\cdot] : \mathfrak{g}\to\mathfrak{g}$ is a derivation: $\operatorname{ad}_X[Y,Z] = [\operatorname{ad}_X Y, Z] + [Y,\operatorname{ad}_X Z]$.

The $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ basis $(e,f,h)$ satisfies the famous relations $[h,e] = 2e$, $[h,f] = -2f$, $[e,f] = h$ — the same relations that drive the rep-theory page's weight diagram. The $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ basis $(E_1, E_2, E_3)$ satisfies $[E_i, E_j] = \varepsilon_{ijk} E_k$, the cross product on $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Why second order? First order: $\exp(tX)\exp(tY) = I + t(X+Y) + O(t^2)$, which is symmetric in $X,Y$ — no commutator yet. The four-fold product is constructed precisely to kill the symmetric part: $X+Y-X-Y = 0$, so the leading nonzero contribution is the antisymmetric piece $t^2(XY-YX)$.

5. SO(3), SU(2), and the double cover

The same Lie algebra can underlie different Lie groups. A clean example: $\mathfrak{su}(2)\cong\mathfrak{so}(3)$ as real Lie algebras (both are 3-dimensional with bracket given by the cross product), but the global groups differ:

grouptopology$\pi_1$$\pi_3$
$\mathrm{SU}(2)$$S^3$ (3-sphere)$0$$\mathbb{Z}$
$\mathrm{SO}(3)$$\mathbb{RP}^3$$\mathbb{Z}/2$$\mathbb{Z}$

$\mathrm{SU}(2)$ is the universal cover of $\mathrm{SO}(3)$. There is a smooth surjective homomorphism

$$\pi : \mathrm{SU}(2) \twoheadrightarrow \mathrm{SO}(3), \qquad \ker\pi = \{\pm I\},$$

making $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ a 2:1 cover. Concretely, identify $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ with unit quaternions $q = a + b\mathbf{i} + c\mathbf{j} + d\mathbf{k}$ (with $a^2+b^2+c^2+d^2=1$), and let $q$ act on $\vec v\in\mathbb{R}^3 = \operatorname{Im}\mathbb{H}$ by conjugation $\vec v\mapsto q\vec v q^{-1}$. This is an orthogonal transformation of determinant $+1$, so an element of $\mathrm{SO}(3)$. The map $q\mapsto (\vec v\mapsto q\vec v q^{-1})$ is the double cover, and its kernel is exactly $\pm 1$.

In matrix terms, given $q = (a,b,c,d)$, the corresponding rotation is

$$R(q) = \begin{pmatrix} a^2+b^2-c^2-d^2 & 2(bc-ad) & 2(bd+ac) \\ 2(bc+ad) & a^2-b^2+c^2-d^2 & 2(cd-ab) \\ 2(bd-ac) & 2(cd+ab) & a^2-b^2-c^2+d^2 \end{pmatrix}.$$
SU(2) → SO(3) double cover$q\mapsto R(q) = R(-q)$

$R(q)\in\mathrm{SO}(3)$

Click "flip $q\to -q$" and watch the matrix not change a single digit. That is the kernel of the double cover laid bare. Geometrically: a $360°$ rotation in $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ is the identity, but in $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ it is $-I$; you have to go around twice to come back to $+I$. This is the spinor sign, and it is the reason fermions are "real" — they live in a representation of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ that does not descend to $\mathrm{SO}(3)$.

Topological summary. $\mathrm{SU}(2) = S^3$ is simply connected. The quotient by $\pm I$ identifies antipodes, giving $\mathbb{RP}^3 = \mathrm{SO}(3)$, which has $\pi_1 = \mathbb{Z}/2$. The nontrivial loop in $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ — a continuous 360° rotation about a fixed axis — lifts to a path from $I$ to $-I$ in $\mathrm{SU}(2)$. Doing it twice (720°) lifts to a closed loop, which is contractible. This is the celebrated "belt trick" or "Dirac plate trick".

6. The adjoint representation

A Lie group acts on its own Lie algebra by conjugation. For each $g\in G$, define

$$\mathrm{Ad}_g : \mathfrak{g}\to\mathfrak{g}, \qquad \mathrm{Ad}_g(X) = g X g^{-1}$$

(for matrix groups; in general it is the differential of conjugation $h\mapsto ghg^{-1}$ at $e$). This gives a homomorphism $\mathrm{Ad} : G\to\mathrm{GL}(\mathfrak{g})$, the adjoint representation of the group.

Differentiate at the identity: $\mathrm{Ad}_{\exp(tX)}(Y) = e^{tX} Y e^{-tX}$, and

$$\frac{d}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0} e^{tX} Y e^{-tX} = XY - YX = [X,Y].$$

So the differential $d\mathrm{Ad}_e : \mathfrak{g}\to\mathrm{End}(\mathfrak{g})$ is

$$\operatorname{ad}_X(Y) = [X,Y],$$

the adjoint representation of the Lie algebra. Jacobi's identity is the statement that $\operatorname{ad}_{[X,Y]} = [\operatorname{ad}_X, \operatorname{ad}_Y]$, i.e., $\operatorname{ad}$ itself is a Lie algebra homomorphism $\mathfrak{g}\to\mathfrak{gl}(\mathfrak{g})$.

The Killing form

From $\operatorname{ad}$ one builds a canonical symmetric bilinear form on $\mathfrak{g}$, the Killing form:

$$B(X,Y) = \operatorname{tr}\!\big(\operatorname{ad}_X\operatorname{ad}_Y\big).$$

For $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ the Killing form is negative definite (all eigenvalues of $\operatorname{ad}_X$ are imaginary, square gives negatives); for $\mathfrak{sl}_2(\mathbb{R})$ it is indefinite of signature $(2,1)$. The sign tells you whether the corresponding compact group is compact ($B<0$) or not.

Cartan's criterion. A Lie algebra is semisimple iff its Killing form is non-degenerate. This single algebraic condition classifies an enormous class of Lie algebras and is the entry point to the root system theory of §7.

Adjoint orbits. The orbits of $G$ on $\mathfrak{g}$ under $\mathrm{Ad}$ are remarkable submanifolds: for $G = \mathrm{SU}(n)$ they are flag manifolds carrying $G$-invariant symplectic structures (Kirillov–Kostant–Souriau), and they parametrise the irreducible unitary representations of $G$ in a precise way (orbit method). For $G = \mathrm{SO}(3)$ the orbits are concentric spheres in $\mathbb{R}^3 = \mathfrak{so}(3)$ — radius $r$ corresponds to the spin-$r$ phase space.

7. Root systems preview: $A_1$ and $A_2$

For a complex semisimple Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}_\mathbb{C}$, the structural classification rests on a beautiful piece of combinatorics: the root system. Pick a maximal abelian subalgebra $\mathfrak{h}\subset\mathfrak{g}_\mathbb{C}$ on which all $\operatorname{ad}_H$ ($H\in\mathfrak{h}$) are simultaneously diagonalisable — a Cartan subalgebra. The simultaneous eigenspace decomposition is

$$\mathfrak{g}_\mathbb{C} = \mathfrak{h} \oplus \bigoplus_{\alpha\in\Phi} \mathfrak{g}_\alpha,$$

where $\Phi\subset\mathfrak{h}^*\setminus\{0\}$ is the finite set of roots: nonzero linear functionals $\alpha:\mathfrak{h}\to\mathbb{C}$ such that $[H,X] = \alpha(H)X$ for all $H\in\mathfrak{h}$ and $X\in\mathfrak{g}_\alpha$.

Type $A_1$: the algebra $\mathfrak{sl}_2$

For $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{sl}_2(\mathbb{C})$ with basis $h,e,f$ as in the rep-theory page, take $\mathfrak{h} = \mathbb{C}\cdot h$. One computes

$$[h,e] = 2e, \qquad [h,f] = -2f, \qquad [h,h]=0.$$

So $\operatorname{ad}_h$ acts diagonally with eigenvalues $0, +2, -2$. The roots are $\alpha:h\mapsto 2$ and $-\alpha:h\mapsto -2$. As a picture in $\mathfrak{h}^*\cong\mathbb{R}$:

Type $A_2$: the algebra $\mathfrak{sl}_3$

For $\mathfrak{sl}_3(\mathbb{C})$, $\mathfrak{h}$ is the 2-dimensional space of traceless diagonal matrices $\operatorname{diag}(h_1,h_2,h_3)$ with $\sum h_i = 0$. Define $\varepsilon_i\in\mathfrak{h}^*$ by $\varepsilon_i(H) = h_i$. The roots are the six functionals $\varepsilon_i - \varepsilon_j$ ($i\ne j$): they form a regular hexagon in the plane $\{\sum h_i = 0\}\cong\mathbb{R}^2$.

Root systems $A_1$ and $A_2$click a root to apply the Weyl reflection through its perpendicular hyperplane

The $A_1$ picture is the same as the rep-theory page's weight diagram for $\mathfrak{sl}_2$: roots are weights of $\operatorname{ad}$, and the "weight 2" arrow on the right is the eigenvalue of $h$ on $e$. The $A_2$ hexagon is the prototype for everything in semisimple theory: pick two adjacent roots as simple roots, the reflections through their perpendicular hyperplanes generate the Weyl group (here $S_3$), and the integers $\langle\alpha,\beta^\vee\rangle\in\mathbb{Z}$ assemble into the Cartan matrix.

8. Connections

Lie groups sit at the centre of the notebook, and what we have built here connects outward in several directions.

  • Representation theory. The rep-theory page's analysis of $\mathfrak{sl}_2(\mathbb{C})$-modules — highest weight vectors, weight-string structure, the irreducible $V_n$ of dimension $n+1$ — is the local model for representation theory of every semisimple Lie algebra. The roots we drew in §7 are the weights of the adjoint representation, and a general highest-weight $V_\lambda$ is built by raising and lowering between weight spaces just as we did for $V_n$.
  • Differential geometry. The one-parameter subgroups $t\mapsto\exp(tX)$ are exactly the geodesics through $e$ for any left-invariant Riemannian metric on $G$ (provided one uses the right connection — the Levi-Civita connection of a bi-invariant metric, which exists when $G$ is compact). So the exponential map of §3 is the Riemannian exponential of differential geometry. The differential-geometry page builds this picture from the manifold side.
  • Differential forms. On any Lie group, the Maurer–Cartan form $\omega = g^{-1}dg$ is a $\mathfrak{g}$-valued 1-form satisfying the structural equation $d\omega + \tfrac12[\omega,\omega] = 0$. The bracket appears as the curvature of trivial connection — and this equation is the prototype for connection 1-forms in gauge theory. Lie algebra cohomology, on the algebra side, mirrors de Rham cohomology of the group.
  • Topology. $\pi_3(G) = \mathbb{Z}$ for every compact simple Lie group $G$ — generated, in $\mathrm{SU}(2) = S^3$, by the identity map. The integer is the instanton number in physics, and the same integer measures Chern–Simons levels in 3-manifold invariants.
  • Algebraic geometry. Algebraic groups (e.g., $\mathrm{GL}_n$ as a group scheme over $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$) bring Lie-theoretic ideas into the world of schemes. Their representations classify principal bundles, and the Borel–Weil theorem realises every irreducible $G$-module as global sections of a line bundle on the flag variety $G/B$.

From here it is a short hop to almost everything else: physics (Yang–Mills, the Standard Model, Wigner classification of particles), geometry (symmetric spaces, locally homogeneous structures), number theory (automorphic forms on $G(\mathbb{A}_F)$), and combinatorics (Schubert calculus, Kazhdan–Lusztig polynomials). The bracket on a 3-dimensional vector space — a one-line definition — really is the seed of an enormous subject.

Suggested next stops in the notebook: representation theory for the highest-weight machinery on $\mathfrak{sl}_2$; differential geometry for connections, geodesics, and curvature; algebraic topology for fundamental groups and covering spaces.

Abstract algebra

Groups, rings, fields — the three ways a set can carry "arithmetic," with one interactive widget per section to build intuition.

1. One, two, three operations

The structures of algebra are what you get when you demand that a set comes equipped with one or two operations that behave like familiar arithmetic. You relax different axioms to get different structures:

group one associative operation, an identity, and inverses. Models symmetry: the invertible transformations of anything.

ring two operations — addition (abelian group) and multiplication (associative, distributive). Models arithmetic like $\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, matrices.

field a ring where multiplication is also a group on nonzero elements. Models numbers — places you can solve linear equations. $\mathbb{Q},\mathbb{R},\mathbb{C}$, and, crucially, the finite fields $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$.

Everything below is a tour through examples where you can actually poke the axioms.

2. Groups via Cayley tables

The cleanest way to see a finite group is its multiplication table. Rows indexed by $g$, columns by $h$, cell $(g,h)$ contains $g\cdot h$. Closure means every cell is an element of $G$; the identity gives a row and column that are exact copies of the header; each row/column is a permutation of $G$ (this is how you cheat to read off Cayley's theorem).

4. The ring ℤ/nℤ

Reducing integers mod $n$ makes $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ a commutative ring of size $n$. Its structure hinges on a single number-theoretic fact: $a\in(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ iff $\gcd(a,n)=1$.

When $n=p$ is prime, every nonzero element is a unit and $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}=\mathbb{F}_p$ is a field. When $n$ is composite, the nonzero non-units are zero divisors: there's some $b\ne0$ with $ab\equiv 0\pmod n$.

5. Polynomial rings & the Euclidean algorithm

For a field $k$, the ring $k[x]$ is a Euclidean domain with "size" = degree. So the same Euclidean algorithm that computes $\gcd$ in $\mathbb{Z}$ computes $\gcd$ in $k[x]$ — and with it, Bézout coefficients, irreducibility tests via divisibility, and field extensions like $\mathbb{Q}(\alpha)=\mathbb{Q}[x]/(m_\alpha)$.

Frobenius orbits partition $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ into Galois-conjugate classes; each orbit of size $d\mid n$ is the roots of some irreducible $m_d\in\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ of degree $d$. 7. Ideals and quotient rings

For commutative rings, the analogue of a normal subgroup is an ideal: a subset $I\subseteq R$ closed under addition and under multiplication by any ring element, i.e. $rI\subseteq I$ for every $r\in R$. Ideals are exactly the kernels of ring homomorphisms, and the quotient ring $R/I$ is well-defined with the usual coset arithmetic $(r+I)+(s+I)=r+s+I,\ (r+I)(s+I)=rs+I$.

Two sub-species matter most. A prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$ satisfies $ab\in\mathfrak{p}\Rightarrow a\in\mathfrak{p}$ or $b\in\mathfrak{p}$, equivalently $R/\mathfrak{p}$ is an integral domain. A maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$ is a proper ideal with no proper ideal strictly containing it, equivalently $R/\mathfrak{m}$ is a field. Concretely in $\mathbb{Z}$ the prime ideals are $(0)$ and $(p)$ for $p$ prime, and the maximal ideals are exactly $(p)$; this is why $\mathbb{Z}/(p)=\mathbb{F}_p$ is a field while $\mathbb{Z}/(6)$ is not even a domain (since $2\cdot 3\equiv 0$).

The Third Isomorphism Theorem for rings, $R/I \big/ (J/I)\cong R/J$ whenever $I\subseteq J$, shows that passing to a quotient just collapses ideals above $I$ onto the corresponding ideals of $R/I$. This is the algebraic counterpart of restricting to a closed subscheme in algebraic geometry.

8. Field extensions and minimal polynomials

A field extension $F\subseteq K$ turns $K$ into an $F$-vector space; its dimension $[K:F]$ is the degree of the extension. An element $\alpha\in K$ is algebraic over $F$ if $p(\alpha)=0$ for some nonzero $p\in F[x]$, and transcendental otherwise. For algebraic $\alpha$, the set of polynomials killing $\alpha$ is an ideal of $F[x]$ — and since $F[x]$ is a principal ideal domain, that ideal is $(m_\alpha)$ for a unique monic irreducible $m_\alpha$, the minimal polynomial.

Adjoining $\alpha$ gives $F(\alpha)\cong F[x]/(m_\alpha)$ by the evaluation map $x\mapsto \alpha$ and the First Isomorphism Theorem: the kernel is exactly $(m_\alpha)$, so the image — a subring of $K$ — is isomorphic to the quotient, which is a field because $(m_\alpha)$ is maximal whenever $m_\alpha$ is irreducible. Its dimension over $F$ is $\deg m_\alpha$, so $[F(\alpha):F]=\deg m_\alpha$. The Tower Law $[L:F]=[L:K]\cdot[K:F]$ then multiplies degrees through a chain $F\subseteq K\subseteq L$, e.g. $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3}):\mathbb{Q}]=2\cdot 2=4$.

A polynomial $f\in F[x]$ of degree $n$ splits in an extension $K/F$ if $f=c\prod_{i=1}^n(x-\alpha_i)$ with each $\alpha_i\in K$; the smallest such $K$ is the splitting field of $f$, always algebraic and of finite degree $\le n!$. Splitting fields are the springboard for Galois theory, where automorphisms of $K$ fixing $F$ permute the roots of $f$.

9. Subgroups, cosets, and Lagrange

Pick a subgroup $H\leq G$. Left multiplication by $g\in G$ is a bijection $H\to gH$, so every left coset has size exactly $|H|$. Distinct cosets are disjoint and together they cover $G$, so the coset partition chops $G$ into blocks of equal size. The number of blocks is the index $[G:H]=|G|/|H|$, and this immediately gives Lagrange's theorem: $|H|$ divides $|G|$.

Right cosets $Hg$ give a parallel partition. In general left- and right-coset partitions disagree. The subgroup $H$ is normal precisely when they agree — equivalently, $gHg^{-1}=H$ for all $g$, or $H$ is the kernel of some homomorphism out of $G$. That is exactly the condition that $G/H$ inherits a group structure in §10.

10. Quotient groups and isomorphism theorems

When $N\trianglelefteq G$, the set of cosets $G/N$ carries a well-defined multiplication $(aN)(bN)=(ab)N$ — well-defined precisely because $N$ is normal, so the choice of representatives doesn't matter. The map $\pi:G\to G/N$, $g\mapsto gN$, is a surjective homomorphism with kernel $N$, and every surjection of $G$ arises this way.

The three isomorphism theorems then organize homomorphism arithmetic:

I $G/\ker\varphi\cong\mathrm{im}\,\varphi$ for any homomorphism $\varphi:G\to H$.

II For $H\leq G$ and $N\trianglelefteq G$: $HN/N\cong H/(H\cap N)$.

III For $N\trianglelefteq M\trianglelefteq G$ with $N\trianglelefteq G$: $(G/N)/(M/N)\cong G/M$.

11. Group actions, orbits, and Burnside

A (left) action of $G$ on a set $X$ is a map $G\times X\to X$ satisfying $e\cdot x=x$ and $(gh)\cdot x=g\cdot(h\cdot x)$. The action partitions $X$ into orbits $G\cdot x=\{g\cdot x: g\in G\}$. For each $x$, its stabilizer $\mathrm{Stab}(x)=\{g: g\cdot x=x\}$ is a subgroup of $G$, and the orbit-stabilizer theorem gives

$|G\cdot x|=[G:\mathrm{Stab}(x)]=|G|/|\mathrm{Stab}(x)|$.

The Burnside/Cauchy–Frobenius lemma counts orbits as an average:

$\#\{\text{orbits}\}=\dfrac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g\in G}|\mathrm{Fix}(g)|$.

12. Sylow theorems

Write $|G|=p^k m$ with $\gcd(p,m)=1$. A Sylow $p$-subgroup is a subgroup of order exactly $p^k$. The three Sylow theorems say:

I Sylow $p$-subgroups exist for every prime $p\mid |G|$.

II Any two Sylow $p$-subgroups are conjugate; in particular any $p$-subgroup of $G$ is contained in some Sylow $p$-subgroup.

III The number $n_p$ of Sylow $p$-subgroups divides $m$ and satisfies $n_p\equiv 1\pmod p$. When $n_p=1$, the unique Sylow $p$-subgroup is normal.

The $n_p=1$ case is the main lever: it forces existence of a normal subgroup and often pins down $G$ up to isomorphism (semidirect product with the other Sylow subgroup).

13. Finite abelian groups

The fundamental theorem of finite abelian groups says every finite abelian group $A$ is (uniquely up to reordering) a direct sum of cyclic groups of prime-power order:

$A\cong\bigoplus_{i}\mathbb{Z}/p_i^{a_i}\mathbb{Z}.$

Equivalently, $A\cong\mathbb{Z}/d_1\oplus\cdots\oplus\mathbb{Z}/d_r$ with $d_1\mid d_2\mid\cdots\mid d_r$ (invariant factors). To count abelian groups of order $n=\prod p^{v_p(n)}$, fix each prime $p\mid n$ independently: the $p$-primary part is a direct sum of cyclic groups whose exponents partition $v_p(n)$. So the count is $\prod_{p\mid n} P(v_p(n))$, where $P(k)$ is the number of integer partitions of $k$.

14. Polynomial rings and irreducibility

Over a field $k$, the ring $k[x]$ is a Euclidean domain: its irreducibles are the prime elements, and every polynomial factors uniquely (up to units) into irreducibles. Over $\mathbb{Z}$, Gauss's lemma says a primitive polynomial in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Z}$ iff it is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$.

Two practical tests for a polynomial $f(x)=a_n x^n+\cdots+a_0\in\mathbb{Z}[x]$:

rational-root every rational root is $\pm p/q$ with $p\mid a_0$ and $q\mid a_n$; testing the finite list either finds a root (reducible) or rules out degree-$1$ factors.

15. Semidirect products and short exact sequences

A short exact sequence of groups is a chain $1\to N\xrightarrow{\iota} G\xrightarrow{\pi} H\to 1$ with $\iota$ injective, $\pi$ surjective, and $\mathrm{im}\,\iota=\ker\pi$. So $N\trianglelefteq G$ and $G/N\cong H$. The sequence splits when $\pi$ admits a homomorphism section $s:H\to G$; equivalently $G$ is isomorphic to a semidirect product $N\rtimes_\varphi H$, where $\varphi:H\to\mathrm{Aut}(N)$, $\varphi(h)(n)=s(h)\,n\,s(h)^{-1}$.

Multiplication in $N\rtimes_\varphi H$ is $(n_1,h_1)(n_2,h_2)=(n_1\varphi_{h_1}(n_2),\,h_1 h_2)$. When $\varphi$ is the trivial homomorphism, this collapses to the direct product $N\times H$. For a nontrivial twist, the resulting $G$ is non-abelian (assuming $N$ is).

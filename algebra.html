Abstract algebra

Groups, rings, fields — the three ways a set can carry "arithmetic," with one interactive widget per section to build intuition.

1. One, two, three operations

The structures of algebra are what you get when you demand that a set comes equipped with one or two operations that behave like familiar arithmetic. You relax different axioms to get different structures:

group one associative operation, an identity, and inverses. Models symmetry: the invertible transformations of anything.

ring two operations — addition (abelian group) and multiplication (associative, distributive). Models arithmetic like $\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, matrices.

field a ring where multiplication is also a group on nonzero elements. Models numbers — places you can solve linear equations. $\mathbb{Q},\mathbb{R},\mathbb{C}$, and, crucially, the finite fields $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$.

Everything below is a tour through examples where you can actually poke the axioms.

2. Groups via Cayley tables

The cleanest way to see a finite group is its multiplication table. Rows indexed by $g$, columns by $h$, cell $(g,h)$ contains $g\cdot h$. Closure means every cell is an element of $G$; the identity gives a row and column that are exact copies of the header; each row/column is a permutation of $G$ (this is how you cheat to read off Cayley's theorem).

Normal subgroup detector. A subgroup $H\leq G$ is normal iff left cosets equal right cosets, i.e. $gH=Hg$ for all $g$. Visually: the coset coloring is the same whether you look at the rows of $H$ or the columns of $H$. When $H\trianglelefteq G$ you can form the quotient $G/H$.

4. The ring ℤ/nℤ

Reducing integers mod $n$ makes $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ a commutative ring of size $n$. Its structure hinges on a single number-theoretic fact: $a\in(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ iff $\gcd(a,n)=1$.

When $n=p$ is prime, every nonzero element is a unit and $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}=\mathbb{F}_p$ is a field. When $n$ is composite, the nonzero non-units are zero divisors: there's some $b\ne0$ with $ab\equiv 0\pmod n$.

5. Polynomial rings & the Euclidean algorithm

For a field $k$, the ring $k[x]$ is a Euclidean domain with "size" = degree. So the same Euclidean algorithm that computes $\gcd$ in $\mathbb{Z}$ computes $\gcd$ in $k[x]$ — and with it, Bézout coefficients, irreducibility tests via divisibility, and field extensions like $\mathbb{Q}(\alpha)=\mathbb{Q}[x]/(m_\alpha)$.

Frobenius orbits partition $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ into Galois-conjugate classes; each orbit of size $d\mid n$ is the roots of some irreducible $m_d\in\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ of degree $d$. Summing orbit sizes gives the classical identity $p^n = \sum_{d\mid n} d\cdot N_d$ where $N_d$ is the number of monic irreducibles of degree $d$ in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$.

7. Ideals and quotient rings

For commutative rings, the analogue of a normal subgroup is an ideal: a subset $I\subseteq R$ closed under addition and under multiplication by any ring element, i.e. $rI\subseteq I$ for every $r\in R$. Ideals are exactly the kernels of ring homomorphisms, and the quotient ring $R/I$ is well-defined with the usual coset arithmetic $(r+I)+(s+I)=r+s+I,\ (r+I)(s+I)=rs+I$.

Two sub-species matter most. A prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$ satisfies $ab\in\mathfrak{p}\Rightarrow a\in\mathfrak{p}$ or $b\in\mathfrak{p}$, equivalently $R/\mathfrak{p}$ is an integral domain. A maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$ is a proper ideal with no proper ideal strictly containing it, equivalently $R/\mathfrak{m}$ is a field. Concretely in $\mathbb{Z}$ the prime ideals are $(0)$ and $(p)$ for $p$ prime, and the maximal ideals are exactly $(p)$; this is why $\mathbb{Z}/(p)=\mathbb{F}_p$ is a field while $\mathbb{Z}/(6)$ is not even a domain (since $2\cdot 3\equiv 0$).

The Third Isomorphism Theorem for rings, $R/I \big/ (J/I)\cong R/J$ whenever $I\subseteq J$, shows that passing to a quotient just collapses ideals above $I$ onto the corresponding ideals of $R/I$. This is the algebraic counterpart of restricting to a closed subscheme in algebraic geometry.

8. Field extensions and minimal polynomials

A field extension $F\subseteq K$ turns $K$ into an $F$-vector space; its dimension $[K:F]$ is the degree of the extension. An element $\alpha\in K$ is algebraic over $F$ if $p(\alpha)=0$ for some nonzero $p\in F[x]$, and transcendental otherwise. For algebraic $\alpha$, the set of polynomials killing $\alpha$ is an ideal of $F[x]$ — and since $F[x]$ is a principal ideal domain, that ideal is $(m_\alpha)$ for a unique monic irreducible $m_\alpha$, the minimal polynomial.

Adjoining $\alpha$ gives $F(\alpha)\cong F[x]/(m_\alpha)$ by the evaluation map $x\mapsto \alpha$ and the First Isomorphism Theorem: the kernel is exactly $(m_\alpha)$, so the image — a subring of $K$ — is isomorphic to the quotient, which is a field because $(m_\alpha)$ is maximal whenever $m_\alpha$ is irreducible. Its dimension over $F$ is $\deg m_\alpha$, so $[F(\alpha):F]=\deg m_\alpha$. The Tower Law $[L:F]=[L:K]\cdot[K:F]$ then multiplies degrees through a chain $F\subseteq K\subseteq L$, e.g. $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3}):\mathbb{Q}]=2\cdot 2=4$.

A polynomial $f\in F[x]$ of degree $n$ splits in an extension $K/F$ if $f=c\prod_{i=1}^n(x-\alpha_i)$ with each $\alpha_i\in K$; the smallest such $K$ is the splitting field of $f$, always algebraic and of finite degree $\le n!$. Splitting fields are the springboard for Galois theory, where automorphisms of $K$ fixing $F$ permute the roots of $f$.

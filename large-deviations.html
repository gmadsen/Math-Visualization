Large deviations

The law of large numbers says $\bar X_n\to\mu$. Large-deviation theory asks how unlikely the rare excursion $\bar X_n\approx x\ne\mu$ actually is — and answers $\mathbb{P}(\bar X_n\approx x)\asymp e^{-nI(x)}$, where the rate function $I$ is the Legendre transform of the log-MGF. Cramér, Sanov, Gärtner–Ellis, Schilder, and Freidlin–Wentzell are five faces of one principle.

1. Cramér's theorem

Let $X_1,X_2,\ldots$ be i.i.d.\ real random variables with finite log-MGF $$\Lambda(\theta)=\log\mathbb{E}[e^{\theta X_1}]<\infty\quad\text{for }\theta\text{ in a neighbourhood of }0,$$ and write $\bar X_n=\tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ and $\mu=\mathbb{E}[X_1]$. The strong law gives $\bar X_n\to\mu$ a.s.; Cramér's theorem quantifies the rare event $\{\bar X_n\ge x\}$ for $x>\mu$: $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\log\mathbb{P}(\bar X_n\ge x)=-I(x),\qquad I(x)=\sup_{\theta\in\mathbb{R}}\bigl\{\theta x-\Lambda(\theta)\bigr\}.$$ The right-hand side is the Legendre–Fenchel transform of $\Lambda$. Tail probabilities decay exponentially in $n$ at rate $I(x)$.

Two-sided: the same identity holds for $\mathbb{P}(\bar X_n\le x)$ when $x<\mu$, and the joint statement $\mathbb{P}(\bar X_n\in F)\lesssim e^{-n\inf_F I}$ (closed $F$), $\mathbb{P}(\bar X_n\in G)\gtrsim e^{-n\inf_G I}$ (open $G$) is the large-deviation principle (LDP) — the canonical packaging.

Why a Legendre transform? The exponential tilt $\mathbb{P}_\theta(dx)=e^{\theta x-\Lambda(\theta)}\mathbb{P}(dx)$ makes $x$ the typical mean: choose $\theta=\theta^*$ so $\Lambda'(\theta^*)=x$. Under $\mathbb{P}_\theta$ the event becomes typical and a Radon–Nikodym change of measure costs $e^{-n(\theta x-\Lambda(\theta))}=e^{-nI(x)}$. The Legendre sup is "pick the cheapest tilt that makes $x$ typical."

Worked Gaussian example. If $X_i\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, then $\Lambda(\theta)=\theta^2/2$. Maximising $\theta x-\theta^2/2$ gives $\theta^*=x$ and $I(x)=x^2/2$. So $\mathbb{P}(\bar X_n\ge x)\sim e^{-nx^2/2}$ — exactly the Gaussian tail bound, but now the constant is sharp on the log scale.

Bernoulli example. If $X_i\sim\text{Bernoulli}(p)$, $\Lambda(\theta)=\log(1-p+pe^\theta)$ and $$I(x)=x\log\tfrac{x}{p}+(1-x)\log\tfrac{1-x}{1-p}=H(\text{Ber}(x)\,\|\,\text{Ber}(p))$$ on $[0,1]$ — exactly the Kullback–Leibler divergence. Coin tossing is the simplest non-Gaussian instance and already exposes the link to information theory.

2. Rate functions and convex duality

Cramér gives a recipe; the rate function $I$ has structural properties worth isolating. A (good) rate function on a Polish space $\mathcal{X}$ is $I:\mathcal{X}\to[0,\infty]$ with:

  • Lower semicontinuity: level sets $\{x:I(x)\le\alpha\}$ are closed.
  • Compactness of level sets (the good qualifier): closed and bounded in $\mathcal{X}$.
  • Vanishing at the LLN limit: $I(\mu)=0$, since $\theta=0$ achieves the sup when $\Lambda'(0)=\mu$.

When $I=\Lambda^*$ for a convex $\Lambda$, the Legendre involution $(\Lambda^*)^*=\Lambda$ holds wherever $\Lambda$ is convex and lower semicontinuous. Geometrically, $\Lambda$ and $I$ are dual: each is the supporting-hyperplane envelope of the other.

Quadratic example. Gaussian: $\Lambda(\theta)=\sigma^2\theta^2/2+\mu\theta\Rightarrow I(x)=(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)$. The rate function is the squared Mahalanobis distance — Gaussian tails are exactly quadratic.

Bernoulli/binomial. $I(x)=H(\text{Ber}(x)\,\|\,\text{Ber}(p))$ on $[0,1]$, $+\infty$ off $[0,1]$. The infinite values force support: the empirical mean cannot exceed $1$, no matter how rare we are willing to consider.

Exponential. If $X_i\sim\text{Exp}(1)$ (mean $1$), $\Lambda(\theta)=-\log(1-\theta)$ for $\theta<1$ and $I(x)=x-1-\log x$ on $(0,\infty)$ — the Itakura–Saito divergence from $1$. Note $I(x)$ is asymmetric: large $x$ is more punishing than small $x$.

Reading off probabilities. If $I$ is strictly convex with minimum at $\mu$, a quadratic Taylor expansion $I(x)\approx\tfrac{1}{2}I''(\mu)(x-\mu)^2$ recovers the central limit theorem: $\sqrt n(\bar X_n-\mu)\Rightarrow\mathcal{N}(0,1/I''(\mu))$. The CLT is the LDP linearised at $\mu$, and $I''(\mu)=1/\sigma^2$.

3. Sanov's theorem and KL divergence

Cramér tracks the empirical mean $\bar X_n$. Sanov's theorem tracks the full empirical distribution $$L_n=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\delta_{X_i}\in\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{X})$$ of i.i.d.\ samples from $\mu$. It is the LDP on the space of probability measures: for measurable $A\subset\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{X})$, $$\mathbb{P}(L_n\in A)\asymp\exp\Bigl(-n\inf_{\nu\in A}H(\nu\,\|\,\mu)\Bigr),$$ where $H(\nu\,\|\,\mu)=\int\log(d\nu/d\mu)\,d\nu$ (= $+\infty$ if $\nu\not\ll\mu$) is the Kullback–Leibler divergence.

This is the most basic instance of the slogan "entropy is the rate function of i.i.d.\ statistics." On a finite alphabet $\mathcal{X}=\{a_1,\ldots,a_k\}$, $L_n$ is just the histogram, and $$H(\nu\,\|\,\mu)=\sum_{i=1}^k\nu_i\log\frac{\nu_i}{\mu_i}.$$ By Pinsker, $H\ge 2\|\nu-\mu\|_{TV}^2$, so KL controls every weaker metric.

Cramér from Sanov via contraction. The empirical mean is a continuous functional of $L_n$: $\bar X_n=\int x\,dL_n(x)$. The contraction principle says: if $L_n$ satisfies an LDP with rate $H(\,\cdot\,\|\,\mu)$ and $f$ is continuous, then $f(L_n)$ has rate $I(y)=\inf\{H(\nu\,\|\,\mu):\int x\,d\nu(x)=y\}$. Solving the constrained minimisation by Lagrange multipliers reproduces $I(y)=\sup_\theta\{\theta y-\Lambda(\theta)\}$.

Worked example: biased coin. $\mu=\text{Ber}(1/2)$, target $\nu=\text{Ber}(p)$. Then $H(\nu\,\|\,\mu)=p\log(2p)+(1-p)\log(2(1-p))$. The probability that $n$ fair coin flips produce a fraction-of-heads close to $p=0.9$ is approximately $e^{-n\cdot 0.368}$. For $n=100$ this is $\approx e^{-36.8}\approx 10^{-16}$ — astronomically rare, but the rate is sharp.

4. Gärtner–Ellis: beyond i.i.d.

Cramér assumes i.i.d.\ summands, which is a strong restriction. The Gärtner–Ellis theorem replaces "i.i.d.\ log-MGF" with a limiting log-MGF at scale $n$: $$\Lambda(\theta)\ :=\ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\log\mathbb{E}\bigl[e^{n\theta\bar X_n}\bigr].$$ If this limit exists, is finite on a neighbourhood of $0$, and is essentially smooth (differentiable in the interior of its effective domain with $|\Lambda'|\to\infty$ at the boundary), then $\bar X_n$ satisfies an LDP with rate $I=\Lambda^*$.

This covers a wide zoo: ergodic Markov chains (where $\Lambda$ comes from the Perron eigenvalue of the tilted transition operator), stationary Gaussian processes (where $\Lambda$ involves the spectral density), and bounded weakly dependent sums.

Worked example: AR(1) chain. Let $X_n=\rho X_{n-1}+\xi_n$ with $\xi_n\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ i.i.d., stationary mean $0$, variance $1/(1-\rho^2)$. The limiting log-MGF for $\bar X_n$ is $$\Lambda(\theta)=\frac{\theta^2}{2(1-\rho)^2},\qquad I(x)=\frac{(1-\rho)^2 x^2}{2}.$$ Persistence ($\rho>0$) flattens the rate function — large deviations become less rare because the chain "drifts" coherently rather than averaging out.

Why essential smoothness? Without it, the upper bound of the LDP still holds, but the lower bound can fail at exposed points $x$ where $I(x)$ is achieved only at the boundary of $\Lambda$'s domain. A classic counterexample: $\Lambda$ piecewise linear (e.g.\ for rare-event-conditioned chains) — then $\Lambda^*$ has flat regions and some atypical values are unreachable by tilting.

5. Applications: Schilder, Freidlin–Wentzell, entropy

The same machinery scales up to path space and to thermodynamics.

Schilder's theorem

Scaled Brownian motion $\sqrt\varepsilon\,W$ on $C([0,T];\mathbb{R}^d)$ satisfies an LDP at speed $1/\varepsilon$ with rate $$I(\phi)=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^T|\dot\phi(t)|^2\,dt\qquad(\phi\text{ absolutely continuous, }\phi(0)=0)$$ and $I(\phi)=+\infty$ otherwise. The minimum-action path between two points is a straight line — Brownian sample paths "want" to stay still, and any deviation costs Dirichlet energy.

Freidlin–Wentzell

For the small-noise SDE $dX^\varepsilon_t=b(X^\varepsilon_t)\,dt+\sqrt\varepsilon\,dW_t$, replace $\dot\phi$ in Schilder's rate by the deviation from the deterministic flow: $$S(\phi)=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^T|\dot\phi(t)-b(\phi(t))|^2\,dt.$$ The quasi-potential $V(x_0,y)=\inf\{S(\phi):\phi(0)=x_0,\phi(T)=y,T\ge 0\}$ governs rare exit times: if $D$ is a basin of an attractor $x_0$, the mean exit time satisfies $$\mathbb{E}_{x_0}[\tau_D]\asymp\exp\bigl(V(x_0,\partial D)/\varepsilon\bigr).$$ Eyring–Kramers refines the prefactor; transition pathways concentrate on the instanton, the action minimiser through the saddle.

Boltzmann entropy

For an ideal gas with $n$ particles in $k$ momentum cells, the empirical occupation $L_n$ satisfies Sanov: $\mathbb{P}(L_n\approx\nu)\asymp e^{-nH(\nu\,\|\,\mu)}$. Equivalently, the number of microstates compatible with macrostate $\nu$ is $W\asymp e^{nS(\nu)}$ where $S=-H(\nu\,\|\,\mu)$ — recovering Boltzmann's $S=k\log W$ with $k=k_B$ the conversion to physical units. The Gibbs measure $e^{-\beta E}/Z$ is the maximiser of $S-\beta\langle E\rangle$, i.e.\ the minimiser of free energy. Statistical mechanics, entirely from large deviations.

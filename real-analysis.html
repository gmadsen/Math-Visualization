Real analysis

Undergraduate analysis on $\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{R}^n$: completeness, continuity, differentiation, Riemann integration, uniform convergence, and the inverse/implicit function theorems. Metric spaces and general topology live on point-set topology; Lebesgue measure and $L^p$ spaces are the subject of measure theory.

1. Real numbers and completeness

Calculus textbooks write $\mathbb{R}$ as "the number line" and move on. Analysis begins by asking what property of $\mathbb{R}$, beyond $\mathbb{Q}$, actually does the work. The answer is the least-upper-bound property: every nonempty subset $S\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above has a smallest upper bound, $\sup S\in\mathbb{R}$. Equivalently — by Dedekind cuts, by Cauchy sequences, or by nested intervals — $\mathbb{R}$ is a complete ordered field, and that word "complete" is what fails for $\mathbb{Q}$.

Why $\mathbb{Q}$ isn't enough. Consider $S=\{q\in\mathbb{Q}: q>0, q^2<2\}\subset\mathbb{Q}$. This set is nonempty and bounded above (by $2$), yet it has no least upper bound in $\mathbb{Q}$: for any rational $q$ with $q^2\ge 2$, one can always find a smaller rational $q'\in\mathbb{Q}$ with $q'>\sqrt 2$. In $\mathbb{R}$ the supremum exists and equals $\sqrt 2\notin\mathbb{Q}$. The rationals have holes exactly where irrational cuts ought to live.

Three equivalent forms of completeness

  • Least upper bound (LUB): every nonempty bounded-above $S\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ has $\sup S\in\mathbb{R}$.
  • Cauchy completeness: every Cauchy sequence in $\mathbb{R}$ converges. (A sequence $(a_n)$ is Cauchy if $\forall\varepsilon>0\;\exists N\;\forall m,n\ge N: |a_m-a_n|<\varepsilon$.)
  • Nested intervals: if $[a_1,b_1]\supseteq [a_2,b_2]\supseteq\cdots$ is a nested sequence of closed bounded intervals with $b_n-a_n\to 0$, then $\bigcap_n [a_n,b_n]$ is a single point.

Over an ordered Archimedean field these are logically interchangeable; losing any one forces one to lose all three. Proofs of the big theorems (intermediate value, extreme value, Heine–Borel on intervals) all reduce to completeness.

Dedekind's construction

A Dedekind cut is a partition $(L,U)$ of $\mathbb{Q}$ with $L$ nonempty, bounded above, downward-closed, and with no maximum. Each cut names a real number: the rationals less than it. Addition, multiplication, and order lift from $\mathbb{Q}$. Cauchy's alternative construction builds $\mathbb{R}$ as equivalence classes of Cauchy rational sequences. Both constructions produce the same — unique up to ordered-field isomorphism — complete ordered field.

As $q$ grows, rational approximations squeeze in on $\sqrt 2$ from both sides, yet neither side ever reaches a rational equal to $\sqrt 2$. The existence of the supremum is therefore not automatic — it is the additional axiom that distinguishes $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$.

Archimedean property. For every real $x$ there is a natural number $n$ with $n>x$. This sounds obvious and follows from completeness: if $\mathbb{N}$ were bounded above, it would have a supremum $s$, but $s-1$ would also be an upper bound. The Archimedean property excludes infinitesimals and is what lets $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ arguments grind forward.

2. Continuity on $\mathbb{R}$

A function $f\colon D\to\mathbb{R}$ is continuous at $a\in D$ if for every $\varepsilon>0$ there exists $\delta>0$ such that

$$ |x-a|<\delta \;\Longrightarrow\; |f(x)-f(a)|<\varepsilon. $$

The definition trades a target tolerance $\varepsilon$ (how close the output must be to $f(a)$) for a domain tolerance $\delta$ (how close the input must be to $a$). The function is continuous on $D$ if it is continuous at every point of $D$.

Two theorems that live and die with completeness

Intermediate value theorem (IVT). If $f\colon[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ is continuous and $y$ lies between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then there exists $c\in[a,b]$ with $f(c)=y$. The proof is a bisection argument: repeatedly halve the interval, keeping the half where $f$ changes sign relative to $y$. Completeness delivers the limit point. Without completeness, the continuous function $f(q)=q^2-2$ on $\mathbb{Q}\cap[0,2]$ would have $f(0)=-2$, $f(2)=2$, but no rational zero.

Extreme value theorem (EVT). A continuous function on a closed bounded interval $[a,b]$ attains its maximum and minimum. This follows from the Heine–Borel fact that $[a,b]$ is compact (every open cover admits a finite subcover) plus the observation that continuous images of compact sets are compact, hence bounded and closed — in $\mathbb{R}$, that means they contain their sup and inf.

Uniform continuity. On a compact set $K$, a continuous function is automatically uniformly continuous: the same $\delta$ works everywhere in $K$, given $\varepsilon$. On non-compact domains this fails — $f(x)=x^2$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$ but not uniformly continuous; $f(x)=1/x$ is continuous on $(0,1]$ but not uniformly continuous either.

3. Differentiation

The derivative at $a$ is a number $f'(a)$, but the right way to read its definition is as a best-linear-approximation statement. A function $f$ is differentiable at $a$ if there exists a linear map $L\colon\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ (multiplication by the single number $f'(a)$) such that

$$ f(a+h) = f(a) + L(h) + r(h),\quad \text{with}\quad \frac{|r(h)|}{|h|}\longrightarrow 0 \text{ as } h\to 0. $$

Equivalently $f'(a)=\lim_{h\to 0}\big(f(a+h)-f(a)\big)/h$. The packaging matters: in §6 the "linear map $L$" becomes the Jacobian, and the limit condition generalizes verbatim.

The chain rule as composition of best approximations

If $g$ is differentiable at $a$ and $f$ is differentiable at $g(a)$, the composition $f\circ g$ is differentiable at $a$ with $(f\circ g)'(a) = f'(g(a))\cdot g'(a)$. Written in best-linear-approximation language, the linear approximation to $f\circ g$ at $a$ is just the composition of the two linear approximations. The product of derivatives is the statement that "composition of one-dimensional linear maps is multiplication."

The mean value theorem

MVT. If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then there exists $c\in(a,b)$ with

$$ f'(c) = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. $$

The widget above exhibits such a $c$ as the green dot. MVT is the workhorse of single-variable analysis: it underwrites Taylor's theorem, the sign of $f'$ controlling monotonicity, and L'Hôpital's rule. Its proof is a one-line reduction to Rolle's theorem, which is in turn an EVT argument.

Taylor's theorem with remainder

If $f$ is $n$-times differentiable on $[a,b]$ and $f^{(n+1)}$ exists on $(a,b)$, then for each $x\in[a,b]$ there exists $\xi$ between $a$ and $x$ such that

$$ f(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k \;+\; \frac{f^{(n+1)}(\xi)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1}. $$

The first sum is the degree-$n$ Taylor polynomial; the last term is Lagrange's form of the remainder (integral and Cauchy forms also exist). The remainder quantifies how well polynomials approximate smooth functions — tightly, if $f^{(n+1)}$ is bounded — and underlies every error estimate in numerical analysis.

4. Uniform convergence

Given a sequence of functions $f_n\colon D\to\mathbb{R}$, two convergence notions compete:

  • Pointwise: $f_n\to f$ pointwise if $\forall x\in D\;\forall\varepsilon>0\;\exists N(x,\varepsilon)\;\forall n\ge N: |f_n(x)-f(x)|<\varepsilon$. The $N$ is allowed to depend on the point $x$.
  • Uniform: $f_n\to f$ uniformly if $\forall\varepsilon>0\;\exists N(\varepsilon)\;\forall n\ge N\;\forall x\in D: |f_n(x)-f(x)|<\varepsilon$. A single $N$ works for every $x$ simultaneously.

The quantifier swap is everything. Uniform convergence is equivalent to $\sup_{x\in D}|f_n(x)-f(x)|\to 0$, and it is the kind of convergence that preserves continuity, boundedness, and Riemann integrability.

Two clean theorems

Continuity preservation. If each $f_n$ is continuous on $D$ and $f_n\to f$ uniformly on $D$, then $f$ is continuous on $D$. The proof is a $3\varepsilon/3$ argument: write

$$ |f(x)-f(y)| \le |f(x)-f_N(x)| + |f_N(x)-f_N(y)| + |f_N(y)-f(y)|, $$

bound the outer terms by uniformity and the middle term by continuity of $f_N$. Pointwise convergence is not enough — the pointwise limit of $x^n$ on $[0,1]$ is discontinuous at $1$.

Integration swap. If each $f_n$ is Riemann-integrable on $[a,b]$ and $f_n\to f$ uniformly, then $f$ is Riemann-integrable and $\int_a^b f_n\to\int_a^b f$. Pointwise convergence on its own does not license this swap — compare the Dirichlet example from measure theory, §1.

Weierstrass M-test. If $\sum M_n<\infty$ and $|f_n(x)|\le M_n$ for all $x\in D$, then $\sum f_n$ converges absolutely and uniformly on $D$. This is the standard tool for producing uniform convergence of power series on compact subsets of their disk of convergence, and Fourier series of sufficiently smooth functions.

5. The Riemann integral

A partition of $[a,b]$ is a finite sequence $P\colon a=x_0upper and lower sums

$$ U(f,P)=\sum_{k=1}^n \Big(\sup_{[x_{k-1},x_k]} f\Big)(x_k-x_{k-1}),\qquad L(f,P)=\sum_{k=1}^n \Big(\inf_{[x_{k-1},x_k]} f\Big)(x_k-x_{k-1}). $$

Refining $P$ makes $U$ go down and $L$ go up. Define the upper and lower Darboux integrals as the $\inf_P U$ and $\sup_P L$. When these agree, $f$ is Riemann-integrable and their common value is $\int_a^b f$.

When is $f$ Riemann-integrable?

A bounded function is Riemann-integrable on $[a,b]$ iff for every $\varepsilon>0$ there is a partition with $U(f,P)-L(f,P)<\varepsilon$. A clean characterization due to Lebesgue: $f$ is Riemann-integrable iff $f$ is bounded and its set of discontinuities has Lebesgue measure zero. Every continuous function on $[a,b]$ qualifies; every monotone function qualifies; the Dirichlet indicator $\mathbf 1_{\mathbb{Q}\cap[0,1]}$ does not.

The fundamental theorem of calculus

FTC I. If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt$, then $F$ is differentiable on $(a,b)$ with $F'(x)=f(x)$. So indefinite integration undoes differentiation.

FTC II. If $F$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $F'=f$ on $(a,b)$ with $f$ Riemann-integrable, then $\int_a^b f=F(b)-F(a)$. So differentiation undoes indefinite integration.

Bridge to measure theory. Pointwise limits break Riemann integrability (Dirichlet); unbounded domains produce only conditional convergence (Fourier's integrals); the class of integrable functions is not a Banach space. These pathologies motivate Lebesgue's slice-the-range construction in measure theory, whose dominated convergence theorem licenses the swap $\int\lim = \lim\int$ under a single integrable dominator.

6. Multivariable differentiation

A map $F\colon U\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$ is differentiable at $a\in U$ if there exists a linear map $L\colon\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$ such that

$$ F(a+h) = F(a) + L(h) + r(h),\qquad \frac{\|r(h)\|}{\|h\|}\longrightarrow 0 \text{ as } h\to 0. $$

The linear map $L$ is the total derivative $DF(a)\in\mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{R}^n,\mathbb{R}^m)$. In the standard basis it is represented by the Jacobian matrix

$$ J_F(a) = \Big[\partial_j F^i(a)\Big]_{\substack{1\le i\le m\\ 1\le j\le n}}. $$

The $i$-th row is the gradient $\nabla F^i$ of the $i$-th component; the $j$-th column is the partial derivative vector $\partial_j F$.

Partials are not enough. Existence of all partial derivatives $\partial_j F^i(a)$ does not imply differentiability. The classical counterexample $f(x,y)=xy/(x^2+y^2)$ (with $f(0,0)=0$) has $\partial_x f(0,0)=\partial_y f(0,0)=0$, yet $f$ is not even continuous at the origin. Sufficient condition: if all partials exist on a neighborhood of $a$ and are continuous at $a$, then $F$ is (totally) differentiable at $a$ — i.e., $C^1$ implies $C^1$-differentiable in the strong sense.

Gradient and directional derivative

For scalar $f\colon U\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$, the gradient $\nabla f(a)=(\partial_1 f,\dots,\partial_n f)^T$ is the vector whose dot product with a unit direction $v$ gives the directional derivative

$$ D_v f(a) = \lim_{t\to 0}\frac{f(a+tv)-f(a)}{t} = \nabla f(a)\cdot v. $$

Hence $\nabla f(a)$ points in the direction of steepest ascent and $\|\nabla f(a)\|$ is the steepest slope. Level sets $\{f=c\}$ are perpendicular to $\nabla f$ wherever $\nabla f\ne 0$.

Chain rule

If $G\colon\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$ is differentiable at $a$ and $F\colon\mathbb{R}^m\to\mathbb{R}^k$ is differentiable at $G(a)$, then $F\circ G$ is differentiable at $a$ with

$$ D(F\circ G)(a) = DF(G(a))\circ DG(a),\qquad J_{F\circ G}(a) = J_F(G(a))\cdot J_G(a). $$

The derivative of a composition is the matrix product of Jacobians. In single-variable language this collapses to $(f\circ g)'(a)=f'(g(a))g'(a)$.

7. Multivariable integration

Integration on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is built on boxes. A closed box is a product $B=[a_1,b_1]\times\cdots\times[a_n,b_n]$; its volume is $\operatorname{vol}(B)=\prod (b_j-a_j)$. A bounded function $f\colon B\to\mathbb{R}$ is Riemann-integrable if upper and lower sums over grid-partitions of $B$ agree, and when they do, $\int_B f$ is their common value. This generalizes §5 verbatim.

Fubini's theorem

Fubini (Riemann form). If $f$ is Riemann-integrable on $B=[a,b]\times[c,d]$ and for each $x\in[a,b]$ the single-variable function $y\mapsto f(x,y)$ is Riemann-integrable on $[c,d]$, then the iterated integral equals the double integral:

$$ \iint_{B} f \;=\; \int_a^b\!\!\bigg(\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\bigg)dx \;=\; \int_c^d\!\!\bigg(\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\bigg)dy. $$

Continuity of $f$ is the usual sufficient hypothesis; Tonelli's theorem extends this to nonnegative measurable functions in the measure-theoretic setting.

Change of variables

Let $\varphi\colon U\to V$ be a $C^1$-diffeomorphism between open sets in $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $f\colon V\to\mathbb{R}$ continuous with compact support in $V$. Then

$$ \int_V f(y)\,dy = \int_U f(\varphi(x))\,|\det J_\varphi(x)|\,dx. $$

The factor $|\det J_\varphi|$ is how local volumes get rescaled by $\varphi$: a small box of volume $dV$ at $x$ is mapped by the linearization to a parallelepiped of volume $|\det J_\varphi(x)|\,dV$.

8. Inverse and implicit function theorems

Both theorems upgrade a statement about a linear map (the Jacobian) at a point to a statement about the nonlinear map near that point. They are the engine behind local coordinates on submanifolds — the starting point of differential geometry.

Inverse function theorem

IFT. Let $F\colon U\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n$ be $C^1$ on an open set $U$ and suppose the Jacobian $J_F(a)$ is invertible at some $a\in U$. Then there exist open neighborhoods $V\ni a$ and $W\ni F(a)$ such that $F\colon V\to W$ is a $C^1$-diffeomorphism, and its inverse satisfies

$$ J_{F^{-1}}(F(a)) = J_F(a)^{-1}. $$

The proof idea: the linear approximation $DF(a)$ is invertible, and the nonlinear remainder is small by differentiability. A fixed-point (contraction-mapping) argument on the map $y\mapsto x + DF(a)^{-1}(y-F(x))$ produces the local inverse; the Banach fixed-point theorem does the analytic heavy lifting.

Implicit function theorem

Implicit FT. Let $F\colon U\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n\times\mathbb{R}^m\to\mathbb{R}^m$ be $C^1$, suppose $F(a,b)=0$, and assume the $m\times m$ Jacobian block $\partial F/\partial y(a,b)$ is invertible. Then there exist neighborhoods $V\ni a$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $W\ni b$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$ and a unique $C^1$ map $g\colon V\to W$ such that for all $x\in V$

$$ F(x, g(x)) = 0, \qquad g(a)=b. $$

Moreover, implicit differentiation gives $Dg(x) = -\big(\partial F/\partial y\big)^{-1}\cdot\big(\partial F/\partial x\big)$. The implicit FT is a restatement of the inverse FT applied to the map $(x,y)\mapsto (x,F(x,y))$.

Why this is the gateway to manifolds

A subset $M\subseteq\mathbb{R}^N$ is a smooth $k$-dimensional submanifold if near each point it looks, after a $C^\infty$ change of coordinates, like $\mathbb{R}^k\subseteq\mathbb{R}^N$. The implicit function theorem is the mechanism: given a $C^1$ map $F\colon\mathbb{R}^N\to\mathbb{R}^{N-k}$ whose Jacobian has full rank at each point of $F^{-1}(0)$, the level set $M=F^{-1}(0)$ is automatically a $k$-dimensional submanifold, parametrized locally by $k$ of the coordinates. This regular-value theorem is the workhorse constructor of manifolds — see smooth manifolds for the coordinate-invariant formulation and the rank theorem, its cleanest generalization.

Where IFT fails. On the folium $x^3+y^3-3xy=0$, both partials vanish at the origin — so near $(0,0)$ the curve is not the graph of $y=g(x)$ or of $x=h(y)$. Geometrically, it self-intersects. IFT guarantees nothing when the relevant Jacobian block is singular; the failure modes are where interesting singularity theory starts.

Stochastic processes

Filtrations as a stream of expanding information; martingales as fair games whose conditional expectations don't drift; Doob's optional-stopping, decomposition, and convergence theorems as the three structural pillars; Brownian motion as the canonical continuous-time martingale with quadratic variation $[W]_t=t$. Every concept is paired with a small simulator you can run.

1. Filtrations and adapted processes

Fix a probability space $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$. A filtration is an increasing chain of sub-$\sigma$-algebras $$\mathcal{F}_0\subseteq\mathcal{F}_1\subseteq\mathcal{F}_2\subseteq\cdots\subseteq\mathcal{F}.$$ Read $\mathcal{F}_n$ as the information available at time $n$. Time advances: events that become decidable at later times can never be lost. The natural filtration of a process $(X_n)$ is $\mathcal{F}_n^X=\sigma(X_0,\ldots,X_n)$ — the smallest $\sigma$-algebra making every past observation measurable.

A stochastic process $(Y_n)_{n\ge 0}$ is adapted to $(\mathcal{F}_n)$ if $Y_n$ is $\mathcal{F}_n$-measurable for each $n$. Every process is adapted to its own natural filtration, by construction. Predictable is one step stricter: $(Y_n)$ is predictable if $Y_n$ is $\mathcal{F}_{n-1}$-measurable — known one step ahead of time.

Why filtrations? Conditional expectations $\mathbb{E}[\,\cdot\mid\mathcal{F}_n]$ live inside the filtration. The martingale identity $\mathbb{E}[M_{n+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=M_n$ is meaningful only once we have nailed down what counts as "information at time $n$". Without the filtration, "fair game" has no formal content.

Stopping times. A random time $\tau\colon\Omega\to\{0,1,\ldots\}\cup\{\infty\}$ is a stopping time w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_n)$ if $$\{\tau\le n\}\in\mathcal{F}_n\quad\text{for every }n.$$ At time $n$, looking only at the data so far, you can decide whether stopping has happened. The first hitting time $\tau=\inf\{n:X_n\in B\}$ of a measurable set is always a stopping time; the last time the process was in $B$ before time $100$ is generally not — that requires looking at the future.

Worked example: hitting times of a random walk. Let $S_n$ be symmetric simple random walk on $\mathbb{Z}$ with $S_0=0$, and $\mathcal{F}_n=\sigma(S_0,\ldots,S_n)$. 2. Discrete-time martingales

An adapted, $L^1$ process $(M_n)_{n\ge 0}$ is a martingale w.r.t.\ the filtration $(\mathcal{F}_n)$ if $$\mathbb{E}[M_{n+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=M_n\qquad\text{for every }n\ge 0.$$ Replace "$=$" with "$\ge$" for a submartingale, "$\le$" for a supermartingale. The martingale identity says: given everything you know at time $n$, the best predictor of the next value is the current value. It is the formal expression of "fair game" — and equivalently, the increments $\Delta M_{n+1}=M_{n+1}-M_n$ have $\mathbb{E}[\Delta M_{n+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=0$.

Examples.

  • Mean-zero random walk: $S_n=X_1+\cdots+X_n$ with $X_i$ i.i.d., $\mathbb{E}[X_i]=0$. Direct check: $\mathbb{E}[S_{n+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=S_n+\mathbb{E}[X_{n+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=S_n+0$.
  • Doob martingale: $M_n=\mathbb{E}[Z\mid\mathcal{F}_n]$ for any integrable $Z$. The tower property gives $\mathbb{E}[M_{n+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[Z\mid\mathcal{F}_{n+1}]\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=\mathbb{E}[Z\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=M_n$. Every UI martingale is of this form for some terminal $Z=M_\infty$.
  • Squared walk minus drift: with $\text{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2$, $S_n^2-n\sigma^2$ is a martingale. Without the $-n\sigma^2$ correction, $S_n^2$ is only a submartingale.
  • Exponential martingale: for any $\theta\in\mathbb{R}$ such that $\varphi(\theta)=\mathbb{E}[e^{\theta X_1}]<\infty$, the process $M_n=e^{\theta S_n}/\varphi(\theta)^n$ is a martingale (Wald's exponential).

Worked example: a biased random walk and a martingale fix. Let $X_i\in\{\pm 1\}$ with $\mathbb{P}(X=+1)=p\ne 1/2$ and $q=1-p$. Then $\mathbb{E}[X_i]=p-q\ne 0$, so $S_n$ is not a martingale. But the centred process $S_n-n(p-q)$ is, and the multiplicative process $M_n=(q/p)^{S_n}$ is a martingale too — the latter is the engine of the gambler's-ruin formula in §3.

3. Optional stopping theorem

Doob's optional-stopping theorem. Let $(M_n)$ be a martingale and $\tau$ a stopping time. If at least one of the following holds:

  1. $\tau$ is bounded a.s.; or
  2. $M$ is uniformly integrable; or
  3. $\tau<\infty$ a.s.\ and the stopped process $(M_{n\wedge\tau})$ is uniformly bounded;
then $$\mathbb{E}[M_\tau]\;=\;\mathbb{E}[M_0].$$ The theorem is a martingale's defining promise made global: the fair-game expectation persists across optional stopping rules — provided the stopping is "honest", in any of the three precise senses above.

Optional-stopping fails without the hypotheses. Symmetric simple random walk $S_n$ stopped at $\tau=\inf\{n:S_n=1\}$ has $\tau<\infty$ almost surely and $\mathbb{E}[S_\tau]=1\ne 0=\mathbb{E}[S_0]$ — none of the three sufficient conditions hold ($\tau$ is a.s.\ finite but unbounded; $M$ is not UI; $(S_{n\wedge\tau})$ wanders to $-\infty$ unboundedly). Drop the hypotheses and the conclusion fails.

Worked example: gambler's ruin. Symmetric random walk on $\{a,a+1,\ldots,b\}$ with $a<0

Wald's identity follows the same template. For i.i.d. $X_i$ with mean $\mu$ and a stopping time $\tau$ with $\mathbb{E}\tau<\infty$, optional stopping on $S_n-n\mu$ gives $\mathbb{E}[S_\tau]=\mu\,\mathbb{E}[\tau]$. Both gambler's ruin and Wald are one-line corollaries of optional stopping applied to the right martingale.

4. Doob decomposition and quadratic variation

Doob decomposition theorem. Every adapted $L^1$ process $(X_n)$ admits a unique decomposition $$X_n=X_0+M_n+A_n,$$ where $(M_n)$ is a martingale with $M_0=0$ and $(A_n)$ is predictable (i.e.\ $A_n$ is $\mathcal{F}_{n-1}$-measurable) with $A_0=0$. Existence is constructive: set $A_n=\sum_{k=1}^n\mathbb{E}[X_k-X_{k-1}\mid\mathcal{F}_{k-1}]$ and $M_n=X_n-X_0-A_n$. Uniqueness follows because two predictable martingales with $A_0=0$ must vanish (predictable + martingale $=$ constant).

The decomposition cleanly splits a process into "drift" ($A$, the predictable compensator) and "noise" ($M$, the unpredictable martingale increment). $X$ is a submartingale precisely when $A$ is non-decreasing; supermartingale when $A$ is non-increasing.

Predictable quadratic variation. For a square-integrable martingale $(M_n)$ with $M_0=0$, the squared process $M_n^2$ is a submartingale. Its Doob compensator is the predictable quadratic variation $$\langle M\rangle_n=\sum_{k=1}^n\mathbb{E}[(M_k-M_{k-1})^2\mid\mathcal{F}_{k-1}],$$ and $M_n^2-\langle M\rangle_n$ is a martingale. Heuristically: the variance of $M$ accumulates at the predictable rate $\langle M\rangle$.

Worked example: $S_n^2$ for symmetric simple random walk. With $X_i=\pm 1$ each w.p. $1/2$ and $S_n=X_1+\cdots+X_n$, the increments of $S_n^2$ are $$S_k^2-S_{k-1}^2=2S_{k-1}X_k+X_k^2.$$ Conditionally on $\mathcal{F}_{k-1}$, $\mathbb{E}[2S_{k-1}X_k\mid\mathcal{F}_{k-1}]=0$ and $\mathbb{E}[X_k^2\mid\mathcal{F}_{k-1}]=1$, so the predictable compensator is $A_n=n$. The martingale part is $M_n=S_n^2-n$ — exactly the example from §2. The discrete predictable quadratic variation is $\langle S\rangle_n=n$, presaging the continuous-time identity $[W]_t=t$ in §6.

5. Martingale convergence theorem

Doob's martingale convergence theorem. If $(M_n)$ is a martingale (or more generally a submartingale) with $$\sup_n\mathbb{E}|M_n|<\infty,$$ then there exists $M_\infty\in L^1$ with $M_n\to M_\infty$ almost surely. The proof goes through Doob's upcrossing inequality: $L^1$-boundedness limits the expected number of times $M_n$ crosses any interval $[a,b]$, so the sequence of values cannot oscillate forever.

$L^1$-boundedness is genuinely weaker than $L^1$-convergence. To upgrade a.s.\ convergence to $L^1$ convergence, we need uniform integrability: a family $\{M_n\}$ is UI if $$\lim_{K\to\infty}\sup_n\mathbb{E}\!\left[|M_n|\,\mathbf{1}_{|M_n|>K}\right]=0.$$ For a martingale, $L^1$-convergence is equivalent to UI. When $(M_n)$ is UI, $M_n\to M_\infty$ in $L^1$ as well as a.s., and $M_n=\mathbb{E}[M_\infty\mid\mathcal{F}_n]$ — every UI martingale is a Doob martingale of its terminal value.

$L^1$-bounded ≠ UI. Let $(X_i)$ be i.i.d. with $\mathbb{P}(X_i=2)=\mathbb{P}(X_i=0)=1/2$, and $M_n=\prod_{i=1}^n X_i$. Then $\mathbb{E}[M_n]=1$ for all $n$ (so the sup-norm is bounded), and $M_n\ge 0$. But the first time some $X_i=0$ is a.s.\ finite, after which $M_n=0$ forever. So $M_n\to 0$ a.s. — yet $\mathbb{E}[M_n]=1\not\to 0=\mathbb{E}[M_\infty]$. The convergence is a.s. but not in $L^1$, and the family is not UI.

Worked example: bounded martingales converge cleanly. Any martingale bounded above and below in absolute value is automatically UI (boundedness $\Rightarrow$ UI), so it converges both a.s.\ and in $L^1$. Doob martingales of bounded random variables ($Z\in L^\infty$) are always UI; sums of i.i.d.\ mean-zero variables truncated to a bounded set are not (they typically diverge). The widget contrasts a bounded martingale (converges to a definite limit) with the doubling product (converges a.s.\ to $0$ while keeping mean $1$).

6. Continuous-time martingales and Brownian motion

The discrete theory passes to continuous time once we replace $\mathbb{N}$ by $[0,\infty)$ and demand path regularity (right-continuity with left limits). The canonical example is Brownian motion $W_t$: $W_0=0$, increments $W_t-W_s\sim\mathcal{N}(0,t-s)$ are independent, and paths are continuous a.s.

Brownian motion as the prototype martingale. Three explicit martingales sit on top of $W$:

  • $W_t$ itself, w.r.t.\ its natural filtration.
  • $W_t^2-t$ — the continuous analogue of $S_n^2-n$. Its predictable quadratic variation is $\langle W\rangle_t=t$.
  • The exponential martingale $\exp(\theta W_t-\theta^2 t/2)$, $\theta\in\mathbb{R}$ — engine of Girsanov's change of measure.

Quadratic variation. For a partition $0=t_0quadratic variation $[W]_t=t$ a.s. Compare this with the total variation: $\sum_k|W_{t_{k+1}}-W_{t_k}|\to+\infty$. Brownian paths are wiggly enough that their quadratic variation is finite and linear in $t$, but rough enough that their total variation diverges. This is what makes ordinary Riemann–Stieltjes integration $\int f\,dW$ fail and forces the Itô integral.

Burkholder–Davis–Gundy. For any continuous local martingale $M$ with $M_0=0$ and any $p\ge 1$, there are universal constants $c_p,C_p$ with $$c_p\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[[M]_t^{p/2}\right]\;\le\;\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sup_{s\le t}|M_s|^p\right]\;\le\;C_p\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[[M]_t^{p/2}\right].$$ BDG converts maximal-norm estimates of a martingale into estimates of its quadratic variation — the bridge to Itô calculus and SDE theory.

Worked example: Brownian quadratic variation. Simulate $W$ on $[0,1]$ with mesh $1/N$. Compute $Q_N=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}(W_{(k+1)/N}-W_{k/N})^2$. As $N$ grows, $Q_N\to 1=t$ in probability — and the variance of the approximation is $\text{Var}(Q_N)=2t^2/N$ by direct calculation (Gaussian fourth moments). The widget plots $Q_N$ as a running sum alongside the constant line at $t$, so you watch the path's quadratic variation accumulate.

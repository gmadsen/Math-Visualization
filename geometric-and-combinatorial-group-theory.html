Geometric and combinatorial group theory

Groups as metric spaces — Cayley graphs, word problems, growth, and the negatively-curved geometry that turns a presentation into a geometric object.

1. Presentations and the word problem

A presentation writes a group as a quotient of a free group. Pick a generating set $S$; form the free group $F(S)$ — formal reduced words in $S \cup S^{-1}$, multiplied by concatenation — then impose relators $R \subset F(S)$, declaring each $r \in R$ to be the identity. Formally:

$$ G \;=\; \langle S \mid R \rangle \;:=\; F(S) \,\big/\, \langle\!\langle R \rangle\!\rangle, $$

where $\langle\!\langle R \rangle\!\rangle$ is the smallest normal subgroup of $F(S)$ containing $R$. "Smallest normal" is essential: setting $r = e$ forces $grg^{-1} = e$ for every $g$ as well, since the kernel of any homomorphism is normal.

Examples. $\mathbb{Z} = \langle a \rangle$, free on one generator. $\mathbb{Z}^2 = \langle a, b \mid aba^{-1}b^{-1} \rangle$ — the commutator relator forces $ab = ba$. The dihedral group $D_n = \langle r, s \mid r^n,\ s^2,\ srsr \rangle$. The trefoil knot group is $\langle a, b \mid aba = bab \rangle$.

$G$ is finitely generated when $|S| < \infty$, finitely presented when $|R|$ is also finite. Tietze (1908) showed any two finite presentations of the same group are connected by a sequence of four elementary moves: add/remove a generator that is a word in the others, add/remove a relator that is a consequence of the others. So the equivalence relation "same group" is generated by these local moves.

The word problem — decide whether a given word in $S^{\pm 1}$ equals $e$ — is undecidable in general (Novikov 1955, Boone 1958), even for finitely-presented groups. It is decidable for finite groups (compute), free groups (reduce), abelian groups (sum exponents), and hyperbolic groups (Dehn's algorithm, §5).

2. Cayley graphs and the word metric

A presentation tells us what $G$ is; the Cayley graph draws a picture of it. Fix a symmetric generating set $S$ (so $s \in S \Rightarrow s^{-1} \in S$). The Cayley graph $\mathrm{Cay}(G, S)$ has:

  • Vertex set $G$ — one node per group element.
  • Edge set $\{(g, gs) : g \in G,\ s \in S\}$ — labelled by the generator $s$ used.

Two algebraic features become geometric. Left multiplication by any $h \in G$ permutes the vertices and preserves edges: if $g \to gs$ is an edge, so is $hg \to hgs = (hg)s$. So $G$ acts on $\mathrm{Cay}(G, S)$ by graph automorphisms, and the action is transitive — the graph looks the same from every vertex. Right multiplication by $s \in S$ moves along the edge labelled $s$ at every vertex; this is not an automorphism (it permutes the edge labels), but it is the action that defines the edges.

The word metric $d_S(g, h) = d_S(e, g^{-1}h) = $ length of the shortest word in $S \cup S^{-1}$ representing $g^{-1}h$ — equivalently, graph distance in $\mathrm{Cay}(G, S)$. Different finite generating sets give different graphs, but any two are bilipschitz equivalent (each generator is a finite word in the other set), so coarse geometry doesn't depend on $S$.

3. Quasi-isometry and large-scale geometry

The Cayley graph depends on $S$, but we want a notion of "shape of the group" that doesn't. The fix is to throw away short-range and bounded-distortion information. A map $f: X \to Y$ between metric spaces is a $(K, C)$-quasi-isometric embedding if for all $x, x' \in X$,

$$ \tfrac{1}{K}\, d_X(x, x') - C \;\le\; d_Y(f(x), f(x')) \;\le\; K\, d_X(x, x') + C. $$

It is a quasi-isometry if additionally $f(X)$ is $C$-dense in $Y$. The relation "quasi-isometric" is an equivalence relation on metric spaces.

Schwarz–Milnor lemma. If $G$ acts properly and cocompactly by isometries on a proper geodesic metric space $X$ (think: a covering action on a compact Riemannian manifold), then $G$ with any word metric is quasi-isometric to $X$. In particular, two finite generating sets of $G$ give quasi-isometric Cayley graphs. So: every finitely-generated group has a well-defined coarse geometry.

Quasi-isometry is brutal — it forgets element order, individual generators, and any local structure — but the surviving invariants are the lifeblood of geometric group theory: growth type, number of ends, hyperbolicity, asymptotic dimension, $\ell^p$-cohomology.

4. Growth functions and Gromov's theorem

How fast does the ball $B_n = \{g \in G : d_S(e, g) \le n\}$ grow? Define

$$ \beta_S(n) \;=\; \#\, B_n. $$

Two growth functions are equivalent when each is bounded by a linear rescaling of the other; up to equivalence, $\beta$ depends only on $G$, not on $S$.

Group$\beta(n)$Type
$\mathbb{Z}^d$$\Theta(n^d)$polynomial, degree $d$
Heisenberg group $H_3(\mathbb{Z})$$\Theta(n^4)$polynomial, degree $4$ (not the rank!)
Free group $F_k$$\Theta((2k-1)^n)$exponential
Hyperbolic surface group ($g \ge 2$)exponentialexponential
Grigorchuk group$e^{n^\alpha}$, $\alpha \approx 0.767$intermediate

Gromov's theorem (1981). A finitely-generated group has polynomial growth if and only if it is virtually nilpotent (contains a finite-index nilpotent subgroup). The $(\Leftarrow)$ direction is classical (Bass, Wolf, Guivarc'h); the $(\Rightarrow)$ direction is Gromov's tour de force, using a limit argument that extracts a Lie group from the asymptotic cone of $G$ via the Montgomery–Zippin solution to Hilbert's fifth problem. It's the prototype "geometry forces algebra" theorem of the field.

5. Gromov-hyperbolic groups

A geodesic metric space $X$ is $\delta$-hyperbolic when every geodesic triangle is $\delta$-thin: each side lies in the $\delta$-neighborhood of the union of the other two. In Euclidean space no finite $\delta$ works (large triangles have large interior gaps); in the hyperbolic plane $\mathbb{H}^2$ all triangles satisfy this with $\delta = \log(1+\sqrt{2})$. Gromov elevated this property to a definition: $G$ is hyperbolic if some Cayley graph $\mathrm{Cay}(G, S)$ is $\delta$-hyperbolic for some $\delta < \infty$. By the Schwarz–Milnor lemma this is independent of $S$.

Examples. Free groups (trees are $0$-hyperbolic). Surface groups $\pi_1(\Sigma_g)$ for $g \ge 2$ — they act properly cocompactly on $\mathbb{H}^2$. Random groups (in Gromov's density model). $\mathbb{Z}^2$ is not hyperbolic — it contains arbitrarily large flat squares, hence arbitrarily fat triangles.

Consequences. Word and conjugacy problems solvable in linear time (Dehn's algorithm). Finitely many conjugacy classes of torsion. A natural compactification by the boundary at infinity $\partial G$, on which $G$ acts as a "convergence group". Quasi-isometric rigidity: in many cases, two hyperbolic groups quasi-isometric to each other are commensurable.

Dehn's algorithm. A presentation $\langle S \mid R \rangle$ is a Dehn presentation if every nontrivial word $w$ representing the identity contains more than half of some cyclic conjugate of a relator. Then we can repeatedly replace that long subword with its shorter complement, strictly decreasing word length each time — the algorithm terminates in $O(|w|)$ steps. Hyperbolic groups always admit a Dehn presentation; this is why the word problem is linear-time.

6. Applications and frontiers

Geometric group theory is now load-bearing across several fields. A non-exhaustive tour:

Decision problems

Three classical Dehn problems are undecidable in general for f.p. groups:

  • Word ($w \stackrel{?}{=} e$): undecidable — Novikov 1955, Boone 1958.
  • Conjugacy ($u \stackrel{?}{\sim} v$): undecidable — C.F. Miller 1971.
  • Isomorphism ($G_1 \stackrel{?}{\cong} G_2$): undecidable — Adian 1955, Rabin 1958.

Restricting to hyperbolic groups makes all three decidable; for many other classes (one-relator, residually finite f.p., automatic) only some are.

Mostow rigidity

For a closed hyperbolic manifold $M$ of dimension $\ge 3$, the fundamental group $\pi_1(M)$ determines the metric: any homotopy equivalence $M \to M'$ between two such manifolds is homotopic to an isometry. This is dramatic — the algebraic invariant $\pi_1$ remembers all geometric data, in stark contrast to $\dim 2$ where surfaces of fixed genus form a 6g−6-dimensional moduli space.

CAT(0) cube complexes

A higher-dimensional generalization of trees: cube complexes satisfying the CAT(0) curvature condition. Daniel Wise's "malnormal special quotient theorem" plus Ian Agol's resolution of the virtual Haken conjecture (2012) hinge on understanding which 3-manifold groups act on CAT(0) cube complexes. Fields Medal territory.

Expander graphs

Cayley graphs of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$ with a fixed generating set, varying $p$, form an expander family — finite graphs whose Cheeger constant is bounded below independently of size. They power error-correcting codes, derandomization, and pseudorandom-generator constructions in TCS.

Connections

Geometric group theory remains a young, active field. The geometry of mapping class groups (Behrstock–Hagen–Sisto's hierarchically hyperbolic spaces), random groups (the Gromov density model), boundary rigidity for higher-rank lattices, and the still-open isomorphism problem for general hyperbolic groups all sit on its current frontier.

  • Abstract algebra — Subgroups, cosets, Lagrange — the finite-index machinery underwrites "virtually $X$" properties (nilpotent, free, abelian).
  • Algebraic topology — Fundamental group — every f.p. group arises as $\pi_1$ of some 2-complex; surface and 3-manifold $\pi_1$ are the geometric flagships.
  • Spectral graph theory — adjacency & Laplacian — expanders from Cayley graphs of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$ tie group theory to spectral geometry.
  • Point-set topology — Metric spaces — the metric-space axioms are the entire foundation of word metrics and quasi-isometry.
  • Lie groups — lattices in semisimple Lie groups are the source of Mostow rigidity and many of the deepest examples in the field.

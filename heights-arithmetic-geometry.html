Heights in arithmetic geometry

A height function turns "how complicated is this number?" into a real-valued invariant. From the digit-count of a fraction to the canonical quadratic form on a Mordell–Weil group, heights are the bridge between algebraic geometry and Diophantine finiteness.

1. Naive height on projective space

A rational point of $\mathbb{P}^n(\mathbb{Q})$ is an equivalence class $[x_0:\dots:x_n]$ under simultaneous rescaling. To pin down "complexity" we choose the canonical representative: clear denominators, divide out by $\gcd(x_0,\dots,x_n)$, and demand all $x_i\in\mathbb{Z}$ with overall $\gcd=1$. The naive (multiplicative) height is $$H(P)\;=\;\max_i|x_i|.$$ On $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{Q})$, identifying $[a:b]$ with $a/b$ (in lowest terms), this is exactly $\max(|a|,|b|)$ — the larger of numerator and denominator. So $H(3/4)=4$, $H(355/113)=355$, $H(7)=H(7/1)=7$.

Why this and not, say, $|a|+|b|$ or $|a|\cdot|b|$? Two reasons: first, $\max$ is the only choice (up to constants) compatible with the product formula $\prod_v|x|_v=1$ for $x\in\mathbb{Q}^*$; second, $H$ has a uniformly bounded multiplicative behaviour under polynomial maps, which is what lets it propagate.

The naive height is the elementary input, but already a deep object: estimating "how many points of $\mathbb{P}^n(\mathbb{Q})$ have $H(P)\le X$" is an arithmetic statistics problem (Schanuel's theorem gives an asymptotic of the form $c_n X^{n+1}$). For us the more important question is qualitative — finiteness — and that requires the logarithmic form.

2. Logarithmic height and Northcott finiteness

Logs turn multiplicative bounds into additive ones, and turn the multiplicative product formula into a sum that we can analyse term-by-term. The logarithmic height is $$h(P)\;=\;\log H(P).$$ On $\mathbb{Q}$, this is just the number of decimal digits needed to write the point down (up to a constant factor). Extend it to $\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$ via places of any number field $K$ containing $P$: $$h(P)\;=\;\frac{1}{[K:\mathbb{Q}]}\sum_{v\in M_K}\log\max_i|x_i|_v.$$ This is independent of $K$ (extending the field doesn't change the value) and the right normalisation for a Galois-invariant function.

Northcott (1949). For every $B\ge 0$ and every integer $d\ge 1$, the set $$\{\,P\in\mathbb{P}^n(\bar{\mathbb{Q}})\;:\;h(P)\le B,\;\;[\mathbb{Q}(P):\mathbb{Q}]\le d\,\}$$ is finite.

The intuition: bounded height bounds the size of integer coordinates; bounded degree bounds the number of algebraic conjugates; together they bound the coefficients of the minimal polynomial of each coordinate, leaving only finitely many candidates. Dropping either bound breaks the theorem — the cyclotomic numbers $\zeta_m\in\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$ all have height $0$ but unbounded degree, and over $\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$ alone there are infinitely many low-height points.

Each $\mathbb{Z}$-pair $(a,b)$ with $\gcd=1$ and $\max(|a|,|b|)\le N$, modulo sign, gives one projective point. Count grows like $\frac{12}{\pi^2}N^2+O(N\log N)$ — Schanuel's $\mathbb{P}^1$ case.

Northcott is the engine behind every "finitely many points of bounded height and bounded degree" theorem in arithmetic geometry. It is sharp: over $\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$ alone, infinitely many roots of unity have $h=0$. The interplay between bounded height (controlling size) and bounded degree (controlling field of definition) is the inviolable input.

3. Weil heights from line bundles

The naive height lives on $\mathbb{P}^n$ and depends on the choice of homogeneous coordinates. To do geometry we want a height on each projective variety $X$, attached to geometric data — namely a line bundle $L$ on $X$.

The recipe: a very ample $L$ defines an embedding $\varphi_L:X\hookrightarrow\mathbb{P}^N$. Define $$h_{X,L}(P)\;=\;h(\varphi_L(P)).$$ A different choice of basis of global sections changes the embedding by a projective linear automorphism, which alters $h$ by an $O(1)$ — bounded function. So $h_{X,L}$ is well-defined up to a bounded function. Extend to general $L$ by writing $L=L_1\otimes L_2^{-1}$ with $L_1,L_2$ very ample: $h_{X,L}=h_{X,L_1}-h_{X,L_2}+O(1)$. The result is the Weil-height machine: $$\mathrm{Pic}(X)\;\to\;\{\text{functions }X(\bar K)\to\mathbb{R}\}\Big/O(1),\qquad L\mapsto h_{X,L}.$$ This is a homomorphism (tensor product becomes addition), and it is functorial: for any morphism $f:X\to Y$ and any $L\in\mathrm{Pic}(Y)$, $$h_{X,f^*L}\;=\;h_{Y,L}\circ f\;+\;O(1).$$

This is the universal way to attach a height to a polarised variety: pick a line bundle, get a height. Different bundles give genuinely different heights (e.g. an ample $L$ controls Northcott; a non-effective $L$ gives a function that need not be bounded below). Functoriality is the key payoff — it lets us track heights along morphisms, which is exactly what one needs to chase rational points across geometric constructions.

4. The Néron–Tate canonical height

On an abelian variety $A$ over a number field $K$ with a symmetric ample line bundle $L$ (e.g. on an elliptic curve, $L=2(O)$), the multiplication map $[n]:A\to A$ acts on heights by

$$h_L([n]P)\;=\;n^2\cdot h_L(P)\;+\;O(1).$$

The bounded-error term is annoying — the naive Weil height is not exactly quadratic. Tate's averaging trick kills it: $$\hat h(P)\;=\;\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{h_L([n]P)}{n^2}\;=\;\lim_{m\to\infty}\frac{h_L([2^m]P)}{4^m}.$$ The limit converges because the $O(1)$ telescopes geometrically. The resulting Néron–Tate canonical height is exactly quadratic:

  • $\hat h([n]P)=n^2\hat h(P)$ exactly, for every $n\in\mathbb{Z}$;
  • $\hat h(P+Q)+\hat h(P-Q)=2\hat h(P)+2\hat h(Q)$ — the parallelogram law;
  • $\hat h(P)\ge 0$ with equality iff $P\in A(K)_{\mathrm{tors}}$;
  • $\hat h=h_L+O(1)$, so Northcott still applies.

Polarising via $\langle P,Q\rangle=\tfrac{1}{2}(\hat h(P+Q)-\hat h(P)-\hat h(Q))$ produces a positive-definite symmetric bilinear pairing on $A(K)/\mathrm{tors}\otimes\mathbb{R}\cong\mathbb{R}^r$. The regulator is the Gram determinant on a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis $P_1,\dots,P_r$: $$R\;=\;\det\!\big(\langle P_i,P_j\rangle\big)_{i,j=1}^r.$$ This is the volume of the Mordell–Weil lattice, and it is one of the factors in the refined Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer formula.

5. Mahler measure and Lehmer's problem

The naive height of an algebraic number $\alpha\in\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$ is computed via its minimal polynomial, and the cleanest formula uses the Mahler measure. For $$f(x)=a\prod_{i=1}^{d}(x-\alpha_i)\;\in\;\mathbb{Z}[x]\qquad\text{(non-zero, primitive)},$$ define $$M(f)\;=\;|a|\,\prod_{i=1}^{d}\max(1,|\alpha_i|).$$ Equivalently, by Jensen's formula, $M(f)=\exp\!\left(\int_0^1\log|f(e^{2\pi i t})|\,dt\right)$ — a logarithmic average over the unit circle. The connection to heights: for an algebraic number $\alpha$ with minimal polynomial $f$, $$h(\alpha)\;=\;\frac{1}{\deg f}\log M(f).$$ So Mahler measure is the height, packaged via the polynomial.

Kronecker (1857). If $f\in\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is monic with $M(f)=1$, then every root of $f$ is a root of unity. So $M(f)=1$ characterises the cyclotomic part of the integers. Equivalently, an algebraic integer with $h(\alpha)=0$ is a root of unity.

Lehmer's question (1933). What is $\inf\{M(f):f\in\mathbb{Z}[x]\text{ monic, }M(f)>1\}$? Lehmer himself found $$L(x)=x^{10}+x^9-x^7-x^6-x^5-x^4-x^3+x+1,\qquad M(L)\approx 1.17628\,081\ldots$$ and no one has found a smaller value in the ninety years since. The question is wide open. A positive answer (a gap below $M(L)$) would have spectacular Diophantine consequences; a negative answer (Mahler measures arbitrarily close to $1$) would already be wildly surprising. Smyth proved the analogous gap for non-reciprocal polynomials, and Dobrowolski's lower bound $M(f)\ge 1+c\,(\log\log d/\log d)^3$ remains the best general bound.

6. Heights and the Mordell conjecture

The crowning Diophantine theorem of the twentieth century is

Faltings (1983, Mordell's conjecture, 1922). Let $C$ be a smooth projective curve of genus $g\ge 2$ over a number field $K$. Then $C(K)$ is finite.

This is a finiteness statement that fits naturally beside Northcott — but it does not follow from Northcott alone, because $C(K)$ can contain points of arbitrarily large height. The theorem says that despite this, only finitely many such points exist. The genus condition is sharp:

Where do heights enter? Faltings introduced his own Faltings height $h_F(A)$ on the moduli space of principally polarised abelian varieties of fixed dimension. A central step in his proof is a theorem of Northcott type:

Faltings' isogeny theorem. For each bound $C$, the set of isogeny classes of abelian varieties over $K$ of dimension $g$ with $h_F\le C$ is finite. Combined with semisimplicity of Tate modules and the Shafarevich conjecture for abelian varieties, this forces only finitely many curves of genus $g\ge 2$ with given good reduction set, and ultimately only finitely many rational points.

Vojta's later (1991) proof replaces the moduli detour with a direct height inequality on $C\times C$, of the form $\hat h_\Theta(P,Q)\le\varepsilon\,(\hat h(P)+\hat h(Q))+O(1)$, whose iteration forces $\hat h(P)$ to be bounded. The latter approach is the modern blueprint behind the Lang and Vojta conjectures.

7. Arakelov heights — a glimpse

From the perspective of $\Spec\mathbb{Z}$, every prime $p$ is a finite point and there is one missing "archimedean" point at infinity. Arakelov geometry compactifies $\Spec\mathbb{Z}$ by attaching that infinite point — and the data needed to describe it turns out to be a Hermitian metric on each line bundle over $X(\mathbb{C})$.

Concretely: a metrised line bundle $\bar L=(L,\|\cdot\|)$ on a regular projective arithmetic scheme $\bar X\to\Spec\mathbb{Z}$ is an algebraic line bundle plus a smooth Hermitian metric on its complex-analytic incarnation $L_\mathbb{C}$. With this data, Gillet–Soulé built an arithmetic intersection theory $\widehat{\mathrm{CH}}^*(\bar X)$ refining classical Chow theory; the arithmetic degree $$\widehat{\deg}:\widehat{\mathrm{CH}}^d(\bar X)\to\mathbb{R}\qquad(\dim\bar X=d)$$ takes values in $\mathbb{R}$ (not just $\mathbb{Z}$) — the metric data smooths the discrete intersection numbers across the archimedean fibre.

The key payoff for us: heights are intersection numbers in disguise. For a section $P:\Spec\mathcal{O}_K\to\bar X$ corresponding to a $K$-rational point, $$\hat h_{\bar L}(P)\;=\;\frac{1}{[K:\mathbb{Q}]}\,\widehat{\deg}\bigl(P^*\bar L\bigr).$$ The right-hand side decomposes as a sum over places $v\in M_K$ of local heights; the finite places give the usual $p$-adic intersection multiplicities, and the archimedean place contributes a Green's-function integral whose role is forced by the product formula.

This frame unifies every height in this topic. The naive height comes from $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}(1)$ with the Fubini–Study metric. The Néron–Tate height comes from a symmetric ample $L$ on an abelian scheme with the cubical metric supplied by the theta-function machinery. Even Faltings' height is an arithmetic degree, computed against the Hodge bundle on the moduli space of abelian varieties. Heights are not many objects — they are one object viewed at many compactifications.

Harmonic analysis & Fourier theory

Decompose a function into waves, watch what survives. From the Hilbert basis $\{e^{2\pi inx}\}$ on the circle to the self-dual Gaussian on $\mathbb{R}$, and onward to Schwartz distributions, the uncertainty principle, and Pontryagin duality on locally compact abelian groups.

1. Fourier series on the circle

Let $\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$ be the circle of circumference $1$. The exponentials

$$e_n(x) := e^{2\pi i n x},\qquad n\in\mathbb{Z}$$

are continuous, periodic, and pairwise orthogonal in $L^2(\mathbb{T})$:

$$\langle e_m, e_n\rangle = \int_0^1 e^{2\pi i (m-n)x}\,dx = \delta_{mn}.$$

Stone–Weierstrass says trigonometric polynomials are dense in $C(\mathbb{T})$, hence in $L^2(\mathbb{T})$. So $\{e_n\}_{n\in\mathbb{Z}}$ is an orthonormal Hilbert basis of $L^2(\mathbb{T})$, and every $f\in L^2(\mathbb{T})$ has the expansion

$$f(x) \;=\; \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} \hat f(n)\, e_n(x),\qquad \hat f(n) = \int_0^1 f(x)\, e^{-2\pi i n x}\,dx,$$

convergent in $L^2$. Pythagoras becomes Parseval / Plancherel:

$$\int_0^1 |f(x)|^2\,dx \;=\; \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} |\hat f(n)|^2.$$

Pointwise convergence is harder than $L^2$ convergence

For $f\in L^2(\mathbb{T})$, $S_N f \to f$ in $L^2$. The deeper question — pointwise a.e. — is the celebrated Carleson theorem (1966): for $f\in L^2(\mathbb{T})$, $S_N f(x) \to f(x)$ for almost every $x$. Hunt later extended this to $L^p$ for $p>1$. In sharp contrast Kolmogorov constructed an $f\in L^1(\mathbb{T})$ whose Fourier series diverges everywhere — the $L^1$ side of the dichotomy is genuinely worse.

Smoothness $\to$ decay. If $f\in C^k(\mathbb{T})$ then $\hat f(n) = O(|n|^{-k})$ — integration by parts $k$ times. Conversely, sufficiently fast decay of $\hat f(n)$ forces $f$ to have many derivatives. This is the prototype of the Schwartz duality we'll exploit on $\mathbb{R}$.

2. Fourier transform on $\mathbb{R}$

On the line we can no longer index by $\mathbb{Z}$ — the dual variable $\xi$ is itself continuous. With our chosen normalization (no extra $2\pi$ factors floating around), define

$$\hat f(\xi) \;:=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} f(x)\, e^{-2\pi i \xi x}\,dx,\qquad f\in L^1(\mathbb{R}).$$

This integral converges absolutely; by dominated convergence, $\hat f$ is continuous. The Riemann–Lebesgue lemma adds that $\hat f(\xi)\to 0$ as $|\xi|\to\infty$. So the transform sends $L^1(\mathbb{R})$ continuously into $C_0(\mathbb{R})$, with $\|\hat f\|_\infty \le \|f\|_1$. (The image is dense but not all of $C_0$; surjectivity fails for subtle reasons.)

The "differentiation $\leftrightarrow$ multiplication by polynomial" interchange is the engine that turns linear constant-coefficient PDEs into algebraic equations on the Fourier side.

Inversion and Plancherel

If $f$ and $\hat f$ are both in $L^1$, the inversion formula

$$f(x) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} \hat f(\xi)\, e^{2\pi i \xi x}\,d\xi$$

recovers $f$ from its transform. The harder content is Plancherel: although the Fourier transform is initially defined only on $L^1$, it extends uniquely to a unitary isomorphism

$$\mathcal{F}\colon L^2(\mathbb{R}) \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} L^2(\mathbb{R}),\qquad \|\hat f\|_2 = \|f\|_2.$$

The route is to first prove this on $L^1\cap L^2$ — where both definitions agree — then density-extend. With Plancherel, $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ becomes the natural home for spectral analysis.

3. Convolution and the convolution theorem

Define the convolution of $f, g\in L^1(\mathbb{R})$ by

$$(f*g)(x) \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} f(y)\, g(x-y)\,dy.$$

By Fubini–Tonelli the integral converges a.e. and $\|f*g\|_1 \le \|f\|_1\,\|g\|_1$ (Young's inequality at the corner $p=q=r=1$). Convolution is commutative, associative, and bilinear; equipped with $*$, $L^1(\mathbb{R})$ becomes a commutative Banach algebra.

The convolution theorem is the algebraic shadow of all this:

$$\widehat{f*g}(\xi) \;=\; \hat f(\xi)\,\hat g(\xi).$$

Proof: substitute and swap the order of integration (Fubini), then change variables $y\mapsto y$ and $x-y \mapsto z$. The dual identity $\widehat{fg} = \hat f * \hat g$ also holds (with appropriate hypotheses on $f, g$), so the Fourier transform swaps the two operations.

Why $L^1$ has no unit

If $L^1(\mathbb{R})$ had a unit $e$ for convolution, then $\hat e \cdot \hat f = \hat f$ for every $f$, forcing $\hat e \equiv 1$. But $\hat e \in C_0$ (Riemann–Lebesgue), so $\hat e \equiv 1$ is impossible. The convolution unit is morally the Dirac delta $\delta$ — a tempered distribution, not an $L^1$ function. We approximate it via approximate identities: families $\varphi_\varepsilon(x) = \varepsilon^{-1}\varphi(x/\varepsilon)$ with $\int\varphi = 1$ supported in shrinking neighborhoods, satisfying $\varphi_\varepsilon * f \to f$ in $L^p$ (and pointwise a.e.) as $\varepsilon\to 0^+$.

Two faces of multiplication. On the function side, multiplication $fg$ is pointwise. On the Fourier side, multiplication $\hat f\,\hat g$ is also pointwise — but it corresponds to convolution on the function side. So "what is multiplication?" depends on which world you are in. The Banach algebra $(L^1, *)$ and the algebra $C_0$ under pointwise product are intertwined by $\mathcal{F}$.

4. Schwartz space and tempered distributions

The Fourier transform behaves cleanly on $L^1$ and $L^2$, but neither space is closed under both differentiation and multiplication by polynomials. We want a single space that is, and on which $\mathcal{F}$ is an automorphism. That space is Schwartz space:

$$\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n) \;=\; \{f\in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n) : \|f\|_{\alpha,\beta} := \sup_{x}|x^\alpha\,\partial^\beta f(x)| < \infty\quad\forall \alpha,\beta\}.$$

Smooth, with every derivative decaying faster than every polynomial. Examples: $e^{-\pi x^2}$ and any polynomial times a Gaussian; non-examples: $1/(1+x^2)$ (only $C^\infty$, but decays only like $x^{-2}$).

The relations

$$\widehat{x^\alpha f} = \tfrac{1}{(-2\pi i)^{|\alpha|}}\partial^\alpha \hat f,\qquad \widehat{\partial^\beta f} = (2\pi i\xi)^\beta \hat f$$

show $\mathcal{F}$ maps each seminorm $\|\cdot\|_{\alpha,\beta}$ continuously to $\|\cdot\|_{\beta,\alpha}$. Combined with the inversion formula on $\mathcal{S}$, this gives:

Theorem (Schwartz). $\mathcal{F}\colon \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n) \to \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ is a topological isomorphism with $\mathcal{F}^4 = \mathrm{id}$.

Tempered distributions

The tempered distributions $\mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R}^n)$ are continuous linear functionals on $\mathcal{S}$. Every $f\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}$ of polynomial growth defines a tempered distribution via $\langle f,\varphi\rangle = \int f\varphi$, but $\mathcal{S}'$ contains much more:

The Fourier transform extends to $\mathcal{S}'$ by duality:

$$\langle \hat T, \varphi\rangle \;:=\; \langle T, \hat\varphi\rangle.$$

This is the right setting for the inversion formula on $L^2$ (where $f$ may not be in $L^1$), for $\widehat{\partial^\alpha f} = (2\pi i \xi)^\alpha \hat f$ to hold for tempered distributions, and for Sobolev spaces $H^s = \{f\in\mathcal{S}': (1+|\xi|^2)^{s/2}\hat f \in L^2\}$ to make sense for any real $s$.

5. Heisenberg uncertainty

You cannot simultaneously concentrate $f$ and $\hat f$. Any attempt to localize one spreads the other.

Heisenberg's inequality. For $f\in\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R})$ with $\|f\|_2 = 1$, $$\Big(\int x^2|f(x)|^2\,dx\Big)^{1/2}\;\Big(\int \xi^2|\hat f(\xi)|^2\,d\xi\Big)^{1/2}\;\ge\;\frac{1}{4\pi}.$$ Equality iff $f$ is a Gaussian $f(x) = c\,e^{-\pi a x^2}$ for some $a>0$.

The proof is a one-line application of Cauchy–Schwarz to the operator identity

$$\left[\frac{d}{dx},\, x\right] = 1,\qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad \tfrac{d}{dx}(xf) - x f' = f,$$

integrated against $\bar f$ and combined with $\widehat{f'} = 2\pi i \xi \hat f$ and Plancherel. The constant $1/(4\pi)$ comes out of the $2\pi$ in the Fourier exponent — change conventions and you change the constant, but the inequality and its physical content stand.

Reading: position / momentum

Think of $|f(x)|^2$ as a position density and $|\hat f(\xi)|^2$ (which is also a probability density, by Plancherel and $\|f\|_2 = 1$) as a "momentum" density. Their standard deviations $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_\xi$ satisfy

$$\sigma_x\,\sigma_\xi \;\ge\; \frac{1}{4\pi}.$$

Heisenberg's $\hbar/2$ in physics is the same statement under the convention $\hat f(p) = \int f(x) e^{-ipx/\hbar}\,dx/\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}$.

6. Poisson summation

One identity binds Fourier series and the Fourier transform on $\mathbb{R}$ — and binds the function on $\mathbb{R}$ to its periodization on $\mathbb{T}$.

Poisson summation. For Schwartz $f\in\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R})$, $$\sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} f(n) \;=\; \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} \hat f(n).$$ More generally, $\sum_n f(x + n) = \sum_n \hat f(n)\,e^{2\pi i n x}$.

Proof sketch: define $F(x) = \sum_n f(x+n)$ on $\mathbb{T}$. Schwartz decay makes the sum and all its derivatives uniformly convergent, so $F\in C^\infty(\mathbb{T})$. Compute its Fourier coefficient:

$$\hat F(k) = \int_0^1 F(x) e^{-2\pi i k x}\,dx = \int_{\mathbb{R}} f(x) e^{-2\pi i k x}\,dx = \hat f(k).$$

The Fourier expansion $F(x) = \sum_k \hat F(k) e^{2\pi i k x} = \sum_k \hat f(k) e^{2\pi i k x}$ is the second identity above; setting $x=0$ gives the first.

The theta function and $\zeta(s)$

Apply Poisson summation to $f_t(x) = e^{-\pi t x^2}$, which has $\hat f_t(\xi) = t^{-1/2} e^{-\pi \xi^2/t}$. The result is the modular transformation of Jacobi's theta function

$$\theta(t) := \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} e^{-\pi n^2 t},\qquad \theta(1/t) \;=\; \sqrt t\, \theta(t),\quad t > 0.$$

Riemann's 1859 derivation of the functional equation $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$ for $\xi(s) = \pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s)$ uses exactly this. Poisson summation is the analytic seed of all such functional equations — see Dirichlet series & Euler products for the L-function story.

Sampling theorem

If $\hat f$ is supported in $[-W, W]$ (band-limited), Poisson summation on the dilated $f(x/2W)$ recovers the Shannon–Nyquist sampling theorem: $f$ is determined by its values on the lattice $\frac{1}{2W}\mathbb{Z}$, with explicit reconstruction

$$f(x) = \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} f\!\left(\frac{n}{2W}\right) \mathrm{sinc}\big(2W x - n\big).$$

Lower the sampling rate and you get aliasing — the high frequencies fold back onto the lattice in exactly the Poisson-summation pattern.

7. Heat kernel and the Gaussian eigenfunction

The Gaussian $g(x) = e^{-\pi x^2}$ is the most important function in Fourier analysis. It satisfies

$$\hat g \;=\; g.$$

The Gaussian is its own Fourier transform: an eigenfunction of $\mathcal{F}$ with eigenvalue $1$. (Proof: $g$ solves $g' + 2\pi x g = 0$; differentiating $\hat g$ via $\widehat{xf}$ gives $\hat g$ solving the same ODE, and matching values at $0$.) Together with translates, dilates, and Hermite polynomials, this seeds an orthonormal basis of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ on which $\mathcal{F}$ has eigenvalues $\{1, -i, -1, i\}$ — and thus $\mathcal{F}^4 = \mathrm{id}$ globally on $L^2$.

Heat equation as multiplication on the Fourier side

Consider the heat equation on $\mathbb{R}$ with initial datum $u(0,\cdot) = f$:

$$\partial_t u \;=\; \partial_x^2 u,\qquad x\in\mathbb{R},\ t>0.$$

Apply $\mathcal{F}$ in $x$:

$$\partial_t \hat u(t,\xi) \;=\; -4\pi^2 \xi^2\, \hat u(t, \xi),\qquad \hat u(0,\xi) = \hat f(\xi).$$

This is a pointwise ODE in $\xi$. Solution: $\hat u(t,\xi) = e^{-4\pi^2 t \xi^2}\,\hat f(\xi)$. Inverting:

$$u(t, x) \;=\; (K_t * f)(x),\qquad K_t(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi t}}\, e^{-x^2/(4t)}.$$

$K_t$ is the heat kernel: a Gaussian of standard deviation $\sigma = \sqrt{2t}$, with mass $1$ for every $t>0$ and $K_t \to \delta$ as $t \to 0^+$. So the heat semigroup $e^{t\partial_x^2}$ is convolution with $K_t$, which on the Fourier side is multiplication by $e^{-4\pi^2 t \xi^2}$ — exponential damping of high frequencies.

Probability connection. The same kernel $K_t$ is the density of standard Brownian motion at time $2t$. Convolution with $K_t$ in PDE land is taking expectation over a Brownian step in probability land — see probability theory for the random-walk picture.

8. Pontryagin duality (teaser)

Fourier series on $\mathbb{T}$ and the Fourier transform on $\mathbb{R}$ are not two theorems — they are one theorem in two key signatures. The unifying frame is Pontryagin duality on locally compact abelian (LCA) groups.

For $G$ an LCA group, the dual group is

$$\widehat G \;=\; \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{cts}}(G,\, U(1)) \;=\; \{\text{continuous group homomorphisms } \chi\colon G\to U(1)\}.$$

Endow $\widehat G$ with the compact-open topology. It is also LCA, and the canonical map $G\to\widehat{\widehat G}$, $g\mapsto (\chi\mapsto \chi(g))$, is a topological group isomorphism. The classical Fourier theorems are special cases:

$G$$\widehat G$"Fourier transform"
$\mathbb{R}$$\mathbb{R}$ (self-dual via $\chi_\xi(x) = e^{2\pi i \xi x}$)continuous Fourier transform
$\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$$\mathbb{Z}$ (via $\chi_n(x) = e^{2\pi i n x}$)Fourier series: $\hat f \in \ell^2(\mathbb{Z})$
$\mathbb{Z}$$\mathbb{T}$discrete-time Fourier transform
$\mathbb{Z}/N$$\mathbb{Z}/N$ (self-dual finite)discrete Fourier transform (DFT)
$\mathbb{Q}_p$$\mathbb{Q}_p$ (self-dual via additive characters)$p$-adic Fourier analysis
$\mathbb{A}_K = \prod'_v K_v$ (adèles)$\mathbb{A}_K$ (self-dual)adelic harmonic analysis (Tate's thesis)

The Plancherel theorem says: with appropriately compatible Haar measures on $G$ and $\widehat G$, the Fourier transform $\mathcal{F}\colon L^1(G)\cap L^2(G) \to L^2(\widehat G)$ extends to a unitary isomorphism $L^2(G)\xrightarrow{\sim} L^2(\widehat G)$. Setting $G = \mathbb{R}$ recovers classical Plancherel; $G = \mathbb{T}$ recovers Parseval; $G = \mathbb{Z}/N$ is unitarity of the DFT matrix divided by $\sqrt N$.

Looking ahead. Tate (1950) used Pontryagin duality on the adèles $\mathbb{A}_K$ to give a unified, conceptual proof of the analytic continuation and functional equation of Hecke L-functions — the same Poisson-summation idea (§6) lifted to LCA groups. This is the bridge from harmonic analysis to modern automorphic forms.

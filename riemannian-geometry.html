Riemannian geometry — metrics, connections, curvature

A smooth manifold carries no intrinsic geometry; the choice of a Riemannian metric gives one. From this single extra datum flow lengths, angles, geodesics, parallel transport, and a rich curvature tensor that decides whether triangles have too much or too little angle.

1. From surfaces to manifolds

The companion page differential geometry develops the geometry of curves and surfaces in $\mathbb{R}^3$: the first fundamental form $I$, the shape operator and second fundamental form, the Gauss map, and Gauss–Bonnet. The objects there are embedded in an ambient Euclidean space, and the metric is inherited by restriction. Those preliminaries are assumed here.

We now drop the ambient space. A smooth manifold $M$ of dimension $n$ is a topological space locally modelled on $\mathbb{R}^n$ via smoothly compatible charts. At each point $p\in M$ there is a tangent space $T_p M$, a real vector space of dimension $n$, constructed without reference to an embedding — as derivations on germs of smooth functions, or equivalence classes of curves through $p$. A smooth manifold carries no intrinsic geometry: tangent vectors at different points cannot be compared, curves have no canonical notion of length, triangles have no angles.

The extra datum. A Riemannian metric $g$ on $M$ is a smooth choice, for each $p\in M$, of a positive-definite symmetric bilinear form $$g_p : T_p M \times T_p M \to \mathbb{R}.$$ Equivalently, $g$ is a smooth section of $\operatorname{Sym}^2 T^*M$ that is pointwise positive-definite. The pair $(M,g)$ is a Riemannian manifold.

With $g$ in hand, each tangent space becomes a Euclidean vector space: vectors have lengths $\|v\|_g = \sqrt{g_p(v,v)}$, and pairs have angles $\cos\theta = g_p(u,v)/(\|u\|\|v\|)$. Smooth curves acquire lengths by integration: $$L(\gamma) = \int_a^b \sqrt{g_{\gamma(t)}(\dot\gamma(t),\dot\gamma(t))}\,dt,$$ and the Riemannian distance is the infimum $$d_g(p,q) = \inf\{ L(\gamma) : \gamma \text{ a piecewise smooth curve from } p \text{ to } q \}.$$ The Hopf–Rinow theorem asserts that on a connected, geodesically complete manifold this infimum is attained by a length-minimizing geodesic.

The fundamental distinction from the surfaces story: only the intrinsic data matter. A surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ and the same surface bent isometrically to a new embedding carry the same $(M,g)$; the embedding was a crutch. Gauss's Theorema Egregium — that the Gauss curvature of a surface depends only on the first fundamental form — is the first hint, and Riemannian geometry is its systematic generalization.

2. The metric tensor

In a chart $(U,x^1,\dots,x^n)$, the coordinate vector fields $\partial_i = \partial/\partial x^i$ form a basis of each tangent space over $U$. The metric is determined by its matrix of components $$g_{ij}(p) = g_p(\partial_i, \partial_j), \qquad g = g_{ij}\, dx^i\otimes dx^j,$$ a symmetric positive-definite matrix varying smoothly with $p$. Einstein summation is in force. Under a change of coordinates $y^a = y^a(x^1,\dots,x^n)$ the components transform as a $(0,2)$-tensor: $$\tilde g_{ab} = \frac{\partial x^i}{\partial y^a}\frac{\partial x^j}{\partial y^b}\, g_{ij}.$$ The inverse matrix is written $g^{ij}$ and used to raise indices; $(g_{ij})$ and $(g^{ij})$ are dual in the sense $g^{ik}g_{kj} = \delta^i_j$.

The sphere's metric in $(\theta,\varphi)$ is $\operatorname{diag}(1,\sin^2\theta)$: it is positive-definite except at the poles, where the chart itself breaks down. The half-plane is the Poincaré model of hyperbolic geometry: as $y\to 0^+$ the metric blows up, so points near the real axis are infinitely far away. The flat torus is a compact example with zero curvature despite being topologically nontrivial.

3. The Levi-Civita connection

To differentiate a vector field $Y$ in the direction of another vector field $X$ we need a rule for comparing tangent vectors at nearby points — a connection. Formally, a connection on $TM$ is an $\mathbb{R}$-bilinear map $\nabla : \mathfrak{X}(M)\times\mathfrak{X}(M)\to\mathfrak{X}(M)$, $(X,Y)\mapsto\nabla_X Y$, satisfying $$\nabla_{fX}Y = f\,\nabla_X Y,\qquad \nabla_X(fY) = X(f)\,Y + f\,\nabla_X Y,$$ for $f\in C^\infty(M)$. The first law says $\nabla_X Y|_p$ depends only on $X_p$ (not on the values of $X$ away from $p$); the second is a Leibniz rule.

There are infinitely many connections on a manifold. The metric $g$ picks out exactly one:

Fundamental theorem of Riemannian geometry. On any Riemannian manifold $(M,g)$ there is a unique connection $\nabla$ satisfying
  1. metric compatibility: $X\bigl(g(Y,Z)\bigr) = g(\nabla_X Y, Z) + g(Y, \nabla_X Z)$;
  2. torsion-freeness: $\nabla_X Y - \nabla_Y X = [X,Y]$.
This $\nabla$ is the Levi-Civita connection. It is written in coordinates via the Christoffel symbols $$\Gamma^k_{ij} = \tfrac12 g^{k\ell}\bigl(\partial_i g_{j\ell} + \partial_j g_{i\ell} - \partial_\ell g_{ij}\bigr),\qquad \nabla_{\partial_i}\partial_j = \Gamma^k_{ij}\,\partial_k.$$

Existence. The Koszul formula, obtained by cyclically combining the metric-compatibility identity, gives $$2\, g(\nabla_X Y, Z) = X g(Y,Z) + Y g(X,Z) - Z g(X,Y) + g([X,Y],Z) - g([X,Z],Y) - g([Y,Z],X).$$ The right side depends only on $g$ and the vector fields, so $\nabla_X Y$ is well-defined and unique. Substituting $X=\partial_i$, $Y=\partial_j$, $Z=\partial_\ell$ and using $[\partial_i,\partial_j]=0$ yields the formula for the Christoffels.

The Christoffels are not tensorial: under a change of coordinates a second-derivative term appears, $$\tilde\Gamma^c_{ab} = \frac{\partial y^c}{\partial x^k}\frac{\partial x^i}{\partial y^a}\frac{\partial x^j}{\partial y^b}\Gamma^k_{ij} + \frac{\partial y^c}{\partial x^k}\frac{\partial^2 x^k}{\partial y^a \partial y^b}.$$ At any single point they can be killed by a clever choice of chart — the normal coordinates — even though the curvature they encode cannot.

Parallel transport

Given a smooth curve $\gamma:[0,1]\to M$, a vector field $V$ along $\gamma$ is parallel if its covariant derivative vanishes, $$\frac{DV}{dt} = \nabla_{\dot\gamma}V = 0,\qquad \dot V^k + \Gamma^k_{ij}\,\dot\gamma^i V^j = 0.$$ This is a linear first-order ODE in the components of $V$; given $V(0)\in T_{\gamma(0)}M$, there is a unique parallel $V(t)\in T_{\gamma(t)}M$. Parallel transport $P_\gamma : T_{\gamma(0)}M \to T_{\gamma(1)}M$ is a linear isometry (by metric compatibility) — but it depends on the path $\gamma$. The failure to be path-independent is the curvature.

On a closed loop $\gamma$ at $p$, the isometry $P_\gamma : T_p M \to T_p M$ is the holonomy of $\gamma$. On the round sphere, parallel transporting a vector around a loop rotates it by an angle equal to the enclosed solid area. This is the first visible manifestation of curvature.

On the unit sphere, the holonomy angle equals the oriented spherical area enclosed by the loop (Gauss–Bonnet for a simply connected region: $\iint K\,dA = \text{area}$ since $K=1$).

4. Geodesics

Among all curves between two points, a geodesic is one that is "auto-parallel": its velocity is parallel-transported along itself, $$\nabla_{\dot\gamma}\dot\gamma = 0.$$ In coordinates this is the system of second-order ODEs $$\ddot\gamma^k + \Gamma^k_{ij}\,\dot\gamma^i\dot\gamma^j = 0.$$ Standard ODE theory gives, for every initial condition $(p,v)\in TM$, a unique geodesic $\gamma_v(t)$ with $\gamma_v(0)=p$, $\dot\gamma_v(0)=v$, defined on a maximal interval about $0$.

Geodesics are also the critical points of the length functional (or, equivalently and more conveniently, the energy functional) — so length-minimizing curves, when they exist, are geodesics. The converse fails globally: a great circle on $S^2$ is a geodesic, but only the shorter arc is length-minimizing.

The exponential map. For $p\in M$ and small $v\in T_p M$ define $$\exp_p(v) = \gamma_v(1).$$ This is a local diffeomorphism from a neighbourhood of $0\in T_p M$ onto a neighbourhood of $p$ in $M$. Its inverse gives normal coordinates — coordinates in which, at $p$, the metric is Euclidean to first order and the Christoffels vanish, even though they do not vanish nearby.

Geodesics in the three examples

  • Sphere $S^2$. Geodesics are great circles. Any geodesic starting at $p$ with unit speed returns to $p$ at time $2\pi$.
  • Hyperbolic half-plane $\mathbb{H}^2$. Geodesics are either vertical lines $\{x=c\}$ or semicircles with both endpoints on the boundary axis $\{y=0\}$. They are infinite in both directions.
  • Flat torus $T^2$. Geodesics are images of straight lines in $\mathbb{R}^2$ under the covering map. A line of rational slope closes up into a finite loop; a line of irrational slope is dense.

5. The Riemann curvature tensor

On Euclidean space, mixed partial derivatives commute: $\partial_i\partial_j = \partial_j\partial_i$. On a curved manifold, the covariant derivatives $\nabla_X\nabla_Y$ do not commute. Their failure is measured by the Riemann curvature tensor.

Riemann curvature tensor. For vector fields $X,Y,Z$, $$R(X,Y)Z = \nabla_X \nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y \nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X,Y]}Z.$$ One checks (in spite of appearances) that $R(X,Y)Z_p$ is tensorial — depending only on the values of $X,Y,Z$ at $p$.

In coordinates $$R^\ell_{ijk} = \partial_i \Gamma^\ell_{jk} - \partial_j \Gamma^\ell_{ik} + \Gamma^\ell_{im}\Gamma^m_{jk} - \Gamma^\ell_{jm}\Gamma^m_{ik},$$ and with the metric we lower an index to get a $(0,4)$-tensor $$R_{ijk\ell} = g_{\ell m} R^m_{ijk}.$$ This $(0,4)$-tensor enjoys the symmetries $$R_{ijk\ell} = -R_{jik\ell} = -R_{ij\ell k}, \qquad R_{ijk\ell} = R_{k\ell ij}, \qquad R_{ijk\ell}+R_{ikj\ell}+R_{ij\ell k}\text{ (first Bianchi)}.$$

Sectional curvature

The curvature tensor is an object of bewildering size — a $(1,3)$-tensor with up to $n^2(n^2-1)/12$ independent components at each point. A single number captures the "amount" of curvature along a $2$-plane $\Pi\subset T_pM$:

Sectional curvature. Given a plane $\Pi\subset T_pM$ with basis $\{u,v\}$, $$K(\Pi) = \frac{g(R(u,v)v, u)}{g(u,u)\,g(v,v) - g(u,v)^2}.$$ This depends only on $\Pi$, not the chosen basis. In dimension $2$, $K$ equals the Gauss curvature.

The Riemann tensor is determined, as a function on $2$-planes, by its sectional curvatures: if $K$ is known at every plane in $T_pM$, then $R$ is determined at $p$.

Ricci and scalar curvature

Two useful contractions are:

  • Ricci tensor: $\operatorname{Ric}_{ij} = R^k_{kij} = g^{k\ell} R_{k i \ell j}$. Equivalently, $\operatorname{Ric}(v,v) = (n-1)\cdot(\text{average of $K$ over planes containing }v)$.
  • Scalar curvature: $S = g^{ij}\operatorname{Ric}_{ij}$. For $n\ge 2$, $S(p) = n(n-1)\cdot(\text{average of $K$ over all planes at }p)$.

The Ricci tensor controls the infinitesimal rate at which small geodesic balls shrink relative to Euclidean: in normal coordinates, $\sqrt{\det g}(x) = 1 - \frac{1}{6}\operatorname{Ric}_{ij}x^i x^j + O(|x|^3)$. Einstein's equations of general relativity are a condition on the Ricci tensor of a Lorentzian manifold.

For a $2$-manifold there is only one plane at each point: the sectional curvature is $K$. For constant-curvature spaces, $R(u,v)w = K\bigl(g(v,w)u - g(u,w)v\bigr)$, so $K(\Pi) = K$ for every plane.

6. Model spaces of constant curvature

A Riemannian manifold has constant sectional curvature $\kappa$ if $K(\Pi) = \kappa$ for every $p\in M$ and every tangent plane $\Pi\subset T_pM$. In dimension $\ge 3$, by Schur's lemma, pointwise-constant already implies globally constant. The complete simply-connected model spaces are, up to isometry:

$\kappa$modelcomment
$+1$sphere $S^n\subset\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$compact; finite volume; every pair of geodesics meets
$0$Euclidean $\mathbb{R}^n$flat; geodesics are straight lines; parallel postulate holds
$-1$hyperbolic $\mathbb{H}^n$non-compact; volume grows exponentially with radius

Every complete simply connected Riemannian manifold of constant curvature $\kappa$ is isometric to one of these (rescaled by $1/\sqrt{|\kappa|}$ if $\kappa\neq 0$). Removing simple-connectedness produces the space forms, obtained as quotients by discrete isometry groups: lens spaces, tori, and hyperbolic manifolds such as closed surfaces of genus $\ge 2$.

Geodesic triangles and angle sum

In Euclidean plane geometry the angles of a triangle sum to $\pi$. On a curved surface the sum deviates, and Gauss–Bonnet for a simply-connected geodesic triangle $\Delta$ says exactly $$\sum_{i=1}^3 \theta_i - \pi = \iint_\Delta K\, dA.$$ In particular, on a constant-curvature surface with $K = \kappa$, the excess $\sum\theta_i - \pi = \kappa\cdot \operatorname{area}(\Delta)$. Positive excess → $\kappa>0$ (sphere); zero → flat; negative → $\kappa<0$ (hyperbolic).

The companion page treats Gauss–Bonnet in the surface-in-$\mathbb{R}^3$ setting. The intrinsic version given here is the prototype of the Chern–Gauss–Bonnet theorem in all even dimensions.

7. Jacobi fields

A geodesic $\gamma:[0,L]\to M$ does not live alone: it sits inside a family of nearby geodesics, parameterized by $s$: $\gamma_s(t)$ with $\gamma_0 = \gamma$. The variation vector field $$J(t) = \left.\frac{\partial \gamma_s(t)}{\partial s}\right|_{s=0}$$ is a vector field along $\gamma$ that measures the infinitesimal separation between $\gamma$ and its neighbours. Differentiating the geodesic equation with respect to $s$ gives the Jacobi equation:

$$\nabla_t \nabla_t J + R(J, \dot\gamma)\dot\gamma = 0.$$

This is a linear second-order ODE for $J$ along $\gamma$; the space of solutions has dimension $2n$. A vector field $J$ satisfying this equation is a Jacobi field. The curvature term is what couples the geodesic spread to the geometry: positive curvature pulls neighbouring geodesics together, negative curvature pushes them apart.

Constant curvature case

On a space of constant sectional curvature $\kappa$, if $J$ is perpendicular to $\dot\gamma$ and $|\dot\gamma|=1$, the Jacobi equation reduces to the scalar ODE $|J|'' = -\kappa\, |J|$, whose solutions are $$|J|(t) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\kappa}}\sin(\sqrt{\kappa}\,t) & \kappa>0\\ t & \kappa=0\\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{-\kappa}}\sinh(\sqrt{-\kappa}\,t) & \kappa<0 \end{cases}\qquad (\text{with }|J|(0)=0,\ |J|'(0)=1).$$ Positive curvature gives sinusoidal behaviour — neighbours refocus; flat gives linear growth; negative gives exponential divergence.

Conjugate points

A point $\gamma(t_0)$ with $t_0>0$ is conjugate to $\gamma(0)$ along $\gamma$ if there exists a nontrivial Jacobi field $J$ along $\gamma$ with $J(0)=J(t_0)=0$. At a conjugate point, nearby geodesics starting at $\gamma(0)$ meet infinitesimally with $\gamma$. On $S^n$ the antipodal point to $\gamma(0)$ is conjugate at distance $\pi$; beyond the first conjugate point the geodesic is no longer length-minimizing.

8. Comparison theorems

A great deal of Riemannian geometry is the strategy: bound the curvature, deduce global geometric consequences. Three classical results:

Bonnet–Myers theorem. If $(M,g)$ is a complete Riemannian manifold of dimension $n$ with $$\operatorname{Ric}(v,v) \ge (n-1)\kappa\, g(v,v)$$ for some $\kappa>0$, then $M$ is compact, and $\operatorname{diam}(M)\le \pi/\sqrt{\kappa}$. The fundamental group is finite.

Positive Ricci curvature forces neighbouring geodesics to refocus sooner than $\pi/\sqrt{\kappa}$ — the sphere's conjugate-point phenomenon, in disguise. A manifold that doesn't close off can't have uniformly positive Ricci.

Synge's theorem. A compact, orientable, even-dimensional Riemannian manifold with positive sectional curvature is simply-connected. (In particular, $S^2$ and $\mathbb{CP}^n$ are simply-connected; $\mathbb{RP}^{2n}$ is ruled out as a space of positive sectional curvature.)
Cartan–Hadamard theorem. A complete, simply-connected Riemannian manifold with non-positive sectional curvature is diffeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$ via $\exp_p : T_p M \to M$ (for any $p$). There are no conjugate points; geodesics between any two points are unique.

9. Ricci curvature and Einstein manifolds

Section 5 introduced the Ricci tensor $\operatorname{Ric}_{ij} = g^{k\ell} R_{kij\ell}$ as a trace of the Riemann tensor. This section treats it on its own. Fix $p\in M$ and let $\{e_1,\ldots,e_n\}$ be an orthonormal basis of $T_pM$ with $e_1 = v/\|v\|$. Then $$\operatorname{Ric}(v,v) = \|v\|^2 \sum_{i=2}^{n} K(\mathrm{span}\{v,e_i\}) = (n-1)\,\|v\|^2\,\overline{K}(v),$$ where $\overline{K}(v)$ is the average sectional curvature over $2$-planes containing $v$. Ricci thus loses the fine directional information of the full Riemann tensor but retains the directional average relevant for volume comparison.

Geometric meaning. In geodesic normal coordinates centred at $p$, $$\sqrt{\det g}(x) = 1 - \tfrac{1}{6}\operatorname{Ric}_{ij}(p)\,x^i x^j + O(|x|^3).$$ So positive Ricci in the direction $v$ means small geodesic balls are smaller than Euclidean balls along $v$; negative Ricci means larger. This is what drives Bishop–Gromov volume comparison (§8), and it is why the Ricci tensor — not the full Riemann tensor — is the right gadget for Bonnet–Myers.

The further contraction $S = g^{ij}\operatorname{Ric}_{ij}$ gives the scalar curvature, and in dimension $n\ge 2$ equals $n(n-1)$ times the average of $K$ over all tangent $2$-planes at $p$. On the round $n$-sphere of radius $1$ with $K\equiv 1$, this reads $S = n(n-1)$.

Einstein manifolds. $(M,g)$ is Einstein if $$\operatorname{Ric} = \lambda\, g$$ for some constant $\lambda\in\mathbb{R}$. Taking traces gives $\lambda = S/n$. Every constant-sectional-curvature space is Einstein; on closed manifolds of dimension $n\ge 3$, Einstein metrics are the critical points of the normalized total scalar curvature $g\mapsto \operatorname{Vol}(M,g)^{(2-n)/n}\int_M S\,d\mathrm{vol}_g$. In Lorentzian signature, the vacuum Einstein field equation $\operatorname{Ric} - \tfrac12 S\,g = 0$ (equivalently $\operatorname{Ric}=0$, i.e. Ricci-flat) is the geometric content of general relativity without sources.

Two recurring examples beyond constant curvature: the Fubini–Study metric on $\mathbb{CP}^n$ is Einstein with positive scalar curvature; Calabi–Yau manifolds are compact Kähler manifolds with $\operatorname{Ric}=0$.

10. Riemannian volume form and divergence

A Riemannian metric on an oriented $n$-manifold distinguishes a canonical top-degree form — the volume form — and with it, a notion of integration, divergence, and a Laplacian. In local oriented coordinates $(x^1,\ldots,x^n)$ with metric components $g_{ij}$: $$d\mathrm{vol}_g \;=\; \sqrt{\det g_{ij}}\;\, dx^1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^n.$$ The factor $\sqrt{\det g}$ is precisely the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the coordinate basis $\partial_1,\ldots,\partial_n$ measured by $g$, so changes of chart multiply $\sqrt{\det g}$ by the absolute Jacobian and $dx^1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^n$ by the signed Jacobian — their product is a well-defined global $n$-form, nowhere vanishing.

Integration of functions is then $\int_M f\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$; on the round sphere $ds^2 = d\theta^2+\sin^2\theta\,d\varphi^2$, $\sqrt{\det g}=\sin\theta$, and $\int_{S^2} d\mathrm{vol}_g = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^\pi \sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\varphi = 4\pi$. This recovers the familiar area.

Divergence and Laplace–Beltrami. For a vector field $X = X^i\partial_i$, the divergence of $X$ is defined by $\mathcal{L}_X d\mathrm{vol}_g = (\operatorname{div}_g X)\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$, equivalently $$\operatorname{div}_g X \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\det g}}\,\partial_i\bigl(\sqrt{\det g}\,X^i\bigr).$$ Combined with the gradient $\operatorname{grad}_g f = g^{ij}\partial_j f\,\partial_i$, this yields the Laplace–Beltrami operator $$\Delta_g f \;=\; \operatorname{div}_g\operatorname{grad}_g f \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\det g}}\,\partial_i\!\Bigl(\sqrt{\det g}\,g^{ij}\partial_j f\Bigr).$$ On Euclidean space ($g_{ij}=\delta_{ij}$) it collapses to the usual Laplacian $\sum_i \partial_i^2 f$. On a closed Riemannian manifold, $\Delta_g$ is symmetric and non-positive for $\int_M (\cdot)(\cdot)\,d\mathrm{vol}_g$; its eigenvalues encode spectral geometry (can you hear the shape of a drum?).

Hodge duality for the metric identifies $k$-forms with $(n-k)$-forms via the Hodge star $\star$, uniquely determined by $\alpha\wedge\star\beta = g(\alpha,\beta)\,d\mathrm{vol}_g$. This packages $\operatorname{grad},\operatorname{div},\operatorname{curl}$ on $\mathbb{R}^3$ and more generally gives the adjoint of $d$, the codifferential $\delta = \pm\star d\star$, and the Hodge Laplacian $\Delta = d\delta + \delta d$ on forms — the starting point for Hodge theory on Riemannian manifolds.

11. Connections to the rest of the notebook

Riemannian geometry sits at a crossroads of the notebook. Three threads worth following:

  • Lie groups (lie-groups.html). A Lie group $G$ with a bi-invariant metric has Levi-Civita connection $$\nabla_X Y = \tfrac12 [X,Y]$$ on left-invariant fields, and its geodesics through the identity are exactly the one-parameter subgroups $\exp(tX)$. The sectional curvature is $K(X,Y) = \tfrac14 \|[X,Y]\|^2 \ge 0$ — compact Lie groups have non-negative curvature. The symmetric spaces $G/H$ generalize the model spaces.
  • Differential forms (differential-forms.html). The Levi-Civita connection can be repackaged via an $\mathfrak{so}(n)$-valued connection $1$-form $\omega$ on the frame bundle, with curvature $2$-form $\Omega = d\omega + \omega\wedge\omega$. The Riemann tensor is the components of $\Omega$ in a local frame. This Cartan formalism is indispensable in modern differential geometry and gauge theory.
  • Algebraic topology. The Gauss–Bonnet theorem $$\int_M K\, dA = 2\pi\,\chi(M)$$ (for a closed surface $M$, with the geodesic-triangle version extended to all of $M$) is the first example of a characteristic-class statement: a purely topological invariant — the Euler characteristic — computed by integrating local curvature. It generalizes to the Chern–Gauss–Bonnet theorem for even-dimensional manifolds and to the Atiyah–Singer index theorem.

The companion differential geometry page treats the Gauss curvature of surfaces, the Theorema Egregium, and Gauss–Bonnet in the embedded setting; this page lifted all of that into the intrinsic, coordinate-free, higher-dimensional world. The move from surface to manifold is the move from "intuition assisted by an ambient $\mathbb{R}^3$" to "geometry done on its own terms."

Where to read next.

Simplicial sets and nerve

Combinatorial models of spaces: $\Delta$, simplicial sets, geometric realization, and the nerve $N(C)$.

1. The simplex category $\Delta$

The simplex category $\Delta$ is the category whose objects are the finite non-empty totally ordered sets $[n] = \{0 < 1 < \cdots < n\}$ and whose morphisms $[m] \to [n]$ are weakly order-preserving maps. It is small (a skeleton of the category of finite non-empty totally ordered sets) and combinatorial: the entire structure is captured by two families of generating morphisms.

The cofaces $d^i\colon [n-1] \to [n]$ ($0 \le i \le n$) are the unique injective order-preserving maps that miss the value $i$; they correspond geometrically to the $i$-th face inclusion of the standard $n$-simplex. The codegeneracies $s^j\colon [n+1] \to [n]$ ($0 \le j \le n$) are the unique surjective order-preserving maps that double the value $j$; they collapse two adjacent vertices into one. Every morphism in $\Delta$ factors uniquely as a composite of codegeneracies followed by cofaces, and the relations among the generators are the cosimplicial identities:

$d^j d^i = d^i d^{j-1}\;(i j+1.\end{cases}$

The morphism count is a useful sanity check: order-preserving maps $[m] \to [n]$ correspond to weakly increasing sequences of length $m+1$ in $\{0,\ldots,n\}$, equivalently multisets of size $m+1$ from $n+1$ symbols, giving $\binom{m+n+1}{m+1}$. So $\Delta([1],[3]) = \binom{5}{2} = 10$. Two of these are degenerate (the constants); the rest split into compositions of the four cofaces and the lone $s^0\colon [1]\to[0]$ — which, taken together with the cosimplicial identities, is enough to generate $\Delta$.

The reason this matters: a presheaf on $\Delta$ — a contravariant functor $\Delta^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$ — is exactly determined by sets $X_n$ together with the dualised generators $d_i\colon X_n \to X_{n-1}$ and $s_j\colon X_n \to X_{n+1}$ satisfying the dual simplicial identities. That is the data of a simplicial set, the subject of §2.

2. Simplicial sets as $\Delta^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$

A simplicial set is a presheaf on $\Delta$, i.e. a functor $X\colon \Delta^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$. Concretely it is the data of a sequence of sets $X_0, X_1, X_2, \ldots$ (the "$n$-simplices" $X_n$) together with face operators $d_i\colon X_n \to X_{n-1}$ ($0 \le i \le n$) and degeneracy operators $s_j\colon X_n \to X_{n+1}$ ($0 \le j \le n$) satisfying the (dualised) simplicial identities — the relations dual to those of §1. Maps of simplicial sets are natural transformations, equivalently graded maps respecting all $d_i, s_j$. The category $\mathbf{sSet} = \mathbf{Set}^{\Delta^{\mathrm{op}}}$ inherits a presheaf topos structure: it has all small limits and colimits, exponentials, and a subobject classifier.

The motivating examples are the standard simplices $\Delta^n = \Delta(-, [n])\colon [m] \mapsto \Delta([m],[n])$ — the representable presheaves. By Yoneda, maps $\Delta^n \to X$ are exactly the elements of $X_n$, so each $n$-simplex $\sigma \in X_n$ is a map $\sigma\colon \Delta^n \to X$. Faces and degeneracies of $\sigma$ are the precompositions of $\sigma$ with the cofaces and codegeneracies of $\Delta$.

The boundary $\partial \Delta^n \subset \Delta^n$ is the subobject consisting of all simplices that factor through some $d^i\colon \Delta^{n-1} \to \Delta^n$ — the union of the $n+1$ codimension-$1$ faces. The horn $\Lambda^n_k \subset \partial \Delta^n$ is the further subobject obtained by removing the $k$-th face: it is the union of all faces except the one opposite vertex $k$. The horn is inner when $0 < k < n$ and outer otherwise.

A useful structural fact: every simplex factors uniquely as a degeneracy of a non-degenerate one (the Eilenberg–Zilber lemma). For $\sigma \in X_n$ there exist a unique surjection $\alpha\colon [n] \twoheadrightarrow [k]$ in $\Delta$ and a unique non-degenerate $\tau \in X_k$ with $\sigma = X(\alpha)(\tau)$. This is what eventually lets the geometric realization of §3 land in CW-complexes — one cell per non-degenerate simplex.

3. Geometric realization $|X|$

The geometric realization functor $|{-}|\colon \mathbf{sSet} \to \mathbf{Top}$ is the left Kan extension of the cosimplicial space $[n] \mapsto |\Delta^n|$ (the standard topological $n$-simplex) along the Yoneda embedding $\Delta \hookrightarrow \mathbf{sSet}$. Concretely:

$|X| \;=\; \Bigl( \coprod_{n \ge 0} X_n \times |\Delta^n| \Bigr) \,\Big/\, \sim,$

where the equivalence relation glues $(\sigma, \alpha_*(t)) \sim (\alpha^*\sigma, t)$ for every $\alpha\colon [m] \to [n]$ in $\Delta$, every $\sigma \in X_n$, and every $t \in |\Delta^m|$. This is exactly the colimit over the category of simplices of $X$ of the diagram sending each simplex to its standard topological simplex.

Geometric realization is the left adjoint to the singular complex functor $\mathrm{Sing}\colon \mathbf{Top} \to \mathbf{sSet}$ defined by $\mathrm{Sing}(Y)_n = \mathrm{Top}(|\Delta^n|, Y)$. The adjunction $|{-}| \dashv \mathrm{Sing}$ is the bedrock of the simplicial approach to homotopy theory. Quillen's theorem upgrades it to a Quillen equivalence of model categories, so the homotopy theories of Kan complexes and topological spaces (with weak equivalences) agree.

Three computations make the picture concrete: $|\Delta^n|$ is the standard $n$-simplex; $|\partial \Delta^n| \cong S^{n-1}$ (its boundary is the $(n-1)$-sphere); and $|\Lambda^n_k|$ is the union of all faces but one — for $n=2$ this is two segments meeting at a vertex, contractible. Because of the Eilenberg–Zilber lemma, $|X|$ has a canonical CW-structure with one $n$-cell per non-degenerate $n$-simplex of $X$. Walk through the construction step by step below.

4. The nerve $N(C)$ of a category

For a small category $C$, the nerve $N(C)$ is the simplicial set with

$N(C)_n \;=\; \mathbf{Cat}([n], C) \;=\; \{\, x_0 \xrightarrow{f_1} x_1 \xrightarrow{f_2} \cdots \xrightarrow{f_n} x_n \,\}$,

the set of length-$n$ composable strings of arrows in $C$ (where we view $[n]$ as the linear poset $0 < 1 < \cdots < n$, hence as a category with a unique arrow $i \to j$ whenever $i \le j$). The face maps $d_i$ act on $(f_1, \ldots, f_n)$ as: $d_0$ drops $f_1$, $d_n$ drops $f_n$, and for $0 < i < n$ the operator $d_i$ composes $f_{i+1} \circ f_i$. The degeneracies $s_j$ insert an identity at position $j$.

This bridges category theory and homotopy theory: $N\colon \mathbf{Cat} \to \mathbf{sSet}$ is a fully faithful right adjoint, with left adjoint $h$ — the homotopy / fundamental category functor that sends a simplicial set $X$ to the category with objects $X_0$, generating arrows $X_1$, and relations $d_1 \sigma = d_0 \sigma \circ d_2 \sigma$ for $\sigma \in X_2$. Because $N$ is fully faithful, two small categories are equivalent iff their nerves are equivalent in the Joyal model structure on $\mathbf{sSet}$.

Two computations to keep in mind. First, for a poset $P$ viewed as a category, $N(P)_n$ is the set of weakly increasing length-$(n+1)$ chains in $P$. Second, for a group $G$ regarded as a one-object groupoid $BG$, $N(BG)_n = G^n$ — the standard simplicial model for the classifying space, with $|N(BG)| \simeq BG$ a $K(G,1)$.

5. Kan complexes

A simplicial set $X$ is a Kan complex (or satisfies the Kan extension condition) if every horn extends to a full simplex: for every $n \ge 1$, every $0 \le k \le n$, and every map of simplicial sets $\Lambda^n_k \to X$, there exists a (not necessarily unique) extension $\Delta^n \to X$:

$\begin{array}{ccc} \Lambda^n_k & \longrightarrow & X \\ \downarrow & \nearrow & \\ \Delta^n & & \end{array}$

Equivalently, $X \to *$ is a Kan fibration: the unique map to the terminal object has the right lifting property against every horn inclusion $\Lambda^n_k \hookrightarrow \Delta^n$. Kan complexes are the fibrant objects in the Kan–Quillen model structure on $\mathbf{sSet}$ — the model structure that makes $|{-}| \dashv \mathrm{Sing}$ into a Quillen equivalence with $\mathbf{Top}$.

The motivating example is the singular complex $\mathrm{Sing}(Y)$ of a topological space $Y$: $|\Lambda^n_k|$ is a strong deformation retract of $|\Delta^n|$, so any continuous map $|\Lambda^n_k| \to Y$ extends to $|\Delta^n|$, and translating across the realization–singular adjunction gives the Kan condition. By Quillen's theorem, $|{-}|\dashv \mathrm{Sing}$ descends to an equivalence between the homotopy category of Kan complexes and the homotopy category of CW-complexes (or, equivalently, of all topological spaces with weak equivalences inverted).

Crucially, the nerve $N(C)$ of a category $C$ is a Kan complex iff $C$ is a groupoid. The reason is the outer horn: an outer horn $\Lambda^2_0 \to N(C)$ is a pair $f\colon a \to b$ and $h\colon a \to c$, and a filler asks for $g\colon b \to c$ with $h = g \circ f$ — this is right-divisibility, and it holds for every pair iff every morphism is invertible. Inner horns alone are easier (they correspond to ordinary composition), and they motivate §6.

6. Inner-horn filling and quasi-categorical nerves

The Kan condition asks for fillers of every horn, including the outer ones $\Lambda^n_0$ and $\Lambda^n_n$ that encode invertibility. Demanding fillers only for inner horns ($0 < k < n$) gives a strictly weaker condition that nevertheless captures all the algebra of composition. A simplicial set in which every inner horn extends is called a quasi-category (or weak Kan complex), and these are the fibrant objects of the Joyal model structure on $\mathbf{sSet}$.

Quasi-categories are Boardman–Vogt and Joyal–Lurie's model for $(\infty, 1)$-categories. The picture: $0$-simplices are objects, $1$-simplices are morphisms, $2$-simplices witness compositions ($d_1$ is "the composite of $d_0$ and $d_2$", but the composite is determined only up to homotopy, giving an entire space of composites), and higher simplices encode coherences between coherences ad infinitum. Inner-horn filling is exactly the assertion "every composable string admits a composition".

The fundamental theorem of nerves: $X = N(C)$ for some small category $C$ iff $X$ is a quasi-category in which every inner horn has a unique filler. Existence of fillers is "$X$ has compositions"; uniqueness is "the composition is a function, not a higher structure" — i.e., $X$ is a $1$-category. This is the precise sense in which classical category theory is the "$1$-truncated" piece of $\infty$-category theory.

Two further refinements. (a) The Kan complexes (all horns fill, possibly non-uniquely) sit inside quasi-categories as those with all morphisms invertible — the $\infty$-groupoids. (b) For an arbitrary quasi-category $\mathcal{C}$, the homotopy category $h\mathcal{C}$ is obtained by quotienting $\mathcal{C}_1$ by homotopy of $1$-simplices; this $h\mathcal{C}$ is an honest $1$-category. The functor $h \dashv N$ is a Quillen pair between the Joyal model on $\mathbf{sSet}$ and $\mathbf{Cat}$, restricting to the inclusion $\mathbf{Cat} \hookrightarrow \mathbf{sSet}$ on the nerve side.

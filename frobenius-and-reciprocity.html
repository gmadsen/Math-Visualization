Frobenius and the reciprocity dictionary

A single element of a Galois group controls how a polynomial factors modulo each prime. Reading that element tells you a reciprocity law — and the grand claim of the Langlands program is that the reading can always be done in terms of automorphic forms.

1. Setup: polynomials, splitting fields, Galois groups

Fix a monic polynomial $f(x) \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$ with distinct roots $\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n \in \overline{\mathbb{Q}}$. Its splitting field $$ K = \mathbb{Q}(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n) \subset \overline{\mathbb{Q}} $$ is a finite Galois extension of $\mathbb{Q}$. The Galois group $$ G \;=\; \mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q}) $$ acts by permuting the roots, so there is an embedding $G \hookrightarrow S_n$. The image is the Galois group of $f$; it records the symmetries among the roots that are invisible over $\mathbb{Q}$.

The surprise of Galois theory (covered in Galois and algebraic number theory) is that the combinatorics of $G$ already tells you a huge amount about how $f$ behaves modulo primes. The way it tells you is the Frobenius element, and the extent to which it determines everything is the content of a reciprocity law.

2. Factoring $f(x) \bmod p$ — cycle shapes appear

Fix a prime $p$ and reduce the coefficients of $f$ mod $p$. Unless $p$ divides the discriminant of $f$ (a finite set of bad primes) the result $\bar f \in \mathbb{F}_p[x]$ is squarefree and factors uniquely: $$ \bar f(x) \;=\; \bar f_1(x)\,\bar f_2(x)\cdots \bar f_r(x), \qquad \deg \bar f_i = d_i, \quad \sum d_i = n. $$ The multiset of degrees $(d_1,d_2,\dots,d_r)$ is the cycle shape of $f$ at $p$. It is a single combinatorial fingerprint — and it is, already, a shadow of a group-theoretic fact.

Try the widget. Pick a polynomial and a prime; watch the factorisation degrees fall out. For $x^2+1$ the shape at $p$ is $(1,1)$ when $p\equiv 1 \pmod 4$ and $(2)$ when $p\equiv 3 \pmod 4$ — exactly the supplementary law for $-1$ that appears in quadratic reciprocity.

3. Splitting types: completely split, inert, partially split

Before we name the group-theoretic reason, let us name the patterns. For an unramified rational prime $p$ in a Galois extension $K/\mathbb{Q}$ of degree $n=[K:\mathbb{Q}]$, the factorisation $$ p\,\mathcal{O}_K \;=\; \mathfrak{P}_1\,\mathfrak{P}_2\cdots \mathfrak{P}_r, \qquad f := [\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{P}_i : \mathbb{F}_p], \qquad r f = n $$ has every prime sharing the same residue degree $f$: in a Galois extension the Galois group $G$ permutes the primes above $p$ transitively, so $r$ and $f$ are the same for all $i$. The pair $(r,f)$ with $rf=n$ is the splitting type of $p$ in $K$.

Three qualitative cases, each with its own name and flavour:

  • Completely split: $r=n,\ f=1$. The polynomial $\bar f(x)$ factors into $n$ distinct linear factors over $\mathbb{F}_p$; cycle shape $(1,1,\dots,1)$. Frobenius is the identity.
  • Inert: $r=1,\ f=n$. The prime $p$ stays prime in $\mathcal{O}_K$; $\bar f(x)$ is irreducible of degree $n$; cycle shape $(n)$. Frobenius is an $n$-cycle, of order $n$ in $G$.
  • Partially split: $1 < r < n$, any intermediate shape compatible with $rf=n$.
  • Ramified: one or more $e_i>1$, i.e. $p\mid\operatorname{disc}(f)$. The cycle-shape dictionary breaks; a finite set of exceptional primes.
A useful mnemonic: completely split primes are the "visible" primes — they carry no Galois twist, so the field $K$ looks, at those primes, just like copies of $\mathbb{F}_p$. Inert primes are the most "twisted" — the full Galois action at $p$ is realised by a single Frobenius generator of order $n$.

4. The Frobenius element

Cycle shapes look combinatorial, but they are group-theoretic. Here is the bridge. Inside the ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$ of the splitting field, the rational prime $p$ factors as a product of prime ideals: $$ p\,\mathcal{O}_K \;=\; \mathfrak{P}_1 \mathfrak{P}_2 \cdots \mathfrak{P}_r^{e}, $$ and at each unramified prime $\mathfrak{P}$ the residue field $\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{P}$ is a finite extension of $\mathbb{F}_p$. The Frobenius at $\mathfrak{P}$ is the unique element $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{P} \in G$ satisfying $$ \mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{P}(x) \equiv x^p \pmod{\mathfrak{P}} \quad \text{for all } x \in \mathcal{O}_K. $$

Changing $\mathfrak{P}$ to a different prime above $p$ conjugates the Frobenius element by an element of $G$. So, for each unramified rational prime $p$, we get a well-defined conjugacy class $$ \mathrm{Frob}_p \;\subset\; G. $$

The cycle shape of $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ acting on the roots of $f$ (as an element of $S_n$ via its permutation action on the $\alpha_i$) equals the cycle shape $(d_1,\dots,d_r)$ of the factorisation $\bar f \bmod p$. Same fingerprint, two different sources.

The finite set of ramified primes (those dividing $\operatorname{disc}(f)$, together with ramification of $K/\mathbb{Q}$) is exceptional and does not get a conjugacy class; they are the "bad reduction" primes for the story.

5. Decomposition and inertia groups

Frobenius did not fall out of the sky — it came from a subgroup. Fix a prime $\mathfrak{P}\subset\mathcal{O}_K$ above $p$. Its decomposition group is the stabiliser $$ D(\mathfrak{P}) \;=\; \{\, \sigma \in G : \sigma(\mathfrak{P}) = \mathfrak{P}\,\} \;\le\; G. $$ By the orbit–stabiliser theorem, $|D(\mathfrak{P})| = |G|/r = ef$, where $r$ is the number of primes above $p$, $e$ is the ramification index, and $f$ is the residue degree. Every $\sigma\in D(\mathfrak{P})$ descends to an automorphism of the residue field $\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{P}$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, giving a natural map $$ \pi_\mathfrak{P}:\ D(\mathfrak{P}) \;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{Gal}\!\left((\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{P})/\mathbb{F}_p\right) \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}/f. $$ The kernel $I(\mathfrak{P}) = \ker \pi_\mathfrak{P}$ is the inertia subgroup at $\mathfrak{P}$, of order $e$.

Putting the two together yields a short exact sequence $$ 1 \longrightarrow I(\mathfrak{P}) \longrightarrow D(\mathfrak{P}) \xrightarrow{\ \pi_\mathfrak{P}\ } \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^f}/\mathbb{F}_p) \longrightarrow 1. $$ At an unramified prime $I(\mathfrak{P})$ is trivial and $\pi_\mathfrak{P}$ is an isomorphism, so $D(\mathfrak{P})$ is cyclic of order $f$. The Frobenius element $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{P}$ is exactly the preimage under $\pi_\mathfrak{P}$ of the $p$-power map $x\mapsto x^p$ — the canonical generator of the residue-field Galois group. Conjugating by $g\in G$ sends $\mathfrak{P}$ to $g\mathfrak{P}$ and carries $D(\mathfrak{P})\to D(g\mathfrak{P})$, so changing the prime above $p$ conjugates both $D$ and Frobenius; only the conjugacy class $\mathrm{Frob}_p\subset G$ is canonical.

Summary: $D(\mathfrak{P})$ is the local Galois group at $\mathfrak{P}$, $I(\mathfrak{P})$ measures ramification, and the quotient $D/I$ is the place where Frobenius lives. Cycle shapes, residue degrees, and decomposition subgroups are three views of the same data.

6. Chebotarev density

One Frobenius is a single data point. But the cycle shapes form a statistical pattern across primes. Let $C \subset G$ be a conjugacy class, and let $$ \pi_C(N) \;=\; \#\{\, p \le N : p \text{ unramified},\ \mathrm{Frob}_p \subset C \,\}. $$ Chebotarev's density theorem says: $$ \frac{\pi_C(N)}{\pi(N)} \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{|C|}{|G|} \qquad (N\to\infty). $$ Every conjugacy class, from every Galois group, gets hit by a positive, explicitly known fraction of primes.

For $f = x^3 - x - 1$, $G = S_3$. Conjugacy classes and sizes: identity (1), transpositions (3), 3-cycles (2). Predicted densities: $1/6$, $3/6=1/2$, $2/6=1/3$. The widget below runs the experiment up to primes $\le N$; watch the bars settle.

7. Weak reciprocity: the abelian miracle

When $G$ is abelian, every conjugacy class is a singleton, so $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ is an honest element of $G$. The abelian reciprocity law is then a stunning statement:

Weak reciprocity (abelian case). If $K/\mathbb{Q}$ is abelian, there is an integer $N$ (the conductor) such that the map $$ p \;\longmapsto\; \mathrm{Frob}_p \in G $$ factors through $(\mathbb{Z}/N)^\times$. That is, the cycle shape of $f$ mod $p$ depends only on a congruence condition on $p$ modulo $N$.

Concrete cases:

  • $f(x) = x^2 - a$. The splitting field is $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a})$; $G = \mathbb{Z}/2$. $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ equals the Legendre symbol $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)$. By quadratic reciprocity this depends only on $p \bmod 4a$.
  • $f(x) = \Phi_N(x)$, the $N$-th cyclotomic polynomial. The splitting field is $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_N)$; $G = (\mathbb{Z}/N)^\times$. Frobenius at $p$ is $p \bmod N$ itself — the congruence class is the element. The cycle shape at $p$ is a function of the order of $p$ modulo $N$.
Every abelian reciprocity law in number theory is a reformulation of this statement. The extension of the principle to all abelian extensions of number fields is class field theory.

8. Strong reciprocity: the non-abelian case

For $G$ non-abelian, no congruence condition on $p$ can detect the conjugacy class of $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ — because the number of conjugacy classes is finite but the field $K$ does not embed into any cyclotomic field. (A theorem of Kronecker–Weber says cyclotomic fields give exactly the abelian extensions.) So weak reciprocity fails outside the abelian world.

The Langlands program replaces the missing congruence condition with an automorphic object. The slogan:

Strong reciprocity (Langlands flavour). For every Galois representation $\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ there is an automorphic object $\pi_\rho$ (a Dirichlet character, modular form, automorphic representation, …) such that $$ \operatorname{tr}\rho(\mathrm{Frob}_p) \;=\; a_p(\pi_\rho) \qquad \text{for every unramified } p. $$ The coefficients of $\pi_\rho$ are the Frobenius traces.

Worked example: $x^3-x-1$ and a weight-1 modular form

The polynomial $f(x) = x^3 - x - 1$ has Galois group $S_3$ and discriminant $-23$. Its splitting field contains $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-23})$, the imaginary quadratic field of class number $3$.

There is a weight-1 cusp form $\eta(z)\eta(23z) = q - q^2 - q^3 + q^6 + q^8 - q^{13} - \cdots = \sum a_n q^n$ of level $23$ and character $\left(\frac{\cdot}{23}\right)$, such that for each unramified prime $p \neq 23$,

$$ a_p \;=\; \begin{cases} 2 & \text{if }p\text{ is of the form }x^2+xy+6y^2,\text{ which happens iff Frob}_p=1\\ -1 & \text{if Frob}_p\text{ is a 3-cycle}\\ 0 & \text{if Frob}_p\text{ is a transposition} \end{cases} $$

Equivalently: $p$ splits completely in the splitting field of $x^3-x-1$ (Frob = identity, cycle shape $(1,1,1)$) iff $p$ is represented by the principal binary quadratic form $x^2+xy+6y^2$ — a single algebraic condition that is neither congruence nor pure shape. The coefficients $a_p$ of that modular form are the Frobenius fingerprints.

What "strong" means

Weak reciprocity reduces Frobenius to a congruence condition — a purely abelian, finite, and totally combinatorial input. Strong reciprocity widens the vocabulary: the right-hand side of the dictionary is now a modular form, or more generally an automorphic representation. The existence of the match is the deep theorem; the computation of the match in any specific case is a modest miracle worthy of its own widget.

9. Where this leads

The reciprocity dictionary is the organising principle of 20th and 21st century number theory. Three directions to look:

  • Galois representations. Promote $\rho$ from a finite group's representation to a continuous representation of the absolute Galois group $G_\mathbb{Q} = \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$, typically into $\mathrm{GL}_n$ of a field such as $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$. The étale cohomology of a variety $X/\mathbb{Q}$ supplies a natural infinite family of such representations.
  • L-functions. Each side of the dictionary has a zeta-type function. Artin $L$-functions on the arithmetic side; Dirichlet, modular, and automorphic $L$-functions on the other. Reciprocity becomes an equality of $L$-functions; the Riemann Hypothesis and its cousins are statements about their zeros.
  • Modularity. The case $\dim \rho = 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$ is exactly the modularity theorem proved (first for semistable elliptic curves) by the Wiles-Taylor and later generalisations. That is why elliptic curves talk to modular forms.
The landmarks to build next are Galois representations (continuous ℓ-adic, the correct home for the reciprocity dictionary) and L-functions (the analytic side that makes reciprocity an equality of analytic objects, not just a match of local data). Links to those pages will go in when they are written.

Reading back across the notebook

Frobenius touches everything:

  • Galois theory supplies the group $G$ and its action on roots.
  • Algebraic number theory gives the prime factorisation in $\mathcal{O}_K$ and the notion of ramification.
  • Quadratic reciprocity is the $\mathbb{Z}/2$ case.
  • Representation theory turns a Galois group into matrices, the only language in which "Frobenius trace" makes sense for higher $n$.
  • $p$-adic numbers are the local completions at a prime where this entire discussion happens one prime at a time.

Characteristic classes

Cohomology classes attached functorially to vector bundles. They obstruct triviality, count zeros of sections, and translate the curvature of a connection into integers — the bridge between bundle topology and integration.

1. Vector bundles

A real (resp. complex) rank-$n$ vector bundle is a continuous surjection $\pi\colon E\to X$ together with a real (resp. complex) $n$-dimensional vector space structure on every fibre $E_x = \pi^{-1}(x)$, such that $X$ has an open cover $\{U_\alpha\}$ with local trivialisations $\varphi_\alpha\colon\pi^{-1}(U_\alpha)\xrightarrow{\sim} U_\alpha\times\mathbb{R}^n$ that are linear on each fibre.

On overlaps the trivialisations differ by transition functions $g_{\alpha\beta}\colon U_\alpha\cap U_\beta\to GL_n$, namely $\varphi_\alpha \circ \varphi_\beta^{-1} = (x,v)\mapsto (x, g_{\alpha\beta}(x)v)$. Cocycle compatibility $g_{\alpha\beta}g_{\beta\gamma} = g_{\alpha\gamma}$ on triple overlaps, modulo trivialisation changes $g_{\alpha\beta}\mapsto h_\alpha g_{\alpha\beta} h_\beta^{-1}$, classifies bundles up to isomorphism. This is exactly Čech 1-cohomology with values in $GL_n$.

Three running examples:

  • The tangent bundle $TM\to M$ of a smooth manifold, with transitions given by the Jacobians of chart changes.
  • For an embedding $\iota\colon N\hookrightarrow M$, the normal bundle $\nu_\iota = \iota^*TM/TN$, controlling local thickening.
  • Line bundles: rank $1$, classified by $H^1(X;\mathbb{R}^\times)$ in the real case and by $H^2(X;\mathbb{Z}) = \mathrm{Pic}(X)$ in the complex case (we will recover the latter from $c_1$).
Trivial vs non-trivial. A bundle $E$ is trivial if $E\cong X\times\mathbb{R}^n$ globally. The trivial line bundle on $S^1$ is the cylinder; the non-trivial one is the Möbius band. 2. Stiefel–Whitney classes

To every real rank-$n$ vector bundle $E\to X$, the Stiefel–Whitney machinery assigns classes $w_i(E) \in H^i(X;\mathbb{Z}/2)$ for $i=0,\ldots,n$ (with $w_0 = 1$), characterised by four axioms:

  • Naturality. $w_i(f^*E) = f^*w_i(E)$ for any $f\colon Y\to X$.
  • Whitney sum. $w(E\oplus F) = w(E)\smile w(F)$, where $w(E) = 1 + w_1 + w_2 + \cdots$ is the total class.
  • Normalisation. $w_1(\gamma_1) \ne 0$ for the tautological line bundle $\gamma_1$ on $\mathbb{RP}^\infty$; equivalently $w_1$ generates $H^1(\mathbb{RP}^\infty;\mathbb{Z}/2)\cong\mathbb{Z}/2$.
  • Vanishing past rank. $w_i(E) = 0$ for $i>\mathrm{rk}\,E$.

These classes are obstructions to geometric structures on $E$:

vanishinggeometric meaning
$w_1(E) = 0$$E$ is orientable
$w_1(E) = w_2(E) = 0$$E$ admits a spin structure
$w(E) = 1$ (all $w_i$ vanish)necessary, not sufficient, for triviality

The most important computational tool is the splitting principle: there is an injection $H^*(X;\mathbb{Z}/2)\hookrightarrow H^*(\widetilde X;\mathbb{Z}/2)$ for some $\widetilde X$ on which $E$ splits as a sum of line bundles $L_1\oplus\cdots\oplus L_n$, in which case $w(E) = \prod_i (1 + w_1(L_i))$. Symmetric functions of the $w_1(L_i)$ then descend to $X$.

Worked example: $\mathbb{RP}^n$. The mod-2 cohomology is $H^*(\mathbb{RP}^n;\mathbb{Z}/2) = \mathbb{Z}/2[a]/(a^{n+1})$ with $|a|=1$. The tautological line bundle $\gamma$ has $w(\gamma) = 1+a$. The tangent bundle satisfies $T\mathbb{RP}^n \oplus \mathbb{R} \cong \gamma^* \oplus\cdots\oplus\gamma^*$ ($n+1$ copies), so $w(T\mathbb{RP}^n) = (1+a)^{n+1}$. In particular $\mathbb{RP}^n$ is orientable iff $n$ is odd.

3. Chern classes

The complex analogue of Stiefel–Whitney is integral and lives in even degrees. To every complex rank-$n$ bundle $E\to X$ we attach Chern classes $c_i(E) \in H^{2i}(X;\mathbb{Z})$, $i=0,\ldots,n$, with $c_0 = 1$. The axioms parallel the real case:

  • Naturality: $c_i(f^*E) = f^*c_i(E)$.
  • Whitney sum: $c(E\oplus F) = c(E)\smile c(F)$ for the total class $c = 1 + c_1 + c_2 + \cdots$.
  • Normalisation: $c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))$ generates $H^2(\mathbb{CP}^\infty;\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

The first Chern class is the universal invariant of complex line bundles: $$c_1\colon \mathrm{Pic}(X) = \{\text{complex line bundles}\}/\!\!\sim\;\xrightarrow{\sim}\; H^2(X;\mathbb{Z}).$$ In particular for $X = \mathbb{CP}^1 = S^2$, line bundles are classified by their degree $\langle c_1(L),[\mathbb{CP}^1]\rangle \in \mathbb{Z}$, and the line bundle of degree $d$ is denoted $\mathcal{O}(d)$.

Splitting principle. Every complex bundle pulls back to a sum of line bundles on some auxiliary space whose cohomology injects back. If $E$ formally splits as $L_1\oplus\cdots\oplus L_n$ with $x_i = c_1(L_i)$, then $$c(E) = \prod_{i=1}^n (1+x_i),\qquad c_k(E) = e_k(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\;(\text{$k$th elementary symmetric}).$$

Two consequences sit at the heart of the subject:

  • The top Chern class $c_n(E)$ equals the Euler class of the underlying oriented real $2n$-bundle (§5).
  • The Chern character $\mathrm{ch}(E) = \sum_i e^{x_i} = n + c_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}(c_1^2 - 2c_2) + \cdots$ is additive on direct sums and multiplicative on tensor products: $\mathrm{ch}(E\otimes F) = \mathrm{ch}(E)\mathrm{ch}(F)$. It descends to a ring map $K^0(X)\to H^{\mathrm{even}}(X;\mathbb{Q})$.

4. Pontryagin classes

Real bundles do not have integral Chern classes — but their complexifications do. For a real rank-$n$ bundle $E\to X$, define the Pontryagin classes $$p_i(E) := (-1)^i\, c_{2i}(E\otimes\mathbb{C}) \;\in\; H^{4i}(X;\mathbb{Z}),\qquad 1\le i \le \lfloor n/2\rfloor.$$ The total Pontryagin class is $p(E) = 1 + p_1 + p_2 + \cdots$.

Why only even Chern classes? The complexification $E\otimes\mathbb{C}$ is isomorphic to its complex conjugate $\overline{E\otimes\mathbb{C}}$ (via $v\otimes z\mapsto v\otimes\bar z$), so $c_k(E\otimes\mathbb{C}) = c_k(\overline{E\otimes\mathbb{C}}) = (-1)^k c_k(E\otimes\mathbb{C})$, forcing $2c_{2i+1}(E\otimes\mathbb{C}) = 0$. The honest integral information is concentrated in degrees divisible by $4$, and the sign $(-1)^i$ is fixed so that $p$ is multiplicative on direct sums modulo $2$-torsion.

Hirzebruch signature theorem. For a closed oriented $4k$-manifold $M$, the signature $\sigma(M)$ of the intersection form on $H^{2k}(M;\mathbb{Q})$ equals $\langle L_k(p_1,\ldots,p_k),[M]\rangle$ where $L_k$ is the universal $L$-polynomial. For surfaces this is vacuous; for $4$-manifolds $\sigma(M) = \tfrac{1}{3}\,p_1(TM)[M]$.

5. Euler class

For an oriented real rank-$n$ vector bundle $E\to X$, there is a distinguished class — the Euler class $$e(E) \in H^n(X;\mathbb{Z})$$ — defined as the primary obstruction to a nowhere-vanishing section. Equivalently, if $s\colon X\to E$ is a generic section, $e(E)$ is the Poincaré dual of the zero locus $s^{-1}(0)$ (with orientation).

The Euler class is multiplicative on direct sums of oriented bundles: $e(E\oplus F) = e(E)\smile e(F)$. For complex bundles regarded as oriented real bundles, $e(E_\mathbb{R}) = c_n(E)$ — the top Chern class. Mod $2$ it reduces to the top Stiefel–Whitney class, $e(E)\equiv w_n(E)\pmod 2$.

Poincaré–Hopf. Let $M^n$ be a closed oriented manifold and $X$ a smooth vector field with isolated zeros $\{p_1,\ldots,p_k\}$. Then $\sum_i \mathrm{ind}_{p_i}(X) = \chi(M) = \langle e(TM),[M]\rangle.$

So the Euler class is the source of three famous invariants at once: the Euler characteristic $\chi(M)$, the index sum of any vector field, and the obstruction to "combing the hair" of $M$. On the torus $T^2 = S^1\times S^1$ a constant vector field has no zeros, so $\chi(T^2) = 0$. On $S^2$ the unit-vector field generated by spinning around an axis has two zeros (north and south poles), each of index $+1$, giving $\chi(S^2) = 2$.

6. Chern–Weil theory

Up to here the construction has been topological — Stiefel–Whitney via Steenrod squares, Chern via $\mathbb{CP}^\infty$, Euler via obstruction theory. Chern–Weil gives a direct differential-geometric construction: pick a connection, take its curvature, feed it into an invariant polynomial, and read off a closed form whose de Rham class is the characteristic class.

Concretely, let $E\to M$ be a complex rank-$n$ bundle with connection $\nabla$, and let $F_\nabla \in \Omega^2(M;\mathrm{End}\,E)$ be its curvature $2$-form. For an $\mathrm{Ad}$-invariant polynomial $P\colon \mathfrak{u}(n)\to\mathbb{C}$, the form $P(F_\nabla)\in\Omega^*(M;\mathbb{C})$ is closed, and its de Rham cohomology class is independent of $\nabla$. The total Chern class arises from the determinant: $$c(E) \;=\; \left[ \det\!\left(I + \tfrac{i}{2\pi}\,F_\nabla\right) \right] \;\in\; H^{\mathrm{even}}(M;\mathbb{R}).$$ The factor $i/2\pi$ is the integrality normalisation: with this convention the de Rham classes of $c_i(E)$ land on the integer lattice $H^{2i}(M;\mathbb{Z})\hookrightarrow H^{2i}(M;\mathbb{R})$.

Why is $[P(F_\nabla)]$ independent of $\nabla$? Two connections $\nabla_0,\nabla_1$ are joined by the affine path $\nabla_t = \nabla_0 + t\,\alpha$ where $\alpha = \nabla_1 - \nabla_0\in\Omega^1(M;\mathrm{End}\,E)$. A direct computation gives $\frac{d}{dt}P(F_{\nabla_t}) = d\,\mathrm{TP}(\alpha,F_{\nabla_t})$ for an explicit transgression form $\mathrm{TP}$ — so $P(F_{\nabla_1}) - P(F_{\nabla_0})$ is exact.

Gauss–Bonnet is the rank-$1$ case for the tangent bundle of a surface. Equip a closed oriented surface $\Sigma$ with a Riemannian metric. The Levi-Civita connection on $T\Sigma$ has curvature $F = -K\,dA$ (regarding $T\Sigma$ as a complex line bundle via the metric and orientation, where $K$ is the Gaussian curvature and $dA$ is the Riemannian area form). Then $$\chi(\Sigma) \;=\; \langle e(T\Sigma),[\Sigma]\rangle \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_\Sigma K\,dA.$$ A sphere of radius $R$ has $K = 1/R^2$ and area $4\pi R^2$, so $\frac{1}{2\pi}\int K\,dA = 2 = \chi(S^2)$. A flat torus has $K\equiv 0$, recovering $\chi(T^2) = 0$.

7. Connections

Characteristic classes are the obstruction-and-invariant theory for vector bundles. They sit at the intersection of topology, geometry, and analysis.

Algebraic topology

Singular cohomology is the receiver: $w_i, c_i, p_i$ live in $H^*(X)$. Steenrod squares, the Wu formula, and Poincaré duality are the algebraic-topology side.

Differential geometry and Chern–Weil

Differential geometry via curvature gives explicit cocycle representatives; the Chern–Weil construction makes Pontryagin numbers integrals of curvature polynomials. Gauss–Bonnet and Gauss–Bonnet–Chern are special cases.

Index theory and physics

The Atiyah–Singer index theorem expresses $\mathrm{ind}(D)$ as a characteristic-class integral; physics uses the same classes for anomalies, instanton numbers, and topological terms in field theories.

Algebraic geometry and Chow

Chern classes lift to Chow rings; Riemann–Roch (Hirzebruch / Grothendieck) is a Chern-class identity in $A^*(X)$ relating Euler characteristic to characteristic numbers.

Open frontiers: K-theory and the Atiyah–Hirzebruch spectral sequence; equivariant characteristic classes; motivic characteristic classes; quantum K-theory and Frobenius-twisted Riemann–Roch.

Lie algebras

Differentiate a Lie group at the identity and you get a vector space with a bracket. The whole zoo of classical and exceptional symmetries — A, B, C, D, plus G₂, F₄, E₆, E₇, E₈ — is decoded from skew-symmetry, Jacobi, and a single bilinear form.

1. Axioms and matrix examples

A Lie algebra over $\mathbb{C}$ (or any field) is a vector space $\mathfrak{g}$ together with a bilinear bracket $[\cdot,\cdot]\colon\mathfrak{g}\times\mathfrak{g}\to\mathfrak{g}$ satisfying

$$[x,x]=0\quad\text{(skew-symmetry)},\qquad [x,[y,z]]+[y,[z,x]]+[z,[x,y]]=0\quad\text{(Jacobi)}.$$

The skew condition $[x,x]=0$ is equivalent to $[x,y]=-[y,x]$ in characteristic $\neq 2$. Every associative algebra $A$ becomes a Lie algebra under the commutator bracket $[a,b]=ab-ba$; this is where every example below comes from.

2. Derivations and the adjoint representation

For each $x\in\mathfrak{g}$ define $\ad(x)\colon\mathfrak{g}\to\mathfrak{g}$ by $\ad(x)(y)=[x,y]$. The Jacobi identity says exactly that $\ad(x)$ is a derivation of the bracket:

$$\ad(x)[y,z]=[\ad(x)y,\,z]+[y,\,\ad(x)z].$$

Equivalently, $[\ad(x),\ad(y)]=\ad([x,y])$, so $\ad\colon\mathfrak{g}\to\mathfrak{gl}(\mathfrak{g})$ is itself a Lie algebra homomorphism — the adjoint representation.

The Killing form

The trace form on the adjoint rep,

$$B(x,y)=\tr\bigl(\ad(x)\,\ad(y)\bigr),$$

is symmetric, bilinear, and $\mathfrak{g}$-invariant: $B([z,x],y)+B(x,[z,y])=0$. It is canonical — the only such form up to scalar on a simple Lie algebra.

3. Solvable and nilpotent Lie algebras

Two filtrations of $\mathfrak{g}$ measure how far it is from being abelian:

  • The derived series $\mathfrak{g}^{(0)}=\mathfrak{g}$, $\mathfrak{g}^{(k+1)}=[\mathfrak{g}^{(k)},\mathfrak{g}^{(k)}]$. $\mathfrak{g}$ is solvable if some $\mathfrak{g}^{(k)}=0$.
  • The lower central series $\mathfrak{g}^{0}=\mathfrak{g}$, $\mathfrak{g}^{k+1}=[\mathfrak{g},\mathfrak{g}^{k}]$. $\mathfrak{g}$ is nilpotent if some $\mathfrak{g}^{k}=0$.

Nilpotent $\Rightarrow$ solvable. Two structure theorems pin them down:

  • Engel. $\mathfrak{g}$ is nilpotent $\iff$ each $\ad(x)$ is a nilpotent operator.
  • Lie. Every finite-dimensional rep of a solvable Lie algebra over $\mathbb{C}$ is simultaneously upper-triangularizable.

4. Cartan, Killing, and the root decomposition

A Lie algebra is semisimple if its largest solvable ideal (the radical) is zero. Cartan's criterion converts this into a linear-algebra check on the Killing form:

$$\mathfrak{g}\text{ semisimple}\ \iff\ B\text{ is nondegenerate}.$$

Now pick a Cartan subalgebra $\mathfrak{h}\subset\mathfrak{g}$ — a maximal toral (ad-diagonalizable) subalgebra. The adjoint action of $\mathfrak{h}$ decomposes $\mathfrak{g}$ into simultaneous eigenspaces:

$$\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{h}\ \oplus\ \bigoplus_{\alpha\in\Phi}\mathfrak{g}_\alpha,\qquad \mathfrak{g}_\alpha=\{x\in\mathfrak{g}:[h,x]=\alpha(h)x\ \forall h\in\mathfrak{h}\}.$$

The nonzero linear functionals $\alpha\in\mathfrak{h}^*$ that show up form a finite set $\Phi\subset\mathfrak{h}^*$ — the root system. It is symmetric ($\Phi=-\Phi$), spans $\mathfrak{h}^*$, and satisfies the integrality conditions that make it one of the rank-$n$ root systems.

5. $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-triples and weight diagrams

Every root $\alpha\in\Phi$ of a semisimple $\mathfrak{g}$ produces an embedded copy of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$:

$$e_\alpha\in\mathfrak{g}_\alpha,\quad f_\alpha\in\mathfrak{g}_{-\alpha},\quad h_\alpha=[e_\alpha,f_\alpha]\in\mathfrak{h},\qquad [h_\alpha,e_\alpha]=2e_\alpha,\ [h_\alpha,f_\alpha]=-2f_\alpha.$$

This is the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-triple attached to $\alpha$. The classification of finite-dimensional representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ is the engine driving everything that follows.

Weight diagrams of $V_n$

For each integer $n\ge 0$ there is a unique (up to iso) irreducible representation $V_n$ of $\mathfrak{sl}_2(\mathbb{C})$ of dimension $n+1$. Its weights — the eigenvalues of $h$ — are

$$n,\ n-2,\ n-4,\ \ldots,\ -n+2,\ -n,$$

each with multiplicity $1$. The action of $e$ raises by $2$, the action of $f$ lowers by $2$.

$\dim V_n = n+1$. Casimir $C=ef+fe+\tfrac12 h^2$ acts on $V_n$ as the scalar $\tfrac{n(n+2)}{2}$ — the analogue of $j(j+1)$ in physics with $j=n/2$.

In a general semisimple $\mathfrak{g}$, the simultaneous $\mathfrak{h}$-eigenspaces of an arbitrary representation $V$ are its weight spaces; restricting along each $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-triple recovers an $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-weight string. The combinatorics of root systems plus this $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ rule is enough to classify all irreducible reps.

6. Classification: A, B, C, D, E, F, G

Cartan and Killing's miraculous theorem: every simple complex Lie algebra is one of the four infinite families

$$A_n=\mathfrak{sl}_{n+1},\quad B_n=\mathfrak{so}_{2n+1},\quad C_n=\mathfrak{sp}_{2n},\quad D_n=\mathfrak{so}_{2n}\ (n\ge 2)$$

or one of the five exceptions $G_2,\ F_4,\ E_6,\ E_7,\ E_8$. The combinatorial fingerprint is the connected Dynkin diagram: nodes are simple roots, bonds encode angles ($1$ bond $= 120^\circ$ equal length, $2$ bonds $=135^\circ$ length-ratio $\sqrt{2}$, $3$ bonds $=150^\circ$ length-ratio $\sqrt{3}$), and the arrow on a multiple bond points from long to short root.

7. Connections

The Cartan–Killing classification is one of the great theorems of 20th-century algebra: it produces a finite list (A, B, C, D plus exceptionals) that surfaces in geometry, representation theory, and number theory in completely different guises.

Lie groups

Lie groups are the global / topological counterpart; the Lie algebra is the linearisation at the identity. The exponential map and the BCH formula are the bridge in both directions.

Representation theory

Representations of semisimple Lie algebras are classified by highest weights; characters, Weyl groups, and the Peter–Weyl theorem are the main organising principles.

Algebraic groups and number theory

Reductive algebraic groups are the algebraic-geometry counterpart of compact Lie groups; group schemes are the framework. Automorphic forms attached to algebraic groups (Langlands) are the modern arithmetic application.

Geometry and physics

Differential geometry consumes Lie-algebra-valued forms (connections, curvature); differential geometry uses these. Gauge theory and conformal field theory rely on infinite-dimensional analogues (Kac–Moody, Virasoro).

Open frontiers: quantum groups and crystal bases; geometric representation theory (D-modules, perverse sheaves); the Langlands program as a representation-theoretic bridge; categorification (Khovanov, Khovanov–Lauda–Rouquier).

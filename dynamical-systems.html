Dynamical systems ŌĆö flows, chaos, and strange attractors

Classical dynamics: how the laws of change weave a geometry out of time. From a vector field on the plane to the Lorenz butterfly, we trace the short arc from "orbit of a point" to "fractal attractor" and meet the tools ŌĆö linearization, Lyapunov exponents, Poincar├® sections, ergodic averages ŌĆö that make chaos intelligible.

1. Orbits, flows, and vector fields

A continuous dynamical system on a smooth manifold $M$ is a smooth vector field $X\colon M\to TM$. The initial-value problem $\dot\gamma(t)=X(\gamma(t)),\ \gamma(0)=p$ has, for each starting point $p$, a unique maximal integral curve $\gamma_p$; when every such curve is defined for all $t\in\mathbb{R}$ we get a flow, a one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms

$\Phi\colon \mathbb{R}\times M \to M,\qquad \Phi_{t+s}=\Phi_t\circ\Phi_s,\qquad \Phi_0=\mathrm{id}_M.$

The orbit of $p$ is the image $\{\Phi_t(p):t\in\mathbb{R}\}\subset M$; the forward orbit keeps $t\ge 0$. A single orbit is either a fixed point ($X(p)=0$), a closed curve (periodic orbit), or a non-self-intersecting path that runs forever.

Existence/uniqueness (PicardŌĆōLindel├Čf). If $X$ is $C^1$ (Lipschitz suffices), then for each $p$ there is an $\varepsilon>0$ and a unique smooth $\gamma\colon(-\varepsilon,\varepsilon)\to M$ with $\gamma(0)=p$ and $\dot\gamma=X(\gamma)$. The flow $\Phi_t$ is smooth in $(t,p)$ wherever it is defined.

Not every flow is complete. The planar field $X(x)=x^2$ on $\mathbb{R}$ starting at $x=1$ gives $x(t)=1/(1-t)$, which blows up at $t=1$. Completeness fails unless $X$ is bounded or $M$ is compact. The next sections assume all flows are complete; when in doubt, restrict to a forward-invariant compact region.

2. Fixed points and linearization

A fixed point $p^*$ is a zero of $X$: $X(p^*)=0$. Near $p^*$ the vector field looks like its linearization $DX(p^*)$, and the local dynamics is usually faithfully captured by this linear picture.

HartmanŌĆōGrobman. If $DX(p^*)$ has no eigenvalue on the imaginary axis (the fixed point is hyperbolic), then there is a homeomorphism from a neighbourhood of $p^*$ in $M$ to a neighbourhood of $0$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ conjugating $\Phi_t^X$ to the linear flow $e^{tDX(p^*)}$.

Hyperbolicity is what makes the linearization honest. Saddles, sinks (all eigenvalues with negative real part), and sources (positive real parts) are hyperbolic and topologically pinned down by their eigenvalues. Centers ($\pm i\omega$) and degenerate fixed points ($0$ eigenvalue) are not ŌĆö a vanishing linear part leaves the nonlinear terms in charge. The borderline example is $\dot x=-x^3$: the linearization vanishes, but $V(x)=x^2$ decreases along orbits, so the origin is asymptotically stable despite the degenerate Jacobian.

Stability classification in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a tale of eigenvalues:

  • Sink: all $\operatorname{Re}\lambda_i<0$ ŌĆö every nearby orbit converges to $p^*$.
  • Source: all $\operatorname{Re}\lambda_i>0$ ŌĆö every nearby orbit runs away.
  • Saddle: some positive, some negative real parts ŌĆö stable and unstable manifolds of complementary dimensions.
  • Center: $\operatorname{Re}\lambda_i=0$ in 2D ŌĆö orbits near $p^*$ close up (in the linear system; the nonlinear fate depends on higher-order terms).

3. Phase portraits in 2D

In two dimensions, a full phase portrait is within reach. Start with the linear systems: the 2├Ś2 Jacobian $A=\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}$ is classified by its trace $\tr{}A=a+d$ and determinant $\det A=ad-bc$. The $(\tr,\det)$-plane is the atlas of 2D linear dynamics:

regionconditiontype
below axis$\det<0$saddle
parabola below$\det>0,\ \tr^2>4\det$node (stable iff $\tr<0$)
parabola above$\det>0,\ \tr^2<4\det$spiral (stable iff $\tr<0$)
y-axis $>0$$\tr=0,\det>0$center

For a nonlinear system, the recipe is mechanical: (1) find all fixed points; (2) classify each by its Jacobian; (3) draw nullclines (zero sets of $\dot x$ and $\dot y$); (4) sample the direction field; (5) glue local pictures into a global portrait, respecting invariant sets and conserved quantities. 4. Periodic orbits and limit cycles

A periodic orbit is a closed curve in phase space traced out by a non-constant solution. A limit cycle is an isolated periodic orbit: there is a neighbourhood containing no other periodic orbit. Limit cycles are the attractors that do not collapse to a point ŌĆö self-sustained oscillators live there.

Poincar├®ŌĆōBendixson. Let $X$ be a smooth vector field on an open subset of $\mathbb{R}^2$ and let $\gamma(t)$ be a forward orbit whose closure is compact and contains only finitely many fixed points. Then the $\omega$-limit set $\omega(\gamma)$ is either (i) a fixed point, (ii) a periodic orbit, or (iii) a union of fixed points connected by heteroclinic/homoclinic orbits. In particular, if $\omega(\gamma)$ contains no fixed point, it IS a periodic orbit.

The theorem is sharp to 2D: in three dimensions, orbits can wander forever without either converging or repeating, producing chaotic attractors. In the plane, orbits cannot cross (PicardŌĆōLindel├Čf), and that topological constraint forces them to settle.

BendixsonŌĆōDulac. If $D\subset\mathbb{R}^2$ is simply connected and there exists a smooth $\phi>0$ on $D$ with $\operatorname{div}(\phi X)$ of constant sign and not identically zero on $D$, then $D$ contains no closed orbit.

The prototype limit cycle is in polar coordinates: $\dot r = r(1-r^2),\ \dot\theta=1$. The fixed point at the origin is unstable, and the circle $r=1$ is a stable limit cycle attracting everything off the origin. The less trivial example below is the Van der Pol oscillator $\ddot x +\mu(x^2-1)\dot x + x = 0$, equivalently $\dot x=y,\ \dot y=\mu(1-x^2)y-x$. Damping is negative when $|x|<1$ (pushing outward) and positive when $|x|>1$ (pulling inward). A stable limit cycle appears for every $\mu>0$. As $\mu$ grows, the cycle becomes increasingly relaxation-like ŌĆö a slow crawl along $y\approx \mu(x^3/3-x)$ punctuated by fast jumps.

5. Iterated maps and the logistic family

Discrete dynamics: pick a map $f\colon X\to X$ and iterate. The orbit of $x$ is the sequence $x,\,f(x),\,f^2(x),\,\ldots$ A fixed point satisfies $f(x^*)=x^*$; a period-$n$ point satisfies $f^n(x^*)=x^*$ with $n$ minimal. Linearization near a fixed point: $x_{k+1}-x^*\approx f'(x^*)(x_k-x^*)$, so stability is governed by the multiplier $f'(x^*)$ ŌĆö attracting iff $|f'(x^*)|<1$.

The workhorse is the logistic family $f_r(x)=rx(1-x)$ on $[0,1]$, where $r\in[0,4]$. Fixed points: $x=0$ and $x^*=1-1/r$ (the nontrivial one, in $[0,1]$ for $r\ge 1$). Multipliers: $f_r'(0)=r$, $f_r'(x^*)=2-r$. So $0$ is attracting for $r<1$, loses stability at $r=1$; $x^*$ is attracting for $1<r<3$, and at $r=3$ the multiplier crosses $-1$ ŌĆö a period-doubling bifurcation.

Cobweb picture. Starting at $x_0$, step up to the curve $y=f(x)$, then across to the diagonal $y=x$ to locate $x_1$. Stability of a fixed point is "does the cobweb spiral in?" ŌĆö it does iff the curve is less steep than the diagonal at the fixed point.

6. The period-doubling cascade

Zoom in on the logistic family at the boundary between order and chaos. As $r$ grows past $3$ the attracting fixed point $x^*=1-1/r$ loses stability and a stable 2-cycle is born; past $r_2\approx 3.449$ the 2-cycle bifurcates to a 4-cycle; then to an 8-cycle, then 16, 32, $\ldots$ The bifurcation values $r_n$ accumulate at $r_\infty\approx 3.5699$, beyond which the orbit becomes (generically) chaotic. The successive intervals $r_n-r_{n-1}$ shrink at a geometric rate

$\delta = \lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{r_{n-1}-r_{n-2}}{r_n-r_{n-1}} \approx 4.66920\ldots$

The Feigenbaum constant $\delta$ is universal: it appears in any smooth one-parameter family of unimodal maps with a quadratic maximum, far beyond the logistic map itself.

Past $r_\infty$ the picture is not a uniform sea of chaos: the bifurcation diagram is shot through with periodic windows where stable cycles of various periods reappear. The widest is the period-$3$ window opening at $r\approx 3.8284$ (and Sharkovskii's theorem says "period 3 implies all periods" ŌĆö so chaos is unavoidable beyond it). 7. Bifurcations

A bifurcation is a qualitative change in the phase portrait as a parameter crosses a critical value. Locally, every codimension-one bifurcation in a 1-parameter family looks (up to smooth change of coordinates) like one of a handful of normal forms:

namenormal formwhat happens
saddle-node$\dot x=\mu-x^2$two fixed points collide and vanish
transcritical$\dot x=\mu x-x^2$two fixed points exchange stability
pitchfork (super)$\dot x=\mu x-x^3$one fixed point splits into three (needs $\mathbb{Z}/2$-symmetry)
Hopf (super)$\dot r=\mu r-r^3,\ \dot\theta=1$spiral loses stability; stable limit cycle is born
period-doubling$x_{k+1}=-(1+\mu)x_k+x_k^3$fixed point loses stability; 2-cycle is born

For iterated maps, the most spectacular cascade is the period-doubling route to chaos of the logistic family. As $r$ grows past $3$, the attracting fixed point bifurcates to a 2-cycle; past $r_2\approx 3.449$ the 2-cycle bifurcates to a 4-cycle; then to an 8-cycle, and so on. The $r_n$ accumulate at $r_\infty\approx 3.5699\ldots$, beyond which the attractor becomes (generically) chaotic. The Feigenbaum constant

$\delta = \lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{r_{n-1}-r_{n-2}}{r_n-r_{n-1}} \approx 4.66920\ldots$

is universal: it appears in any smooth one-parameter family of unimodal maps with quadratic maximum.

8. Chaos and Lyapunov exponents

Chaos, as formalized by Devaney, requires three things of a map $f\colon X\to X$:

  • Sensitive dependence on initial conditions (SDIC): there exists $\delta>0$ such that for every $x\in X$ and every neighbourhood $U\ni x$, some $y\in U$ and $n\ge 0$ give $d(f^n(x),f^n(y))\ge\delta$.
  • Topological transitivity: for every pair of non-empty open sets $U,V$, some $n$ gives $f^n(U)\cap V\neq\emptyset$ ŌĆö equivalently, a dense orbit exists.
  • Dense periodic points.

The standard quantitative surrogate for SDIC is the Lyapunov exponent. For a 1D map,

$\lambda(x_0) = \lim_{N\to\infty}\dfrac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\log|f'(x_k)|.$

When $\lambda>0$, infinitesimal errors grow exponentially like $e^{\lambda N}$; when $\lambda<0$, orbits contract onto a stable attractor. For the doubling map $f(x)=2x\bmod 1$, $|f'|\equiv 2$, so $\lambda=\log 2\approx 0.693$. For the tent map, also $\log 2$. For the logistic map at $r=4$, a semiconjugacy to the tent map gives $\lambda=\log 2$ again.

Higher dimensions. An $n$-D flow has $n$ Lyapunov exponents $\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\cdots\ge\lambda_n$. For a non-trivial orbit of a smooth flow one exponent is always zero (the flow direction). The signature $(+,0,-)$ on a 3D attractor is the fingerprint of low-dimensional chaos; the Lorenz attractor has approximately $(+0.906,\ 0,\ -14.57)$.

9. Conservative vs dissipative flows

How does a flow treat phase-space volume? The answer is contained in the divergence of the vector field and Liouville's theorem.

Liouville's theorem. For a smooth vector field $X$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ (or a Riemannian manifold with volume form) and a bounded measurable set $U$, $$\dfrac{d}{dt}\,\mathrm{vol}(\Phi_t(U)) = \int_{\Phi_t(U)} \operatorname{div}X\,d\mathrm{vol}.$$ Consequences: $\operatorname{div}X\equiv 0$ ŌćÆ flow preserves volume (conservative). $\operatorname{div}X<0$ everywhere ŌćÆ volumes shrink exponentially (dissipative).

Hamiltonian systems are the canonical conservative example. With coordinates $(q,p)$ on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ and Hamiltonian $H$, the flow $\dot q=\partial H/\partial p,\ \dot p=-\partial H/\partial q$ has

$\operatorname{div}X = \sum_i \dfrac{\partial^2 H}{\partial q_i\partial p_i} - \sum_i \dfrac{\partial^2 H}{\partial p_i\partial q_i} = 0$

by equality of mixed partials. In fact Hamiltonian flows preserve much more than volume: they preserve the symplectic form $\omega=\sum dq_i\wedge dp_i$.

The Lorenz system is the poster child for dissipation: $\operatorname{div}X = -\sigma-1-\beta = -41/3\approx -13.67$ with classical parameters, so any 3-volume shrinks by a factor of $e^{-41/3}\approx 1.2\times 10^{-6}$ each unit time. The attractor (the $t\to\infty$ limit of forward images of a ball of initial conditions) therefore has Lebesgue 3-volume zero ŌĆö yet it attracts a set of positive 3-volume. Reconciling these facts is exactly what "strange attractor" means.

10. Poincar├® sections and return maps

How do you study a cycle without getting tangled in continuous time? Cut across it. Choose a codimension-1 submanifold $\Sigma\subset M$ transverse to the flow, and look at the sequence of hits.

Poincar├® map. Let $\Sigma$ be smooth, codimension $1$, and transverse to $X$ at $p^*\in\Sigma$. For $x$ near $p^*$ there is a smooth first-return time $\tau(x)$ with $\Phi_{\tau(x)}(x)\in\Sigma$. The first-return map is $P\colon\Sigma\to\Sigma$, $P(x)=\Phi_{\tau(x)}(x)$. $P$ is a diffeomorphism of a neighbourhood of $p^*$ into $\Sigma$; $\tau$ is smooth by the implicit function theorem applied to the crossing condition.

A periodic orbit crossing $\Sigma$ once per period becomes a fixed point of $P$. The stability of the cycle = the stability of that fixed point of $P$. Its derivative $dP(p^*)$ has eigenvalues called Floquet multipliers; the cycle is attracting iff all have modulus less than 1 (except for the trivial direction along the flow, which gets collapsed by working on $\Sigma$). A 3D flow ŌåÆ 2D map ŌåÆ limit cycle stability reduces to a 2├Ś2 eigenvalue problem.

Poincar├® sections also turn chaos into something you can draw. For a forced pendulum or the R├Čssler system, taking $\Sigma=\{z=0,\dot z>0\}$ produces a scatter plot of return points ŌĆö typically a fractal curve or a thin twisted sheet ŌĆö that lets you see the chaotic attractor one slice at a time. 11. Symbolic dynamics

Symbolic dynamics translates a dynamical system into a language on an alphabet. Partition state space into finitely many labelled cells $A_0,\ldots,A_{k-1}$; the itinerary of $x$ is the sequence $\sigma(x)=(a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots)$ where $a_n$ is the label of the cell containing $f^n(x)$. Under mild conditions the map $\sigma$ becomes a topological conjugacy ŌĆö or at least a semiconjugacy ŌĆö to the shift $\sigma_s\colon (s_0,s_1,\ldots)\mapsto (s_1,s_2,\ldots)$ on the sequence space $\Omega_k=\{0,\ldots,k-1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$.

The cleanest example is the doubling map $f(x)=2x\bmod 1$ on $[0,1)$. Binary-expand $x=0.b_1b_2b_3\ldots_2$. Then $f(x)=0.b_2b_3b_4\ldots_2$ ŌĆö the shift on binary sequences, word for word. The map is topologically conjugate to $(\sigma_s,\Omega_2)$ everywhere except at dyadic rationals (which have two binary expansions, a countable measure-zero set).

Why bother? Because on $\Omega_k$ the dynamics is trivially transparent: a sequence is periodic of period $n$ iff it repeats after $n$ shifts; periodic points are dense (fix any prefix, repeat forever); transitivity is "a sequence containing every finite word generates a dense orbit." And every claim transports back to the original system via the conjugacy. Counting period-$n$ points of the full $k$-shift is elementary: there are $k^n$ length-$n$ words, so $k^n$ fixed points of $\sigma_s^n$.

12. Ergodicity and invariant measures

Add a measure, and time averages turn into space averages. A measurable map $T\colon X\to X$ on a probability space $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu)$ is measure-preserving if $\mu(T^{-1}A)=\mu(A)$ for every $A\in\mathcal{B}$. It is ergodic if every $T$-invariant measurable set has measure $0$ or $1$.

Birkhoff ergodic theorem. Let $T$ be measure-preserving on $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu)$ with $\mu(X)=1$, and $f\in L^1(\mu)$. Then the time average $$A_N f(x)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}f(T^k x)$$ converges almost everywhere to a $T$-invariant function $\bar f\in L^1$. If $T$ is ergodic, then $\bar f = \int f\,d\mu$ a.e.: time average = space average.

This is the mathematical statement of the ergodic hypothesis. On the doubling map, $\mu=$ Lebesgue is invariant and ergodic; applying Birkhoff to the indicator of $[1/2,1)$ recovers the Borel normal-numbers theorem: for a.e. $x\in[0,1)$, the asymptotic frequency of $1$'s in the binary expansion of $x$ equals $1/2$. Applying Birkhoff to $f(x)=x$ gives a.e. convergence of $\tfrac{1}{N}\sum T^k(x)$ to $\int_0^1 x\,dx=1/2$.

Ergodicity is a rigidity: no non-trivial observable remains conserved under iteration. Irrational rotations $T(x)=x+\alpha\bmod 1$ are ergodic (in fact uniquely ergodic); rational rotations split $[0,1)$ into finitely many cycles and are NOT ergodic; the identity is maximally non-ergodic. Bernoulli shifts on $\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$ with product measure are mixing (hence ergodic) for every $p\in(0,1)$.

13. Strange attractors

A strange attractor is an invariant set $\Lambda$ that

  • attracts a neighbourhood of positive Lebesgue volume,
  • has sensitive dependence on initial conditions on $\Lambda$,
  • has a fractal (non-integer) dimension ŌĆö and in a dissipative flow, necessarily Lebesgue-measure zero.

The canonical 3D example is the Lorenz system:

$\dot x = \sigma(y-x),\quad \dot y = \rho x - y - xz,\quad \dot z = xy - \beta z,$

with classical parameters $(\sigma,\beta,\rho)=(10,8/3,28)$. Three fixed points: the origin (a saddle), and two symmetric points $C_\pm=(\pm\sqrt{\beta(\rho-1)},\pm\sqrt{\beta(\rho-1)},\rho-1)$. For $\rho>\rho_H\approx 24.74$, $C_\pm$ lose stability in a subcritical Hopf bifurcation; by $\rho=28$ all three equilibria are unstable, yet trajectories remain bounded ŌĆö and they wrap around $C_\pm$ alternately on the famous butterfly-wing surface. Divergence is $-\sigma-1-\beta=-41/3$; the attractor therefore has zero 3-volume. Its box-counting dimension, computed numerically, is $\approx 2.06$.

Why "strange" is hard to prove. For the Lorenz system specifically, the existence of the strange attractor was conjectured by Lorenz in 1963 and only rigorously established by Warwick Tucker in 1999, using interval arithmetic and careful numerical analysis. The R├Čssler attractor $\dot x=-y-z,\ \dot y=x+ay,\ \dot z=b+z(x-c)$ is a simpler model whose chaos is easier to analyze; it inhabits a similar fractal folded sheet.

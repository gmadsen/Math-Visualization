Commutative algebra, from primes to dimension

Rings, their ideals, and the geometry hiding inside them. A working tour through the six things you actually use: spectra, modules, localization, flatness, Nakayama, and Krull dimension.

1. Ideals, primes, and the spectrum

Every ring in sight is commutative with $1$. A prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}\subset A$ is one where $A/\mathfrak{p}$ is an integral domain; maximal if $A/\mathfrak{p}$ is a field. The spectrum

$\mathrm{Spec}(A) = \{\mathfrak{p}\subset A : \mathfrak{p}\text{ prime}\}$

is a topological space with closed sets $V(I)=\{\mathfrak{p}:I\subseteq\mathfrak{p}\}$. Two pictures to keep side by side:

arithmetic $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ is the primes $(2),(3),(5),\dots$ plus a generic point $(0)$. Each closed point is a "residue $\mathbb{F}_p$".

geometric $\mathrm{Spec}(k[x,y])$ has closed points $(x-a,y-b)$ (the actual points of $\mathbb{A}^2_k$), height-1 primes $(f)$ for irreducible $f$ (curves), and the generic point $(0)$.

Ideals are the fundamental subobjects. Below, the Hasse diagram of ideals for small rings. Primes are the ones sitting "below" chain tops in a very particular sense — geometrically, closed irreducible subsets.

In $\mathbb{Z}/n$, the ideals correspond to divisors of $n$: $(d)$ for $d|n$. The prime ideals are $(p)$ for primes $p|n$, and in $\mathbb{Z}/n$ every prime is maximal (Artinian). The nilradical $\mathrm{Nil}(A)=\bigcap\mathfrak{p}$ is the intersection of all primes.

2. Modules and tensor products

A module $M$ over $A$ is an abelian group with an $A$-action — "linear algebra over rings". When $A$ is a field this is just a vector space; when $A=\mathbb{Z}$ it is an abelian group. The interesting thing is that over $\mathbb{Z}$, unlike over a field, modules can have torsion: $2\cdot \bar 1 = 0$ in $\mathbb{Z}/2$.

The tensor product $M\otimes_A N$ is the universal object through which $A$-bilinear maps $M\times N\to P$ factor. Two operational rules do all the work:

$$(m_1+m_2)\otimes n = m_1\otimes n + m_2\otimes n, \qquad (am)\otimes n = m\otimes (an).$$ Consequence: $\mathbb{Z}/m \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{Z}/n \;=\; \mathbb{Z}/\gcd(m,n)$. Because $n\cdot (1\otimes 1) = 1\otimes n = 0$ and $m\cdot(1\otimes 1)=0$.

For f.g. abelian groups, tensor is additive in each slot; so by the structure theorem you can tensor piece-by-piece. This is a computation, not a theory.

Two things to notice. (i) Tensoring with $\mathbb{Q}$ kills torsion: $\mathbb{Z}/n\otimes_\mathbb{Z}\mathbb{Q}=0$. (ii) $\mathbb{Z}\otimes_\mathbb{Z}\mathbb{Z}/n=\mathbb{Z}/n$ (identity). Over $\mathbb{Z}$ the tensor product is not left-exact — we'll see this bite in §4.

3. Localization

Localization is the algebraic shadow of "zooming in". Pick a multiplicatively closed set $S\subset A$ (with $1\in S$, closed under multiplication, not containing $0$ unless you want to kill the ring). The localization

$S^{-1}A = \{a/s : a\in A, s\in S\} / \sim, \qquad a/s \sim a'/s' \iff \exists t\in S,\; t(a s' - a' s) = 0.$

The extra "$t$" is there to handle zero-divisors. Two important special cases:

$A_f$ invert a single element $f$. Geometrically: remove $V(f)$ from $\mathrm{Spec}$. $\mathrm{Spec}(A_f) = D(f)$.

$A_\mathfrak{p}$ invert everything outside a prime $\mathfrak{p}$. The result is a local ring: one maximal ideal, namely $\mathfrak{p}A_\mathfrak{p}$. This is the local ring "at the point $\mathfrak{p}$".

Primes of $S^{-1}A$ = primes of $A$ disjoint from $S$. Hence $\mathrm{Spec}(A_\mathfrak{p})=\{\mathfrak{q}\subseteq\mathfrak{p}\}$ and $\mathrm{Spec}(A_f)=\{\mathfrak{q}:f\notin\mathfrak{q}\}$. The residue field $A_\mathfrak{p}/\mathfrak{p}A_\mathfrak{p} = \mathrm{Frac}(A/\mathfrak{p})$ is the "function field of the stalk".

A clean example worth carrying around: $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$ = rationals with denominator coprime to $p$. It's local, its maximal ideal is $(p)$, its residue field is $\mathbb{F}_p$. Inverting $p$ instead gives $\mathbb{Z}[1/p]$, whose Spec is $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})\setminus\{(p)\}$.

4. Flatness

Tensoring is right-exact but not always left-exact. An $A$-module $M$ is flat if tensoring with $M$ preserves injections — equivalently, preserves all short exact sequences:

$0\to N'\to N\to N''\to 0$ remains exact after $-\otimes_A M$.

Equivalently: $\mathrm{Tor}_1^A(M,-)=0$. The implication chain is

free $\Rightarrow$ projective $\Rightarrow$ flat $\Rightarrow$ torsion-free,

and over a PID the last implication reverses, so over $\mathbb{Z}$: flat = torsion-free. Hence $\mathbb{Z}/n$ is not flat over $\mathbb{Z}$ for any $n\ge 2$.

The canonical failure. The sequence $0\to\mathbb{Z}\xrightarrow{n}\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{Z}/n\to 0$ tensored with $\mathbb{Z}/n$ gives $$\mathbb{Z}/n\xrightarrow{n=0}\mathbb{Z}/n\to\mathbb{Z}/n\to 0,$$ so the first map becomes zero and is no longer injective. The kernel is $\mathrm{Tor}_1^\mathbb{Z}(\mathbb{Z}/n,\mathbb{Z}/n)=\mathbb{Z}/n$.
Flatness tester
tensor a SES with a module M

Recipe: tensoring with $M$ is a functor, so it turns $0\to A\xrightarrow{n}B\to C\to 0$ into $A\otimes M\xrightarrow{n}B\otimes M\to C\otimes M\to 0$ (the tail is exact; the head might not be). Injectivity of "multiplication by $n$" on $A\otimes M$ is what flatness of $M$ buys you. Whenever $M$ has $n$-torsion, you lose it.

Two facts organize the rest of the theory. Local criterion. For $A$ Noetherian and $M$ finitely generated, $M$ is flat iff $M_\mathfrak{p}$ is free over $A_\mathfrak{p}$ for every prime $\mathfrak{p}$; equivalently $\mathrm{Tor}_1^A(A/\mathfrak{m},M)=0$ at every maximal ideal. Flat = geometric fibres nice. A morphism $f\colon X\to Y$ is flat iff $\mathcal{O}_{X,x}$ is flat over $\mathcal{O}_{Y,f(x)}$ everywhere; in a flat family the Hilbert polynomial of the fibre is locally constant, which is the right notion of "continuously varying scheme".

5. Nakayama's lemma

Let $(A,\mathfrak{m})$ be a local ring (one maximal ideal) with residue field $k=A/\mathfrak{m}$.

Nakayama. If $M$ is a finitely generated $A$-module with $\mathfrak{m}M=M$, then $M=0$.

The standard use: $M/\mathfrak{m}M$ is a $k$-vector space, and a family $m_1,\dots,m_r\in M$ generates $M$ iff their images generate $M/\mathfrak{m}M$. So the dimension of $M/\mathfrak{m}M$ is the minimal number of generators.

Applied to $M=\mathfrak{m}$ itself: $\mathfrak{m}/\mathfrak{m}^2$ is a finite-dimensional $k$-vector space whose dual is the Zariski tangent space at the closed point. For a regular local ring this dimension equals $\dim A$; the discrepancy $\dim_k(\mathfrak{m}/\mathfrak{m}^2) - \dim A$ measures singularity.

For $A = k[x_1,\dots,x_n]_{(x_1-a_1,\dots,x_n-a_n)}/(f)$ (the local ring of $V(f)$ at a point $p=(a_1,\dots,a_n)$), the Zariski tangent space is simply the kernel of the Jacobian of $f$ evaluated at $p$:

$T_pV(f) = \ker\left(\left.\nabla f\right|_p\colon k^n\to k\right).$

If $V(f)$ has dimension $n-1$ and $T_p$ has dimension $n-1$ also, $p$ is smooth. If $T_p = k^n$ — i.e. all partials vanish — $p$ is singular.

The proof of Nakayama is a one-line determinant trick: if $m_1,\dots,m_n$ generate $M$ and $\mathfrak{m}M=M$, write each $m_i=\sum_j a_{ij}m_j$ with $a_{ij}\in\mathfrak{m}$; Cayley–Hamilton applied to the matrix $a=(a_{ij})$ yields $\det(I-a)\cdot m_i = 0$ for all $i$. Since $\det(I-a)\equiv 1\pmod{\mathfrak{m}}$ it is a unit, forcing $M=0$. The same trick gives the determinantal form: if $\phi\colon M\to M$ satisfies $\phi(M)\subset\mathfrak{a}M$ for an ideal $\mathfrak{a}$, then $\phi$ satisfies a monic polynomial with non-leading coefficients in $\mathfrak{a}$.

6. Integral extensions and Noether normalization

Fix a ring extension $A\subseteq B$. An element $b\in B$ is integral over $A$ if it satisfies a monic polynomial

$b^n + a_{n-1}b^{n-1} + \cdots + a_1 b + a_0 = 0,\qquad a_i\in A.$

Equivalently, $A[b]\subseteq B$ is a finitely generated $A$-module. The set of integral elements is a subring — the integral closure of $A$ in $B$ — and $B$ is an integral extension when every element is integral. This generalizes "algebraic number" from $\mathbb{Z}\subseteq\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$ to arbitrary rings; $\mathcal{O}_K$ is the integral closure of $\mathbb{Z}$ in the number field $K$.

Three foundational theorems.
  • Lying over. For $A\subseteq B$ integral and $\mathfrak{p}\subset A$ prime, there exists a prime $\mathfrak{q}\subset B$ with $\mathfrak{q}\cap A=\mathfrak{p}$; the induced map $\mathrm{Spec}(B)\to\mathrm{Spec}(A)$ is surjective.
  • Going up. A chain $\mathfrak{p}_0\subsetneq\mathfrak{p}_1\subsetneq\cdots$ in $A$ lifts to a chain $\mathfrak{q}_0\subsetneq\mathfrak{q}_1\subsetneq\cdots$ in $B$ above it; hence $\dim A=\dim B$ for integral extensions.
  • Incomparability. If $\mathfrak{q}\subsetneq\mathfrak{q}'$ are primes of $B$ with $\mathfrak{q}\cap A=\mathfrak{q}'\cap A$, then $\mathfrak{q}=\mathfrak{q}'$ — fibers are zero-dimensional.

Noether normalization. If $B$ is a finitely generated $k$-algebra of Krull dimension $d$, there exist algebraically independent $y_1,\dots,y_d\in B$ with $k[y_1,\dots,y_d]\hookrightarrow B$ a finite (in particular integral) extension. Geometrically: every affine variety admits a finite surjection to some $\mathbb{A}^d_k$, projecting it onto a polynomial coordinate system. This is the engine behind Hilbert's Nullstellensatz: maximal ideals of $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ with $k=\overline k$ are exactly $(x_1-a_1,\dots,x_n-a_n)$, proved by showing any residue field $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]/\mathfrak{m}$ is finite over $k$ via normalization, hence equals $k$.

Concretely: for $B=k[x,y]/(y^2-x^3-x)$ one can take $y_1=x$; then $B$ is a degree-2 extension of $k[x]$, realized by the covering map $(x,y)\mapsto x$ that exhibits the elliptic curve as a double cover of the line branched at the roots of $x^3+x$. Going-up is why this projection is surjective on points.

7. Dimension and primary decomposition

The Krull dimension of a ring $A$ is the supremum of lengths $n$ of chains

$\mathfrak{p}_0\subsetneq\mathfrak{p}_1\subsetneq\cdots\subsetneq\mathfrak{p}_n$

of prime ideals. It's the algebraic shadow of "dimension of a variety". The basic table:

$\dim \mathbb{Z} = 1$ (chains $(0)\subsetneq(p)$) · $\dim k = 0$ · $\dim k[x] = 1$ · $\dim k[x_1,\dots,x_n] = n$ · $\dim \mathbb{Z}[x] = 2$. In general $\dim A[x]=\dim A + 1$ for $A$ Noetherian.

For quotients: $\dim A/I$ = longest chain of primes of $A$ containing $I$. Example: $k[x,y]/(xy)$ has $\dim = 1$: its Spec is two coordinate lines meeting at the origin; the longest chain is (generic point of one line) $\subsetneq$ (origin).

Primary decomposition generalizes the fundamental theorem of arithmetic. Every ideal in a Noetherian ring admits $I=\bigcap_i Q_i$ with each $Q_i$ primary; the associated primes $\sqrt{Q_i}$ are intrinsic. For $(n)\subset\mathbb{Z}$ this is just $n=\prod p_i^{e_i}$.

Notice how $k[x,y]/(xy)$ (union of axes) has dimension $1$ even though it sits inside the $2$-dimensional affine plane: Krull dimension sees the biggest component. The height of a prime is its length as a chain ending there; the coheight (dimension of the closure) complements it to $\dim A$ in nice cases (catenary rings).

Primary decomposition preview

For $I=(xy)\subset k[x,y]$ the decomposition is $I=(x)\cap(y)$, recovering the two irreducible components. For $I=(x^2, xy)=(x)\cap(x,y)^2$ in $k[x,y]$ — two components, one embedded in the other — primary decomposition is how scheme-theoretic multiplicity makes its appearance.

The pattern running through these six sections: every classical operation (taking a subvariety, zooming in, linearizing, perturbing) has an exact algebraic shadow — quotient, localization, tangent space via $\mathfrak{m}/\mathfrak{m}^2$, flatness. Commutative algebra is the language that lets you turn geometric questions into finite computations on ideals and modules, and vice versa. The payoff, once you start using schemes seriously, is that the two pictures collapse into one.

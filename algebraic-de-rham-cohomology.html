Algebraic de rham cohomology

Hypercohomology of $\Omega^\bullet_{X/k}$: Hodge filtration, Betti comparison, Hodge diamond.

1. Kähler differentials $\Omega^1_{X/k}$

The classical de Rham theorem identifies smooth de Rham cohomology of a real manifold with its singular cohomology with $\mathbb{R}$-coefficients, and the proof uses partitions of unity — a tool that is unavailable on a scheme over a general field. Grothendieck's algebraic de Rham cohomology side-steps the analytic input entirely: the differential forms are constructed by a universal property in commutative algebra, the differential is forced by the Leibniz rule, and the resulting cohomology theory matches singular cohomology over $\mathbb{C}$ but also makes sense over $k = \overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ or a $p$-adic field. The starting point is a single sheaf, $\Omega^1_{X/k}$, the module of Kähler differentials.

Universal property. For $A$ a $k$-algebra, $\Omega^1_{A/k}$ is the $A$-module characterised by a $k$-derivation $d\colon A \to \Omega^1_{A/k}$ such that any other $k$-derivation $D\colon A \to M$ into an $A$-module factors uniquely through $d$: $$\mathrm{Hom}_A(\Omega^1_{A/k}, M) \;\xrightarrow{\sim}\; \mathrm{Der}_k(A, M), \qquad \varphi \longmapsto \varphi \circ d.$$ Concretely, $\Omega^1_{A/k}$ is generated as an $A$-module by symbols $\{da : a \in A\}$ subject to the three relations forced by being a $k$-derivation: $k$-linearity ($d\lambda = 0$ for $\lambda \in k$), additivity ($d(a + b) = da + db$), and Leibniz ($d(ab) = a\,db + b\,da$). Sheafifying gives the coherent sheaf $\Omega^1_{X/k}$ on $X$ — for affine $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,A$ it is just $\widetilde{\Omega^1_{A/k}}$, and the construction glues because the universal property is local.

The smooth case. When $X/k$ is smooth of dimension $n$, $\Omega^1_{X/k}$ is locally free of rank $n$ — étale-locally it has a basis $dx_1, \ldots, dx_n$ for any étale coordinates $x_1, \ldots, x_n$. The exterior powers $\Omega^p_{X/k} := \bigwedge^p \Omega^1_{X/k}$ are then locally free of rank $\binom{n}{p}$, and the top-exterior $\omega_X := \Omega^n_{X/k}$ is the canonical line bundle. The non-smooth case is exactly where this fails: $\Omega^1_{X/k}$ retains the universal property but stops being locally free, and the rank jumps at singular points. This is why "smooth" is the hypothesis under which de Rham cohomology behaves classically.

Two fundamental exact sequences control how $\Omega^1$ moves through morphisms. For $f\colon X \to Y$ a morphism of $k$-schemes there is a relative cotangent sequence $f^*\Omega^1_{Y/k} \to \Omega^1_{X/k} \to \Omega^1_{X/Y} \to 0$ (exact on the right), and for $i\colon Z \hookrightarrow X$ a closed immersion cut out by an ideal $\mathcal{I}$ there is a conormal sequence $\mathcal{I}/\mathcal{I}^2 \to i^*\Omega^1_{X/k} \to \Omega^1_{Z/k} \to 0$ (also right-exact, with leftmost term $\mathcal{I}/\mathcal{I}^2$ the conormal sheaf of $Z$ in $X$). Both become left-exact in the smooth setting and underlie almost every $\Omega$-computation.

Worked example IV: a singular curve. For the cuspidal cubic $A = k[x, y]/(y^2 - x^3)$, the conormal sequence reads $(y^2 - x^3) / (y^2 - x^3)^2 \to k[x, y]\,dx \oplus k[x, y]\,dy \to \Omega^1_{A/k} \to 0$. The middle map sends $y^2 - x^3 \mapsto 2y\,dy - 3x^2\,dx$, so $\Omega^1_{A/k}$ is the cokernel — at the cusp $(0, 0)$ the relation $2y\,dy - 3x^2\,dx = 0$ degenerates and $\Omega^1$ jumps in rank. Smoothness of $\Omega^1$ is exactly smoothness of $X$.

2. The algebraic de Rham complex $\Omega^\bullet_{X/k}$

The exterior derivative on $\Omega^1$ extends uniquely to a $k$-linear differential $d\colon \Omega^p_{X/k} \to \Omega^{p+1}_{X/k}$ on each exterior power, characterised by $d(\alpha \wedge \beta) = d\alpha \wedge \beta + (-1)^{\deg \alpha} \alpha \wedge d\beta$ and $d(da) = 0$. The two relations together force $d^2 = 0$ on every $\Omega^p$ — the same calculation as in smooth de Rham, but now an algebraic identity in $\Omega^\bullet_{X/k}$. The result is a complex of quasi-coherent (sometimes coherent, on a smooth $X$) sheaves $$\Omega^\bullet_{X/k} : \quad \mathcal{O}_X \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^1_{X/k} \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^2_{X/k} \xrightarrow{d} \cdots \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^n_{X/k}$$ of length $\dim X$ on a smooth variety. The differentials are not $\mathcal{O}_X$-linear (Leibniz costs an $\mathcal{O}_X$-multiplication) — only $k$-linear — so $\Omega^\bullet_{X/k}$ is a complex in $\mathrm{D}(k\text{-mod on } X_{\mathrm{Zar}})$, not in $\mathrm{D}(\mathrm{QCoh}(X))$.

Algebraic de Rham cohomology is the hypercohomology of this complex: $$H^n_{dR}(X/k) := \mathbb{H}^n(X, \Omega^\bullet_{X/k}).$$ The hypercohomology gadget is needed because $\Omega^\bullet_{X/k}$ is a complex of sheaves, not a single sheaf — naïvely taking $H^n$ of global sections forgets the higher sheaf cohomology of each individual $\Omega^p$. There are two equivalent computational handles. (i) The Čech-de-Rham double complex: on an affine cover $\{U_i\}$ build the double complex $C^p(\{U_i\}, \Omega^q_{X/k})$ with horizontal differential Čech and vertical differential de Rham; the total complex's cohomology is $H^*_{dR}(X/k)$. (ii) The Hodge-to-de-Rham spectral sequence: $$E_1^{p, q} = H^q(X, \Omega^p_{X/k}) \;\Longrightarrow\; H^{p+q}_{dR}(X/k),$$ which on a smooth projective variety over $\mathbb{C}$ degenerates at $E_1$ (Deligne's theorem) — the source of the Hodge decomposition.

Affine vs. projective. On affine $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,A$, every $\Omega^p$ is acyclic ($H^q(X, \Omega^p) = 0$ for $q \ge 1$) and the spectral sequence collapses to give $H^n_{dR}(X/k) = H^n(\Omega^\bullet_{A/k})$ — the cohomology of global sections, no sheafy correction needed. So computing $H^n_{dR}$ on $\mathbb{A}^n_k$ is just polynomial algebra. On projective $X$ the higher $H^q(X, \Omega^p)$ generally do not vanish, and the Čech computation or the spectral sequence is genuinely needed — most of the interesting Hodge theory lives there.

3. Comparison with Betti cohomology

Grothendieck's algebraic-de-Rham theorem (1966): for $X$ a smooth (not necessarily proper) variety over $\mathbb{C}$, there is a canonical isomorphism $$H^*_{dR}(X/\mathbb{C}) \;\xrightarrow{\sim}\; H^*(X(\mathbb{C}); \mathbb{C}),$$ where the right-hand side is the singular cohomology of the underlying complex-analytic space with $\mathbb{C}$-coefficients. This is striking: the left-hand side is computed entirely in commutative algebra — modules of differentials of polynomial rings, hypercohomology of a complex of coherent sheaves on the Zariski site — while the right-hand side is the topology of $X$ as a CW-complex. The bridge is constructed by comparing the algebraic complex $\Omega^\bullet_{X/\mathbb{C}}$ with the analytic de Rham complex $\Omega^{\bullet, \mathrm{an}}_{X^{\mathrm{an}}}$ and invoking the Poincaré lemma plus GAGA.

Why is this a theorem at all? A priori the algebraic forms are vastly more restricted than smooth or analytic forms — only polynomial / rational coefficients are allowed. The miracle is that on a smooth complex variety the inclusion of complexes $\Omega^\bullet_{X/\mathbb{C}} \hookrightarrow \Omega^{\bullet, \mathrm{an}}_{X^{\mathrm{an}}}$ is a quasi-isomorphism on each Zariski open: the analytic Poincaré lemma says the analytic complex is a resolution of the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{C}}$, and a result of Grothendieck (the ancestor of GAGA in this context) says the algebraic complex on $X^{\mathrm{an}}$ already computes the same thing. Singular = $H^*(X^{\mathrm{an}}; \underline{\mathbb{C}})$ then closes the loop.

Worked example — the punctured line $\mathbb{C}^*$. Take $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[z, z^{-1}]$. As a topological space $X(\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}^*$ deformation-retracts onto the unit circle, so $H^0(\mathbb{C}^*; \mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}$, $H^1(\mathbb{C}^*; \mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}$, all higher $= 0$. Algebraically: $\Omega^1_{X/\mathbb{C}} = \mathbb{C}[z, z^{-1}] \cdot dz$. The differential $d\colon \mathbb{C}[z, z^{-1}] \to \mathbb{C}[z, z^{-1}]\,dz$ sends $z^n \mapsto n\,z^{n-1}\,dz$. Image $= \mathrm{span}\{z^{n-1}\,dz : n \ne 0\}$ — every $z^{n-1}\,dz$ except $z^{-1}\,dz$. Hence $H^1_{dR}(X/\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C} \cdot \frac{dz}{z}$, recovering the topological $H^1$ with explicit algebraic generator $\omega = \frac{dz}{z}$.

This generator is the algebraic shadow of the winding-number cocycle: integrating $\frac{dz}{z}$ over the unit circle gives $2\pi i$, the canonical pairing between $H^1_{dR}$ and $H_1^{\mathrm{sing}}$. The same form makes sense over any field (it's a regular 1-form on $\mathbb{G}_m = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[z, z^{-1}]$), and its image in $H^1_{dR}(\mathbb{G}_m / k)$ is always the generator — the algebraic theory predicts the Betti answer.

Caveats and refinements. The comparison fails if $X$ is singular (Grothendieck's theorem is for smooth $X$; you need de Rham of a desingularisation, or use Du Bois / mixed-Hodge complexes). It fails for non-finite-type schemes — the polynomial / rational-functions side becomes too small. Over a non-algebraically-closed base $k \subset \mathbb{C}$ the comparison still holds after extending scalars: $H^*_{dR}(X/k) \otimes_k \mathbb{C} \xrightarrow{\sim} H^*(X(\mathbb{C}); \mathbb{C})$. And the sharper Deligne version equips both sides with extra structure (mixed Hodge, weight filtration) so that the isomorphism respects more than just dimensions.

Period numbers and transcendence. The comparison is canonical over $\mathbb{C}$ but over a smaller field $k$ it relates two distinct $k$-vector spaces: $H^*_{dR}(X/k)$ and $H^*(X(\mathbb{C}); k)$ (when $k \subset \mathbb{C}$). The change-of-basis matrix between them, computed via integrals like $\int_\gamma \omega$ for $\omega \in \Omega^\bullet$ and $\gamma$ a topological cycle, is the period matrix of $X$. For $X = \mathbb{G}_m$ the period is $2\pi i$; for elliptic curves the periods are the lattice generators; the Grothendieck period conjecture predicts the transcendence degree of the field generated by all periods of $X$.

4. The Hodge filtration $F^\bullet$

The de Rham complex $\Omega^\bullet_{X/k}$ comes equipped with a tautological decreasing filtration: forget the first $p$ terms. The trivial truncation $\sigma^{\ge p} \Omega^\bullet_{X/k}$ is the subcomplex $$0 \to 0 \to \cdots \to 0 \to \Omega^p_{X/k} \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^{p+1}_{X/k} \xrightarrow{d} \cdots,$$ where the first $p$ entries are zero. The natural inclusion $\sigma^{\ge p} \Omega^\bullet \hookrightarrow \Omega^\bullet$ induces a map on hypercohomology, and the Hodge filtration is its image: $$F^p H^n_{dR}(X/k) := \mathrm{im}\big(\mathbb{H}^n(X, \sigma^{\ge p} \Omega^\bullet) \to H^n_{dR}(X/k)\big).$$ This gives a chain $H^n_{dR}(X/k) = F^0 \supseteq F^1 \supseteq F^2 \supseteq \cdots$ that eventually drops to zero (after $n + 1$ steps for $n$-forms with $\dim X = n$).

The Hodge-to-de-Rham spectral sequence. The trivial truncation is the canonical filtration whose associated graded computes $H^q$ of the cokernel — i.e. $H^q(X, \Omega^p_{X/k})$ — and its spectral sequence is exactly the Hodge-to-de-Rham one we met in §2: $$E_1^{p, q} = H^q(X, \Omega^p_{X/k}) \;\Longrightarrow\; H^{p+q}_{dR}(X/k).$$ The associated graded of $F^\bullet$ on $H^n_{dR}$ is $\mathrm{gr}^p_F H^n = E_\infty^{p, n-p}$. Deligne's degeneration theorem: for $X$ smooth proper over $\mathbb{C}$ (and more generally smooth proper over any field of characteristic $0$, by Deligne–Illusie even for many positive-characteristic cases), this spectral sequence degenerates at $E_1$, so $\mathrm{gr}^p_F H^n_{dR}(X/\mathbb{C}) = H^{n-p}(X, \Omega^p_X)$.

Hodge decomposition. Combined with complex conjugation, $E_1$-degeneration upgrades the filtration to a direct-sum decomposition over $\mathbb{C}$: $$H^n(X(\mathbb{C}); \mathbb{C}) = H^n_{dR}(X/\mathbb{C}) = \bigoplus_{p + q = n} H^{p, q}(X), \qquad H^{p, q}(X) := H^q(X, \Omega^p_X),$$ with $\overline{H^{p, q}} = H^{q, p}$ (complex conjugation swaps $p$ and $q$, because conjugating an algebraic form swaps "holomorphic-degree" and "antiholomorphic-degree" in the underlying $C^\infty$ picture). The Hodge filtration is recovered as $F^p H^n = \bigoplus_{p' \ge p} H^{p', n-p'}$.

Worked example — $X = \mathbb{P}^n$. Bott vanishing gives $H^q(\mathbb{P}^n, \Omega^p) = \mathbb{C}$ if $p = q \le n$ and $0$ otherwise. So $H^{2k}_{dR}(\mathbb{P}^n) = H^{k, k}(\mathbb{P}^n) = \mathbb{C}$ and all odd-degree $H^{2k+1}_{dR}(\mathbb{P}^n) = 0$, with Hodge filtration $F^k H^{2k} = \mathbb{C}$, $F^{k+1} H^{2k} = 0$. For $\mathbb{P}^1$ this gives $F^0 H^0 = F^1 H^2 = \mathbb{C}$, all other steps trivial — a one-step filtration on each piece.

Why the Hodge filtration is robust. Three properties make $F^\bullet$ the right structure to track. (i) Functoriality: $F^\bullet$ is preserved by pullback along morphisms of smooth proper varieties, so $f^*\colon H^n_{dR}(Y) \to H^n_{dR}(X)$ is a morphism of filtered vector spaces. (ii) Defined over $k$: unlike the Hodge decomposition $H^n = \bigoplus H^{p,q}$ (which uses complex conjugation and only exists over $\mathbb{C}$), the filtration $F^p H^n_{dR}(X/k)$ is defined for $X/k$ over any base — this is what makes "Hodge filtration" a well-formed concept in $p$-adic Hodge theory or over $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$. (iii) Variation: in a family $X \to S$, the filtration $F^\bullet$ varies holomorphically with the parameter (Griffiths transversality: $\nabla F^p \subseteq F^{p-1} \otimes \Omega^1_S$), giving the structure of a variation of Hodge structure.

5. Hodge numbers $h^{p,q}(X)$

The Hodge numbers of a smooth projective variety $X$ over $\mathbb{C}$ are the dimensions $$h^{p, q}(X) := \dim_\mathbb{C} H^q(X, \Omega^p_X).$$ Each $h^{p,q}$ is a coherent-sheaf cohomology dimension — computable, in principle, by Čech cohomology on a finite affine cover or by Serre duality plus a vanishing theorem. They are arranged in the Hodge diamond: rows indexed by $n = p + q$ (the de Rham degree), columns by $p$ (the holomorphic degree), with row $n$ containing $h^{0, n}, h^{1, n-1}, \ldots, h^{n, 0}$. By $E_1$-degeneration the $n$-th Betti number is the row sum $b_n = \sum_{p + q = n} h^{p, q}$, so the diamond simultaneously refines and reconstructs the topology.

Two symmetries make the diamond a diamond. First, Serre duality on a smooth projective $X$ of dimension $n$ gives $H^q(X, \Omega^p) \cong H^{n-q}(X, \Omega^{n-p})^\vee$, so $$h^{p, q} = h^{n - p, n - q} \qquad (\text{Serre duality / antipodal symmetry}).$$ Second, complex conjugation acts on $H^n(X(\mathbb{C}); \mathbb{C}) = \bigoplus H^{p, q}$ swapping $H^{p, q}$ with $H^{q, p}$, so $$h^{p, q} = h^{q, p} \qquad (\text{conjugation / reflection across the diagonal}).$$ The Klein four-group generated by these two reflections fixes any cell in the diamond up to its three orbits — exactly the diamond's $D_2$-symmetry.

Worked example — $X = \mathbb{P}^n_\mathbb{C}$. The Bott vanishing theorem gives $h^{p, q}(\mathbb{P}^n) = 1$ if $p = q \le n$ and $0$ otherwise. The diamond is concentrated on the diagonal: $h^{0, 0} = h^{1, 1} = \cdots = h^{n, n} = 1$. So $b_{2k}(\mathbb{P}^n) = 1$ and $b_{2k+1} = 0$, recovering the cell decomposition of complex projective space. Note both symmetries: $h^{k,k} = h^{n-k, n-k}$ ✓ and $h^{k,k} = h^{k,k}$ ✓ trivially.

Worked example — a K3 surface. $X$ smooth projective of complex dimension $2$ with $K_X \cong \mathcal{O}_X$ and $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}) = 0$. Serre duality plus the Calabi–Yau hypothesis force the diamond: $$\begin{array}{ccccc} & & 1 & & \\ & 0 & & 0 & \\ 1 & & 20 & & 1 \\ & 0 & & 0 & \\ & & 1 & & \end{array}$$ $h^{2, 0} = h^{0, 2} = 1$ are the holomorphic / antiholomorphic 2-forms; $h^{1, 1} = 20$ comes from $\chi(\mathcal{O}_X) = 2$ + Euler characteristic $= 24$ + Noether's formula. Total Betti: $1 + 0 + 22 + 0 + 1 = 24$, the topological Euler characteristic of any K3.

6. Worked example: de Rham of a smooth curve

Curves are the cleanest place to compute. Let $C$ be a smooth projective curve of genus $g$ over $\mathbb{C}$. The de Rham complex is short — $\Omega^\bullet_C : \mathcal{O}_C \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^1_C$, just two terms — and the Hodge spectral sequence has only two columns. Even better: the spectral sequence degenerates at $E_1$ for trivial dimensional reasons (no room for higher $d_r$), so the Hodge decomposition is automatic on a curve.

The four Hodge numbers. By Serre duality, $H^1(C, \mathcal{O}_C) \cong H^0(C, \Omega^1_C)^\vee$ and $H^1(C, \Omega^1_C) \cong H^0(C, \mathcal{O}_C)^\vee$. The two on-the-right hand sides are easy: $H^0(C, \mathcal{O}_C) = \mathbb{C}$ (one global function — the constants) and $H^0(C, \Omega^1_C) = \mathbb{C}^g$ (the space of holomorphic 1-forms — this is the analytic definition of $g$, equivalent to the topological/arithmetic ones). So $$h^{0, 0} = 1, \quad h^{1, 0} = g, \quad h^{0, 1} = g, \quad h^{1, 1} = 1.$$

The Hodge diamond of $C$: $$\begin{array}{ccc} & 1 & \\ g & & g \\ & 1 & \end{array}$$ The four cells encode: top — connectedness; middle row — holomorphic and antiholomorphic 1-forms (their dimensions equal by complex conjugation); bottom — the canonical class. Symmetry: $h^{p, q} = h^{q, p}$ holds (middle row), $h^{p, q} = h^{1-p, 1-q}$ holds (corners ↔ corners).

Computing $H^*_{dR}$. Reading off from the diamond and $E_1$-degeneration: $$H^0_{dR}(C/\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}, \qquad H^1_{dR}(C/\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}^{2g}, \qquad H^2_{dR}(C/\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}.$$ The total Betti is $1 + 2g + 1 = 2g + 2$, which equals the topological Euler characteristic $\chi(C) = 2 - 2g$ plus the alternating-sum reformulation $\sum (-1)^i b_i = 2 - 2g$ ✓. The Hodge filtration on $H^1_{dR}$ has just one nontrivial step: $F^1 H^1 = H^0(C, \Omega^1_C)$ (the holomorphic 1-forms, dimension $g$), $F^0 H^1 = H^1_{dR}$ (everything, dimension $2g$). The quotient $\mathrm{gr}^0_F = H^1(C, \mathcal{O}_C)$ has dimension $g$.

Worked example — an elliptic curve. $g = 1$. Diamond cells $1, 1, 1, 1$. $H^1_{dR} = \mathbb{C}^2$ with $F^1 = \mathbb{C}\langle dx/y \rangle$ and quotient $\mathbb{C}\langle x\,dx/y \rangle$ (in Weierstrass coordinates $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$). The first form is holomorphic; the second is a "second-kind" form — algebraic, with poles, but representing a non-trivial de Rham class. The two together give the period lattice $\mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2 \subset \mathbb{C}$ that defines the complex torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda \cong E$.

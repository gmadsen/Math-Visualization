Probability theory

Measure-theoretic probability from sample spaces to Brownian motion: random variables as push-forwards, expectation as a Lebesgue integral, the law of large numbers and central limit theorem as convergence theorems, and Markov chains, martingales, and Brownian motion as the principal stochastic processes. Every concept comes with a toy to poke.

1. Sample spaces, events, and probability measures

A probability space is a measure space $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ where $\mathbb{P}(\Omega)=1$. Points $\omega\in\Omega$ are outcomes; measurable subsets $A\in\mathcal{F}$ are events; and the probability measure $\mathbb{P}\colon\mathcal{F}\to[0,1]$ is countably additive with $\mathbb{P}(\varnothing)=0$ and $\mathbb{P}(\Omega)=1$.

Everything in probability lives on top of this single triple. The $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}$ encodes what is "observable"; events are the questions we can legitimately ask; and $\mathbb{P}$ assigns numerical weights to those questions.

Why $\sigma$-algebras? Finite additivity — $\mathbb{P}(A\sqcup B)=\mathbb{P}(A)+\mathbb{P}(B)$ — is trivial. The leap from finite to countable is exactly what makes probability agree with limits: if $A_n\uparrow A$, then $\mathbb{P}(A_n)\uparrow\mathbb{P}(A)$. This continuity of measure is the engine behind every convergence theorem below.

Worked example: rolling two dice. Take $\Omega=\{1,\ldots,6\}^2$, $\mathcal{F}=\mathcal{P}(\Omega)$ (finite sample space — every subset is measurable), and $\mathbb{P}=\tfrac{1}{36}\#$. Then the event "sum $\ge 10$" is $\{(4,6),(5,5),(5,6),(6,4),(6,5),(6,6)\}$ with probability $6/36=1/6$. Countable additivity degenerates to finite, and the entire apparatus is elementary — but the structure generalises to uncountable $\Omega$ without change.

Continuity of measure. For increasing $A_n\uparrow A$, countable additivity gives $\mathbb{P}(A)=\lim_n\mathbb{P}(A_n)$. Dually, for $A_n\downarrow A$ with $\mathbb{P}(A_1)<\infty$, $\mathbb{P}(A)=\lim\mathbb{P}(A_n)$. These are the set-theoretic twin of MCT/DCT.

2. Conditional probability and Bayes' rule

Given $\mathbb{P}(B)>0$, the conditional probability of $A$ given $B$ is $$\mathbb{P}(A\mid B)=\frac{\mathbb{P}(A\cap B)}{\mathbb{P}(B)}.$$ Two events are independent when $\mathbb{P}(A\cap B)=\mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(B)$; equivalently $\mathbb{P}(A\mid B)=\mathbb{P}(A)$.

Combining the product rule with itself flipped gives Bayes' rule: $$\mathbb{P}(A\mid B)=\frac{\mathbb{P}(B\mid A)\,\mathbb{P}(A)}{\mathbb{P}(B)}.$$ Here $\mathbb{P}(A)$ is the prior, $\mathbb{P}(B\mid A)$ the likelihood, and $\mathbb{P}(A\mid B)$ the posterior.

The law of total probability, for a partition $\{B_i\}$ of $\Omega$ with $\mathbb{P}(B_i)>0$: $$\mathbb{P}(A)=\sum_i\mathbb{P}(A\mid B_i)\,\mathbb{P}(B_i),$$ gives the denominator $\mathbb{P}(B)$ in Bayes' rule a computable form.

Worked example: medical test. A disease has prevalence $1\%$. A test has sensitivity (true-positive rate) $99\%$ and specificity (true-negative rate) $95\%$ (so false-positive rate $5\%$). Given a positive test, what is the probability of disease? $$\mathbb{P}(D\mid +)=\frac{\mathbb{P}(+\mid D)\mathbb{P}(D)}{\mathbb{P}(+\mid D)\mathbb{P}(D)+\mathbb{P}(+\mid\neg D)\mathbb{P}(\neg D)}=\frac{0.99\cdot 0.01}{0.99\cdot 0.01+0.05\cdot 0.99}\approx 16.7\%.$$ Posteriors on rare conditions are dominated by the false-positive rate — a $99\%$-accurate test produces mostly false positives when the disease is rare.

3. Random variables

A random variable on $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ is a measurable function $X\colon\Omega\to\mathbb{R}$. Measurable means: for every Borel $B\in\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, the preimage $X^{-1}(B)=\{\omega:X(\omega)\in B\}$ lies in $\mathcal{F}$ — i.e., "$X\in B$" is an event.

The law (or distribution) of $X$ is the push-forward measure $\mathbb{P}_X$ on $(\mathbb{R},\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$: $$\mathbb{P}_X(B)=\mathbb{P}(X^{-1}(B))=\mathbb{P}(X\in B).$$ Once we have $\mathbb{P}_X$, the original $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ recedes: everything we can say about $X$ alone lives on $(\mathbb{R},\mathcal{B})$.

The cumulative distribution function is $F_X(x)=\mathbb{P}(X\le x)=\mathbb{P}_X((-\infty,x])$. It is non-decreasing, right-continuous, and determines $\mathbb{P}_X$ uniquely (by the $\pi$-$\lambda$ theorem applied to half-lines, which generate $\mathcal{B}$).

Independence of random variables. $X$ and $Y$ are independent iff $\mathbb{P}_{(X,Y)}=\mathbb{P}_X\otimes\mathbb{P}_Y$, i.e. the joint law factorises. Equivalently $\mathbb{P}(X\in A, Y\in B)=\mathbb{P}(X\in A)\mathbb{P}(Y\in B)$ for all Borel $A,B$.

Worked example: inverse CDF transform. Let $U\sim\text{Uniform}[0,1]$ and $F$ be a CDF with inverse $F^{-1}(u)=\inf\{x:F(x)\ge u\}$. Then $X=F^{-1}(U)$ has CDF $F$: indeed $\mathbb{P}(X\le x)=\mathbb{P}(F^{-1}(U)\le x)=\mathbb{P}(U\le F(x))=F(x)$. This is how every simulator generates non-uniform random variables from a uniform random source.

4. Supports, distributions, and densities

Named distributions come in two flavours:

  • Discrete: supported on a countable set; described by a PMF $p_X(k)=\mathbb{P}(X=k)$ with $\sum_k p_X(k)=1$.
  • Absolutely continuous: supported on an interval; described by a PDF $f_X\ge 0$ with $\int f_X\,dx=1$, and $\mathbb{P}(X\in B)=\int_B f_X\,dx$.

A distribution need be neither, but these two cover most applications. Mixtures and pathologies (e.g.\ the Cantor distribution) arise in theory and show that a general $\mathbb{P}_X$ decomposes into absolutely-continuous + singular-continuous + discrete parts (Lebesgue decomposition).

5. Expectation, variance, and moments

The expectation (or expected value) of a random variable $X\in L^1(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ is the Lebesgue integral $$\mathbb{E}[X]=\int_\Omega X\,d\mathbb{P}.$$ Equivalently, via the push-forward, $\mathbb{E}[X]=\int_\mathbb{R} x\,d\mathbb{P}_X(x)$. For a discrete variable with PMF $p_X$, this is $\sum_k k\,p_X(k)$; for an absolutely continuous one with PDF $f_X$, it is $\int_\mathbb{R} x\,f_X(x)\,dx$.

Linearity: $\mathbb{E}[aX+bY]=a\mathbb{E}[X]+b\mathbb{E}[Y]$, always (no independence needed). This is just linearity of the integral.

Variance: $\text{Var}(X)=\mathbb{E}[(X-\mathbb{E}[X])^2]=\mathbb{E}[X^2]-\mathbb{E}[X]^2$, defined when $X\in L^2$. Variance measures spread around the mean; $\sqrt{\text{Var}}$ is the standard deviation.

Covariance: $\text{Cov}(X,Y)=\mathbb{E}[(X-\mathbb{E}X)(Y-\mathbb{E}Y)]=\mathbb{E}[XY]-\mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$. For independent $X,Y$, $\text{Cov}(X,Y)=0$ (so $\text{Var}(X+Y)=\text{Var}(X)+\text{Var}(Y)$); the converse fails.

Markov inequality: for $X\ge 0$ and $a>0$, $$\mathbb{P}(X\ge a)\le\frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{a}.$$ Applied to $|X-\mu|^2$: Chebyshev: $\mathbb{P}(|X-\mu|\ge\varepsilon)\le\text{Var}(X)/\varepsilon^2$. These two inequalities do most of probability's quantitative work.

Worked example: expectation as centre of mass. Place a unit mass at $+1$ and a unit mass at $-1$ on the real line, each with weight $1/2$. The centre of mass is at $0$, which is exactly $\mathbb{E}[X]=\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 1+\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot(-1)=0$. Now shift the masses and vary the weights: the balance point tracks the expectation. This geometric picture is exactly what the widget animates.

6. Moment and characteristic functions

Transforms of probability distributions are to probability what Fourier and Laplace are to analysis: they turn convolutions (sums of independents) into products, and they encode the entire distribution in one function of a real (or complex) parameter.

Moment-generating function (MGF): $M_X(t)=\mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$, defined where the integral converges. If $M_X$ is finite on an open interval around $0$, then $$M_X(t)=\sum_{k=0}^\infty\mathbb{E}[X^k]\,t^k/k!,$$ so the $k$-th derivative at $0$ is the $k$-th moment: $M_X^{(k)}(0)=\mathbb{E}[X^k]$. Hence "moment-generating".

Characteristic function (CF): $\varphi_X(t)=\mathbb{E}[e^{itX}]$, always defined on $\mathbb{R}$ because $|e^{itX}|=1$. The CF is the Fourier transform of the distribution. Two distributions are equal iff their CFs are equal (Lévy's uniqueness), and pointwise convergence of CFs with continuity at $0$ is equivalent to convergence in distribution (Lévy's continuity theorem).

Transforms of independent sums. If $X,Y$ are independent, $$M_{X+Y}(t)=M_X(t)\,M_Y(t),\qquad\varphi_{X+Y}(t)=\varphi_X(t)\,\varphi_Y(t).$$ This turns the CLT proof into a line of calculus and a Taylor expansion.

Worked example: Gaussian transforms. For $X\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$: $$M_X(t)=\exp(\mu t+\sigma^2t^2/2),\qquad\varphi_X(t)=\exp(i\mu t-\sigma^2t^2/2).$$ Check: $M_X'(0)=\mu=\mathbb{E}[X]$; $M_X''(0)=\mu^2+\sigma^2=\mathbb{E}[X^2]$; variance $\sigma^2$. The Gaussian is the unique distribution whose CF has compact-support-like decay $e^{-ct^2}$.

Named distributions and their MGFs:

  • Bernoulli$(p)$: $M(t)=1-p+pe^t$.
  • Poisson$(\lambda)$: $M(t)=\exp(\lambda(e^t-1))$.
  • Exponential$(\lambda)$: $M(t)=\lambda/(\lambda-t)$ for $t<\lambda$.
  • Gaussian$(\mu,\sigma^2)$: $M(t)=\exp(\mu t+\sigma^2t^2/2)$.

7. Modes of convergence

A sequence of random variables $X_n$ can converge to a limit $X$ in several non-equivalent senses. From strongest to weakest (in the chain of implications):

  • Almost surely ($X_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}X$): $\mathbb{P}(\{\omega:X_n(\omega)\to X(\omega)\})=1$. Pointwise convergence everywhere except on a null set.
  • In $L^p$: $\mathbb{E}|X_n-X|^p\to 0$. Requires $X_n,X\in L^p$.
  • In probability: $\mathbb{P}(|X_n-X|\ge\varepsilon)\to 0$ for every $\varepsilon>0$.
  • In distribution ($X_n\xrightarrow{d}X$): $\mathbb{E}[f(X_n)]\to\mathbb{E}[f(X)]$ for every bounded continuous $f$; equivalently $F_{X_n}(x)\to F_X(x)$ at continuity points of $F_X$.
Implication diagram. $$X_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}X \implies X_n\xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}}X \implies X_n\xrightarrow{d}X$$ $$X_n\xrightarrow{L^p}X \implies X_n\xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}}X$$ All reverse implications fail without extra hypotheses.

Counterexamples. (i) The "typewriter sequence" $X_n=\mathbf{1}_{I_n}$ with $I_n$ cycling through dyadic intervals of shrinking length converges to $0$ in probability but NOT a.s. (ii) $X_n=n\cdot\mathbf{1}_{(0,1/n)}$ converges to $0$ a.s.\ but $\mathbb{E}[X_n]=1$, so NOT in $L^1$. (iii) The CLT gives convergence in distribution but NOT in probability — the sample mean itself does not converge (strongly).

Borel–Cantelli lemmata. For events $(A_n)$:

  • If $\sum\mathbb{P}(A_n)<\infty$, then $\mathbb{P}(A_n\text{ i.o.})=0$.
  • If $(A_n)$ are independent and $\sum\mathbb{P}(A_n)=\infty$, then $\mathbb{P}(A_n\text{ i.o.})=1$.
These are the canonical tools for proving a.s.\ statements.

Worked example: $X_n\to 0$ a.s.\ via Borel–Cantelli. Let $X_n=n\cdot\mathbf{1}_{A_n}$ with $\mathbb{P}(A_n)=1/n^2$. Then $\sum\mathbb{P}(A_n)=\pi^2/6<\infty$, so by the first Borel-Cantelli lemma, $\mathbb{P}(A_n\text{ i.o.})=0$: a.s., $A_n$ occurs only finitely often. Hence $X_n=0$ eventually a.s., so $X_n\to 0$ a.s.

8. Law of large numbers

Let $X_1,X_2,\ldots$ be i.i.d.\ with $\mathbb{E}|X_1|<\infty$ and $\mathbb{E}[X_1]=\mu$. The sample mean $\bar X_N=\tfrac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N X_i$ concentrates at $\mu$ as $N\to\infty$.

Weak law (WLLN). Under finite variance $\sigma^2$: $\bar X_N\xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}}\mu$. Proof is a line of Chebyshev: $\text{Var}(\bar X_N)=\sigma^2/N$, so $$\mathbb{P}(|\bar X_N-\mu|\ge\varepsilon)\le\sigma^2/(N\varepsilon^2)\to 0.$$

Strong law (SLLN — Kolmogorov). Under only $\mathbb{E}|X_1|<\infty$: $\bar X_N\xrightarrow{a.s.}\mu$. This is stronger: the set of $\omega$ for which $\bar X_N(\omega)\to\mu$ has probability $1$.

The hypothesis $\mathbb{E}|X_1|<\infty$ is sharp. For the Cauchy distribution, $\mathbb{E}|X_1|=\infty$ and $\bar X_N$ does not converge — in fact, the sample mean of i.i.d.\ Cauchy is again Cauchy, so spread does not shrink with $N$.

Proof sketch (SLLN via Etemadi/truncation). Truncate: $Y_n=X_n\mathbf{1}_{|X_n|\le n}$. Show $\sum\text{Var}(Y_n)/n^2<\infty$ under $\mathbb{E}|X_1|<\infty$. Apply Chebyshev plus Borel-Cantelli on a subsequence, then fill in gaps by monotonicity. The elegance of the argument is due to Etemadi (1981) — pairwise independence suffices.

Worked example: Monte Carlo integration. To compute $\int_0^1 g(x)\,dx$ numerically, sample $U_1,\ldots,U_N$ i.i.d.\ $\text{Uniform}[0,1]$. By SLLN, $\tfrac{1}{N}\sum_i g(U_i)\to\int_0^1 g\,dx$ a.s. The error is $O(1/\sqrt{N})$ (CLT — next section), independent of dimension. This is why Monte Carlo beats deterministic quadrature in high dimensions.

9. Central limit theorem

Let $X_1,X_2,\ldots$ be i.i.d.\ with mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2>0$. The classical CLT says: $$\frac{\sqrt{N}\,(\bar X_N-\mu)}{\sigma}\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,1).$$ Equivalently, $\bar X_N\approx\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2/N)$ for large $N$, with error $O(1/\sqrt{N})$ in Kolmogorov distance (Berry-Esseen).

Proof sketch (via characteristic functions). Let $Y_i=(X_i-\mu)/\sigma$, so $\mathbb{E}[Y_i]=0$, $\text{Var}(Y_i)=1$. Expand $\varphi_Y(t)=1-t^2/2+o(t^2)$ as $t\to 0$. Then $$\varphi_{(\sum Y_i)/\sqrt{N}}(t)=\varphi_Y(t/\sqrt{N})^N=\left(1-\tfrac{t^2}{2N}+o(1/N)\right)^N\to e^{-t^2/2},$$ the characteristic function of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Apply Lévy's continuity theorem (pointwise convergence of CFs + continuity of the limit at $0$) to conclude convergence in distribution.

What goes wrong without finite variance? The $\alpha$-stable distributions ($0<\alpha\le 2$) form the universal scaling-limit family. $\alpha=2$ is the Gaussian (CLT); $\alpha=1$ is the Cauchy; intermediate $\alpha$ give the heavy-tailed stable laws appearing in anomalous diffusion and Lévy flights.

Lindeberg's condition. For triangular arrays $X_{n,k}$ with $s_n^2=\sum_k\text{Var}(X_{n,k})$, the CLT holds iff for every $\varepsilon>0$, $\frac{1}{s_n^2}\sum_k\mathbb{E}[X_{n,k}^2\mathbf{1}_{|X_{n,k}|>\varepsilon s_n}]\to 0$. This "no single summand dominates" condition is necessary and sufficient under uniform asymptotic negligibility.

Worked example: sum of Bernoulli. $S_N=\sum_{i=1}^N X_i$ with $X_i$ i.i.d.\ Bernoulli$(1/2)$. Then $\mathbb{E}[S_N]=N/2$, $\text{Var}(S_N)=N/4$. The CLT says $(S_N-N/2)/\sqrt{N/4}\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. For $N=100$ and $S_{100}\ge 60$: the $Z$-score is $(60-50)/5=2$, so $\mathbb{P}(S_{100}\ge 60)\approx\mathbb{P}(Z\ge 2)\approx 0.0228$. Exact binomial: $0.0284$ — good agreement at $N=100$.

10. Markov chains

A (homogeneous) Markov chain on a finite state space $S$ is a stochastic process $(X_n)_{n\ge 0}$ with the memoryless property: $$\mathbb{P}(X_{n+1}=y\mid X_0,\ldots,X_n)=\mathbb{P}(X_{n+1}=y\mid X_n)=P_{X_n,y},$$ encoded by a transition matrix $P\in[0,1]^{S\times S}$ with row sums $1$. The $n$-step transitions are the entries of $P^n$.

The space of transition matrices on $S$ is a semigroup under matrix multiplication, with identity $I$ (the trivial chain): semigroup composition $P^n=P\cdot P\cdots P$ encodes repeated application of the chain.

Stationary distributions. A row vector $\pi$ is stationary for $P$ if $\pi P=\pi$ and $\sum_i\pi_i=1$. Every finite-state chain has at least one stationary distribution (fixed-point theorem).

Fundamental ergodic theorem. If a finite-state chain is irreducible (every state is accessible from every other) and aperiodic (gcd of return times to each state is $1$), then:

  • the stationary distribution $\pi$ is unique;
  • $P^n\to\Pi$ where every row of $\Pi$ is $\pi$ — the chain forgets its starting state.

Reversibility and detailed balance. A chain is reversible w.r.t.\ $\pi$ if $\pi_i P_{ij}=\pi_j P_{ji}$ for all $i,j$. Summing over $i$ gives stationarity, so reversibility implies $\pi$ is stationary (but not conversely). Reversible chains are important because they can be analysed spectrally — $P$ is self-adjoint w.r.t.\ the weighted inner product $\langle f,g\rangle_\pi=\sum\pi_i f_i g_i$.

Worked example: 3-state weather chain. States: $S,C,R$ (sunny/cloudy/rainy). Transition: $$P=\begin{pmatrix}0.7&0.2&0.1\\0.3&0.4&0.3\\0.2&0.3&0.5\end{pmatrix}.$$ Solve $\pi P=\pi$ with $\sum\pi=1$: $\pi\approx(0.47,0.28,0.25)$. The widget below animates this chain; starting anywhere, the running frequency bars converge to $\pi$.

Counterexample: reducible chain, non-unique stationary. On $\{1,2,3,4\}$ with two disjoint 2-cycles, each component has its own stationary distribution, and any convex combination is stationary — the simplex of stationary distributions is one-dimensional.

11. Martingales

A filtration $(\mathcal{F}_n)_{n\ge 0}$ is an increasing sequence of sub-$\sigma$-algebras of $\mathcal{F}$: $\mathcal{F}_0\subseteq\mathcal{F}_1\subseteq\cdots\subseteq\mathcal{F}$. Intuitively, $\mathcal{F}_n$ is the information available at time $n$.

A stochastic process $(M_n)_{n\ge 0}$ is adapted to $(\mathcal{F}_n)$ if $M_n$ is $\mathcal{F}_n$-measurable for each $n$.

An adapted, $L^1$ process $(M_n)$ is a martingale if $$\mathbb{E}[M_{n+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=M_n\quad\forall n.$$ Replace $=$ with $\ge$ for a submartingale, with $\le$ for a supermartingale. The defining identity is the formal expression of "fair game": the best predictor of the next value, given all past information, is the current value.

Stopping times. A random time $\tau\colon\Omega\to\{0,1,\ldots,\infty\}$ is a stopping time w.r.t.\ $(\mathcal{F}_n)$ if $\{\tau\le n\}\in\mathcal{F}_n$ for each $n$: at time $n$, we can decide from the data so far whether to stop.

Doob's optional-stopping theorem. If $M$ is a martingale and $\tau$ a stopping time, then $\mathbb{E}[M_\tau]=\mathbb{E}[M_0]$ provided at least one of the following holds:

  1. $\tau$ is bounded;
  2. $M$ is uniformly integrable (UI); or
  3. $\tau<\infty$ a.s.\ and $(M_{n\wedge\tau})$ is uniformly bounded.
Without such a hypothesis optional-stopping fails: simple symmetric random walk stopped at $\tau=\inf\{n:S_n=1\}$ satisfies $\tau<\infty$ a.s.\ but $\mathbb{E}[S_\tau]=1\ne 0=\mathbb{E}[S_0]$.

Doob's martingale convergence theorem. If $\sup_n\mathbb{E}|M_n|<\infty$, then $M_n\to M_\infty$ a.s.\ for some $M_\infty\in L^1$. If additionally $M$ is UI, then $M_n\to M_\infty$ in $L^1$ as well. $L^1$-bounded without UI is a genuinely weaker condition — e.g.\ the product martingale $M_n=\prod_{i=1}^n X_i$ with $\mathbb{P}(X_i=2)=\mathbb{P}(X_i=0)=1/2$ has $\mathbb{E}[M_n]=1$ for all $n$ but $M_n\to 0$ a.s.

Doob's maximal inequality. For a non-negative submartingale, $\mathbb{P}(\max_{k\le n}M_k\ge\lambda)\le\mathbb{E}[M_n]/\lambda$. The $L^p$ version: $\|\max_{k\le n}M_k\|_p\le(p/(p-1))\|M_n\|_p$ for $p>1$. These are the two most-used inequalities in the whole subject.

Worked example: random walk is a martingale. $S_n=\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ with $X_i$ i.i.d., $\mathbb{E}[X_i]=0$, and $\mathcal{F}_n=\sigma(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$. Then $$\mathbb{E}[S_{n+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=\mathbb{E}[S_n+X_{n+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_n]=S_n+\mathbb{E}[X_{n+1}]=S_n,$$ using that $S_n$ is $\mathcal{F}_n$-measurable and $X_{n+1}$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_n$. So $(S_n)$ is a martingale. Consequently, $\mathbb{E}[S_n]=\mathbb{E}[S_0]=0$ for all $n$ (a fact obvious by linearity, but here illustrating the machinery).

12. Brownian motion

Standard Brownian motion (Wiener process) is the continuous-time stochastic process $(W_t)_{t\ge 0}$ characterised by:

  • $W_0=0$ a.s.;
  • $(W_t)$ has independent increments: $W_{t_1}-W_{t_0},\ldots,W_{t_n}-W_{t_{n-1}}$ are independent for $0\le t_0
  • $W_t-W_s\sim\mathcal{N}(0,t-s)$ for $0\le s
  • paths $t\mapsto W_t$ are continuous a.s.
Existence is non-trivial — constructed e.g.\ via Kolmogorov's extension + continuity criterion, or Lévy's construction (dyadic interpolation), or as the scaling limit of random walks (Donsker's invariance principle).

Key properties.

  • Martingale: $W_t$ is a (continuous-time) martingale w.r.t.\ its natural filtration. So is $W_t^2-t$ (variance correction). The "exponential martingale" $e^{\theta W_t-\theta^2 t/2}$ is also a martingale — the engine of Girsanov's theorem.
  • Quadratic variation: $\sum_k(W_{t_{k+1}}-W_{t_k})^2\to t$ in probability as the mesh shrinks, even though the total variation is $+\infty$. Unbounded variation + finite quadratic variation is the signature of Brownian paths.
  • Scaling: $(W_{ct})_{t\ge 0}\overset{d}{=}(\sqrt{c}\,W_t)_{t\ge 0}$ for $c>0$. The process is self-similar with Hurst index $H=1/2$.
  • Nowhere differentiable: a.s., the sample path $t\mapsto W_t$ fails to be differentiable at every $t$.

Donsker's invariance principle says that rescaled random walks converge to Brownian motion in $C([0,1])$: for i.i.d.\ $(X_i)$ with $\mathbb{E}[X_i]=0$ and $\text{Var}(X_i)=1$, the linearly-interpolated process $$W^{(N)}_t=\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}S_{\lfloor Nt\rfloor}+\frac{Nt-\lfloor Nt\rfloor}{\sqrt{N}}X_{\lfloor Nt\rfloor+1}\xrightarrow{d}(W_t)_{t\in[0,1]}.$$ This is the functional CLT: the CLT is the finite-dimensional projection at a single $t$.

Stays-a-preview. Brownian motion here is a first-glance tour, not a full theory. Kolmogorov's consistency, Lévy's construction, the Markov and strong Markov properties, Itô calculus, and the connection to PDEs (heat equation, harmonic measure) are full courses on their own.

Worked example: Black–Scholes option pricing. Model stock price $S_t=S_0\exp((\mu-\sigma^2/2)t+\sigma W_t)$ — geometric Brownian motion. Under the risk-neutral measure, the Black–Scholes formula expresses a European call price as $$C(S_0,T)=S_0\Phi(d_1)-Ke^{-rT}\Phi(d_2),$$ with $d_1=(\ln(S_0/K)+(r+\sigma^2/2)T)/(\sigma\sqrt{T})$, $d_2=d_1-\sigma\sqrt{T}$. Itô's formula applied to $e^{-rt}\ln S_t$ gives the computation; Brownian motion is the fundamental source of randomness in mathematical finance.

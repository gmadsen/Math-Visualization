Fixed-point theorems

Four classical existence results — Banach, Brouwer, Schauder, Lefschetz — plus Kakutani's set-valued upgrade, organized by the structure they exploit: completeness, compactness, convexity, and homology.

1. Banach contraction principle

If $(X,d)$ is a complete metric space and $f\colon X\to X$ satisfies a uniform Lipschitz bound $d(f(x),f(y))\le L\,d(x,y)$ with $L<1$, then $f$ has exactly one fixed point. The proof is constructive: pick any $x_0$, iterate $x_{n+1}=f(x_n)$, and the sequence is Cauchy with geometric decay $d(x_n,x_*) \le \tfrac{L^n}{1-L}d(x_0,x_1)$.

The two hypotheses are tight. Drop completeness and the iteration may flee the space; relax $L<1$ to $L\le 1$ (or even $|f'(x)|<1$ pointwise) and a fixed point can fail to exist — try $f(x)=x+e^{-x}$ on $\mathbb{R}$.

2. Brouwer fixed-point theorem

Every continuous map $f\colon D^n\to D^n$ from the closed $n$-ball to itself has a fixed point. Equivalently, every continuous self-map of a non-empty compact convex subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ has a fixed point. The theorem fails on the circle $S^1$ (antipodal map) and on the open ball (translation toward the boundary), so both compactness and contractibility matter.

The standard proof is a contradiction: if $f$ had no fixed point, the ray from $f(x)$ through $x$ would meet the boundary at a point $r(x)\in S^{n-1}$, defining a continuous retraction $r\colon D^n\to S^{n-1}$. But $H_{n-1}(D^n)=0$ while $H_{n-1}(S^{n-1})=\mathbb{Z}$, so the composition $S^{n-1}\hookrightarrow D^n\xrightarrow{r}S^{n-1}$ cannot be the identity — contradiction.

3. Schauder fixed-point theorem

The infinite-dimensional generalisation: if $C$ is a non-empty closed convex subset of a Banach space $X$ and $T\colon C\to C$ is continuous with $T(C)$ contained in a compact set, then $T$ has a fixed point. The variant most used in PDE: a compact operator $T$ mapping a closed bounded convex set into itself has a fixed point.

Why compactness can't be dropped: in any infinite-dimensional Banach space the closed unit ball is non-compact (Riesz's lemma), and one can construct fixed-point-free continuous self-maps of it — Kakutani's example $T(x_1,x_2,\ldots) = (\sqrt{1-\|x\|^2},x_1,x_2,\ldots)$ on the closed unit ball of $\ell^2$ is the textbook witness.

4. Lefschetz fixed-point theorem

Let $X$ be a compact triangulable space and $f\colon X\to X$ continuous. The Lefschetz number is $$\Lambda(f) = \sum_{k\ge 0} (-1)^k\,\tr\bigl(f_*\colon H_k(X;\mathbb{Q})\to H_k(X;\mathbb{Q})\bigr).$$ If $\Lambda(f)\ne 0$, then $f$ has a fixed point. The implication is one-way: $\Lambda(f)=0$ is silent. For $f=\mathrm{id}$ the trace on $H_k$ is $b_k$, so $\Lambda(\mathrm{id})=\chi(X)$, the Euler characteristic — Lefschetz recovers the fact that a non-zero Euler characteristic forbids a fixed-point-free self-homeomorphism homotopic to the identity.

Brouwer is the contractible special case: on $D^n$ all higher homology vanishes, so $\Lambda(f)=\tr(f_*\colon H_0\to H_0)=1\ne 0$.

5. Kakutani fixed-point theorem

Replace single-valued continuous $f$ with a set-valued map $\varphi\colon C\rightrightarrows C$ assigning to each $x\in C$ a non-empty closed convex subset $\varphi(x)\subseteq C$. If $C$ is non-empty compact convex (in $\mathbb{R}^n$) and $\varphi$ has closed graph, then there exists $x^*\in C$ with $x^*\in\varphi(x^*)$.

The motivating example is Nash's existence proof: in a finite game each player's best-response set $B_i(\sigma_{-i})$ is non-empty compact convex but typically not a singleton when ties occur. The product correspondence $\sigma\mapsto\prod_i B_i(\sigma_{-i})$ on the product of mixed-strategy simplices satisfies Kakutani's hypotheses, so a fixed point — a Nash equilibrium — exists.

6. Applications across mathematics

Every theorem above shows up as the existence engine in a downstream result:

  • Picard–Lindelöf for ODEs — Banach contraction. The Picard operator $T(\varphi)(t)=x_0+\int_{t_0}^t f(s,\varphi(s))\,ds$ is a contraction on $C([t_0-h,t_0+h])$ when $f$ is Lipschitz in its second argument and $h$ is small.
  • Implicit function theorem — Banach contraction. A Newton-style operator $y\mapsto y-D_yF(x_0,y_0)^{-1}F(x,y)$ is a contraction on a small ball, and its fixed point is the implicit solution $y=g(x)$.
  • Markov-chain stationary distribution — Brouwer. The simplex $\Delta\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ is non-empty compact convex; $\pi\mapsto\pi P$ is continuous; a stationary $\pi^*$ exists for any stochastic $P$.
  • Existence of solutions to nonlinear elliptic PDEs — Schauder. A compact solution operator on a closed bounded set in a function space provides existence without uniqueness.
  • Nash equilibrium — Kakutani. The best-response correspondence on the product simplex satisfies the hypotheses; its fixed point is the equilibrium.

Connections

Fixed-point machinery is the existence proof of last resort across analysis, geometry, and economics. The frontier today: variational and topological methods (Conley index, mountain-pass) for fixed points of non-compact maps on infinite-dimensional manifolds; computational degree theory; and equilibrium existence for general-equilibrium economies where Kakutani is replaced by Eilenberg–Montgomery or Browder.

  • Functional analysis — compact operators — the natural input class for Schauder.
  • Algebraic topology — singular homology — supplies the trace whose alternating sum is the Lefschetz number.
  • Point-set topology — compactness — the abstract hypothesis common to Brouwer, Schauder, Kakutani, and Lefschetz.
  • Partial differential equations — Schauder gives existence for nonlinear elliptic problems via a compact solution operator.
  • Dynamical systems — periodic orbits are fixed points of iterated maps, opening the door to Lefschetz–Nielsen counting.

Algebraic spaces

Étale quotients of schemes — the missing rung between schemes and stacks.

1. Why schemes aren't enough

Schemes — locally ringed spaces glued from $\operatorname{Spec}$s of commutative rings — are remarkably elastic, but they are not closed under quotients that we morally want to take. The easiest concrete failure is $\mathbb{A}^1_k / \mathbb{Z}$: the affine line over a field $k$, with $\mathbb{Z}$ acting freely by translation $x \mapsto x + n$. Topologically the action is free and properly discontinuous in the étale sense, so a quotient ought to exist. But there is no scheme $X$ representing this quotient — any candidate would have to be a smooth $1$-dimensional $k$-variety dominated by $\mathbb{A}^1$, and a Zariski-dense fibre of $\mathbb{Z}$-orbits forces $X$ to fail to be quasi-separated.

The deeper symptom is that the moduli problem one wants to solve is a sheaf, not a representable functor. Étale-locally the quotient looks like a piece of $\mathbb{A}^1$ — at the level of stalks, $\mathbb{Z}$ acts freely, so a small étale neighbourhood is a section of the quotient map. But globally, gluing those étale sections via the equivalence relation $R = \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{A}^1 \rightrightarrows \mathbb{A}^1$ produces a sheaf on the étale site whose representability fails for set-theoretic reasons (too many $\bar k$-points to fit into a Zariski-cover of $\operatorname{Spec}$s of finite type).

Algebraic spaces, due to M. Artin and Knutson, are the minimal enlargement of schemes that does contain these quotients. The slogan is: keep the representable definition (an étale sheaf with representable diagonal and a scheme cover) but allow the cover to glue along an étale equivalence relation rather than a Zariski one. The category of schemes embeds fully faithfully; one gets a category closed under étale equivalence quotients, with descent + fibre products + morphism properties (étale, smooth, proper, finite type) all working "as if it were a scheme." 2. Étale equivalence relations

An étale equivalence relation on a scheme $U$ is a monomorphism $j = (s, t)\colon R \hookrightarrow U \times U$ such that:

  • both projections $s, t \colon R \to U$ (the source and target maps of $j$) are étale;
  • $R$ is an equivalence relation in the categorical sense — i.e. $R(T) \subset U(T) \times U(T)$ is an equivalence relation on $U(T)$ for every test scheme $T$. Concretely: reflexivity ($\Delta_U$ factors through $R$), symmetry ($R = \sigma(R)$ under the swap $\sigma$), and transitivity (the fibred composition $R \times_{s,U,t} R \to U \times U$ factors through $R$).

The étale-projection condition is the powerful one. It says that $R$ is an étale "thickening" of the diagonal — locally on $U$, $R$ looks like a disjoint union of copies of $U$ indexed by an étale-local equivalence class. This forces the equivalence classes to be discrete in the étale topology: no two distinct equivalent points can collide tangentially. In particular, free actions of finite étale group schemes give étale equivalence relations, but so do many quotients without a global group action.

The big theorem (Artin–Knutson) is that the quotient sheaf $U / R$ on the big étale site is always an algebraic space, and conversely every algebraic space arises this way. The diagonal of $U/R$ is precisely $j\colon R \hookrightarrow U \times U$, which is representable by schemes; the étale projection $U \twoheadrightarrow U/R$ is the announced scheme atlas. Two étale equivalence relations $R_1 \rightrightarrows U_1$ and $R_2 \rightrightarrows U_2$ define isomorphic algebraic spaces iff they become isomorphic after pulling back to a common étale refinement.

3. Algebraic spaces as étale sheaves

The clean definition (Knutson, after M. Artin): an algebraic space is a sheaf $X\colon (\mathrm{Sch}/S)^{\mathrm{op}}_{\acute{e}t} \to \mathrm{Set}$ such that

  1. the diagonal $\Delta_X\colon X \to X \times_S X$ is representable by schemes — i.e. for every $T \to X \times_S X$ from a scheme $T$, the fibre product $T \times_{X \times X} X$ is a scheme;
  2. there exists an étale surjection $U \twoheadrightarrow X$ from a scheme $U$ (the atlas).

The two conditions together pin the object down rigidly. (1) ensures that diagonals — and hence morphisms from schemes to $X$ — are tractable; (2) lets you compute everything étale-locally on a scheme. Their interplay is nontrivial: given (2), the fibre product $U \times_X U$ is the equivalence relation $R \rightrightarrows U$ from §2, and $X = U/R$ as étale sheaves. Conversely, any étale equivalence relation $R \rightrightarrows U$ defines via $U/R$ a sheaf satisfying both (1) and (2). So the two perspectives — étale-sheaf with extra structure versus étale equivalence quotient — are equivalent.

A few points worth remembering. (a) "Representable diagonal" is strictly weaker than "scheme": if $X$ is a scheme, $\Delta_X$ is a locally closed immersion, but a generic algebraic space has only an étale (or even just unramified) representable diagonal. (b) The diagonal being representable is the algebraic-space analogue of the Hausdorff condition — it controls how points "separate." The condition quasi-separated for an algebraic space asks $\Delta_X$ to also be quasi-compact, recovering the usual scheme-theoretic notion. (c) Most authors implicitly assume $X$ is at least quasi-separated; non-quasi-separated algebraic spaces (like $\mathbb{A}^1/\mathbb{Z}$ above) exist but are pathological.

4. Morphisms of algebraic spaces

A morphism $f \colon X \to Y$ of algebraic spaces is just a morphism of étale sheaves. Concretely, if $X = U/R_U$ and $Y = V/R_V$ are presented by atlases, $f$ is given by a morphism of schemes $\tilde f \colon U \to V$ that intertwines the equivalence relations: for any $T$-point $(u_1, u_2) \in R_U(T)$, the pair $(\tilde f(u_1), \tilde f(u_2))$ lies in $R_V(T)$. Two such liftings $\tilde f, \tilde f'$ define the same $f$ iff they agree after pulling back to a common étale refinement of $U$.

The pleasant fact is that every familiar property of scheme morphisms transports verbatim. A property $P$ of scheme morphisms is "étale-local on the target" if $f$ has $P$ iff $f \times_Y \mathrm{id}_V \colon X \times_Y V \to V$ has $P$ for some / any étale cover $V \to Y$. Étale, smooth, flat, locally of finite presentation, locally of finite type, unramified, finite, proper, separated, surjective, monomorphism, closed immersion, open immersion, isomorphism — all are étale-local on the target. So we define $f$ to have property $P$ iff one (equivalently, every) presentation $\tilde f\colon U \to V$ has it.

The composition $g \circ f$ inherits intersections of stable properties (étale + étale = étale, proper + proper = proper, etc.), and base change preserves them all. Two slightly subtle points: (a) the diagonal $\Delta_f \colon X \to X \times_Y X$ of a morphism is again representable by schemes, and "$f$ separated" means $\Delta_f$ is a closed immersion (note: not always automatic!); (b) a morphism of algebraic spaces between schemes is a morphism of schemes — the embedding is fully faithful.

5. Examples: free quotients and a non-scheme

Three families of examples populate the category of algebraic spaces.

(i) Schemes. Any scheme $X$ is an algebraic space: take $U = X$, $R = \Delta_X \subset X \times X$. The diagonal is a locally closed immersion (so certainly representable), the atlas $X \to X$ is the identity (étale and surjective), and the étale equivalence relation is just the diagonal. The functor (schemes) $\hookrightarrow$ (algebraic spaces) is fully faithful — a morphism of algebraic spaces between schemes is a morphism of schemes.

(ii) Free étale group quotients. Let $G$ be a finite group acting freely on a scheme $U$ (in the étale-topology sense: stabilisers are trivial étale-locally). Then $U/G$ is an algebraic space, with atlas $U \to U/G$ étale and $R = G \times U$ via $(g, u) \mapsto (u, g \cdot u)$. When the action is also quasi-projective in the sense that orbits fit inside affines, $U/G$ is a scheme; otherwise — even for very simple $G$ like $\mathbb{Z}/2$ — it can fail. The example $\mathbb{A}^1 / \mathbb{Z}$ from §1 is the prototype non-scheme algebraic space.

(iii) Hironaka's smooth proper non-scheme. The deepest classical example. Hironaka constructed a smooth proper $3$-dimensional complex algebraic space $H$ that is not a scheme: it has a closed curve $C \subset H$ whose intersection number with itself satisfies $C \cdot C = 0$ in a way that's incompatible with $H$ being a scheme (no scheme can have a free $\mathbb{Z}/2$ action exchanging two cycles whose blowups are linearly equivalent). 6. Algebraic spaces sit between schemes and DM stacks

The four-rung hierarchy of algebro-geometric "spaces" can be read as a story about how much isotropy the object is allowed to carry, where "isotropy" means the automorphism group $\mathrm{Aut}(x)$ of a geometric point $x$.

  • Schemes. No isotropy at all in the categorical sense — every geometric point has trivial automorphism group. The diagonal is a locally closed immersion. Glued from $\operatorname{Spec}$s by the Zariski topology.
  • Algebraic spaces. Still no isotropy — every geometric point has trivial automorphism group — but the étale topology replaces the Zariski one for the gluing. Equivalently: a stack whose automorphism groups are all trivial. The diagonal is representable by schemes.
  • Deligne–Mumford (DM) stacks. Geometric points are allowed to carry finite étale automorphism groups. Diagonal is unramified (= étale + monomorphism); admits an étale atlas by a scheme. Examples: $B(\mathbb{Z}/n) = [\mathrm{pt}/(\mathbb{Z}/n)]$, $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g,n}$ for $2g - 2 + n > 0$.
  • Artin stacks. Geometric points carry arbitrary algebraic group automorphisms. Diagonal merely required to be representable; admits a smooth atlas. Examples: $BG$ for $G$ a positive-dimensional algebraic group, $\mathcal{M}_g$ at the locus of curves with continuous automorphisms, the moduli of vector bundles.

The "if and only if" characterisation is clean: an Artin stack $\mathcal{X}$ is

  • a DM stack iff $\Delta_{\mathcal{X}}$ is unramified — equivalently, all geometric stabilisers are finite étale group schemes;
  • an algebraic space iff $\Delta_{\mathcal{X}}$ is a monomorphism — equivalently, all geometric stabilisers are trivial;
  • a scheme iff $\Delta_{\mathcal{X}}$ is a locally closed immersion and there is a Zariski (not just étale) atlas.

So algebraic spaces are precisely the rung where you have given up Zariski-locality but kept "no automorphism information." They are the right home for moduli problems where every parameter has a unique geometric incarnation (no internal symmetries to keep track of) but the gluing is genuinely étale-local.

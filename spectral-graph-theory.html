Spectral graph theory

A finite graph hides a small symmetric matrix; the eigenvalues of that matrix know how connected it is, how fast a random walk mixes, and how cleanly it splits in two. From the algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2$ to Cheeger, expanders, and spectral clustering — graphs as discrete differential geometry.

1. Adjacency, degree, and the Laplacian

Fix a finite simple graph $G=(V,E)$ with $n=|V|$ vertices. Numbering them $1,\dots,n$ once and for all, the adjacency matrix is the symmetric $n\times n$ matrix

$A_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \{i,j\} \in E,\\ 0 & \text{otherwise}.\end{cases}$

The degree matrix $D=\operatorname{diag}(d_1,\dots,d_n)$ where $d_i = \sum_j A_{ij}$ is the number of neighbours of vertex $i$. The combinatorial Laplacian is

$L \;=\; D - A.$

This is the discrete analogue of $-\Delta$ on a Riemannian manifold. To see why, look at how $L$ acts on a function $f\colon V\to\mathbb{R}$ — viewed as a vector $f\in\mathbb{R}^n$:

$(Lf)(i) \;=\; d_i\,f(i) - \sum_{j\sim i} f(j) \;=\; \sum_{j\sim i}\bigl(f(i)-f(j)\bigr).$

Each entry measures how much $f$ disagrees with the average of its neighbours — exactly what $-\Delta$ does on a smooth manifold by averaging second derivatives. Iterating $L$ runs heat backwards on the graph; iterating $-L$ smooths.

The basic spectral facts are immediate from $L=D-A$ being real symmetric:

  • $L$ is positive semidefinite: $f^\top L f = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\{i,j\}\in E}(f(i)-f(j))^2 \ge 0$. This energy form is the central object of the subject.
  • $L$ has $n$ real eigenvalues $0 = \lambda_1 \le \lambda_2 \le \cdots \le \lambda_n$ with an orthonormal eigenbasis $u_1,\dots,u_n$.
  • The constant vector $\mathbf{1}=(1,\dots,1)^\top$ always satisfies $L\mathbf{1}=0$, so $\lambda_1=0$ with eigenvector $\mathbf{1}$ on a connected graph.

Three small graphs to keep in mind throughout:

The cycle row is the discrete Fourier transform writ small: eigenvectors are the columns of the DFT matrix, eigenvalues are real because $A$ is symmetric. This is the recurring theme — Laplacian eigenvectors are the "Fourier modes" of a graph.

2. The kernel of $L$ counts components

The eigenvalue $\lambda_1=0$ comes for free from $L\mathbf{1}=0$, but that is only one eigenvector. What about the rest of the kernel?

Theorem. The multiplicity of $0$ as an eigenvalue of $L$ equals the number of connected components of $G$.

Proof. If $G$ has components $C_1,\dots,C_k$, then for each $\ell$ the indicator vector $\mathbf{1}_{C_\ell}$ satisfies $L\mathbf{1}_{C_\ell}=0$ (no edge crosses between components). These $k$ vectors are linearly independent, so $\dim\ker L \ge k$. Conversely, suppose $Lf=0$. Then $f^\top L f = \sum_{\{i,j\}\in E}(f(i)-f(j))^2 = 0$, forcing $f$ to be constant on each component, so $f$ is a linear combination of the indicators. $\blacksquare$

This is the first piece of the dictionary between graph topology and Laplacian spectrum. Add an edge between two components and you collapse two zero eigenvalues into one zero plus one positive — the gap that appears is $\lambda_2$, which §3 will turn into a numerical measure of how "barely" the merged graph holds together.

The energy form. The identity $f^\top L f = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\{i,j\}\in E}(f(i)-f(j))^2$ is the unsung hero of the entire page. Every spectral inequality below — Courant–Fischer, Rayleigh, Cheeger — is a statement about minimising or constraining this Dirichlet energy.

3. Algebraic connectivity and the Fiedler vector

Order the spectrum $0=\lambda_1\le\lambda_2\le\cdots\le\lambda_n$. The second-smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_2$ is the algebraic connectivity, and its eigenvector $u_2$ is the Fiedler vector (Miroslav Fiedler, 1973).

Two facts make $\lambda_2$ enormously useful. The first is the Courant–Fischer characterisation:

$\displaystyle \lambda_2 \;=\; \min_{\substack{f\perp\mathbf{1}\\ \|f\|=1}} f^\top L f \;=\; \min_{f\perp\mathbf{1},\,\|f\|=1}\frac{1}{1}\sum_{\{i,j\}\in E}(f(i)-f(j))^2.$

The minimiser is exactly $u_2$, and $u_2$ is the smoothest function on $G$ that is orthogonal to the constant. Smooth here means small Dirichlet energy: $u_2$ varies as little as possible across edges, subject to having mean zero.

The second fact is that $\lambda_2 > 0$ iff $G$ is connected — a one-line corollary of §2. So $\lambda_2$ is a quantitative refinement of "connected": small but positive means "barely held together by a thin set of edges", while $\lambda_2$ on the order of $n$ means "robustly mixed".

The path and cycle die off as $1/n^2$ — they are "long thin" graphs that almost disconnect into two halves at the middle. The complete graph is maximally connected. Stars are dominated by their hub.

Cutting with the Fiedler vector

Because $u_2$ is the smoothest mean-zero function, it tries to take one sign on roughly half the graph and the opposite sign on the other half — and its sign change tells you where to cut. This is the Fiedler bisection heuristic:

  1. Compute the Fiedler vector $u_2$.
  2. Sort vertices by $u_2(i)$.
  3. Cut between the negative and positive halves (or at the median).

This produces a partition $V = S \sqcup \bar S$ with empirically very few crossing edges. §4 makes the empirical claim into the Cheeger inequality.

4. Cheeger's inequality

The edge expansion (or conductance, or Cheeger constant) of $G$ is

$\displaystyle h(G) \;=\; \min_{\substack{S\subset V\\ 1\le|S|\le n/2}} \frac{|\partial S|}{|S|},$

where $\partial S = \{\{i,j\}\in E : i\in S,\,j\notin S\}$ is the edge boundary. Small $h(G)$ means there is a vertex set $S$ that touches the rest of the graph through few edges — a sparse cut. The minimum is taken over $|S|\le n/2$ to break the trivial symmetry $S\leftrightarrow\bar S$.

Computing $h(G)$ exactly is NP-hard. The miracle:

Cheeger inequality (graphs, 1980s — Alon, Milman, Tanner). For a connected $d$-regular graph $G$, $$\frac{\lambda_2}{2} \;\le\; h(G) \;\le\; \sqrt{2 d \,\lambda_2}.$$ For an arbitrary graph, replace $\lambda_2$ with the second eigenvalue of the normalised Laplacian (§5).

The lower bound is easy and clean; the upper bound is the celebrated sweep cut argument:

The sweep cut

  1. Compute the Fiedler vector $u_2$.
  2. Sort vertices $i_1, i_2, \dots, i_n$ so that $u_2(i_1) \le u_2(i_2) \le \cdots \le u_2(i_n)$.
  3. For each $k=1,\dots,n-1$, let $S_k = \{i_1,\dots,i_k\}$ and compute $|\partial S_k|/\min(|S_k|,n-|S_k|)$.
  4. Output the best such $S_k$.

The output is provably within a factor $\sqrt{2d/\lambda_2}$ of optimal — a constant-factor approximation in the dense regime, polynomial-time computable. This spectral approximation algorithm was the original motivation for Fiedler's work in numerical analysis and the seed of all spectral clustering.

Continuous analogue. Cheeger's original (1970) inequality is for compact Riemannian manifolds: $\lambda_2(-\Delta) \ge h(M)^2/4$, where $h(M)$ is the infimum of $\operatorname{vol}_{n-1}(\partial S)/\operatorname{vol}_n(S)$ over open sets $S$ with $\operatorname{vol}_n(S)\le \operatorname{vol}_n(M)/2$. The graph version was the discrete reflection — exactly the right way to think of $L=D-A$ as $-\Delta$.

5. Random walks and the spectral gap

The simple random walk on $G$ steps from a vertex to a uniformly random neighbour. Its transition matrix is

$P \;=\; D^{-1} A,\qquad P_{ij} \;=\; \mathbb{1}[j\sim i]/d_i.$

This is row-stochastic but not symmetric. To get a symmetric operator (and a spectral theorem) one passes to the normalised Laplacian

$\mathcal{L} \;=\; D^{-1/2} L D^{-1/2} \;=\; I - D^{-1/2} A\, D^{-1/2}.$

Its spectrum $0=\nu_1 \le \nu_2 \le \cdots \le \nu_n \le 2$ controls the random walk: the walk's transition matrix $P = D^{-1}A$ is similar to $D^{-1/2}A D^{-1/2} = I - \mathcal{L}$, so its eigenvalues are exactly $1-\nu_k$, sandwiched in $[-1,1]$.

The spectral gap of the walk is

$\gamma \;=\; 1 - \max(|1-\nu_2|,|1-\nu_n|) \;=\; \min(\nu_2, 2-\nu_n),$

and the mixing time — the time for the walk to forget its start, in total variation — satisfies

$\displaystyle t_{\mathrm{mix}}(\varepsilon) \;\le\; \frac{1}{\gamma}\,\log\!\frac{1}{\pi_{\min}\varepsilon},$

where $\pi$ is the stationary distribution $\pi_i = d_i/(2|E|)$ and $\pi_{\min}=\min_i\pi_i$. Big spectral gap ⇒ fast mixing. Tiny gap ⇒ a near-disconnection or near-bipartiteness that the walk can detect.

A walk on a graph is a heat equation

Iterating $P^t$ on a starting distribution is the discrete heat equation: on each step every distribution mass averages with neighbours, weighted by $1/d_i$. As $t\to\infty$ the distribution converges to $\pi$. The eigenvalues $1-\nu_k$ are exactly the rates at which the modes $u_k$ decay:

$\delta_v\,P^t \;-\; \pi \;=\; \sum_{k\ge 2} \langle\delta_v,u_k\rangle (1-\nu_k)^t u_k.$

The slowest mode is whichever of $\nu_2$ or $2-\nu_n$ is smaller. If $\nu_2$ is the bottleneck, the walk is slow because the graph almost disconnects (Cheeger). If $2-\nu_n$ is the bottleneck, the walk is slow because the graph is almost bipartite — the walk oscillates between two sides for a long time before dispersing.

6. Bipartite graphs and expanders

The normalised Laplacian's top eigenvalue $\nu_n$ has a beautiful combinatorial characterisation:

Bipartite criterion. For a connected graph $G$, the normalised Laplacian's largest eigenvalue $\nu_n = 2$ if and only if $G$ is bipartite. In that case, both $\nu_1=0$ and $\nu_n=2$ have multiplicity equal to the number of bipartite components (and the eigenvector at $\nu_n$ is $D^{1/2}\mathbf{1}_L - D^{1/2}\mathbf{1}_R$ where $L,R$ is the bipartition).

The intuition is direct: for a bipartite graph $G=L\sqcup R$ the random walk strictly alternates sides, so its transition matrix has eigenvalue $-1$. Translating $-1 = 1 - \nu$ gives $\nu=2$. Non-bipartite graphs have $\nu_n < 2$ — and the gap $2-\nu_n$ measures how far the graph is from bipartite (equivalently, how quickly the walk's parity oscillation dies out).

Expanders and the Alon–Boppana bound

An expander family is a sequence $(G_n)$ of $d$-regular graphs with $|V(G_n)|\to\infty$ and a uniform spectral gap: $\lambda_2(G_n) \ge c$ for some $c>0$ independent of $n$. Equivalently — by Cheeger — $h(G_n)\ge c'$ uniformly.

Expanders are the most useful objects in theoretical computer science. They are sparse (only $dn/2$ edges) but well-connected ($\lambda_2$ uniformly bounded away from $0$), so a random walk on an expander mixes in $O(\log n)$ steps. They power error-correcting codes, derandomization, the PCP theorem, and Cayley graphs of finite simple groups.

How spectrally good can a $d$-regular graph be? In terms of the second-largest eigenvalue $|\lambda|$ of $A$ (the largest in absolute value among all eigenvalues other than the trivial $d$):

Alon–Boppana (1986). For any infinite family of $d$-regular graphs $(G_n)$ with $|V(G_n)|\to\infty$, $$\liminf_{n\to\infty} \lambda_2(A_{G_n})\;\ge\; 2\sqrt{d-1}.$$

A $d$-regular graph achieving $|\lambda_2(A)|\le 2\sqrt{d-1}$ is called a Ramanujan graph. Ramanujan graphs are essentially-optimal expanders. Lubotzky–Phillips–Sarnak (1988) constructed infinite Ramanujan families using arithmetic — Cayley graphs of $\mathrm{PGL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$ — and the proof reduces to deep facts about modular forms (eigenvalues of Hecke operators on weight-$2$ forms). The story is one of the most beautiful applications of automorphic representations to combinatorics.

7. Spectral clustering: eigenvectors as coordinates

Fiedler bisection used the sign of one eigenvector. Spectral clustering is the natural generalisation: use the first $k$ non-trivial eigenvectors as coordinates, then run any geometric clustering algorithm (typically $k$-means) in $\mathbb{R}^k$.

Algorithm (Shi–Malik / Ng–Jordan–Weiss).
  1. Build the normalised Laplacian $\mathcal{L} = I - D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2}$.
  2. Compute eigenvectors $u_1, u_2, \dots, u_k$ for the $k$ smallest eigenvalues.
  3. Form the $n\times k$ matrix $U=[u_1\,u_2\,\cdots\,u_k]$. Each row $U_i\in\mathbb{R}^k$ is the embedding of vertex $i$.
  4. Optionally normalise each row to lie on $S^{k-1}$.
  5. Run $k$-means on the rows of $U$.

Why does this work? When the graph has a clear $k$-cluster structure — $k$ tightly-knit communities joined by few edges — the bottom $k$ eigenvalues of $\mathcal{L}$ are nearly $0$ and their eigenvectors are nearly the indicator vectors of the clusters (a perturbation of the "$k$ disconnected components" case from §2). Embedding by these eigenvectors maps each cluster to a small ball in $\mathbb{R}^k$, separated from the others, so $k$-means recovers them.

This is why spectral methods dominate community detection on graphs and image segmentation: they convert a combinatorial cut problem into geometry, where Euclidean clustering is well-understood.

Coda: a graph is a discrete manifold

The single conceptual move worth taking away is the analogy

Every theorem on the right side is a discretisation of a theorem on the left, and conversely every algorithmic technique pioneered for graphs (Cheeger sweep cut, spectral clustering, expander graphs) has a continuous analogue (heat-kernel cuts, manifold learning, Selberg-style trace formulas). The dictionary is two-way and unfinished: things like discrete Ricci curvature (Bakry–Émery on graphs, Ollivier–Ricci) are still active research extending it.

What spectral graph theory really teaches is that finite objects can be analysed by tools designed for the continuum, and the answers — eigenvalues, eigenvectors, mixing times, expansion constants — encode combinatorial facts in analytical language. The price of admission is one matrix; the return is the whole machinery of self-adjoint operators, applied to a graph.

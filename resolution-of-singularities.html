Resolution of singularities

Pinch a curve, crease a surface — algebraic varieties degenerate at points where the Jacobian drops rank. Resolution replaces a singular variety by a smooth one through a controlled sequence of blow-ups, an isomorphism away from the bad locus. Hironaka (1964) proved this is always possible in characteristic zero; in characteristic $p$ it is still open in dimension $\ge 4$.

1. Singular vs smooth: the Jacobian criterion

A point $p$ on an affine variety $X = V(f_1, \ldots, f_r) \subset \mathbb{A}^n$ is smooth when the Jacobian matrix $$J(p) = \left( \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}(p) \right)_{i,j}$$ has the expected rank $n - \dim X$. Equivalently, the Zariski tangent space $T_p X = \ker J(p)$ has the right dimension. At a singular point the rank drops and $T_p X$ jumps.

The tangent cone $C_p X$ refines the tangent space. After translating $p$ to the origin, expand $f_i = f_i^{(d_i)} + \text{higher}$ where $f_i^{(d_i)}$ is the homogeneous lowest-degree term. Then $C_p X = V(f_1^{(d_1)}, \ldots, f_r^{(d_r)})$. At a smooth point each $d_i = 1$ and $C_p X = T_p X$. At a node $f = y^2 - x^2 - x^3$ the lowest-degree piece is $y^2 - x^2 = (y-x)(y+x)$, so $C_p X$ is the union of two lines inside the larger tangent space. At a cusp $y^2 - x^3$ the cone is the doubled line $y^2 = 0$.

2. Blowing up a point

The blow-up of $\mathbb{A}^n$ at the origin is the closed subvariety $$\mathrm{Bl}_0 \mathbb{A}^n \;=\; \{ (x, [\ell]) \in \mathbb{A}^n \times \mathbb{P}^{n-1} : x \in \ell \},$$ together with the projection $\pi : \mathrm{Bl}_0 \mathbb{A}^n \to \mathbb{A}^n$. The fiber over the origin is the entire $\mathbb{P}^{n-1}$ — every line through the origin — while every other fiber is a single point. So $\pi$ replaces one point by its $\mathbb{P}^{n-1}$ of tangent directions, and is an iso elsewhere.

For a closed subvariety $Z \subset X$, the blow-up $\mathrm{Bl}_Z X$ is characterised by a universal property: it is universal among $X$-schemes $f : Y \to X$ for which the pullback $f^{-1} \mathcal{I}_Z$ is a locally principal (Cartier) ideal sheaf. Concretely, $\mathrm{Bl}_Z X = \mathrm{Proj}\!\left( \bigoplus_{n \ge 0} \mathcal{I}_Z^n \right)$.

The point of resolution: blow up a singular curve and the strict transform $\tilde X \subset \mathrm{Bl}_p X$ — the closure of $\pi^{-1}(X \setminus \{p\})$ — is often less singular than $X$. For the cusp $y^2 = x^3$, a single blow-up converts it into a smooth curve.

3. Hironaka's theorem

Hironaka (1964): for any variety $X$ over a field $k$ of characteristic zero, there exists a proper birational morphism $$\pi : \tilde X \longrightarrow X$$ with $\tilde X$ smooth, such that $\pi$ is an isomorphism over the smooth locus of $X$. Moreover $\pi$ can be taken to be a finite composition of blow-ups along smooth centres, each contained in the singular locus at its stage.

The proof tracks a numerical invariant — the Hilbert–Samuel function, or modern variants thereof — that strictly decreases under each blow-up at a sufficiently chosen centre, and by Noetherian descent must terminate. Subsequent reformulations (Bierstone–Milman, Włodarczyk, Kollár) made the resolution functorial: it commutes with smooth morphisms and group actions, so it can be globalised on stacks.

Why characteristic zero? The proof uses tangential flattening lemmas built on Bertini-type genericity. In characteristic $p$, generic hyperplane sections of a singular variety can fail to be transverse, and the inductive invariant can fail to drop. Abhyankar settled char $p$ surfaces; Cossart–Piltant settled threefolds. Dimensions $\ge 4$ remain open.

4. Curves: nodes, cusps, normalisation

One-dimensional resolution is classical and elementary. Three constructions all give the same answer:

  • Normalisation. If $C = \Spec A$ is an affine curve with function field $K(C)$, the integral closure $\tilde A$ of $A$ in $K(C)$ defines a smooth curve $\tilde C = \Spec \tilde A$ with a finite birational map $\nu : \tilde C \to C$.
  • Iterated blow-up. Blow up the singular point; if the strict transform is still singular, blow up again. Curve singularities have finite multiplicity and the process terminates.
  • Newton–Puiseux. Any curve singularity $f(x, y) = 0$ admits formal parametrisations $y = \sum_n a_n x^{n/N}$ — one branch per Puiseux series, exhibiting smooth local pieces.

For the cusp $y^2 = x^3$ all three give the smooth model $\mathbb{A}^1$ with parametrisation $t \mapsto (t^2, t^3)$. For the node $y^2 = x^2(1 + x)$, normalisation separates the two analytic branches into two distinct preimages of the origin.

5. Surfaces and the ADE classification

Surface singularities admit a classification of their simplest forms — the rational double points, also called Du Val or Klein singularities. They are exactly the local equations

typeequationgroup $\Gamma \subset \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{C})$
$A_n$$x^2 + y^2 + z^{n+1} = 0$cyclic of order $n+1$
$D_n$$x^2 + y^2 z + z^{n-1} = 0$binary dihedral, order $4(n-2)$
$E_6$$x^2 + y^3 + z^4 = 0$binary tetrahedral, order 24
$E_7$$x^2 + y^3 + y z^3 = 0$binary octahedral, order 48
$E_8$$x^2 + y^3 + z^5 = 0$binary icosahedral, order 120

Each is realised as a quotient $\mathbb{C}^2 / \Gamma$ for a finite subgroup $\Gamma \subset \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{C})$ (Klein, Brieskorn). The minimal resolution $\tilde X \to X$ has exceptional divisor a tree of smooth rational curves $\mathbb{P}^1$, each with self-intersection $-2$, whose dual graph is the Dynkin diagram of the named ADE type. The McKay correspondence matches non-trivial irreducible representations of $\Gamma$ with these exceptional curves bijectively.

6. Applications and frontiers

Resolution of singularities is a foundational tool for working with arbitrary projective varieties: it lets a theorem proved for smooth varieties extend to singular ones by passage through $\tilde X$.

  • Minimal model program (Mori). Start from a smooth projective $\tilde X$, then run a sequence of contractions of $K$-negative extremal rays (and flips) to reach a minimal or canonical model. Bridges birational classification to the geometry of the canonical bundle.
  • Motivic integration (Kontsevich). Integrals over arc spaces of singular varieties are pulled back to log resolutions; the change-of-variables formula gives a stringy Euler characteristic that agrees on crepant resolutions — first hints of the McKay correspondence in arbitrary dimension.
  • Weak factorisation. Any birational map between smooth projective varieties factors as a sequence of smooth blow-ups and blow-downs — proven via log resolution by Włodarczyk and Abramovich–Karu–Matsuki–Włodarczyk.
  • Log resolution. Resolves a pair $(X, D)$ so that the total transform $\pi^{-1}(D) \cup \mathrm{Exc}(\pi)$ is a simple normal crossing divisor — the technical hypothesis under which most modern birational geometry operates.
  • Open frontier — characteristic $p$. Abhyankar settled char $p$ for surfaces, Cossart–Piltant for threefolds. Dimension $\ge 4$ remains open. de Jong's alterations give a generically finite cover by a smooth variety in any characteristic — a strictly weaker substitute that nevertheless suffices for many applications (étale cohomology, Bertini).

Connections

Resolution of singularities sits at a crossroads: it is the input that lets birational geometry assume "everything is smooth", and its detailed structure (exceptional divisors, log discrepancies) is exactly the data that the minimal model program then manipulates. The next frontier is making positive characteristic and singular-foliation versions work — open problems whose resolution would unlock huge swaths of arithmetic and dynamical algebraic geometry.

  • Singular cubics & reduction — the simplest singular varieties, their group laws, and how they arise via reduction mod $p$.
  • Algebraic curves: higher genus — once a singular curve is normalised, its higher-genus invariants come into play.
  • Moduli spaces — the Deligne–Mumford compactification adds nodal curves (one of the simplest singular degenerations).
  • Mirror symmetry — crepant resolutions of Calabi–Yau orbifolds underlie stringy invariants and the McKay correspondence in higher dimension.
  • Étale cohomology — de Jong's alterations are the char-$p$ substitute for resolution that powers many cohomological arguments.

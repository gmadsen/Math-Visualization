Random walks and mixing

Markov chains, stationary distributions, total-variation mixing, the spectral-gap bound on mixing time, the coupling method, and Markov-chain Monte Carlo. Every concept is paired with a small chain you can drive: step it, watch the empirical frequencies converge to $\pi$, and read off the cost of forgetting where you started.

1. Markov chains and transition matrices

A (discrete-time, time-homogeneous) Markov chain on a state space $S$ is a stochastic process $(X_n)_{n\ge 0}$ with the memoryless property $$\mathbb{P}(X_{n+1}=y\mid X_0,\ldots,X_n)\;=\;\mathbb{P}(X_{n+1}=y\mid X_n)\;=\;P_{X_n,y}.$$ Conditional on the present, the past tells you nothing extra about the future. The numbers $P_{xy}$ assemble into a transition matrix $P\in[0,1]^{S\times S}$ which is row-stochastic: each row $P(x,\cdot)$ is a probability distribution over next states, so $\sum_y P_{xy}=1$.

The $n$-step law is the matrix power: $\mathbb{P}(X_n=y\mid X_0=x)=(P^n)_{xy}$. The space of row-stochastic matrices is closed under multiplication (Chapman–Kolmogorov $P^{m+n}=P^m P^n$), so iterating the chain is just iterating $P$.

Discrete vs.\ continuous time. Replace $P^n$ by a one-parameter semigroup $P_t=e^{tQ}$ with infinitesimal generator $Q$ (rates instead of probabilities) and you get a continuous-time chain. The phenomenology — stationary distributions, mixing, ergodicity — is parallel; we'll work in discrete time.

Worked example: a 4-state graph walk. On a graph $G=(V,E)$ the simple random walk steps from $x$ to a uniformly random neighbour: $P_{xy}=\mathbb{1}[xy\in E]/d_x$. The widget below lets you step a chip on a small graph and watches the running visit-frequency.

2. Stationary distributions

A probability vector $\pi$ on $S$ is stationary for $P$ if $\pi P=\pi$, i.e.\ $\pi$ is fixed by one step of the chain. Equivalently, $\pi^\top$ is a right eigenvector of $P^\top$ with eigenvalue $1$. Every finite-state chain has at least one stationary $\pi$ (Brouwer / Perron–Frobenius applied to the row-stochastic matrix $P$).

Fundamental ergodic theorem (finite state). If a finite-state chain is irreducible (every state reaches every other) and aperiodic ($\gcd$ of return times to each state is $1$), then $\pi$ is unique and $P^n\to\Pi$ where every row of $\Pi$ equals $\pi$. The chain forgets its starting state.

For reversible chains, detailed balance gives $\pi$ on a plate: $\pi$ satisfies $\pi_i P_{ij}=\pi_j P_{ji}$ for all $i,j$. Summing over $i$ recovers stationarity, but reversibility is strictly stronger — asymmetric drift breaks it.

Reducible counterexample. Two disjoint closed classes give a one-parameter family of stationary distributions: any convex combination of the per-class stationaries. The widget animates the matrix powers $P^n$ side-by-side so you can watch the row-collapse.

3. Mixing time

How fast does $P^n(x,\cdot)$ approach the stationary $\pi$? Quantify the gap with total variation distance: $$\|\mu-\nu\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\;=\;\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{x\in S}|\mu(x)-\nu(x)|\;=\;\sup_{A\subseteq S}|\mu(A)-\nu(A)|.$$ The factor of $\tfrac{1}{2}$ converts the $L^1$ norm into the supremum-of-events characterisation. TV distance is a metric on probability measures, takes values in $[0,1]$, and is monotone non-increasing along the chain (the data-processing inequality).

Define the worst-case TV from start state $x$ at time $t$: $$d(t)\;=\;\max_{x\in S}\;\|P^t(x,\cdot)-\pi\|_{\mathrm{TV}}.$$ Then the mixing time is $$t_{\mathrm{mix}}(\varepsilon)\;=\;\min\{t\ge 0 : d(t)\le\varepsilon\}.$$ The convention $t_{\mathrm{mix}}=t_{\mathrm{mix}}(1/4)$ is standard (any constant works, by submultiplicativity $d(s+t)\le 2d(s)d(t)$).

Why $1/4$? Submultiplicativity of $d(t)$ implies $d(kt_{\mathrm{mix}})\le 2^{-k}$. So $t_{\mathrm{mix}}(\varepsilon)\le \lceil\log_2(1/\varepsilon)\rceil\,t_{\mathrm{mix}}$. The threshold constant only affects $\log(1/\varepsilon)$ factors, not the order of $t_{\mathrm{mix}}$.

4. Spectral gap and rapid mixing

For a reversible chain (i.e.\ $\pi_i P_{ij}=\pi_j P_{ji}$), the transition matrix $P$ is self-adjoint with respect to the weighted inner product $\langle f,g\rangle_\pi=\sum_i \pi_i f_i g_i$. So $P$ has real eigenvalues $$1=\lambda_1\;>\;\lambda_2\;\ge\;\cdots\;\ge\;\lambda_n\;\ge\;-1.$$ The spectral gap is $\gamma=1-\lambda_2$ (the relaxation gap), and the absolute spectral gap is $\gamma_*=1-\max(|\lambda_2|,|\lambda_n|)$.

Spectral mixing-time bound (reversible). $$\frac{\gamma_*}{2}\,\log\!\frac{1}{2\varepsilon}\;\le\;t_{\mathrm{mix}}(\varepsilon)\;\le\;\frac{1}{\gamma_*}\,\log\!\frac{1}{\pi_{\min}\varepsilon},$$ where $\pi_{\min}=\min_i\pi_i$. Big gap ⇒ fast mixing; tiny gap ⇒ either a near-disconnection ($\lambda_2\to 1$, Cheeger) or a near-bipartite oscillation ($\lambda_n\to -1$).

Why does $\lambda_2$ control the rate? Decompose any starting distribution in the basis of $P$-eigenvectors $u_1,\ldots,u_n$. The component along $u_1\propto\pi$ is preserved; every other component $\langle p,u_k\rangle$ decays by a factor of $\lambda_k$ per step. After $t$ steps the surviving contribution is at most $|\lambda_{\max\;{\rm nontrivial}}|^t$ in $\ell^2(\pi)$ — Cauchy–Schwarz then converts this to TV.

The two slow modes. $\lambda_2$ near $1$ marks a slow geometric mode that pools mass on one side of a sparse cut (Cheeger's inequality is the rigorous version). $\lambda_n$ near $-1$ marks a parity oscillation: the walk alternates between halves of a near-bipartite graph, and the parity needs many steps to relax.

5. Coupling for upper bounds on mixing

A coupling of two probability measures $\mu,\nu$ on $S$ is a joint distribution on $S\times S$ with marginals $\mu$ and $\nu$. The space of couplings is non-empty (the product $\mu\otimes\nu$ is one), and total variation distance is the optimal coupling cost: $$\|\mu-\nu\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\;=\;\inf_{(X,Y)\sim\text{coupling}}\;\mathbb{P}(X\ne Y).$$

For Markov chains, a Markovian coupling is a process $(X_t,Y_t)_{t\ge 0}$ on $S\times S$ where each marginal evolves as the chain $P$, and the chains agree forever after the first meeting time $\tau=\inf\{t:X_t=Y_t\}$. The coupling inequality $$\|\mathcal{L}(X_t)-\mathcal{L}(Y_t)\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\;\le\;\mathbb{P}(\tau>t)$$ is the workhorse of mixing-time upper bounds: design a coupling under which meeting is fast, and rapid mixing falls out.

Lazy walk on the hypercube. On $\{0,1\}^n$ the lazy walk picks a coordinate uniformly and randomises it. Couple $X,Y$ to share both choices: same coordinate, same new bit. After every coordinate has been visited at least once, $X_t=Y_t$. Coupon collector gives meeting time $\sim n\log n$, hence $t_{\mathrm{mix}}=O(n\log n)$. The actual mixing time is $\frac{1}{2}n\log n+O(n)$ (Aldous).

Card shuffling. For top-in-at-random shuffles, couple two decks by shuffling the same card to the same target. Once every card has been touched, the decks agree — coupon collector again. Riffle shuffles need a more delicate coupling and give $\sim\frac{3}{2}\log_2 n$ shuffles for $n$ cards (Bayer–Diaconis).

6. Markov-chain Monte Carlo

Suppose we want to sample from a target distribution $\pi$ on $S$ — perhaps a posterior, a Boltzmann distribution, or a uniform distribution over a complicated combinatorial set. Direct sampling may be impossible, but MCMC sidesteps this: build a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is $\pi$, run it long enough to mix, and read off samples.

The Metropolis–Hastings recipe constructs such a chain from any irreducible proposal kernel $q(y\mid x)$. From the current state $x$, propose $y\sim q(\cdot\mid x)$, then accept with probability $$\alpha(x,y)\;=\;\min\!\Big(1,\;\frac{\pi(y)\,q(x\mid y)}{\pi(x)\,q(y\mid x)}\Big),$$ otherwise stay at $x$. Detailed balance holds by construction, so $\pi$ is stationary. For symmetric proposals ($q(y\mid x)=q(x\mid y)$, e.g.\ Metropolis, Gaussian random-walk), $\alpha=\min(1,\pi(y)/\pi(x))$.

Why mixing time is the cost. Independent samples from $\pi$ would be ideal, but consecutive MCMC samples are highly correlated. Roughly, only after $t_{\mathrm{mix}}$ steps is the chain "almost independent" of its starting point. So the cost of one effectively independent draw is $\Theta(t_{\mathrm{mix}})$, and the spectral-gap bound becomes a statistical bound on sampling cost.

The acceptance/exploration trade-off. A bold proposal (large jumps) explores the state space quickly but is often rejected when $\pi$ has narrow support; a timid proposal accepts often but barely moves. The widget below shows this on a small bimodal target.

7. Connections

Random walks sit at the intersection of probability, spectral theory, and combinatorics — the mixing-time question alone has driven major breakthroughs in each.

Markov chains and stochastic processes

Discrete-time martingales and the optional-stopping theorem give the cleanest proofs of hitting-time identities; probability theory supplies the convergence machinery.

Spectral graph theory

Spectral graph theory is the linear-algebra side: $\lambda_2(L)$ controls mixing, isoperimetric inequalities (Cheeger), expander graphs, and explicit constructions (Ramanujan graphs). The walk on a graph is the discrete Brownian motion.

Algorithms and MCMC

Markov-chain Monte Carlo (Metropolis, Glauber, Gibbs) reduces sampling from intractable distributions to mixing of an explicit chain. Path-coupling and conductance arguments are how rigorous mixing bounds are proved.

Probabilistic and extremal combinatorics

Probabilistic method proofs that go through random-walk concentration; extremal combinatorics applications via spectral / Cheeger arguments.

Open frontiers: rapid mixing of Glauber dynamics for Ising and hard-core models (recent breakthroughs via spectral independence); high-dimensional expansion (HDX) and local-to-global theorems; quantum walks and exotic mixing rates.

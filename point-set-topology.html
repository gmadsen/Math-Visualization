Point-set topology

From $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ to opens: start with metric spaces, forget the numbers, and keep only the lattice of open sets. Continuity, compactness, connectedness, and the separation hierarchy are what survive — the invariants on which algebraic topology and measure theory build.

1. Metric spaces

A metric on a set $X$ is a function $d\colon X\times X\to[0,\infty)$ satisfying, for all $x,y,z\in X$,

  1. $d(x,y)=0\iff x=y$ (identity of indiscernibles),
  2. $d(x,y)=d(y,x)$ (symmetry),
  3. $d(x,z)\le d(x,y)+d(y,z)$ (triangle inequality).

The pair $(X,d)$ is a metric space. The three axioms are the minimum you need to talk about "how close" two points are without leaning on linear structure.

The standard examples

spacemetriccomment
$\mathbb{R}^n$$d_2(x,y)=\sqrt{\sum_i(x_i-y_i)^2}$Euclidean; the default

The open ball of radius $\varepsilon>0$ around $x\in X$ is $$B(x,\varepsilon)=\{y\in X:d(x,y)<\varepsilon\}.$$ A set $U\subseteq X$ is open if every $x\in U$ lies inside some $B(x,\varepsilon)\subseteq U$. The open balls are a basis: the family of open sets is exactly the arbitrary unions of open balls. Closed sets are complements of opens, equivalently the sets containing every limit point of any sequence they contain.

Continuity, $\varepsilon$–$\delta$. A map $f\colon(X,d_X)\to(Y,d_Y)$ is continuous at $x_0$ if $$\forall\varepsilon>0,\ \exists\delta>0: d_X(x,x_0)<\delta\implies d_Y(f(x),f(x_0))<\varepsilon.$$ Equivalently, $f^{-1}(B_Y(f(x_0),\varepsilon))\supseteq B_X(x_0,\delta)$. The $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ dance is really a statement about preimages of opens.

Cauchy sequences and completeness

A sequence $(x_n)$ in $(X,d)$ is Cauchy if $d(x_n,x_m)\to 0$ as $n,m\to\infty$. Every convergent sequence is Cauchy; the converse is the defining property of a complete metric space. $\mathbb{R}^n$ is complete. $\mathbb{Q}$ with the usual metric is not — its completion is $\mathbb{R}$. $\mathbb{Q}$ with the $p$-adic metric has a different completion, $\mathbb{Q}_p$ (see $p$-adic numbers). Completeness is a metric property, not a topological one: it depends on $d$, not just on the opens — $(0,1)$ and $\mathbb{R}$ are homeomorphic but only one is complete under the inherited Euclidean metric.

Banach fixed-point theorem. If $(X,d)$ is complete and $T\colon X\to X$ is a contraction ($d(Tx,Ty)\le k\,d(x,y)$ for some $k<1$), then $T$ has a unique fixed point, reached by iterating $Tx_0,T^2x_0,\dots$ from any $x_0$. This single theorem gives Picard existence for ODEs, the inverse function theorem, and the convergence of Newton's method.

Which brings us to the first mind-stretch of the subject. The shape of an open ball depends drastically on the choice of metric — a disk in $d_2$, a square in $d_\infty$, a diamond in $d_1$, a single point in the discrete metric — but the three metrics $d_1, d_2, d_\infty$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ all yield the same collection of open sets and so define the same topology. Only the discrete metric has a different topology, because its balls of radius $<1$ are singletons. 2. Topological spaces

The widget above gave away the punchline: three different metrics on $\mathbb{R}^2$ produced the same family of open sets. So the natural object to keep is the family of opens itself, and the natural generalisation is to drop the metric.

A topology on a set $X$ is a collection $\tau\subseteq\mathcal{P}(X)$ of "open" subsets satisfying:

  1. $\emptyset\in\tau$ and $X\in\tau$,
  2. arbitrary unions of opens are open: $U_\alpha\in\tau\implies\bigcup_\alpha U_\alpha\in\tau$,
  3. finite intersections of opens are open: $U_1,\dots,U_n\in\tau\implies U_1\cap\cdots\cap U_n\in\tau$.

The pair $(X,\tau)$ is a topological space. Every metric space is a topological space with $\tau$ the collection generated by open balls. Most of the theorems below assume only the axioms — the metric is scaffolding you remove once built.

Why infinite intersections are banned. In $\mathbb{R}$, $\bigcap_{n\ge 1}(-1/n,1/n)=\{0\}$, which is not open. Arbitrary intersections of opens can collapse to non-open sets; only finite intersections are safe.

Extremes and small examples

On any set $X$ there are two trivial topologies:

  • the discrete topology $\tau=\mathcal{P}(X)$ (every subset open);
  • the indiscrete topology $\tau=\{\emptyset,X\}$ (only the trivial opens).

Between these extremes sits everything interesting. On $\mathbb{R}$, the usual topology has as opens exactly the arbitrary unions of open intervals. Another topology on $\mathbb{R}$ is the cofinite topology: opens are $\emptyset$ and the complements of finite sets. It satisfies the axioms and is strictly coarser than the Euclidean one.

Sierpiński space. $S=\{0,1\}$ with $\tau=\{\emptyset,\{1\},\{0,1\}\}$. Despite having just two points, it is the universal "observable" space: a function $f\colon X\to S$ is continuous iff $f^{-1}(\{1\})$ is open in $X$, i.e. continuous functions to $S$ are open subsets. It recurs all over pointless topology and computer science.

Bases and subbases

Specifying a topology by listing every open set is impractical. A basis $\mathcal{B}$ for a topology on $X$ is a family of subsets such that (i) every $x\in X$ lies in some $B\in\mathcal{B}$, and (ii) if $x\in B_1\cap B_2$ there is $B_3\in\mathcal{B}$ with $x\in B_3\subseteq B_1\cap B_2$. The topology generated by $\mathcal{B}$ is the set of arbitrary unions of basis elements. For $\mathbb{R}^n$, the open balls are a basis; so are the open rectangles. A subbasis is any collection whose finite intersections form a basis — usually the easiest way to specify large topologies (e.g. the product topology).

Subspace and product topologies

If $(X,\tau)$ is a topological space and $A\subseteq X$, the subspace topology on $A$ has opens $\{U\cap A:U\in\tau\}$. If $(X_i,\tau_i)_{i\in I}$ is a family, the product topology on $\prod_i X_i$ has as subbasis the cylinders $\pi_i^{-1}(U_i)$ for $U_i\in\tau_i$; equivalently, a basis is the collection of boxes $\prod_i U_i$ with $U_i\in\tau_i$ and $U_i=X_i$ for all but finitely many $i$. For finite products the "boxes" description is enough, and matches your intuition from $\mathbb{R}^2=\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}$.

3. Continuity and homeomorphism

With opens as primitive, the definition of continuity no longer needs $\varepsilon$ or $\delta$:

Definition. $f\colon(X,\tau_X)\to(Y,\tau_Y)$ is continuous iff $f^{-1}(V)\in\tau_X$ for every $V\in\tau_Y$. Equivalently, preimages of closed sets are closed.

On metric spaces this definition is equivalent to $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ (proof: unwind "$f^{-1}(B(f(x_0),\varepsilon))$ is open" as "contains an open ball around $x_0$"). But the abstract version is robust: it composes trivially ($f^{-1}g^{-1}(V) = (g\circ f)^{-1}(V)$), it handles product and subspace topologies without fuss, and it extends to spaces where no natural metric exists (Zariski, étale, Stone–Čech).

Homeomorphisms

A homeomorphism is a continuous bijection $f\colon X\to Y$ whose inverse $f^{-1}$ is also continuous. Equivalently, $f$ is a bijection under which opens correspond to opens in both directions. Homeomorphisms are the isomorphisms of the category $\mathsf{Top}$; two spaces that differ by a homeomorphism are indistinguishable to a topologist.

Continuity alone is not enough: the identity map $(\mathbb{R},\tau_{\text{discrete}})\to(\mathbb{R},\tau_{\text{usual}})$ is continuous (preimage of any Euclidean open is a subset of the discrete space, hence open) but its inverse is not. The two topologies are not homeomorphic.

Classifying invariants

To show two spaces are not homeomorphic, exhibit a topological invariant they differ on: compactness, connectedness, number of components, number of ends, cardinality of the topology, homotopy type, cohomology, the cut-point count. The classical warm-up:

The open interval and the circle are not homeomorphic. $(0,1)$ has the property: deleting any point disconnects the space into two pieces. $S^1$ does not: deleting one point of a circle leaves a connected arc. So any candidate homeomorphism would send a cut point to a non-cut point, a contradiction with being a bijection that preserves connectedness.
$[0,1]$ and $(0,1)$ are not homeomorphic. $[0,1]$ is compact, $(0,1)$ is not (§4). Or: $[0,1]$ has two non-cut points (the endpoints), $(0,1)$ has none.
Continuous images of connected are connected; of compact are compact. These two facts are the workhorses — every invariant in this course boils down to one of them.

4. Compactness

Compactness is the topological analogue of finiteness. Its technical form is unintuitive at first, but the consequences — extreme value theorem, uniform continuity, Heine–Borel, Tychonoff's theorem — make it the single most useful property a space can have.

Definition. A topological space $X$ is compact if every open cover has a finite subcover: given any family $\{U_\alpha\}$ of opens with $\bigcup_\alpha U_\alpha=X$, there are finitely many indices $\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n$ with $U_{\alpha_1}\cup\cdots\cup U_{\alpha_n}=X$.

Equivalent formulations: every family of closed sets with the finite-intersection property has nonempty total intersection; every net (or filter) has a convergent subnet (Bolzano–Weierstrass–style). On metric spaces, compact also means sequentially compact: every sequence has a convergent subsequence. Sequential compactness and open-cover compactness are equivalent for metric spaces but can diverge on general topological spaces.

Heine–Borel and its kin

Heine–Borel. In $\mathbb{R}^n$ (with the Euclidean topology), a subset is compact iff it is closed and bounded.

So $[0,1]$, $S^1$, $S^n$, and closed balls are compact. Open intervals, half-lines, all of $\mathbb{R}^n$, and $\mathbb{Q}\cap[0,1]$ are not. "Closed and bounded" is special to finite-dimensional normed spaces — in $\ell^2$ the closed unit ball is not compact because the standard basis sequence $e_1,e_2,\dots$ has no convergent subsequence.

Consequences

  • Continuous image. If $X$ is compact and $f\colon X\to Y$ is continuous, $f(X)$ is compact. Immediate corollary: the extreme value theorem — a continuous $\mathbb{R}$-valued function on a compact space attains its max and min.
  • Uniform continuity. Continuous $f\colon K\to(Y,d_Y)$ with $K$ compact metric is automatically uniformly continuous.
  • Closed map. Compact $\to$ Hausdorff continuous maps are automatically closed, hence homeomorphisms if bijective.
  • Tychonoff. Arbitrary products of compact spaces are compact (in the product topology). Equivalent to the axiom of choice.
Compact Hausdorff is the "good" category. In a compact Hausdorff space, every continuous bijection to a Hausdorff space is a homeomorphism (since a continuous map from compact to Hausdorff is closed). This is why compact Hausdorff is the target category for Stone duality, Gelfand duality, and most of functional analysis.

5. Connectedness

The intuitive picture is a space "in one piece." Formally:

Definition. A space $X$ is connected if it cannot be written as $X=U\sqcup V$ with $U,V\in\tau$ nonempty disjoint opens. Equivalently, the only clopen (simultaneously open and closed) subsets are $\emptyset$ and $X$.

Typical connected spaces: intervals in $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{R}^n$, the circle, any convex subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$, $\mathsf{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ (though $\mathsf{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$ is not). Disconnected: $\mathbb{Q}$ (take $U=\{x:x<\sqrt{2}\}$, $V=\{x:x>\sqrt 2\}$), $\{0\}\cup\{1\}$ as a subspace of $\mathbb{R}$, any finite space with the discrete topology.

Path-connectedness

$X$ is path-connected if for any $x,y\in X$ there is a continuous $\gamma\colon[0,1]\to X$ with $\gamma(0)=x,\gamma(1)=y$. Every path-connected space is connected: otherwise a path would pull back the separation into a disconnection of $[0,1]$, contradicting the connectedness of $[0,1]$ (which is the key atomic fact, proven from the supremum property of $\mathbb{R}$).

The converse fails.

The topologist's sine curve. Let $T=\{(x,\sin(1/x)):x\in(0,1]\}\cup\{0\}\times[-1,1]$. The limit segment on the $y$-axis is glued to the oscillating graph by closure in $\mathbb{R}^2$. $T$ is connected (closure of a connected set is connected, and the graph is connected) but not path-connected — any path from a point on the $y$-axis to a point on the graph would have to traverse infinitely many oscillations in finite time, impossible for a continuous path.

Components

The connected components of $X$ are the maximal connected subsets — they are closed, and they partition $X$. Similarly the path components partition $X$ and refine the connected components. Path components can fail to be closed (again the sine curve).

Intermediate value theorem. If $X$ is connected and $f\colon X\to\mathbb{R}$ is continuous, $f(X)$ is an interval. Combined with "connected subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ are intervals," this is Bolzano's IVT in one line.

6. Separation and countability axioms

The bare definition of a topological space is very permissive — the indiscrete topology is a topology, the Sierpiński space is a topology, the cofinite topology on $\mathbb{R}$ is a topology. Before you can prove theorems you often need to exclude pathologies. The separation axioms are a graded hierarchy of "points and closed sets can be pried apart by opens."

axiomstatement
$T_0$distinct points have distinct neighborhood filters
$T_1$for $x\ne y$, some open contains $x$ but not $y$ (equivalently, every singleton is closed)
$T_2$ Hausdorffdistinct points have disjoint open neighborhoods
$T_3$ regular$T_1$ + a point and a closed set not containing it have disjoint opens around them
$T_4$ normal$T_1$ + two disjoint closed sets have disjoint opens around them

The implications run $T_4\Rightarrow T_3\Rightarrow T_2\Rightarrow T_1\Rightarrow T_0$. Each reverse arrow fails: there are $T_1$-non-Hausdorff, Hausdorff-non-regular, etc. Every metric space is normal. Hausdorff is usually the lowest rung worth demanding because limits of sequences become unique — sequences can have at most one limit iff the space is Hausdorff. Compact Hausdorff spaces are automatically normal.

Two critical payoffs

Urysohn's lemma. In a normal space, any two disjoint closed sets $A,B$ can be separated by a continuous function: there exists continuous $f\colon X\to[0,1]$ with $f\equiv 0$ on $A$, $f\equiv 1$ on $B$. Functions distinguish closed sets, not just opens.
Urysohn's metrization theorem. A regular, second-countable Hausdorff space is metrizable — there is a metric on $X$ inducing its topology.

Countability axioms

  • First-countable. Every point has a countable neighborhood basis. On such spaces, sequences suffice: closure = limits of sequences. Every metric space is first-countable.
  • Second-countable. There is a countable basis for the topology. Stronger; implies separable (has a countable dense subset) and Lindelöf (every open cover has a countable subcover). $\mathbb{R}^n$ is second-countable.
Classical pathologies. Sierpiński $\{0,1\}$ with $\tau=\{\emptyset,\{1\},\{1,0\}\}$: $T_0$ but not $T_1$. $\mathbb{R}$ with the cofinite topology: $T_1$ but not Hausdorff (any two nonempty opens meet). The double-origin line ($\mathbb{R}$ with two copies of $0$ glued away from $0$): Hausdorff fails at the two origins — any opens around them must intersect.

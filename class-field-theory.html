Class field theory

Every abelian extension of a number field is controlled by a purely internal invariant of the field — its ray class groups. From Kronecker–Weber at the bottom to the idèle-class reciprocity at the top, CFT is the first crossing of arithmetic and Galois that works in full generality.

1. Motivation: reciprocity for abelian extensions

Algebraic number theory begins with the question: given a polynomial $f(x)\in\mathbb{Z}[x]$, for which primes $p$ does it split into linear factors modulo $p$? When $f$ has abelian Galois group, the answer is astonishingly clean: it depends only on congruence conditions on $p$.

The archetype is the cyclotomic case. The $n$-th cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$ is Galois over $\mathbb{Q}$ with

$$ \operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q}) \;\cong\; (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times, \qquad \sigma_a(\zeta_n)=\zeta_n^{a}. $$

A prime $p\nmid n$ is unramified, and its Frobenius is exactly the class $\sigma_p\in(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$. So the splitting of $p$ depends only on $p\bmod n$ — a congruence law. The Kronecker–Weber theorem says this is the whole story over $\mathbb{Q}$:

Kronecker–Weber. Every finite abelian extension $L/\mathbb{Q}$ is contained in some cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$. Equivalently, the maximal abelian extension $\mathbb{Q}^{\mathrm{ab}}=\bigcup_n \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$.

Class field theory is the generalization to an arbitrary number field $K$: abelian extensions of $K$ are governed not by $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$, but by the ray class groups of $K$. Before we state that, we need the (ordinary) class group.

2. The ideal class group

Let $K$ be a number field with ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$. Not every $\mathcal{O}_K$ is a UFD: $\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$ has $6 = 2\cdot 3 = (1+\sqrt{-5})(1-\sqrt{-5})$ with no common refinement by irreducibles. The remedy, due to Dedekind, is to replace numbers by ideals.

A fractional ideal of $K$ is a nonzero $\mathcal{O}_K$-submodule $I\subset K$ such that $dI\subset\mathcal{O}_K$ for some $d\in\mathcal{O}_K$. The fractional ideals form a free abelian group $I_K$ under multiplication, generated by the prime ideals. The principal fractional ideals $P_K=\{(\alpha):\alpha\in K^\times\}$ form a subgroup. The quotient

$$ \operatorname{Cl}(K) \;=\; I_K / P_K $$

is the ideal class group. It is finite, its order is the class number $h_K$, and $h_K=1$ precisely when $\mathcal{O}_K$ is a PID (equivalently a UFD). So $\operatorname{Cl}(K)$ is a numerical measure of the failure of unique factorization.

Example. $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$, $\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$, class number $2$. The non-trivial class is represented by $\mathfrak{p}=(2,1+\sqrt{-5})$. Indeed $\mathfrak{p}^2 = (2)$ — the square is principal, so $[\mathfrak{p}]^2 = 1$ in $\operatorname{Cl}(K)$.

For imaginary quadratic fields $K=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$ with $d<0$, the class number is computable and small for small $|d|$. A classical result (Heegner, Stark): there are exactly nine imaginary-quadratic fields with $h_K=1$, namely $d=-1,-2,-3,-7,-11,-19,-43,-67,-163$.

3. The Hilbert class field

Class groups would be a curious but isolated invariant — except that they are Galois groups. Specifically, associated to every number field $K$ there is a distinguished abelian extension, the Hilbert class field $H$, characterized as follows.

Theorem (Furtwängler, Artin). There exists a unique finite abelian extension $H/K$ with:
  1. every prime of $K$ is unramified in $H$ (and the infinite places are also unramified);
  2. $H$ is maximal with this property;
  3. the Artin map gives an isomorphism $\operatorname{Cl}(K) \xrightarrow{\sim} \operatorname{Gal}(H/K)$.
In particular $[H:K] = h_K$.

So the class group of $K$ is literally the Galois group of a canonical abelian unramified cover of $K$. The "deformed" unique factorization in $K$ becomes honest unique factorization in $H$ (for ideals coming from $K$): any ideal $\mathfrak{a}$ of $\mathcal{O}_K$ becomes principal when extended to $\mathcal{O}_H$ (this is the principal ideal theorem).

Worked example: $K=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$

We have $h_K=2$, so $[H:K]=2$ and $H$ is a quadratic extension of $K$, everywhere unramified, and quadratic, biquadratic, or cyclic over $\mathbb{Q}$. Computation (or a table) gives

$$ H \;=\; K(\sqrt{-1}) \;=\; \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5}, i) \;=\; \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-1},\sqrt{5}). $$

Note $H/\mathbb{Q}$ is biquadratic of degree $4$, abelian with Galois group $(\mathbb{Z}/2)^2$, and contains the cyclotomic $\mathbb{Q}(i,\sqrt{5}) \subset \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_{20})$, consistent with Kronecker–Weber. The ramified primes of $H/\mathbb{Q}$ are exactly the ramified primes of $K/\mathbb{Q}$ (namely $2$ and $5$): no new ramification has been introduced.

4. The Artin (reciprocity) map

Let $L/K$ be a finite abelian extension of number fields, and let $\mathfrak{p}$ be a prime of $K$ unramified in $L$. Choose any prime $\mathfrak{P}$ of $L$ above $\mathfrak{p}$. The decomposition group $D_\mathfrak{P}\subset\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)$ has a canonical generator, the Frobenius $\operatorname{Frob}_\mathfrak{P}$, characterized by

$$ \operatorname{Frob}_\mathfrak{P}(x) \equiv x^{N\mathfrak{p}} \pmod{\mathfrak{P}}\quad\text{for all } x\in\mathcal{O}_L, $$

where $N\mathfrak{p} = \#(\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{p})$ is the absolute norm. Because $L/K$ is abelian, conjugation of $\mathfrak{P}$ by $\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)$ leaves the decomposition group unchanged, so $\operatorname{Frob}_\mathfrak{P}$ depends only on $\mathfrak{p}$. We write $\bigl(\tfrac{L/K}{\mathfrak{p}}\bigr)$ or $\operatorname{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}$.

Extending multiplicatively to ideals prime to the ramified set yields a homomorphism

$$ \Big(\,\tfrac{L/K}{\cdot}\,\Big):\; I_K^{S} \;\longrightarrow\; \operatorname{Gal}(L/K), $$

where $S$ is the (finite) set of ramified primes.

Artin reciprocity (main theorem, conductor form). For every finite abelian $L/K$ there is a modulus $\mathfrak{m}$ (its conductor) supported on $S\cup\{\infty\}$ such that the Artin map factors through the ray class group $\operatorname{Cl}_\mathfrak{m}(K)$ and gives a surjection $$ \operatorname{Cl}_\mathfrak{m}(K)\twoheadrightarrow \operatorname{Gal}(L/K), $$ with kernel generated by the classes of primes that split completely in $L$. In particular the Frobenius of $\mathfrak{p}$ depends only on the class of $\mathfrak{p}$ in $\operatorname{Cl}_\mathfrak{m}(K)$.

For the Hilbert class field ($\mathfrak{m}=1$) this recovers the isomorphism $\operatorname{Cl}(K)\xrightarrow{\sim}\operatorname{Gal}(H/K)$. For $K=\mathbb{Q}$, $L=\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$, the conductor is $(n)\cdot\infty$ and the Artin map is $p\mapsto\sigma_p$, $\sigma_p(\zeta_n)=\zeta_n^p$ — the cyclotomic reciprocity law.

5. Ray class fields, conductors, idèles

The Hilbert class field controls unramified abelian extensions. To capture all abelian extensions, we allow a controlled amount of ramification, tracked by a modulus.

Modulus and ray class group

A modulus $\mathfrak{m}$ of $K$ is a formal product $\mathfrak{m} = \mathfrak{m}_{\mathrm{f}}\cdot\mathfrak{m}_\infty$ where $\mathfrak{m}_{\mathrm{f}}\subset\mathcal{O}_K$ is an ideal and $\mathfrak{m}_\infty$ is a subset of the real embeddings of $K$. Define

  • $I_K^\mathfrak{m}$ = fractional ideals coprime to $\mathfrak{m}_{\mathrm{f}}$;
  • $P_K^\mathfrak{m}$ = principal $(\alpha)$ with $\alpha \equiv 1 \pmod{\mathfrak{m}_{\mathrm{f}}}$ and $\alpha$ positive at every real place in $\mathfrak{m}_\infty$.

The ray class group modulo $\mathfrak{m}$ is $\operatorname{Cl}_\mathfrak{m}(K) = I_K^\mathfrak{m}/P_K^\mathfrak{m}$. It is finite. For $\mathfrak{m}=1$ it recovers $\operatorname{Cl}(K)$; for $K=\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathfrak{m}=(n)\cdot\infty$, it is $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$.

Existence theorem. For every modulus $\mathfrak{m}$ of $K$ there is a unique abelian extension $K(\mathfrak{m})/K$ — the ray class field of conductor $\mathfrak{m}$ — with $$ \operatorname{Gal}(K(\mathfrak{m})/K)\;\cong\;\operatorname{Cl}_\mathfrak{m}(K). $$ Moreover every finite abelian $L/K$ sits inside $K(\mathfrak{m})$ for some (minimal) $\mathfrak{m}$, and this minimal modulus is the conductor $\mathfrak{f}(L/K)$. The conductor is supported exactly on the ramified primes.

Idèles — the modulus-free formulation

Taking inverse limits over all moduli, the ray class groups pack into a single topological group. Define the idèles

$$ \mathbb{A}_K^\times \;=\; \prod_v{}' K_v^\times $$

(restricted product over completions $K_v$, with $x_v\in\mathcal{O}_v^\times$ for almost all finite $v$), and the idèle-class group $C_K=\mathbb{A}_K^\times / K^\times$ (diagonal embedding). Then:

Artin reciprocity (idelic form). There is a continuous surjection $$ \theta_K\;:\; C_K \;\twoheadrightarrow\; \operatorname{Gal}(K^{\mathrm{ab}}/K), $$ with kernel the connected component of $1$. Finite abelian extensions $L/K$ correspond bijectively to finite-index open subgroups $N\subset C_K$, via $N = \ker\bigl(C_K\to\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)\bigr)$.

This packages Kronecker–Weber ($K=\mathbb{Q}$, $C_\mathbb{Q}/\text{connected}$ $\cong\widehat{\mathbb{Z}}^\times$), the Hilbert class field (all-ramified = $\mathcal{O}_K^\times$ kernel), and all ray class fields into one statement.

6. Takagi's existence theorem

Artin reciprocity gives, for each abelian $L/K$, a surjection from a ray class group (or from the idèle class group $C_K$) onto $\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)$. Takagi's existence theorem provides the converse: it says the correspondence is a bijection, so any admissible congruence subgroup is actually the kernel of the Artin map for a genuine abelian extension.

Existence theorem (Takagi). Fix a number field $K$.
  • Classical form. For every modulus $\mathfrak{m}$ and every subgroup $H$ with $P_K^{\mathfrak{m}}\subseteq H\subseteq I_K^{\mathfrak{m}}$ of finite index, there is a unique finite abelian extension $L/K$, unramified outside $\mathfrak{m}$, with $H = \ker\!\bigl(I_K^{\mathfrak{m}}\to\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)\bigr)$. In particular $\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)\cong I_K^{\mathfrak{m}}/H$.
  • Idelic form. Finite abelian extensions $L/K$ are in bijection with finite-index open subgroups $N\subset C_K$, via $N=\ker\theta_{L/K}$. Under this bijection $L\subseteq L'\Leftrightarrow N\supseteq N'$, so the lattice of abelian extensions is anti-isomorphic to the lattice of open finite-index subgroups of $C_K$.

Combined with the reciprocity theorem, existence turns class field theory into an algorithm: to build the abelian extension of $K$ with Galois group $G$, find a finite-index subgroup $N\subset C_K$ with $C_K/N\cong G$ and read off the fixed field. For $K=\mathbb{Q}$ this recovers Kronecker–Weber — every finite-index $N\subset C_\mathbb{Q}$ containing the connected component cuts out a subfield of some $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$ — and for imaginary quadratic $K$ it underlies the theory of complex multiplication.

7. The conductor-discriminant formula

Every character $\chi:\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)\to\mathbb{C}^\times$ of a finite abelian extension has an Artin conductor $\mathfrak{f}(\chi)\subset\mathcal{O}_K$: the smallest modulus through which $\chi$ factors on the ray class side, or equivalently the product over ramified primes $\mathfrak{p}$ of $\mathfrak{p}^{n(\chi,\mathfrak{p})}$ where $n(\chi,\mathfrak{p})$ measures how deeply $\chi$ restricts to the higher ramification filtration of $\mathfrak{p}$. The conductor of the extension itself is $\mathfrak{f}(L/K)=\operatorname{lcm}_\chi \mathfrak{f}(\chi)$.

The conductor-discriminant formula (Führerdiskriminantenproduktformel, Hasse) expresses the discriminant of $L/K$ as a product over characters:

$$ \mathfrak{d}_{L/K} \;=\; \prod_{\chi\in \widehat{\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)}} \mathfrak{f}(\chi). $$

So ramification in $L/K$ is controlled character-by-character: an unramified prime contributes to no conductor and to no factor of the discriminant, a tamely ramified prime $\mathfrak{p}$ contributes $\mathfrak{p}$ to exactly the $\chi$ that are non-trivial on inertia, and wildly ramified primes contribute with higher multiplicity via the Swan conductor. Specialising to $L=\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q}$ recovers the classical formula $\operatorname{disc}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q}) = \prod_{p\mid n} p^{\,\varphi(n)(1-\tfrac{1}{p-1})/\cdots}$ as the product over Dirichlet characters mod $n$ of their individual conductors.

8. Quadratic reciprocity as the baby case

Gauss's law of quadratic reciprocity sits inside class field theory as the simplest non-trivial instance of Artin reciprocity over $\mathbb{Q}$. Recall the Legendre symbol $\bigl(\tfrac{q}{p}\bigr)$ equals $+1$ if $q$ is a nonzero square mod $p$, $-1$ otherwise, and $0$ if $p\mid q$.

For a prime $q$, set $q^* = (-1)^{(q-1)/2} q$, so that $q^*\equiv 1\pmod 4$ and $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{q^*})$ is the unique quadratic subfield of $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_q)$. Then for any odd prime $p\ne q$:

Shape of the argument.
  • $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{q^*})/\mathbb{Q})\cong\{\pm 1\}$.
  • $\operatorname{Frob}_p$ in this extension equals $+1$ iff $p$ splits, iff $q^*$ is a square mod $p$, iff $\bigl(\tfrac{q^*}{p}\bigr)=+1$.
  • But $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{q^*})\subset\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_q)$, so by cyclotomic reciprocity $\operatorname{Frob}_p$ depends only on $p\bmod q$: it lands in the unique index-$2$ subgroup of $(\mathbb{Z}/q)^\times$, namely the squares.
  • Therefore $\bigl(\tfrac{q^*}{p}\bigr) = \bigl(\tfrac{p}{q}\bigr)$. Unwinding the sign of $q^*$ gives quadratic reciprocity.

In one line: the Artin map for $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{q^*})/\mathbb{Q}$ factors through $(\mathbb{Z}/q)^\times$, and that factorization is quadratic reciprocity. The general Artin reciprocity theorem extends this idea to every abelian extension of every number field. See quadratic reciprocity for a dedicated exploration, and Frobenius and reciprocity for the general dictionary.

9. What lies beyond — the Langlands program

Class field theory is a complete, beautiful description of the abelian quotient of the absolute Galois group. The full group $G_K = \operatorname{Gal}(\overline{K}/K)$ is very far from abelian, and its non-abelian quotients control such things as the splitting of non-solvable polynomials, points on modular curves, and the arithmetic of elliptic curves.

The Langlands program is the sweeping generalization: it predicts that all finite-dimensional continuous representations of $G_K$ correspond to certain analytic objects called automorphic representations. In one line:

Within this picture:

  • Abelian (CFT). $n=1$. Reciprocity map $C_K\to G_K^{\mathrm{ab}}$. Proved completely by the 1930s.
  • 2-dimensional, over $\mathbb{Q}$. Galois representations attached to modular forms, and in the other direction modularity of elliptic curves. See modular forms and elliptic curves. The modularity theorem for elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ is a highly non-trivial special case.
  • General $n$. Still largely conjectural. Proved for $\mathrm{GL}_n$ over function fields (L. Lafforgue, V. Lafforgue), open over number fields.

For further exploration within the notebook, the closest siblings to CFT are

  • algebraic number theory — rings of integers, ideals, splitting of primes;
  • Galois theory — extensions and their groups;
  • quadratic reciprocity — the baby case, worked in detail;
  • Frobenius and reciprocity — the general dictionary of splitting;
  • $p$-adic numbers — the local completions that build the idèles;
  • Dirichlet series and Euler products — the $L$-functions on the analytic side.

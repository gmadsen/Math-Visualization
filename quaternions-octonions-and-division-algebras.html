Quaternions, octonions, and division algebras

Hamilton carved $i^2=j^2=k^2=ijk=-1$ on a Dublin bridge in 1843. Doubling that algebra one more time gives the octonions; doubling again breaks. Frobenius and Hurwitz tell us why the tower stops at $\mathbb{O}$ — and why $1,2,4,8$ are the only dimensions where multiplication can preserve length.

1. Quaternions $\mathbb{H}$

The quaternions $\mathbb{H}=\mathbb{R}\langle 1,i,j,k\rangle$ form a 4-dimensional real algebra defined by the single bracelet of relations Hamilton carved on Brougham Bridge:

$$i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1.$$

From these one derives $ij=k$, $jk=i$, $ki=j$ — and reversing any factor flips the sign: $ji=-k$, $kj=-i$, $ik=-j$. Multiplication is associative (one realisation: $\mathbb{H}\hookrightarrow M_2(\mathbb{C})$, sending $1,i,j,k$ to specific Pauli-like matrices) but emphatically not commutative. Every element $q=a+bi+cj+dk$ has a conjugate $\overline q=a-bi-cj-dk$ and norm $$|q|^2 = q\,\overline q = a^2+b^2+c^2+d^2,$$ giving $\mathbb{H}$ the underlying inner-product space $\mathbb{R}^4$. For $q\neq 0$ the inverse is $q^{-1}=\overline q/|q|^2$, so $\mathbb{H}$ is a (skew) division algebra.

The imaginary quaternions $\mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{H}=\{bi+cj+dk\}$ form a 3-dimensional subspace, naturally identified with $\mathbb{R}^3$. Restricted to imaginaries, the bracket $[u,v]=uv-vu$ is twice the cross product $2(u\times v)$, and $u\overline v + v\overline u = -2\langle u,v\rangle$ recovers the dot product. The quaternions therefore package both products of $\mathbb{R}^3$ into a single associative law.

2. Unit quaternions and the cover $\mathrm{SU}(2)\to\mathrm{SO}(3)$

The unit quaternions $\mathrm{Sp}(1)=\{q\in\mathbb{H}:|q|=1\}$ are the 3-sphere $S^3\subset\mathbb{R}^4$, equipped with quaternion multiplication. They form a compact Lie group, isomorphic as a Lie group to $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ via $a+bi+cj+dk\mapsto\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}a+bi&c+di\\-c+di&a-bi\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$. The Lie algebra is $\mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{H}\cong\mathfrak{su}(2)\cong\mathfrak{so}(3)$ — three dimensions of pure imaginary, with bracket = quaternion commutator.

Each unit $q$ acts on $\mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{H}\cong\mathbb{R}^3$ by conjugation: $$\rho_q\colon v\longmapsto q\,v\,q^{-1}=q\,v\,\overline q.$$ The map $q\mapsto\rho_q$ is a smooth group homomorphism $\mathrm{Sp}(1)\to\mathrm{SO}(3)$. If $q=\cos(\theta/2)+\sin(\theta/2)\,\hat n$ for a unit imaginary $\hat n$, then $\rho_q$ is the rotation through angle $\theta$ about $\hat n$. The half-angle is the source of the famous double cover: $q$ and $-q$ implement the same rotation, so $\ker\rho=\{\pm 1\}$ and $\mathrm{SO}(3)\cong\mathrm{Sp}(1)/\{\pm 1\}\cong\mathbb{RP}^3$.

3. The Cayley–Dickson tower

Given a real $*$-algebra $A$ (associative, with conjugation $a\mapsto a^*$), the Cayley–Dickson construction produces a new algebra $A'=A\oplus A$ with

$$(a,b)(c,d) = (ac - d^*b,\ da + bc^*),\qquad (a,b)^* = (a^*,-b).$$

Iterating from $A_0=\mathbb{R}$ (where $a^*=a$) generates the canonical chain

$$\mathbb{R}\subset\mathbb{C}\subset\mathbb{H}\subset\mathbb{O}\subset\mathbb{S}\subset\cdots,$$

each step doubling dimension. At every doubling something is sacrificed, and the loss is sharp: once a property fails, no later algebra recovers it.

4. Octonions $\mathbb{O}$

The octonions $\mathbb{O}$ are the Cayley–Dickson double of $\mathbb{H}$. As a real vector space they are $\mathbb{R}^8$ with basis $\{1,e_1,\dots,e_7\}$, and the multiplication is conveniently encoded by the Fano plane: a triangle with three medians and an inscribed circle, whose seven oriented lines list the seven triples that multiply like $\{i,j,k\}$.

If a directed line passes through $e_a\to e_b\to e_c$, then

$$e_a e_b = e_c,\quad e_b e_c = e_a,\quad e_c e_a = e_b,\qquad e_b e_a = -e_c,\quad\text{etc.}$$

Each of the 7 oriented lines therefore generates a copy of the quaternions $\mathbb{H}\hookrightarrow\mathbb{O}$. But triples that don't lie on a common line associate badly: $e_1(e_2 e_4) \neq (e_1 e_2)e_4$ in general. What survives is the alternative law: $$x(xy)=(xx)y,\qquad (yx)x = y(xx).$$ Equivalently (Artin's theorem), every subalgebra of $\mathbb{O}$ generated by two elements is associative.

5. Frobenius's theorem

Theorem (Frobenius, 1877). The only finite-dimensional associative division algebras over $\mathbb{R}$ are $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$, and $\mathbb{H}$.

Sketch. Let $D$ be such an algebra. Pick $x\in D\setminus\mathbb{R}$. Its minimal polynomial is real and irreducible, hence linear or an irreducible quadratic — so $\mathbb{R}[x]\cong\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$. If $D=\mathbb{R}[x]$ we are done. Otherwise the orthogonal complement $V=\{x:\tr(x)=0,\,x^2\le 0\}$ to $\mathbb{R}\cdot 1$ is a real Euclidean space (use the trace as inner product). For $u,v\in V$ unit vectors, $u^2=-1$ and $(uv+vu)\in\mathbb{R}$. Anti-commuting unit pairs $u,v$ generate a subalgebra $\cong\mathbb{C}$ extended by $v$, i.e. $\cong\mathbb{H}$. A third unit vector $w$ anti-commuting with $u,v$ forces $w=\pm uv$ by associativity — there is no room for a fourth. So $\dim V\in\{0,1,3\}$ and $\dim D\in\{1,2,4\}$.

6. Hurwitz's theorem

A composition algebra over $\mathbb{R}$ is a (not-necessarily-associative) unital algebra equipped with a non-degenerate quadratic form $N$ satisfying the multiplicative law

$$N(xy) = N(x)\,N(y)\qquad\text{for all }x,y.$$

Theorem (Hurwitz, 1898). The only finite-dimensional real composition algebras are $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$, $\mathbb{H}$, and $\mathbb{O}$, of dimensions $1$, $2$, $4$, and $8$.

The dimensions $1,2,4,8$ are exactly the ones at which Cayley–Dickson still produces a division algebra. They also coincide with the dimensions $n$ for which $\mathbb{R}^n$ admits a continuous bilinear product without zero divisors — a deep theorem of Bott–Milnor and Kervaire (independently, 1958), proved using the cohomology of real projective spaces.

The norm-multiplicativity in $\mathbb{H}$ is the famous four-square identity of Euler:

$$\bigl(a^2+b^2+c^2+d^2\bigr)\bigl(e^2+f^2+g^2+h^2\bigr) = (ae-bf-cg-dh)^2+(af+be+ch-dg)^2+(ag-bh+ce+df)^2+(ah+bg-cf+de)^2.$$

The eight-square identity follows similarly from $\mathbb{O}$. A nine-square identity does not exist; this is one of the most striking elementary consequences of Hurwitz's theorem.

Connections

The 1, 2, 4, 8 phenomenon reaches well beyond algebra. The same dimensions parametrise the parallelisable spheres ($S^0,S^1,S^3,S^7$), the Hopf fibrations $S^1\to S^3\to S^2$ and $S^3\to S^7\to S^4$ and $S^7\to S^{15}\to S^8$, and the exceptional Lie group $G_2=\operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb O)$. Open frontiers include the role of $\mathbb O$ in $11$-dimensional supergravity, the $E_8$ lattice realised inside $\mathbb O\otimes\mathbb O$, and the non-commutative geometry of quaternionic and octonionic projective spaces.

  • Lie algebras — axioms — $\mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{H}$ with quaternion commutator $[u,v]=uv-vu$ is exactly $\mathfrak{so}(3)\cong\mathfrak{su}(2)$.
  • Lie groups — SO(3) vs SU(2) — the unit quaternions are the Lie group $\mathrm{Sp}(1)\cong\mathrm{SU}(2)$, double-covering $\mathrm{SO}(3)$.
  • Representation theory — $\mathbb{H}$ is the unique non-commutative irreducible real division algebra, and underlies the type-H representations in real Schur theory.
  • Abstract algebra — field extensions — Frobenius's theorem refines the algebraic-closure fact "$[\mathbb C:\mathbb R]=2$" to a classification of associative division algebras.
  • Galois cohomology & Brauer — over $\mathbb{R}$ the Brauer group is $\mathrm{Br}(\mathbb R)=\mathbb Z/2$, with non-trivial class represented by $\mathbb H$ — Frobenius read cohomologically.

Schrödinger equation

A self-adjoint Hamiltonian on $L^2$, a unitary one-parameter group, and the spectral theorem in disguise — quantum mechanics is functional analysis with a physical interpretation.

1. Time-dependent Schrödinger equation

The state of a quantum particle is a unit vector $\psi \in L^2(\mathbb{R}^n)$, evolving by

$$ i\hbar\,\partial_t \psi = H\psi, \qquad H = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\Delta + V(x). $$

$H$ is the Hamiltonian: kinetic energy $-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\Delta$ plus potential $V$. Self-adjointness of $H$ on its natural domain (Sobolev space $H^2$ for nice $V$) is the analytic content; once you have it, Stone's theorem hands you a one-parameter unitary group $U_t = e^{-itH/\hbar}$ and the solution is simply $\psi(t) = U_t \psi(0)$. Unitarity of $U_t$ gives the conservation law

$$ \tfrac{d}{dt}\|\psi(t)\|^2 = \tfrac{d}{dt}\langle \psi, \psi\rangle = \langle (-iH/\hbar)\psi,\psi\rangle + \langle \psi,(-iH/\hbar)\psi\rangle = 0, $$

so $\int |\psi(x,t)|^2\,dx \equiv 1$: total probability is preserved forever. Strip away the physics and Schrödinger evolution is a structure theorem: every self-adjoint operator generates a one-parameter unitary group, and that group is the dynamics.

2. Stationary states and the eigenvalue problem

Look for solutions of the special form $\psi(x,t) = e^{-iEt/\hbar}\varphi(x)$. Plugging into the time-dependent equation, the time derivative on the left gives $E\,\psi$, and the right gives $H\varphi$ times the same phase. Strip the phase:

$$ H\varphi = E\varphi. $$

This is the time-independent Schrödinger equation — an eigenvalue problem for a self-adjoint operator. By the spectral theorem, $H$ admits a resolution into eigenstates plus continuous-spectrum generalized eigenfunctions, and any state decomposes

$$ \psi = \sum_{n} c_n \varphi_n + \int c(E)\,\varphi_E\,dE. $$

The discrete sum runs over bound states — eigenvalues with $L^2$ eigenfunctions. The integral runs over scattering states — generalized eigenfunctions that are not in $L^2$ but participate in the spectral measure. A bound state evolves only by the phase $e^{-iE_n t/\hbar}$, so the probability density $|\varphi_n|^2$ doesn't change with time — that's why they're called stationary.

3. Harmonic oscillator and ladder operators

The simplest non-trivial Hamiltonian after the free particle is the harmonic oscillator,

$$ H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2, \qquad p = -i\hbar\,\partial_x. $$

Define the dimensionless ladder operators

$$ a = \sqrt{\tfrac{m\omega}{2\hbar}}\Bigl(x + \tfrac{i}{m\omega}p\Bigr), \qquad a^\dagger = \sqrt{\tfrac{m\omega}{2\hbar}}\Bigl(x - \tfrac{i}{m\omega}p\Bigr). $$

The canonical commutator $[x,p] = i\hbar$ collapses to the textbook identity $[a,a^\dagger] = 1$, and a short calculation gives $H = \hbar\omega(a^\dagger a + \tfrac{1}{2})$. Now $a^\dagger a$ has non-negative eigenvalues, and $a$ lowers any eigenvalue by 1 while $a^\dagger$ raises it by 1. The lowest state $\varphi_0$ must be killed by $a$ (else lowering would produce a negative eigenvalue), and successive states are $\varphi_n = (a^\dagger)^n \varphi_0 / \sqrt{n!}$. The spectrum is

$$ E_n = \hbar\omega\bigl(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigr), \qquad n = 0, 1, 2, \dots $$

The eigenfunctions are Hermite polynomials times a Gaussian. The same algebra reappears wherever you see equally-spaced spectra: photons of frequency $\omega$ in QED, phonons in a crystal, second quantization in general.

4. Hydrogen atom

For an electron in the Coulomb field of a proton, $V(r) = -e^2/r$. Spherical symmetry — $[H, L^2] = [H, L_z] = 0$ — means simultaneous eigenfunctions of $H, L^2, L_z$ exist, and separation of variables gives

$$ \varphi_{n\ell m}(r,\theta,\phi) = R_{n\ell}(r)\,Y_\ell^m(\theta,\phi), $$

with the angular part the spherical harmonics $Y_\ell^m$. The radial equation reduces to a 1D Schrödinger problem with effective potential $V_\mathrm{eff}(r) = -e^2/r + \hbar^2 \ell(\ell+1)/(2mr^2)$. Bound-state energies turn out to depend only on the principal quantum number $n = n_r + \ell + 1$, not on $\ell$:

$$ E_n = -\frac{E_0}{n^2}, \qquad E_0 = \frac{me^4}{2\hbar^2} \approx 13.6\,\mathrm{eV}. $$

The accidental degeneracy across $\ell$ at fixed $n$ — total multiplicity $n^2$ — is no accident: it comes from a hidden $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ symmetry generated by angular momentum together with the conserved Runge–Lenz vector. The continuous spectrum $[0, \infty)$ is the scattering regime: ionized electrons.

5. Scattering states and barrier transmission

For a potential that decays at infinity, the spectrum of $H = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\Delta + V$ above the asymptotic value of $V$ is purely continuous. The generalized eigenstates are plane-wave solutions $e^{ikx}$ — not in $L^2$, but participating in the spectral measure. Physically, they describe a particle of definite momentum $p = \hbar k$.

Send a wave $e^{ikx}$ from the left into a barrier. After it interacts with the potential it splits into a transmitted piece $T\,e^{ikx}$ on the right and a reflected piece $R\,e^{-ikx}$ on the left. Probability conservation — equivalently, unitarity of the S-matrix — gives

$$ |T|^2 + |R|^2 = 1. $$

The startling feature: even when the energy $E$ is below the barrier height $V_0$, transmission $|T|^2 > 0$. The wavefunction inside the barrier decays exponentially, $\psi(x) \propto e^{-\kappa x}$ with $\kappa = \sqrt{2m(V_0-E)}/\hbar$, but it doesn't reach zero, so a thin enough barrier always lets some amplitude through. This is quantum tunneling — the engine of $\alpha$-decay, the scanning tunneling microscope, and Josephson junctions.

6. Path integral and the classical limit

Feynman re-derived quantum mechanics in 1948 by writing the propagator $K(x_f, t_f; x_i, t_i) = \langle x_f | e^{-iH(t_f-t_i)/\hbar}| x_i\rangle$ as an integral over all paths $q(t)$ from $x_i$ to $x_f$:

$$ K(x_f, t_f; x_i, t_i) = \int_{q(t_i)=x_i}^{q(t_f)=x_f} e^{i S[q]/\hbar}\,\mathcal{D}q, \qquad S[q] = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} L(q,\dot q)\,dt. $$

Each path is weighted by the phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$ where $S$ is the classical action. The Schrödinger equation is recovered by deriving the propagator's evolution from short-time slices. The integral is heuristic — there's no honest Lebesgue measure $\mathcal{D}q$ on path space — but rigorous via Wick rotation $t \to -i\tau$, which converts the oscillatory integral into the Wiener integral over Brownian paths.

The classical limit is geometric: as $\hbar \to 0$, the phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$ oscillates faster and faster, and contributions from neighboring paths cancel — except where $\delta S = 0$. The stationary points of $S$ are exactly the solutions of the Euler–Lagrange equation. So quantum mechanics says: take all paths; classical mechanics says: take only the stationary one. Stationary phase makes that the same statement at $\hbar \to 0$.

Connections

The Schrödinger equation is the meeting point of three frameworks: the spectral theorem (eigenstates as the diagonalization of $H$), elliptic PDE theory (regularity and self-adjoint extensions of $-\Delta + V$), and the path-integral / functional-integral approach that powers quantum field theory. Open frontiers: rigorous many-body Schrödinger operators (Coulomb stability of matter, BCS theory), nonlinear Schrödinger equations (Bose–Einstein condensates, soliton dynamics), and the still-mysterious measurement problem in the foundations.

  • Spectral theory — unbounded self-adjoint operators — the rigorous home of $-\Delta + V$ and Stone's theorem $H \leftrightarrow e^{-itH/\hbar}$.
  • Functional analysis — Hilbert spaces — $L^2$ as the state space, where the inner product encodes probabilities $|\langle\varphi,\psi\rangle|^2$.
  • PDE classification — Schrödinger sits between parabolic (heat) and hyperbolic (wave) by the imaginary unit, with infinite-speed propagation but conservation of $L^2$.
  • Harmonic analysis — the Fourier transform diagonalizes the free Hamiltonian and underlies the momentum representation.
  • Operator algebras — observables as a $C^*$-algebra; states as positive linear functionals; the Heisenberg-picture dual to wave mechanics.

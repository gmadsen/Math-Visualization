The projective plane

Add one point for each direction and the world snaps into place: parallel lines meet, every two conics intersect in four points, and the elliptic curve grows an inflection point at infinity. A working tour of $\mathbb{P}^2$.

1. Where parallel lines meet

Stand on a long straight road. The two rails of a railway track, which you know are parallel, appear to meet at a single point on the horizon. A Renaissance painter calls this the vanishing point; a projective geometer calls it a point at infinity.

The thought experiment generalizes. In the affine plane $\mathbb{A}^2$ a family of parallel lines (all of slope $m$, say) refuses to meet — and we declare this an inconvenience to be fixed. Add one new "ideal" point $\infty_m$ for each direction $m\in\mathbb{R}\cup\{\infty\}$, and decree that every line of slope $m$ passes through $\infty_m$. Two lines of the same slope now meet there; two lines of different slopes meet in the usual finite point. Every pair of distinct lines now meets in exactly one point.

The collection of all the new points $\{\infty_m\}$ forms a single new "line" — the line at infinity $\ell_\infty$. Affine plane plus line at infinity equals projective plane.

2. Definition of $\mathbb{P}^2(K)$

Fix a field $K$. The projective plane over $K$ is the quotient $$\mathbb{P}^2(K) \;=\; (K^3 \setminus \{0\}) / {\sim}, \qquad (X,Y,Z) \sim (\lambda X, \lambda Y, \lambda Z) \text{ for } \lambda \in K^\times.$$ A point $[X:Y:Z]$ is the equivalence class — the colon notation reminds you that only the ratio matters. These are the homogeneous coordinates.

Equivalently, $\mathbb{P}^2(K)$ is the set of lines through the origin in $K^3$: each non-zero $(X,Y,Z)$ spans a one-dimensional subspace, and rescaling by $\lambda$ doesn't change the line.

Same idea, two pictures.
  • Quotient: the points are equivalence classes of triples up to common scaling.
  • Geometric: the points are lines through the origin in $K^3$.
A "point" of $\mathbb{P}^2$ is a 1-dimensional linear subspace of $K^3$. A "line" of $\mathbb{P}^2$ is a 2-dimensional linear subspace of $K^3$, i.e.\ a plane through the origin, cut out by a single homogeneous linear equation $aX+bY+cZ=0$.

Two points determine a unique line and two distinct lines determine a unique point. (In linear-algebra terms: two distinct 1D subspaces span a unique 2D subspace; two distinct 2D subspaces in $K^3$ intersect in a unique 1D subspace.) No exceptions, no parallels.

3. Affine charts

$\mathbb{P}^2$ is built from three overlapping copies of $K^2$. Let $$U_Z = \{[X:Y:Z]\in\mathbb{P}^2 : Z \ne 0\}.$$ The map $$\varphi_Z\colon U_Z \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} K^2, \qquad [X:Y:Z] \mapsto (X/Z,\, Y/Z)$$ is a bijection: in any equivalence class with $Z\ne 0$ choose the representative with $Z = 1$, namely $[X/Z : Y/Z : 1]$. The first two coordinates give a point of $K^2$, and conversely every $(x,y)\in K^2$ comes from $[x:y:1]$.

The same construction with $X$ or $Y$ in the role of $Z$ gives charts $\varphi_X\colon U_X \to K^2$ and $\varphi_Y\colon U_Y \to K^2$. Together $U_X \cup U_Y \cup U_Z = \mathbb{P}^2$ (every point has at least one nonzero coordinate), so $\mathbb{P}^2$ is covered by three "affine patches."

Transition maps

On the overlap $U_Y \cap U_Z = \{Y \ne 0,\, Z \ne 0\}$ the change of coordinates is $$\varphi_Z \circ \varphi_Y^{-1}\colon (u, w) \;\mapsto\; \bigl(u/w,\; 1/w\bigr),$$ where $(u,w) = (X/Y, Z/Y)$ on the $Y$-chart and $(x,y) = (X/Z, Y/Z) = (u/w, 1/w)$ on the $Z$-chart. These rational transition maps are the prototype of algebraic-geometric gluing: $\mathbb{P}^2$ is a scheme glued from three copies of $\mathbb{A}^2$.

4. The line at infinity

Fix the chart $U_Z$. Its complement is $\{Z = 0\}$, the locus of all points $[X:Y:0]$ with $(X,Y) \ne (0,0)$. As $X,Y$ range over non-zero pairs, the equivalence class $[X:Y:0]$ depends only on the ratio $X:Y$ — so this complement is itself a copy of $\mathbb{P}^1(K)$, the line at infinity $\ell_\infty^{(Z)}$.

Now interpret a direction in the affine chart. A line in $K^2$ with direction vector $(a,b)$ has parametrization $(x,y) = (x_0 + ta, y_0 + tb)$. Lifting to homogeneous coordinates, $$[x:y:1] \;=\; [x_0 + ta : y_0 + tb : 1].$$ Multiplying by $1/t$ and letting $t\to\infty$, $$[x_0/t + a : y_0/t + b : 1/t] \;\longrightarrow\; [a : b : 0].$$ So every line of direction $(a,b)$ passes through the point $[a:b:0]\in\ell_\infty$, regardless of where the line sits in the plane. Two parallel lines (same $(a,b)$ up to scale) meet at this single point at infinity. Two non-parallel lines have different $(a,b)$ and so meet at different points of $\ell_\infty$ — hence at a finite point of $\mathbb{A}^2$.

Bookkeeping. $\mathbb{P}^2 = \mathbb{A}^2 \sqcup \ell_\infty = K^2 \sqcup \mathbb{P}^1(K)$. The cardinalities check out (over $\mathbb{F}_q$): $|\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{F}_q)| = q^2 + q + 1 = q^2 + (q+1)$.

5. Homogenization

An affine plane curve is the zero set of a polynomial $f(x,y)\in K[x,y]$. To work projectively we need a homogeneous polynomial — one whose every monomial has the same total degree. The recipe is mechanical.

If $f$ has degree $d$, set $$F(X,Y,Z) \;=\; Z^{\,d}\cdot f(X/Z,\, Y/Z).$$ Each monomial $x^iy^j$ with $i+j\le d$ becomes $X^iY^jZ^{d-i-j}$, restoring total degree $d$. The original affine curve is recovered by setting $Z=1$.

6. Projective transformations

The natural symmetries of $\mathbb{P}^2(K)$ are projective linear transformations: invertible $3\times 3$ matrices $A\in\mathrm{GL}_3(K)$ act on $\mathbb{P}^2$ by $$A\cdot[X:Y:Z] = [A(X,Y,Z)^T].$$ Two matrices give the same map iff they differ by a non-zero scalar, so the symmetry group is $$\mathrm{PGL}_3(K) \;=\; \mathrm{GL}_3(K)/K^\times.$$ This is an algebraic group of dimension $9 - 1 = 8$ over $K$.

The action is highly transitive in the following precise sense.

Fundamental theorem of projective geometry (qualitative form).

  • A projective line is determined by 2 points and 2 points always determine a unique line.
  • A projective conic (smooth) is determined by $5$ general points.
  • A projective cubic is determined by $9$ general points (the basis for the geometry of elliptic curves).
  • $\mathrm{PGL}_3(K)$ acts simply transitively on ordered $4$-tuples of points in general position (no 3 collinear).

In particular, two smooth plane conics are projectively equivalent over $\bar{K}$: every smooth conic can be brought into the form $X^2+Y^2 = Z^2$ (a circle) or $XY = Z^2$ (a parabola) — over the algebraic closure they are the same curve.

7. Projective duality

The equation $aX+bY+cZ=0$ that defines a line in $\mathbb{P}^2$ is itself determined by a triple $[a:b:c]$ up to non-zero scalar. So lines in $\mathbb{P}^2$ are parametrized by another copy of $\mathbb{P}^2$, called the dual projective plane $(\mathbb{P}^2)^*$. A point $[X_0:Y_0:Z_0]\in\mathbb{P}^2$ lies on the line $[a:b:c]\in(\mathbb{P}^2)^*$ exactly when $$a X_0 + b Y_0 + c Z_0 \;=\; 0,$$ and this pairing is perfectly symmetric in the two variables. Fixing a point cuts out a line's worth of lines through it; fixing a line cuts out a line's worth of points on it. The resulting involution $\mathbb{P}^2 \leftrightarrow (\mathbb{P}^2)^*$ is projective duality.

Because the pairing is symmetric, every incidence theorem has a dual obtained by swapping "point" with "line" and "lies on" with "passes through". The most basic example is the axiom pair: "any two distinct points determine a unique line" dualizes to "any two distinct lines meet in a unique point" — the projective replacement for parallelism. A deeper example is Pascal (six points on a conic $\Rightarrow$ three collinear points) dualizing to Brianchon (six tangents to a conic $\Rightarrow$ three concurrent lines). Over $\mathbb{F}_q$ the duality is visible at the level of counts: $|\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{F}_q)| = |(\mathbb{P}^2)^*(\mathbb{F}_q)| = q^2+q+1$, and for $q=2$ both sides equal $7$ — the Fano plane, self-dual with seven points and seven lines.

8. The cross-ratio

Given four distinct collinear points $A,B,C,D$ on a projective line with affine coordinates $a,b,c,d$, their cross-ratio is $$[A,B;C,D] \;=\; \frac{(a-c)(b-d)}{(a-d)(b-c)} \;\in\; K \cup\{\infty\}.$$ For instance $[0,1;2,3] = \dfrac{(-2)(-2)}{(-3)(-1)} = \dfrac{4}{3}$. The cross-ratio is independent of the choice of affine chart on the line and, crucially, invariant under $\mathrm{PGL}_2(K)$: a Möbius transformation $t\mapsto(\alpha t+\beta)/(\gamma t+\delta)$ preserves it. It is, up to functional identities, the unique projective invariant of four points on a line — so two quadruples on two different lines are projectively equivalent iff their cross-ratios match.

Permuting the four points changes the cross-ratio: only the Klein four-group $V_4\subset S_4$ of double-transpositions fixes $\lambda=[A,B;C,D]$. The remaining six cosets give the orbit $\{\lambda,\,1-\lambda,\,1/\lambda,\,1/(1-\lambda),\,\lambda/(\lambda-1),\,(\lambda-1)/\lambda\}$, and the rational function $j(\lambda) = \dfrac{256(\lambda^2-\lambda+1)^3}{\lambda^2(\lambda-1)^2}$ is $S_4$-invariant — this is the same $j$ that will reappear as the $j$-invariant of an elliptic curve on elliptic-curves.html, via the four ramification points of a hyperelliptic map. The cross-ratio is classical projective geometry's most portable computation: it lets you detect equivalence, construct harmonic conjugates ($\lambda=-1$), and — on a conic — recover the conic's own parameter from four marked points.

9. Why projective?

Three answers, in order of how often they will save your life.

(a) Bézout

Bézout's theorem says that two projective plane curves of degrees $m$ and $n$ with no common component meet in exactly $mn$ points, counted with multiplicity, in $\mathbb{P}^2(\bar K)$. The theorem fails in $\mathbb{A}^2$ for two reasons that projective space corrects:

  • Parallel lines "miss"; in $\mathbb{P}^2$ they meet on $\ell_\infty$.
  • Asymptotes hide intersection points; in $\mathbb{P}^2$ they show up as points at infinity.

For example, two distinct smooth conics in $\mathbb{A}^2$ may visibly intersect in $0$, $1$, $2$, $3$, or $4$ real points, but in $\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{C})$ they always intersect in exactly $4$ points counted properly. (This will get its own page in Wave 2.)

(b) Weierstrass form for elliptic curves

A smooth plane cubic $C\subset\mathbb{P}^2$ has $9$ inflection points in $\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{C})$ (Bézout: $C$ meets its Hessian, also a cubic, in $3\cdot 3 = 9$ points). Choose one inflection point $O$, send it to $[0:1:0]$ by a projective change of coordinates with the tangent line at $O$ becoming $Z=0$, and one obtains the Weierstrass form $$Y^2 Z + a_1 XYZ + a_3 YZ^2 \;=\; X^3 + a_2 X^2 Z + a_4 XZ^2 + a_6 Z^3.$$ The chosen point $O = [0:1:0]$ is the identity of the group law on $C$. The full story is on elliptic-curves.html; see the next widget for the picture.

(c) Compactification

$\mathbb{P}^n$ is the smallest natural compactification of $\mathbb{A}^n$ that adds one point per direction. It plays the role of "the space of all directions plus the affine space," and the construction generalizes to any toric variety, blow-up, or proj of a graded ring. Most theorems of algebraic geometry assume properness (the algebraic substitute for compactness), and projective space is the universal source of properness.

10. Higher dimensions, and a note on $\mathbb{RP}^2$ vs $\mathbb{CP}^2$

The construction generalizes verbatim. The projective $n$-space over $K$ is $$\mathbb{P}^n(K) \;=\; (K^{n+1} \setminus 0)/\sim$$ with $(X_0,\dots,X_n) \sim (\lambda X_0,\dots,\lambda X_n)$. As a topological space (when $K = \mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$) it has dimension $n$ over $\mathbb{R}$ in the real case and $n$ over $\mathbb{C}$ (so $2n$ over $\mathbb{R}$) in the complex case.

The real projective plane $\mathbb{RP}^2$

Realize $\mathbb{RP}^2$ as the upper hemisphere of $S^2$ with antipodal boundary points glued. It is a compact non-orientable surface; it cannot be embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$ without self-intersection (cf.\ Boy's surface, the Roman surface). Topologically it is a Möbius band glued to a disk along their common circular boundary.

The complex projective plane $\mathbb{CP}^2$

$\mathbb{CP}^2$ is a $4$-real-dimensional, simply connected, smooth, oriented manifold with $H^*(\mathbb{CP}^2;\mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[h]/(h^3)$ where $h$ is the class of a projective line. It is the natural setting for plane algebraic curves over $\mathbb{C}$, where Bézout becomes a clean count of intersection numbers.

Cell decomposition

$\mathbb{P}^n = \mathbb{A}^n \sqcup \mathbb{P}^{n-1} = \mathbb{A}^n \sqcup \mathbb{A}^{n-1} \sqcup \cdots \sqcup \mathbb{A}^0$. This recursive decomposition gives the famous Poincaré polynomial $1 + t^2 + t^4 + \cdots + t^{2n}$ for $\mathbb{CP}^n$, i.e.\ one even Betti number equal to $1$ in each even degree up to $2n$.

Differential geometry — curves, surfaces, curvature

The classical story from Frenet frames on space curves to the Gauss–Bonnet theorem, with a gentle bridge to abstract smooth and Riemannian manifolds. Curvature is the throughline: how a curve bends in its ambient space, how a surface bends intrinsically, and how those bendings conspire with topology.

1. Curves in the plane and in space

A parametrized smooth curve in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a $C^\infty$ map $\gamma\colon I \to \mathbb{R}^n$ from an open interval $I \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. It is regular if $\dot\gamma(t) \neq 0$ for all $t$; this is the only condition we need to talk about tangent lines and unit tangent vectors.

The arc length from $t_0$ to $t$ is $s(t) = \int_{t_0}^t \lVert \dot\gamma(\tau) \rVert\,d\tau$. Since $\dot s = \lVert \dot\gamma \rVert > 0$, the map $t \mapsto s(t)$ is a diffeomorphism onto its image, and we can reparametrize by arc length. Throughout this section, primes denote $d/ds$, dots denote $d/dt$. When $\gamma$ is arc-length parametrized, $\lVert \gamma'(s) \rVert = 1$ identically — the unit tangent $T(s) = \gamma'(s)$.

Differentiating $T \cdot T = 1$ gives $T \cdot T' = 0$: the derivative of the unit tangent is always perpendicular to $T$. For a plane curve there is a preferred unit normal: rotate $T$ by $+\pi/2$ to get $N$. The signed curvature is defined by

$T'(s) = \kappa(s)\,N(s)$,

and equivalently $\kappa = \det(\dot\gamma, \ddot\gamma) / \lVert \dot\gamma \rVert^3$ in any parametrization. Positive $\kappa$ means left-turning, negative means right-turning. The sign flips if you traverse the curve in reverse.

In $\mathbb{R}^3$ things are more interesting. We still have $T(s) = \gamma'(s)$, and $T' \neq 0$ whenever the curve isn't locally a straight line. The principal normal and curvature are

$N(s) = T'(s)/\lVert T'(s) \rVert, \qquad \kappa(s) = \lVert T'(s) \rVert \ge 0.$

(For space curves, $\kappa \ge 0$ by convention — the signed version requires a preferred orientation of the normal plane.) Complete the right-handed frame by setting $B = T \times N$, the binormal. The plane spanned by $T$ and $N$ is the osculating plane; the curve locally lives in this plane up to $o(s^2)$.

How fast does the osculating plane itself twist out of itself? That is measured by the torsion $\tau(s)$, defined by $B'(s) = -\tau(s)\,N(s)$. Combining everything yields the Frenet–Serret formulas:

Frenet–Serret. For an arc-length parametrized space curve with $\kappa > 0$, $$\begin{pmatrix} T' \\ N' \\ B' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \kappa & 0 \\ -\kappa & 0 & \tau \\ 0 & -\tau & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} T \\ N \\ B \end{pmatrix}.$$ The matrix is skew-symmetric because $(T,N,B)$ is orthonormal. Curvature $\kappa$ is the in-plane turning rate; torsion $\tau$ is the out-of-plane twisting rate.

The fundamental theorem of space curves says that $\kappa(s) > 0$ and $\tau(s)$, as functions of arc length, determine $\gamma$ up to a rigid motion of $\mathbb{R}^3$. Two curves with the same curvature and torsion profiles are congruent. Curvature and torsion are the complete Euclidean invariants of a space curve.

Arc length is rigid and coordinate-free, but in practice you almost never parametrize by it — the formulas in a general parametrization are

$\kappa = \dfrac{\lVert \dot\gamma \times \ddot\gamma \rVert}{\lVert \dot\gamma \rVert^3}, \qquad \tau = \dfrac{(\dot\gamma \times \ddot\gamma)\cdot\dddot\gamma}{\lVert \dot\gamma \times \ddot\gamma \rVert^2}.$

Things to notice while playing with the widget:

  • On a circle of radius $r$, $\kappa = 1/r$ everywhere and the osculating circle equals the curve itself.
  • On the ellipse, $\kappa$ is largest at the two ends of the major axis — the curve bends the most there — and smallest at the ends of the minor axis.
  • The cardioid has a cusp at the origin where $\dot\gamma = 0$; the curvature blows up there. The radius $1/|\kappa|$ goes to zero.
  • The parabola flattens out at infinity: $\kappa \to 0$.

For a space curve we lose the "left vs right" sign on $\kappa$, but we gain torsion. The simplest test case is the helix $\gamma(t) = (a\cos t, a\sin t, bt)$, for which one computes $\kappa = a/(a^2+b^2)$ and $\tau = b/(a^2+b^2)$ — both constants. Setting $b = 0$ gives a circle (no torsion); setting $a=0$ gives a straight line (both vanish). A helix is uniquely characterized among space curves as "the curve of constant $\kappa$ and constant $\tau$."

2. Osculating circles, evolutes, involutes

The circle of radius $1/|\kappa|$ tangent to the curve at $p$, lying on the concave side, is the osculating circle. It agrees with the curve up to order two: $\gamma$ and the osculating circle have the same value, same tangent direction, and same curvature at $p$. Its center, the center of curvature, is $p + (1/\kappa)\,N$.

Trace the center of curvature as $p$ sweeps along $\gamma$: the resulting curve is called the evolute of $\gamma$. The evolute has beautiful properties:

  • The tangent line to the evolute at the center-of-curvature for $\gamma(s)$ coincides with the normal line of $\gamma$ at $s$.
  • Cusps of the evolute occur at points where $d\kappa/ds = 0$ — vertices of $\gamma$.
  • The evolute of an ellipse is an asteroid-like curve with four cusps, one per vertex.
  • The evolute of a cycloid is another cycloid, shifted by half a period.

The involute is the inverse operation: start with a curve $\gamma$, unroll a taut string along it, and trace the tip. Involutes are used in gear-tooth design because two involute-shaped gears mesh with a constant transmission ratio. The involute of a circle is the spiral that starts tight and grows linearly; it is the classical involute gear profile.

A circle has constant curvature — no evolute worth drawing, since all centers of curvature pile up at one point. A parabola's evolute is a semicubical parabola (Neil's parabola), with a single cusp at the vertex. These facts all follow from the elementary observation that $\kappa$ records second-order information and its derivative records third-order.

Vertex theorem

A simple closed convex plane curve has at least four vertices — points where $\kappa'(s) = 0$, i.e. local extrema of $\kappa$. Circles achieve infinitely many (every point is a vertex); generic ovals have exactly four. The vertex theorem is an early example of a global geometric statement: local behaviour of curvature constrained by the topology of being a closed convex curve.

3. Surfaces in $\mathbb{R}^3$

A regular surface $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$ is a subset such that around every point $p \in S$ there is an open neighborhood $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$, an open $W \ni p$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$, and a smooth map $\mathbf{x}\colon U \to W \cap S$ that is a homeomorphism onto its image, with $d\mathbf{x}_q$ injective at every $q \in U$. The pair $(\mathbf{x}, U)$ is a local parametrization (or chart) of $S$.

Concretely: $\mathbf{x}(u,v) = (x(u,v), y(u,v), z(u,v))$ is a smooth function of two variables with linearly independent partial derivatives $\mathbf{x}_u = \partial \mathbf{x}/\partial u$ and $\mathbf{x}_v = \partial \mathbf{x}/\partial v$. The condition "$d\mathbf{x}$ injective" is equivalent to $\mathbf{x}_u \times \mathbf{x}_v \neq 0$.

The tangent plane at $p = \mathbf{x}(q)$ is

$T_pS = \operatorname{span}\{\mathbf{x}_u(q), \mathbf{x}_v(q)\}$.

It is a two-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$, independent of the chart (different charts give different bases for the same plane). The unit normal at $p$ is

$\mathbf{n}(p) = \dfrac{\mathbf{x}_u \times \mathbf{x}_v}{\lVert \mathbf{x}_u \times \mathbf{x}_v \rVert}$.

Like orientations of a line, $\mathbf{n}$ has a sign ambiguity globally — some surfaces (Möbius band, Klein bottle) admit no continuous choice of unit normal; they are non-orientable. An orientable surface has two smooth normal fields $\mathbf{n}$ and $-\mathbf{n}$ and a choice between them is an orientation.

4. The first fundamental form

The ambient Euclidean inner product on $\mathbb{R}^3$, restricted to each tangent plane $T_pS$, gives the surface an intrinsic way to measure lengths of tangent vectors, angles between them, and areas. Expressed in a local chart $(u,v)$, with $E = \mathbf{x}_u \cdot \mathbf{x}_u$, $F = \mathbf{x}_u \cdot \mathbf{x}_v$, $G = \mathbf{x}_v \cdot \mathbf{x}_v$, the restricted inner product on a tangent vector $a\,\mathbf{x}_u + b\,\mathbf{x}_v$ has squared length $Ea^2 + 2Fab + Gb^2$. Writing $du, dv$ for the dual basis of tangent covectors:

First fundamental form. $$I = E\,du^2 + 2F\,du\,dv + G\,dv^2, \qquad E = \mathbf{x}_u\cdot\mathbf{x}_u,\ F = \mathbf{x}_u\cdot\mathbf{x}_v,\ G = \mathbf{x}_v\cdot\mathbf{x}_v.$$ It is a positive-definite symmetric bilinear form on each tangent plane. It determines lengths, angles, and areas on $S$ intrinsically — no reference to the ambient $\mathbb{R}^3$ needed after $(E,F,G)$ are known as functions of $(u,v)$.

Once you have $I$, the following are automatic:

  • Length of a curve $\gamma(t) = \mathbf{x}(u(t), v(t))$ on $S$: $$\mathrm{length}(\gamma) = \int \sqrt{E\dot u^2 + 2F\dot u\dot v + G\dot v^2}\,dt.$$
  • Angle between two tangent vectors $w_1, w_2 \in T_pS$: $\cos\theta = I(w_1,w_2)/\sqrt{I(w_1)I(w_2)}$.
  • Area element: $dA = \sqrt{EG - F^2}\,du\,dv$. Note $EG - F^2 = \lVert \mathbf{x}_u\times\mathbf{x}_v\rVert^2$, so this matches the Euclidean area of the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{x}_u, \mathbf{x}_v$.

Two surfaces $S_1, S_2$ are locally isometric at $p_1 \in S_1$ and $p_2 \in S_2$ if there exist charts $\mathbf{x}_1$ of $S_1$ around $p_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$ of $S_2$ around $p_2$ with the same first fundamental form (as functions of $(u,v)$). Equivalently, there is a local diffeomorphism $\phi\colon (S_1, p_1) \to (S_2, p_2)$ preserving lengths of curves.

Key examples of isometric surfaces:

  • Plane and cylinder. The cylinder $\mathbf{x}(u,v) = (\cos u, \sin u, v)$ has $E = G = 1$ and $F = 0$ — the same first fundamental form as the flat plane. You can roll a piece of paper into a cylinder without stretching; this is why cylinders are developable surfaces.
  • Plane and cone. Cut a cone along a generator and unroll it; the first fundamental form in the rolled-out coordinates is flat.
  • Catenoid and helicoid. Two strikingly different-looking surfaces — one a surface of revolution, one a ruled surface — are in fact locally isometric (you can continuously deform one into the other through a family of isometric minimal surfaces).
  • Sphere and anything. The round sphere is not locally isometric to the plane — no flat map of the globe preserves distances. This fact is essentially Gauss's Theorema Egregium.

Whenever you compute something on $S$ from $(E,F,G)$ alone, without referring to the embedding, that quantity is intrinsic. Length, angle, area, and (surprise!) Gaussian curvature are intrinsic. Mean curvature, principal curvatures, and the second fundamental form are not — they depend on how $S$ sits in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

5. The Gauss map and the second fundamental form

Orient the surface $S$ with a unit normal field $\mathbf{n}\colon S \to S^2$. This is the Gauss map. It records how the tangent plane rotates in $\mathbb{R}^3$ as you move over $S$. If $S$ is flat (a plane), $\mathbf{n}$ is constant. If $S$ is a sphere of radius $R$, $\mathbf{n}(p) = p/R$ — the Gauss map is an isomorphism (up to scale), hitting every direction on $S^2$ exactly once.

Differentiating $\mathbf{n}$ at $p$ gives a linear map $dN_p\colon T_pS \to T_{\mathbf{n}(p)}S^2$. Since $T_{\mathbf{n}(p)}S^2$ is the plane perpendicular to $\mathbf{n}(p)$ — which is exactly $T_pS$ — we can view

$dN_p\colon T_pS \to T_pS$,

a linear operator on the tangent plane. It is called the Weingarten map or shape operator (some authors use $S_p = -dN_p$). The shape operator captures how the surface curves in its ambient space.

The second fundamental form is the associated quadratic form

$II_p(w) = -\langle dN_p(w), w\rangle = \langle S_p(w), w\rangle.$

Equivalently, $II_p(w) = \langle \ddot\gamma(0), \mathbf{n}(p)\rangle$ for any curve $\gamma$ on $S$ with $\gamma(0)=p$ and $\dot\gamma(0)=w$. It measures how much an arc on $S$ in the direction $w$ departs from its tangent plane.

In local coordinates, writing $e = \mathbf{x}_{uu}\cdot \mathbf{n}$, $f = \mathbf{x}_{uv}\cdot \mathbf{n}$, $g = \mathbf{x}_{vv}\cdot \mathbf{n}$,

$II = e\,du^2 + 2f\,du\,dv + g\,dv^2$.

The shape operator, in the basis $(\mathbf{x}_u, \mathbf{x}_v)$, has matrix

$[S_p] = I^{-1}\,II = \begin{pmatrix} E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix}^{-1} \begin{pmatrix} e & f \\ f & g \end{pmatrix}.$

It is self-adjoint with respect to $I$, hence diagonalizable with real eigenvalues. The eigenvalues are the principal curvatures $k_1 \ge k_2$; the eigenvectors are the principal directions. Two rotationally invariant quantities are the Gaussian curvature and mean curvature:

$$K(p) = \det S_p = k_1 k_2 = \frac{eg - f^2}{EG - F^2}, \qquad H(p) = \tfrac{1}{2}\operatorname{tr} S_p = \tfrac{1}{2}(k_1 + k_2) = \frac{eG - 2fF + gE}{2(EG - F^2)}.$$

Classification of points by $K$:

  • Elliptic: $K > 0$. Both principal curvatures have the same sign; the surface looks like a dome locally. Sphere, ellipsoid, convex region of a torus.
  • Hyperbolic: $K < 0$. Principal curvatures have opposite signs; saddle shape. Saddle surface, inside of torus.
  • Parabolic: $K = 0$ but not both $k_i = 0$. Flat in one direction, curved in the other. Cylinder, cone.
  • Planar: $k_1 = k_2 = 0$. Plane.

Two qualitative behaviours stand out in the widget. For the sphere the Gauss map is a diffeomorphism onto $S^2$: as $p$ runs over $S^2_R$, $\mathbf{n}(p)$ runs over $S^2$ exactly once. The degree of the Gauss map is $+1$. For a saddle, moving $p$ around a loop near the origin produces an image on $S^2$ that winds backwards — the Gauss map has negative Jacobian, which is exactly $K < 0$. For the cylinder, $\mathbf{n}$ stays on the equator of $S^2$; the image is one-dimensional, and $K = 0$ everywhere.

More quantitatively: the Jacobian of the Gauss map at $p$ equals $K(p)$ in absolute value, with sign matching the orientation behaviour. This identifies Gaussian curvature as a local measure of "how much $\mathbf{n}$ spreads on $S^2$" per unit area on $S$.

6. Gauss's Theorema Egregium

The shape operator, the second fundamental form, the principal curvatures, and even the mean curvature $H$ all depend on how $S$ is embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$. If you bend an infinite cylinder — wrap a piece of paper around it — the second fundamental form changes (the mean curvature of the flat sheet is zero, but it's nonzero on the cylinder). These are extrinsic invariants.

What Gauss discovered, to his own astonishment:

Theorema Egregium. The Gaussian curvature $K$ depends only on the first fundamental form $I$, not on the embedding. That is, $K$ is an intrinsic invariant of the surface.

Formally: any local isometry preserves $K$. If you can deform $S_1$ to $S_2$ without stretching or tearing — without changing the induced metric — then their Gaussian curvature functions match up point by point.

This is the reason no flat map of the Earth can be distance-preserving: $K = 1/R^2 > 0$ on the sphere and $K = 0$ on any flat sheet of paper, so no local isometry exists. All world maps must distort either distances or angles (or both). Mercator preserves angles (conformal) at the cost of area; equal-area projections distort angles.

Why it's "extraordinary"

The intrinsic side of the first fundamental form speaks in terms of $(E, F, G)$ and derivatives of these functions. The extrinsic shape operator speaks in terms of $(e, f, g)$ — the second derivatives of the parametrization in the normal direction. There is no obvious reason the product $k_1 k_2$, built from $(e,f,g)$, should be expressible in terms of $(E,F,G)$ alone. The Brioschi formula makes it explicit (one version of many):

Brioschi. For $F = 0$ (orthogonal coordinates), $$K = \frac{-1}{2\sqrt{EG}}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial u}\frac{G_u}{\sqrt{EG}} + \frac{\partial}{\partial v}\frac{E_v}{\sqrt{EG}}\right).$$ Only $(E,G)$ and their derivatives appear on the right — no $(e,f,g)$.

A second formulation, via the Christoffel symbols $\Gamma^k_{ij}$ (which themselves only depend on $I$), gives

$K = R_{1212}/(EG - F^2)$,

where $R_{ijkl}$ is the Riemann curvature tensor built from $\Gamma$. The equation $K = R_{1212}/(EG-F^2)$ in two dimensions foreshadows the structure we will see in general Riemannian geometry (§10): curvature is always derivable from the metric, via the Levi-Civita connection.

The infinitesimal-triangle intuition

Here is the cleanest intuition for why $K$ is intrinsic. Consider a small geodesic triangle on $S$ with interior angles $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$. The angle defect — the amount by which $\alpha + \beta + \gamma$ fails to equal $\pi$ — equals the integral of $K$ over the triangle:

$\alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi = \displaystyle\int_\triangle K\,dA.$

But "the angles in a geodesic triangle" depends only on distances and angles — things $I$ sees. So the angle defect is intrinsic. Dividing by area and shrinking the triangle shows $K$ is intrinsic.

Concretely: a spherical triangle with three right angles (one vertex at the pole, two on the equator at 90° longitude apart) has $\alpha + \beta + \gamma = 3\pi/2 > \pi$. The extra $\pi/2$ equals $\int K\,dA = (1/R^2)\cdot(R^2\cdot\pi/2) = \pi/2$ — area $R^2\pi/2$ is one-eighth of the total sphere area $4\pi R^2$. Check.

On a hyperbolic surface ($K < 0$), triangle angles add to less than $\pi$; you get an angle deficit. On the pseudosphere ($K = -1$), a triangle of area $A$ has angle deficit exactly $A$.

7. Geodesics

A geodesic on a surface is the analog of a straight line: a curve that is "locally length-minimizing" (more precisely, its length is critical among all nearby curves with the same endpoints). The Euler–Lagrange equations for the arc-length functional $\int \sqrt{g_{ij}\dot\gamma^i\dot\gamma^j}\,dt$ give, after switching to the arc-length parametrization,

Geodesic equation. $$\ddot\gamma^k + \Gamma^k_{ij}\,\dot\gamma^i \dot\gamma^j = 0, \qquad k = 1, 2,$$ where $\Gamma^k_{ij}$ are the Christoffel symbols of the metric, $$\Gamma^k_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2} g^{kl}\bigl(\partial_i g_{jl} + \partial_j g_{il} - \partial_l g_{ij}\bigr).$$

An equivalent extrinsic characterization: a curve $\gamma$ on $S$ is a geodesic iff $\ddot\gamma$ is everywhere normal to $S$. In other words, the acceleration of a geodesic, as measured in $\mathbb{R}^3$, points straight out of the surface — it has no tangential component. An ant walking in a "straight line" on a curved surface feels no sideways force.

Known geodesics:

  • On the plane: straight lines. Christoffels vanish in Cartesian coordinates; the equation is $\ddot\gamma^k = 0$.
  • On the sphere: great circles. The ant walks in a circle whose center is the sphere's center.
  • On the cylinder: helices (including vertical generators and horizontal circles as limiting cases). Unroll the cylinder to a strip; a straight line on the strip becomes a helix when re-rolled.
  • On the cone: unroll it along a generator into a flat sector; geodesics are straight lines in the sector. When re-rolled, they spiral around the cone.
  • On a torus: complicated. Rational and irrational windings; a closed geodesic corresponds to a rational winding.

The exponential map $\exp_p\colon T_pS \to S$ sends a tangent vector $v$ to the point at arc-length $\lVert v \rVert$ along the geodesic from $p$ in direction $v/\lVert v \rVert$. On a small enough neighborhood it is a diffeomorphism; its derivative at $0$ is the identity. The exponential map gives a canonical system of normal coordinates around $p$: the metric becomes $\delta_{ij} + O(\lVert v \rVert^2)$, with the quadratic correction governed by the curvature tensor.

8. Gauss–Bonnet

For a geodesic triangle with interior angles $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ on a surface $S$, the angle sum is

$\alpha + \beta + \gamma = \pi + \displaystyle\int_\triangle K\,dA.$

If the boundary of the region is not geodesic but a piecewise smooth curve with exterior angles $\epsilon_i$ at its corners and geodesic curvature $\kappa_g$ along its smooth parts, the general local version is:

Gauss–Bonnet (local). For a compact region $R \subseteq S$ with piecewise smooth boundary, $$\int_R K\,dA + \int_{\partial R} \kappa_g\,ds + \sum_i \epsilon_i = 2\pi.$$ The exterior angles $\epsilon_i$ are the turning angles at corners.

On a geodesic $\kappa_g \equiv 0$, so for a geodesic polygon with $n$ vertices and exterior angles $\epsilon_i = \pi - (\text{interior angle}_i)$, the formula reduces to the angle-sum formula.

The global version pastes local Gauss–Bonnet together over a triangulation of a closed surface, and the exterior-angle contributions telescope to the Euler characteristic:

Gauss–Bonnet (global). For a compact oriented surface $S$ without boundary, $$\int_S K\,dA = 2\pi\,\chi(S),$$ where $\chi(S) = V - E + F$ is the Euler characteristic of any triangulation.

Three striking consequences:

  • The total curvature of a sphere is $4\pi$, regardless of how dented it is. You can deform a sphere by pushing some regions to negative curvature, but the total always returns to $4\pi = 2\pi\cdot 2 = 2\pi\chi(S^2)$.
  • The total curvature of a torus is $0$. If you look at the outside (positive $K$) and the inside (negative $K$) of an embedded torus, they exactly cancel. $\chi(T^2) = 0$.
  • A genus-$g$ surface has $\chi = 2 - 2g$. A double torus has $\chi = -2$ and total curvature $-4\pi$. This topologically constrains the distribution of positive and negative curvature.

Worked example: the round sphere

Sphere of radius $R$. First fundamental form in spherical coordinates $(u, v) = (\theta, \phi)$ with $\mathbf{x}(\theta, \phi) = (R\sin\theta\cos\phi, R\sin\theta\sin\phi, R\cos\theta)$:

$E = R^2, \qquad F = 0, \qquad G = R^2\sin^2\theta.$

Second fundamental form and Gauss map: $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{x}/R$, shape operator equals $(1/R)\cdot\mathrm{id}$. So $k_1 = k_2 = 1/R$, hence $K = 1/R^2$ everywhere. Integral: $\int_S K\,dA = (1/R^2)\cdot 4\pi R^2 = 4\pi$. And $\chi(S^2) = 2$, so $2\pi\chi = 4\pi$. Check.

Euler characteristic via triangulation

You can compute $\chi$ combinatorially: triangulate the surface, count $V$ vertices, $E$ edges, $F$ faces; then $\chi = V - E + F$ is an invariant of the surface, not of the triangulation. For a tetrahedron triangulating $S^2$: $V=4, E=6, F=4$, so $\chi = 2$. For a torus triangulated nicely: say $V=9, E=27, F=18$, $\chi=0$.

Discrete Gauss–Bonnet: polyhedra

There is a combinatorial version of Gauss–Bonnet for polyhedra, going back to Descartes. On a polyhedron, curvature is not spread over faces (which are flat) or along edges (where face tangent planes hinge, but the metric is locally Euclidean after unfolding). All the curvature is concentrated at the vertices, and at each vertex $p$ it takes the form of the angular defect

$\delta(p) = 2\pi - \displaystyle\sum_{\text{faces at } p} \alpha_i(p),$

where $\alpha_i(p)$ is the interior angle at $p$ of the $i$-th face meeting there. If the total were exactly $2\pi$, you could unfold the neighborhood of $p$ flat — so $\delta(p)$ measures how far the neighborhood is from being locally Euclidean, which is the discrete analog of $\int_\text{disk} K\,dA$ over an infinitesimal disk around $p$.

Descartes' theorem (discrete Gauss–Bonnet). For any convex polyhedron, $$\sum_{p \in V} \delta(p) = 2\pi\,\chi.$$ Since $\chi(S^2) = 2$, the total defect of any polyhedron homeomorphic to a sphere is exactly $4\pi$.

For a Platonic solid with regular $n$-gon faces, $k$ meeting at each vertex, the interior face angle is $(n-2)\pi/n$ and the defect at every vertex is

$\delta = 2\pi - k\cdot\dfrac{(n-2)\pi}{n}.$

Check the cube: $n=4, k=3$, so $\delta = 2\pi - 3\cdot(\pi/2) = \pi/2$. Eight vertices times $\pi/2$ is $4\pi$. $\checkmark$

As you refine a polyhedron (subdividing faces, flattening each patch), each vertex's defect shrinks but vertices multiply. In the limit, the sum of defects becomes $\int K\,dA$ and the statement becomes the smooth Gauss–Bonnet theorem. Descartes' theorem is its combinatorial skeleton.

Parallel transport and holonomy on the sphere
transport a tangent vector around a loop · drag to rotate

Parallel transport around a closed loop does not return the vector to itself — it rotates by an angle equal to $\int K\,dA$ over the enclosed region. This is the holonomy of the Levi-Civita connection; on a flat surface it would be zero, and the fact that it isn't is exactly curvature. It is the local-to-global shadow of the exterior angle contribution in Gauss–Bonnet.

9. Abstract smooth manifolds

So far $S$ has been an embedded surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The abstract notion frees us from the ambient space. A smooth $n$-manifold is a topological space $M$ (second-countable Hausdorff) with an atlas $\{(U_\alpha, \varphi_\alpha)\}$: each $\varphi_\alpha$ is a homeomorphism from $U_\alpha$ to an open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$, and the transition maps $\varphi_\beta \circ \varphi_\alpha^{-1}$ are $C^\infty$ wherever defined.

A function $f\colon M \to \mathbb{R}$ is smooth if $f \circ \varphi_\alpha^{-1}$ is smooth for every chart. The ring of smooth functions is $C^\infty(M)$. Smooth maps between manifolds, diffeomorphisms, submanifolds, tangent vectors, etc., are all defined chart-wise.

There are three equivalent definitions of the tangent space $T_pM$:

  • Via velocities. Equivalence classes of smooth curves $\gamma\colon (-\epsilon, \epsilon) \to M$ with $\gamma(0) = p$, under the relation $\gamma_1 \sim \gamma_2$ if their derivatives in some (equivalently, every) chart agree at $0$.
  • Via derivations. $\mathbb{R}$-linear maps $D\colon C^\infty(M) \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying the Leibniz rule $D(fg) = f(p)\,D(g) + g(p)\,D(f)$.
  • Via charts. Fix a chart $(U, \varphi)$ with $p \in U$; define $T_pM = T_{\varphi(p)}\mathbb{R}^n = \mathbb{R}^n$ up to the change-of-chart rule given by Jacobians of transition maps.

These are all canonically isomorphic; the derivation picture is the most elegant, the velocity picture the most hands-on. The tangent bundle $TM = \bigsqcup_{p\in M} T_pM$ is itself a $2n$-manifold with natural projection $\pi\colon TM \to M$. Sections of $\pi$ are vector fields; they form a Lie algebra under the commutator bracket $[X, Y]f = X(Yf) - Y(Xf)$.

Classic examples of smooth manifolds that are not given as embedded surfaces:

  • $\mathbb{R}P^n$, $\mathbb{C}P^n$ — real and complex projective spaces.
  • Lie groups: $GL_n(\mathbb{R})$, $SO(n)$, $U(n)$, $SU(n)$, Sp($n$). Each carries a smooth manifold structure, and multiplication and inversion are smooth.
  • Grassmannians $\mathrm{Gr}(k, n)$ — the space of $k$-planes in $\mathbb{R}^n$. $\mathrm{Gr}(1, n+1) = \mathbb{R}P^n$.
  • Configuration spaces: the space of $n$ distinct points on a surface, the space of classical mechanics trajectories, etc.
  • The 7-sphere $S^7$ has exotic smooth structures (Milnor, 1956): topologically the same as the round $S^7$ but diffeomorphically inequivalent. Dimension $4$ is even wilder — $\mathbb{R}^4$ itself has uncountably many smooth structures.

A key early fact: every smooth manifold admits an embedding into some $\mathbb{R}^N$ (Whitney's embedding theorem, $N = 2n$ is enough). So at the cost of remembering a huge ambient space, you can always pretend to be a submanifold of Euclidean space. But intrinsic statements — things that depend only on the manifold structure, not the embedding — are cleaner.

10. Riemannian manifolds

A Riemannian metric $g$ on $M$ is a smooth, positive-definite, symmetric bilinear form on each tangent space $T_pM$, varying smoothly with $p$. Equivalently, it is a smooth section of $T^*M \otimes T^*M$. In local coordinates, $g = g_{ij}(x)\,dx^i\,dx^j$ — this is the multi-dimensional analog of $(E, F, G)$.

Given $g$, the Levi-Civita connection $\nabla$ is the unique connection on $TM$ that is both:

  • Metric-compatible: $\partial_k g(X,Y) = g(\nabla_k X, Y) + g(X, \nabla_k Y)$ for any vector fields $X, Y$.
  • Torsion-free: $\nabla_X Y - \nabla_Y X = [X, Y]$.

Explicitly in coordinates, the Christoffel symbols are again

$\Gamma^k_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}g^{kl}(\partial_i g_{jl} + \partial_j g_{il} - \partial_l g_{ij}),$

and $\nabla_i \partial_j = \Gamma^k_{ij}\partial_k$. This is a purely algebraic formula from the metric coefficients.

The Riemann curvature tensor is

$R(X, Y)Z = \nabla_X \nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y \nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X,Y]}Z.$

In coordinates, $R^l_{ijk} = \partial_i \Gamma^l_{jk} - \partial_j \Gamma^l_{ik} + \Gamma^m_{jk}\Gamma^l_{im} - \Gamma^m_{ik}\Gamma^l_{jm}.$ Lowering an index: $R_{lijk} = g_{lm}R^m_{ijk}$.

Symmetries: $R_{ijkl} = -R_{jikl} = -R_{ijlk} = R_{klij}$, plus the first Bianchi identity $R_{i[jkl]} = 0$. In dimension $n$ these constrain $R$ to $n^2(n^2-1)/12$ independent components at each point: $1$ in dim $2$, $6$ in dim $3$, $20$ in dim $4$, matching the classical tally.

The sectional curvature of a 2-plane $\sigma \subseteq T_pM$ spanned by orthonormal $u, v$ is

$K(\sigma) = g(R(u,v)v, u).$

When $M$ is a surface, $K$ is the Gaussian curvature of §5. In higher dimensions, $K(\sigma)$ at each point is a function on the Grassmannian of 2-planes. Manifolds of constant sectional curvature are the space forms: spheres, Euclidean space, and hyperbolic space — up to scaling and quotients.

Ricci curvature

Contracting once: $\mathrm{Ric}_{ij} = R^k_{kij}$. This is a symmetric 2-tensor. Geometrically, $\mathrm{Ric}(v, v)$ is (essentially) the average of sectional curvatures over all 2-planes containing $v$. Ricci curvature governs the behaviour of volumes of geodesic tubes: where Ricci is positive, volumes grow slower than Euclidean; where negative, faster. This is the curvature that enters Einstein's field equations $\mathrm{Ric} - \tfrac{1}{2}R\,g + \Lambda g = 8\pi T$ in general relativity.

Scalar curvature

Contracting once more: $R = g^{ij}\mathrm{Ric}_{ij}$. A single number at each point, capturing the average curvature in all planes. In dimension 2, $R = 2K$. In higher dimensions $R$ is much weaker than $\mathrm{Ric}$, which is much weaker than the full Riemann tensor.

A hierarchy of invariants emerges:

Riemann tensor $R^l_{ijk}$ $\Rightarrow$ sectional curvature $K(\sigma)$ $\Rightarrow$ Ricci $\mathrm{Ric}_{ij}$ $\Rightarrow$ scalar $R$.

Each arrow discards information. Theorems that control $R$ — e.g. the Gauss-Bonnet theorem in dim 2 and its higher-dimensional generalization the Chern-Gauss-Bonnet theorem — are more accessible than theorems controlling $\mathrm{Ric}$, which are more accessible than theorems controlling the full curvature tensor.

11. Coda — what's next

Differential geometry is a crossroads. From the basic framework laid out above, several major directions fan out:

  • Lie groups and principal bundles. A Lie group is a smooth manifold with a group structure where multiplication and inversion are smooth. The tangent space at the identity is a Lie algebra, and the adjoint representation ties the geometry to linear algebra. Principal $G$-bundles are the right setting for gauge theory; connections on them generalize the Levi-Civita connection. (Future notebook page: lie-groups.html.)
  • Symplectic geometry. A symplectic manifold is a smooth manifold with a closed non-degenerate 2-form $\omega$. The prototype is $T^*M$ with the canonical 2-form — the phase space of classical mechanics. Hamiltonian flows, Liouville's theorem, moment maps, and symplectic reduction live here. (Future: symplectic.html.)
  • Complex geometry and Kähler manifolds. A complex structure $J$ on $M$ compatible with a Riemannian metric $g$ gives a Hermitian manifold; if the associated 2-form is closed, you get a Kähler manifold — the intersection of complex, symplectic, and Riemannian geometries. Examples: $\mathbb{C}P^n$ with the Fubini-Study metric, Calabi-Yau manifolds. (Future: complex-geometry.html.)
  • Global analysis and index theory. On a Riemannian manifold, elliptic operators — the Laplacian, Dirac, Dolbeault, de Rham — have Fredholm index determined by topology (Atiyah-Singer). This knits differential geometry to topology and analysis.
  • General relativity. Einstein's theory is pseudo-Riemannian geometry in dimension 4 with signature $(-, +, +, +)$. The Einstein field equations relate curvature of spacetime to the stress-energy of matter. Schwarzschild, Kerr, FLRW solutions; black holes, cosmology.
  • Geometric analysis. Curvature flows (Ricci flow, mean curvature flow) deform metrics toward canonical forms. Ricci flow was the key tool in Perelman's proof of the Poincaré conjecture.

Classical differential geometry — the story of this page — is the common trunk. Every one of the branches above has its own character, its own questions, and its own techniques; but they all start with $(M, g)$, a smooth manifold and a way to measure.

The big picture: a smooth manifold is a space where calculus works. A Riemannian metric is a local inner product that lets you measure. Curvature is the obstruction to the metric being flat. Topology constrains the total curvature (Gauss-Bonnet); conversely, controlled curvature constrains topology (Bonnet–Myers, Synge, Hadamard, Cheeger-Gromoll). The dialogue between curvature and topology is the deep engine of modern Riemannian geometry.

Recap.

  1. A curve has curvature ($\kappa$) and torsion ($\tau$) as its Euclidean invariants; a plane curve has signed $\kappa(s)$, a space curve adds $\tau(s)$. Together they determine the curve up to rigid motion.
  2. A surface carries a first fundamental form $I$ (intrinsic — lengths, angles, areas) and a second fundamental form $II$ (extrinsic — how it bends in ambient space). The shape operator is $I^{-1}II$.
  3. Gaussian curvature $K = \det(S_p) = k_1 k_2$ is miraculously intrinsic (Theorema Egregium).
  4. Geodesics satisfy $\ddot\gamma^k + \Gamma^k_{ij}\dot\gamma^i\dot\gamma^j = 0$; on the sphere they are great circles, on the cylinder they are helices, on the plane they are straight lines.
  5. Gauss–Bonnet: $\int_S K\,dA = 2\pi\chi(S)$. Curvature totals up to a topological invariant.
  6. Abstract smooth manifolds generalize from $\mathbb{R}^3$; Riemannian manifolds add a metric; Levi-Civita is unique torsion-free metric-compatible; Riemann curvature, Ricci, and scalar curvatures are successive contractions.

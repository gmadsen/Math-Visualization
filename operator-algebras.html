Operator algebras, C*-algebras, and Gelfand duality

A compact Hausdorff space and its algebra of continuous functions are the same object viewed two ways. Drop commutativity and you have invented noncommutative geometry. The story is set up as analytic backbone for everything that calls itself a "spectrum."

1. From a space to its algebra of functions

Fix a compact Hausdorff space $X$. The set

$$C(X) \;=\; \{\, f\colon X\to\mathbb{C}\ \text{continuous}\,\}$$

is a complex vector space under pointwise operations. Multiply pointwise too: $(fg)(x)=f(x)g(x)$. Take the constant function $1$ for a unit, and complex conjugation as an involution: $f^* := \overline f$, with $(f^*)(x)=\overline{f(x)}$. The natural norm is the supremum norm

$$\|f\|_\infty \;=\; \sup_{x\in X} |f(x)|.$$

Compactness of $X$ guarantees the sup is attained, and $C(X)$ is complete in this norm — uniform limits of continuous functions are continuous. So $C(X)$ is a unital, commutative Banach *-algebra: a Banach algebra with a conjugate-linear, multiplication-reversing, idempotent map $a\mapsto a^*$.

One identity stands out. For any $f\in C(X)$ and any $x\in X$, $|\overline{f(x)}\, f(x)| = |f(x)|^2$, so

$$\|f^* f\|_\infty \;=\; \sup_x |f(x)|^2 \;=\; \|f\|_\infty^2.$$

This innocuous equality is the seed of everything that follows. Algebras satisfying it abstractly are called $C^*$-algebras, and we will see that every commutative unital one is of the form $C(X)$ for a unique $X$.

Background on the analytic side lives at functional analysis (Banach spaces, bounded operators, the spectrum) and on the topological side at topology (compact Hausdorff, weak-* topology, Stone–Čech). Holomorphic input — used in section 3 — comes from complex analysis.

What does $C(X)$ remember about $X$?

Already at the level of a Banach algebra, $C(X)$ is a startlingly faithful invariant. Banach–Stone: two compact Hausdorff $X$, $Y$ are homeomorphic iff $C(X)$ and $C(Y)$ are isometrically isomorphic as Banach spaces (no algebra structure needed). With the algebra structure, the same conclusion holds even more rigidly. Gelfand duality (section 6) is a sharp upgrade: $C(\,\cdot\,)$ is essentially a categorical equivalence.

2. Banach *-algebras and C*-algebras

Strip $C(X)$ down to its bones. A $*$-algebra over $\mathbb{C}$ is an associative $\mathbb{C}$-algebra $A$ with a map $a\mapsto a^*$ satisfying

  • $(a+b)^* = a^* + b^*$ and $(\lambda a)^* = \overline{\lambda}\, a^*$ (conjugate linearity),
  • $(ab)^* = b^* a^*$ (anti-multiplicative),
  • $(a^*)^* = a$ (involution).

A Banach $*$-algebra is a $*$-algebra carrying a complete submultiplicative norm $\|ab\|\le\|a\|\,\|b\|$ for which $\|a^*\|=\|a\|$. A $C^*$-algebra is a Banach $*$-algebra satisfying the $C^*$-identity

$$\boxed{\quad \|a^* a\| = \|a\|^2 \quad\text{for every } a\in A.\quad}$$

The $C^*$-identity ties norm to involution to multiplication so tightly that only one norm on a given $*$-algebra can ever satisfy it (uniqueness of the $C^*$-norm). This is what gives $C^*$-algebras their rigidity; in particular it forces $\|a^*a\|\ge \|a\|^2$, so combined with submultiplicativity we automatically have $\|a^*\|=\|a\|$ — that axiom is redundant once the $C^*$-identity is in play.

For $a\in A$, special elements get names:

  • Self-adjoint if $a^*=a$. In $C(X)$: real-valued functions. In $B(H)$: Hermitian operators. Spectrum is real.
  • Normal if $a^*a = a a^*$. The class for which a continuous functional calculus exists.
  • Unitary if $a^* a = a a^* = 1$ (in unital $A$). In $C(X)$: $|f|=1$ pointwise. Spectrum lies in the unit circle $\mathbb{T}$.
  • Positive if $a = b^* b$ for some $b$. Equivalently for normal $a$: spectrum $\subseteq [0,\infty)$. The positive elements form a cone.
  • Projection if $a = a^* = a^2$. Idempotent self-adjoints.

Sub-algebras, ideals, *-homomorphisms

A $*$-homomorphism $\pi\colon A\to B$ is an algebra map with $\pi(a^*) = \pi(a)^*$. A foundational analytic fact: any $*$-homomorphism between $C^*$-algebras is automatically contractive ($\|\pi(a)\|\le\|a\|$); if injective it is automatically isometric. So $C^*$-algebras have no slack — algebraic structure determines the norm.

GNS construction. Every $C^*$-algebra embeds isometrically into $B(H)$ for some Hilbert space $H$. So abstract $C^*$-algebras and concrete operator algebras are the same thing — a result one should keep in mind whenever a definition feels too axiomatic.

3. Spectrum in Banach algebras

Let $A$ be a unital Banach algebra and $a\in A$. The spectrum of $a$ is

$$\sigma(a) \;=\; \{\,\lambda\in\mathbb{C}\ :\ a-\lambda\cdot 1\ \text{is not invertible in } A\,\}.$$

Three foundational facts (Banach algebra-level, no $C^*$ structure used yet):

  • $\sigma(a)$ is a non-empty compact subset of $\mathbb{C}$, contained in the closed disk of radius $\|a\|$.
  • The resolvent $\lambda\mapsto (a-\lambda)^{-1}$ is a holomorphic $A$-valued function on $\rho(a):=\mathbb{C}\setminus\sigma(a)$.
  • Spectral radius formula (Gelfand 1941). $\displaystyle r(a):=\sup_{\lambda\in\sigma(a)} |\lambda| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\|a^n\|^{1/n}.$ This computes a complex-analytic invariant from norms of iterates — surprising every time.

Holomorphic functional calculus

If $f$ is holomorphic on an open neighborhood $U\supset \sigma(a)$ and $\Gamma$ is a contour in $U$ winding once counter-clockwise around $\sigma(a)$,

$$f(a) \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\Gamma f(\lambda)\,(\lambda-a)^{-1}\,d\lambda \;\in\; A.$$

This is the Riesz–Dunford functional calculus. It satisfies the spectral mapping theorem $\sigma(f(a)) = f(\sigma(a))$, is multiplicative ($(fg)(a)=f(a)g(a)$), and recovers polynomials in the obvious way.

Strict inequalities are possible. On a general Banach algebra $r(a)<\|a\|$ can happen: the nilpotent $N=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}\in M_2(\mathbb{C})$ has $\|N\|=1$ but $N^2=0$ forces $r(N)=0$. On a $C^*$-algebra one gets $r(a)=\|a\|$ for every normal $a$, a consequence of the $C^*$-identity $\|a^*a\|=\|a\|^2$ applied iteratively.

4. Continuous functional calculus

If $A$ is a $C^*$-algebra and $a\in A$ is normal ($a^*a=aa^*$), the holomorphic calculus extends to a contractive $*$-homomorphism

$$\Phi_a\colon C(\sigma(a)) \;\longrightarrow\; A,\qquad f\mapsto f(a),$$

which is in fact isometric onto the unital $C^*$-subalgebra $C^*(a,1)\subseteq A$ generated by $a$ and the unit. So you can plug a continuous (not just holomorphic) function into any normal element. This is the analytic engine behind the spectral theorem for self-adjoint operators on Hilbert space.

Why "normal" is the right hypothesis. If $a$ commutes with $a^*$, then the unital $*$-subalgebra it generates is commutative. Apply Gelfand duality (section 7) to that subalgebra: it must be $C(Y)$ for some compact Hausdorff $Y$. The character argument then identifies $Y$ with $\sigma(a)$.

What the calculus buys you

  • Square roots of positive elements. If $a\ge 0$, then $\sqrt{a}:=\sqrt{t}(a)$ is the unique positive element with $\sqrt{a}^2=a$.
  • Absolute value. $|a|:=\sqrt{a^*a}$ for any $a$; the analogue of $|z|=\sqrt{\bar z z}$.
  • Polar decomposition $a=v|a|$ with $v$ a partial isometry (in $B(H)$ or any von Neumann algebra).
  • Spectral projections $\chi_E(a)$ for Borel $E\subseteq\sigma(a)$ — not continuous, but available in the Borel calculus on von Neumann algebras.

5. Characters and the Gelfand spectrum

Now restrict to a commutative unital Banach algebra $A$ over $\mathbb{C}$. A character of $A$ is a non-zero algebra homomorphism

$$\varphi\colon A\to\mathbb{C},\qquad \varphi(ab)=\varphi(a)\varphi(b),\quad\varphi(1)=1.$$

Equivalently, the kernel of $\varphi$ is a maximal ideal of $A$ of codimension $1$. In the commutative Banach setting, every maximal ideal arises this way uniquely (because $A/\mathfrak m \cong \mathbb{C}$ is forced by Gelfand–Mazur: a complex Banach division algebra is $\mathbb{C}$).

Two key bounds:

  • Each character is automatically continuous with $\|\varphi\|\le 1$. (Idea: $\varphi(a)\in\sigma(a)$, hence $|\varphi(a)|\le r(a)\le \|a\|$.)
  • Conversely, the values $\varphi(a)$ as $\varphi$ ranges over characters are exactly the spectrum of $a$ in the commutative case: $\sigma(a) = \{\varphi(a):\varphi\in\hat A\}$.

The Gelfand spectrum (or character space, or maximal ideal space) is

$$\hat A \;=\; \{\,\varphi\colon A\to\mathbb{C}\ \text{nonzero algebra hom}\,\} \;\subset\; A^*$$

topologized as a subspace of the dual $A^*$ with the weak-$*$ topology — pointwise convergence on $A$.

Theorem. $\hat A$ is a compact Hausdorff space. Compactness is Banach–Alaoglu: $\hat A$ sits in the closed unit ball of $A^*$, which is weak-$*$ compact, and the multiplicativity condition is closed under weak-$*$ convergence.

What characters look like, in pictures

  • $A = \mathbb{C}^n$ (functions on $n$ points). The characters are the $n$ coordinate projections $\mathrm{ev}_k(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=x_k$. So $\hat A = \{1,2,\ldots,n\}$ as a discrete space.
  • $A = C(X)$, $X$ compact Hausdorff. Every character is evaluation at some point: $\varphi = \mathrm{ev}_x$ for a unique $x\in X$. So the natural map $X\to\hat{C(X)}$, $x\mapsto\mathrm{ev}_x$, is a homeomorphism. The space recovers itself.
  • $A = \ell^1(\mathbb{Z})$ with convolution. Characters are exponentials $\varphi_z((a_n)) = \sum a_n z^n$ for $z\in\mathbb{T}$, so $\hat A = \mathbb{T}$ (the unit circle). This is the Fourier transform in disguise.
  • $A = $ disk algebra $\mathcal A(\overline{\mathbb D})$ (continuous on $\overline{\mathbb D}$, holomorphic on $\mathbb D$). Characters are evaluation at points of $\overline{\mathbb D}$, so $\hat A = \overline{\mathbb D}$.

6. The Gelfand transform

Given a commutative unital Banach algebra $A$ and its character space $\hat A$ (compact Hausdorff, by section 4), define

$$\Gamma\colon A \;\longrightarrow\; C(\hat A),\qquad \Gamma(a)(\varphi) = \varphi(a).$$

That is, an element $a\in A$ becomes the function on $\hat A$ that "asks each character what it thinks $a$ is." This map is automatically:

  • a unital algebra homomorphism, $\Gamma(ab) = \Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)$, $\Gamma(1) = 1$;
  • contractive, $\|\Gamma(a)\|_\infty \le \|a\|$;
  • has $\|\Gamma(a)\|_\infty = r(a)$, the spectral radius.

So in general the Gelfand transform can fail in two ways: it can have a kernel (the Jacobson radical $\bigcap_{\varphi\in\hat A}\ker\varphi$), and it can fail to be isometric (when $r(a) < \|a\|$ for some $a$).

Gelfand–Naimark (commutative case, 1943). If $A$ is a commutative unital $C^*$-algebra, the Gelfand transform $\Gamma\colon A\to C(\hat A)$ is an isometric $*$-isomorphism. So every commutative unital $C^*$-algebra is, up to canonical isomorphism, $C(X)$ for $X = \hat A$.

Two ingredients make this work:

  1. Characters preserve $*$. If $a = a^*$ in a commutative $C^*$-algebra, then the spectrum is real (because the unital $C^*$-subalgebra generated by $a$ is $C(\sigma(a))$, in which the involution is complex conjugation). So $\varphi(a)\in\sigma(a)\subset\mathbb{R}$, hence $\varphi(a^*)=\overline{\varphi(a)}$. Splitting into real and imaginary parts gives $\varphi(a^*)=\overline{\varphi(a)}$ in general.
  2. The image is dense by Stone–Weierstrass: $\Gamma(A)\subseteq C(\hat A)$ is a unital $*$-subalgebra that separates points (different characters give different functions). Hence it is dense. The $C^*$-identity then promotes "isometric" — $\|a^*a\|=\|a\|^2$ matches $\|\Gamma(a^*a)\|_\infty=\|\Gamma(a)\|_\infty^2$ — to an actual equality $\|\Gamma(a)\|=\|a\|$, hence the image is closed, hence everything.

7. Gelfand duality as an equivalence of categories

We have two functors going back and forth:

$$\mathsf{CHaus}\;\xrightleftharpoons[\,\hat{(-)}\,]{\,C(-)\,}\;\mathsf{cCAlg}_1^{\mathrm{op}}.$$

Here $\mathsf{CHaus}$ is the category of compact Hausdorff spaces with continuous maps, and $\mathsf{cCAlg}_1$ is the category of commutative unital $C^*$-algebras with unital $*$-homomorphisms. The arrows reverse: a continuous map $\phi\colon X\to Y$ pulls back to $\phi^*\colon C(Y)\to C(X)$, $\phi^*(f)=f\circ\phi$.

The two unit/counit isomorphisms are:

  • $X \;\xrightarrow{\sim}\; \hat{C(X)}$, $x\mapsto \mathrm{ev}_x$ — every character of $C(X)$ is evaluation at a unique point.
  • $A \;\xrightarrow{\sim}\; C(\hat A)$, $a\mapsto \Gamma(a)$ — Gelfand–Naimark for commutative unital $C^*$-algebras.
Theorem (Gelfand duality, 1943). $C(-)$ and $\widehat{(-)}$ are mutually quasi-inverse contravariant equivalences of categories between $\mathsf{CHaus}$ and $\mathsf{cCAlg}_1$.

Functoriality and consequences

  • Surjective maps $\phi\colon X\twoheadrightarrow Y$ ↔ injective $*$-homs $\phi^*\colon C(Y)\hookrightarrow C(X)$. Quotients of spaces correspond to subalgebras of functions.
  • Closed embeddings $Y\hookrightarrow X$ ↔ surjective $*$-homs $C(X)\twoheadrightarrow C(Y)$. Closed subspaces correspond to quotient algebras (kernel is the ideal of functions vanishing on $Y$).
  • Products $X\times Y$ ↔ tensor products $C(X)\otimes C(Y) \cong C(X\times Y)$ (in a $C^*$-suitable sense — every reasonable cross-norm agrees on commutative $C^*$-algebras).
  • One-point compactification on the topological side ↔ unitization $A\mapsto A^+$ on the algebraic side. This packages the non-unital $C_0(X)$ ↔ locally compact Hausdorff $X$ duality as a corollary.

Compare with the categorical machinery in category theory (Yoneda, adjoints) and the dual setup for schemes in functor of points.

8. The noncommutative turn

Drop commutativity. A general $C^*$-algebra $A$ has a perfectly good "spectrum" of an element (section 3), but its character space $\hat A$ collapses dramatically — and often is uninteresting.

$M_n(\mathbb{C})$ has no characters at all for $n\ge 2$. A character $\varphi\colon M_n(\mathbb{C})\to\mathbb{C}$ would factor through a maximal ideal of codimension $1$, but $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ is simple — its only ideals are $0$ and itself. Conclusion: $\hat{M_n(\mathbb{C})}=\emptyset$. So you cannot recover a "space" of points from a noncommutative $C^*$-algebra in this naive way.

The right replacement, due to Connes and others, is to declare:

Slogan (noncommutative geometry). A $C^*$-algebra is a noncommutative compact Hausdorff space. All geometric concepts — points, measures, vector bundles, K-theory, differential forms, Riemannian structure — are reformulated as algebraic-analytic data on a $C^*$-algebra (or on a richer object built from it, like a spectral triple).

Examples worth knowing

  • Group $C^*$-algebras. $C^*(G)$ for a locally compact group $G$. Commutative iff $G$ is abelian; in that case $\widehat{C^*(G)}$ is the Pontryagin dual $\hat G$. Non-abelian $G$ gives genuinely noncommutative algebras whose representation theory is the Plancherel decomposition.
  • The Calkin algebra. $B(H)/K(H)$ for $H$ separable infinite-dimensional. Captures "operators modulo small perturbation" and is the home of Brown–Douglas–Fillmore index theory.
  • AF algebras. Inductive limits of finite-dimensional $C^*$-algebras. Classified up to isomorphism by their ordered $K_0$ (Elliott).
  • Cuntz algebras $\mathcal O_n$. Generated by isometries $s_1,\ldots,s_n$ with $\sum s_i s_i^* = 1$. Models for endomorphisms; appear in self-similar / fractal contexts.
  • Foliation algebras. Connes attaches a $C^*$-algebra to a foliation; the leaf space — usually a horrible quotient as a topological space — becomes a perfectly behaved noncommutative space.

Widget — the noncommutative torus $A_\theta$

The irrational rotation algebra (or noncommutative torus) $A_\theta$ is the universal $C^*$-algebra generated by two unitaries $u, v$ subject to

$$v\,u \;=\; e^{2\pi i \theta}\, u\, v.$$

For $\theta = 0$ this is just $C(\mathbb{T}^2)$ (commutative; spectrum is the 2-torus). For $\theta\notin\mathbb{Q}$ it is simple, has a unique trace, and has no nontrivial characters — its classical character spectrum is empty even though it is a perfectly good "noncommutative 2-torus."

9. Von Neumann algebras and the double commutant

Fix a Hilbert space $H$. A unital $*$-subalgebra $M\subseteq B(H)$ is a von Neumann algebra if it is closed in the weak operator topology. Equivalently — and this is von Neumann's 1929 miracle — if $M=M''$, where for $S\subseteq B(H)$ the commutant $S'=\{x\in B(H):xs=sx\ \forall s\in S\}$ is the set of operators commuting with every element of $S$.

Double commutant theorem. For a unital $*$-subalgebra $M\subseteq B(H)$, $\overline{M}^{\text{SOT}}=\overline{M}^{\text{WOT}}=M''$. So WOT-closure, SOT-closure, and "taking commutants twice" all give the same algebra. The norm-closure, in general, is strictly smaller: a $C^*$-algebra is norm-closed; a von Neumann algebra is additionally WOT-closed, which lets it contain spectral projections of its normal elements.

Examples: $B(H)$ itself; $L^\infty(X,\mu)$ acting on $L^2(X,\mu)$ by multiplication (this is the prototype of an abelian von Neumann algebra, paralleling $C(X)$ in the $C^*$ world); the group von Neumann algebra $L(G)=(\lambda(\mathbb{C}G))''\subseteq B(\ell^2(G))$; $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ on $\mathbb{C}^n$. Crucially, the compact operators $K(H)$ are a norm-closed $C^*$-algebra but their WOT-closure is all of $B(H)$ — so $K(H)$ is not a von Neumann algebra.

10. Factors and type classification

A von Neumann algebra $M\subseteq B(H)$ is a factor if its centre is trivial: $Z(M)=M\cap M'=\mathbb{C}\cdot 1$. Every von Neumann algebra decomposes — via direct integral over its centre — into factors, so factors are the "simple" building blocks. Murray and von Neumann (1936–43) classified them by the structure of their projections:

  • Type $\mathrm{I}_n$ ($n<\infty$): $M\cong M_n(\mathbb{C})$. Projections form a finite lattice of dimensions $0,1,\ldots,n$.
  • Type $\mathrm{I}_\infty$: $M\cong B(H)$ for $\dim H=\infty$. Has minimal projections (rank-one), and a semi-finite trace.
  • Type $\mathrm{II}_1$: has a faithful finite normal trace $\tau$ with $\tau(1)=1$, and no minimal projections — the dimension of projections takes all values in $[0,1]$. The hyperfinite II$_1$ factor $\mathcal R$ is unique up to isomorphism and arises as $(\bigotimes_{n\ge 1} M_2(\mathbb{C}))''$.
  • Type $\mathrm{II}_\infty$: $\mathcal R\,\bar\otimes\, B(H)$, semi-finite trace unbounded on the identity.
  • Type $\mathrm{III}$: no semi-finite trace; every nonzero projection is infinite. These show up in quantum field theory (local algebras of observables on spacetime regions are generically type III$_1$).

11. Murray–von Neumann equivalence and dimension

Two projections $p,q\in M$ (a von Neumann algebra) are Murray–von Neumann equivalent, written $p\sim q$, if there is a partial isometry $v\in M$ with

$$v^*v = p, \qquad v v^* = q.$$

In other words, $v$ maps the range of $p$ isometrically onto the range of $q$, and vanishes on the orthogonal complement. On a factor the equivalence classes are totally ordered by "subequivalence" $p\preceq q\iff p\sim q'\le q$; on a II$_1$ factor this order descends to a continuous dimension function

$$d\colon \mathrm{Proj}(M)\to[0,1],\qquad d(p)=\tau(p),$$

taking every value in $[0,1]$. This is the sense in which II$_1$ factors behave like "continuous-dimensional" matrix algebras — they interpolate between $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ and $B(H)$, with fractional dimensions.

12. Positive elements

An element $a$ of a $C^*$-algebra $A$ is positive, written $a\ge 0$, if $a=b^*b$ for some $b\in A$. Equivalently, $a$ is self-adjoint and its spectrum lies in $[0,\infty)$. The positive elements form a closed cone $A_+ \subseteq A_{\mathrm{sa}}$ — closed under non-negative scaling and addition — and this cone induces a partial order on self-adjoints by $a\le b\iff b-a\in A_+$. In $C(X)$ this is exactly the pointwise order $f\ge 0\iff f(x)\ge 0$ for all $x$; in $B(H)$ it is the Löwner order $\langle Tx,x\rangle\ge 0$.

A state on a unital $C^*$-algebra $A$ is a positive linear functional $\varphi\colon A\to\mathbb{C}$ with $\varphi(1)=1$. Positivity means $\varphi(a^*a)\ge 0$ for every $a\in A$; when $A=C(X)$ the Riesz–Markov theorem identifies states with Borel probability measures on $X$, so states are the $C^*$-algebraic generalization of probability distributions. The state space $S(A)=\{\varphi\colon\varphi\text{ state}\}$ is weak-$*$ compact convex, and its extreme points — the pure states — play the role of points in the noncommutative geometry dictionary. The GNS construction turns each state into a cyclic $*$-representation $\pi_\varphi\colon A\to B(H_\varphi)$ with $\varphi(a)=\langle\pi_\varphi(a)\xi_\varphi,\xi_\varphi\rangle$, and taking the direct sum over all states yields the universal embedding $A\hookrightarrow B(H)$ promised by Gelfand–Naimark.

13. States and pure states

A state on a unital $C^*$-algebra $A$ is a positive linear functional $\varphi\colon A\to\mathbb{C}$ with $\varphi(1)=1$ — equivalently, $\varphi(a^*a)\ge 0$ for all $a\in A$ and $\varphi(1)=1$. The state space $S(A)=\{\varphi:\varphi\text{ a state}\}$ is a weak-$*$ closed, convex, compact subset of the unit ball of $A^*$. When $A=C(X)$, the Riesz–Markov theorem identifies $S(A)$ with Borel probability measures on $X$, so states are the $C^*$-algebraic generalisation of probability distributions.

The extreme points of $S(A)$ are the pure states. When $A=C(X)$, these are exactly the point evaluations $\mathrm{ev}_x$ — Dirac measures, one per point of $X$. So pure states play the role of "points" in the noncommutative dictionary: for general $A$, the pure-state space carries the structure that $X$ did. The Krein–Milman theorem guarantees $S(A)=\overline{\mathrm{conv}}(\partial_e S(A))$ — every state is a weak-$*$ limit of convex combinations of pure states.

14. GNS construction

Every state $\omega$ on a $C^*$-algebra $A$ gives a cyclic $*$-representation $(\pi_\omega,H_\omega,\Omega_\omega)$ — this is the Gelfand–Naimark–Segal construction. Define a sesquilinear form on $A$ by

$$\langle a, b\rangle_\omega := \omega(b^*a).$$

This is positive semidefinite (because $\omega$ is positive), so $N_\omega=\{a:\omega(a^*a)=0\}$ is a left ideal and $A/N_\omega$ inherits a genuine inner product. Complete to get $H_\omega$; let $A$ act on $A/N_\omega$ by left multiplication (the ideal property makes this well-defined and bounded), extend to $H_\omega$, and take $\Omega_\omega=[1]$ as the cyclic vector. Then

$$\omega(a) = \langle \Omega_\omega,\, \pi_\omega(a)\Omega_\omega\rangle_\omega,$$

and every cyclic representation of $A$ arises this way (up to unitary equivalence). Summing over all states gives the universal representation: every $C^*$-algebra embeds isometrically into $B(H)$ for some $H$ — this is the noncommutative Gelfand–Naimark theorem.

15. Approximate units and amenability

A non-unital $C^*$-algebra $A$ still has a workable substitute for the identity: an approximate unit is a net $(e_\lambda)_{\lambda\in\Lambda}\subseteq A$ with

  • $0\le e_\lambda\le 1$ for all $\lambda$,
  • $\lambda\le\mu\Rightarrow e_\lambda\le e_\mu$ (increasing),
  • $\|e_\lambda a - a\|\to 0$ and $\|a e_\lambda - a\|\to 0$ for every $a\in A$.

Every $C^*$-algebra has one — for instance, the net of positive contractions in the positive cone, ordered by $\le$, works. Concretely for $A=C_0(X)$ ($X$ locally compact Hausdorff), take $e_K=$ a compactly supported bump equal to $1$ on the compact $K$, and increase $K$; for $A=K(H)$, take finite-rank projections onto an increasing chain of subspaces.

Nuclearity & amenability. Refining this, $A$ is nuclear if the identity map of $A$ factors approximately through matrix algebras — there exist completely positive maps $\varphi_\alpha\colon A\to M_{n(\alpha)}$ and $\psi_\alpha\colon M_{n(\alpha)}\to A$ with $\psi_\alpha\circ\varphi_\alpha\to\mathrm{id}_A$ in point-norm. A discrete group $G$ is amenable iff $C^*_r(G)$ is nuclear iff the group von Neumann algebra $L(G)$ is hyperfinite. Nuclearity is the "finite-dimensional approximation" property that powers classification theorems (Elliott, Kirchberg–Phillips) for simple $C^*$-algebras.

16. The Grothendieck parallel — Spec of a ring

The user's aside, made precise. Both Gelfand and Grothendieck recover a "geometric object" from a "ring of functions" by looking at its ideal theory and re-topologizing. They are the two great commutative-to-geometric dualities of the twentieth century, each adapted to its own world.

Side-by-side: characters vs prime ideals

Three honest differences

  1. $C^*$-Spec sees only points. For $A=C(X)$, every multiplicative functional is $\mathrm{ev}_x$ for some $x\in X$, so $\hat A = X$ literally. There is no "generic point" the way $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[x]$ has the prime $(0)$ in addition to all the maximal ideals $(x-a)$. The reason is analytic: a $\mathbb{C}$-valued character on $C(X)$ is forced to be evaluation by Stone–Weierstrass, while a $\mathbb{C}[x]$-algebra map to a domain can land in $\mathbb{C}(x)$.
  2. Schemes need a structure sheaf; $C(X)$ does not. The data $(\mathrm{Spec}\,R, \mathcal O_{\mathrm{Spec}\,R})$ is a locally ringed space — the topology alone forgets too much (the topology of $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$ is just the cofinite topology, identical for every Dedekind domain with countably many primes). On the analytic side, $X$ alone determines $C(X)$ up to canonical iso; the topology already carries everything.
  3. Analytic data lets you go noncommutative cleanly. The norm and involution on a $C^*$-algebra survive when commutativity is dropped, and you keep tools (functional calculus on normal elements, GNS, K-theory, Connes's spectral triples). On the algebraic side, dropping commutativity for a ring leaves you only with module categories — there is no "$\mathrm{Spec}\,R$" for noncommutative $R$ in the naive sense, and noncommutative algebraic geometry uses derived/categorical replacements (DG-categories, abelian categories of modules, $A_\infty$-algebras).

Where to read more

  • Functional analysis — the Banach/Hilbert backbone, $B(H)$, the spectral theorem.
  • Complex analysis — the Cauchy integral that powers holomorphic functional calculus.
  • Topology — compact Hausdorff, weak-$*$ topology, Stone–Čech compactification.
  • Schemes and commutative algebra — the Grothendieck side of this table.
  • Functor of points — the categorical re-statement of Spec, parallel to "$X = \hat{C(X)}$."
  • Sheaves — what's missing on the analytic side and present on the algebraic side.
  • Category theory — the equivalence-of-categories language.

  1. Spectra and the spectral radius for bounded operators (functional analysis).

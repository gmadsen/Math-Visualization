Group schemes

$\mathbb{G}_a, \mathbb{G}_m, \mu_n, \alpha_p$: group objects in schemes, Hopf algebras, and torsors.

1. What is a group scheme?

A group scheme over a base scheme $S$ is an $S$-scheme $\pi\colon G \to S$ together with three structure morphisms over $S$:

  • a multiplication $m\colon G \times_S G \to G$,
  • an identity $e\colon S \to G$ (a section of $\pi$),
  • an inverse $\iota\colon G \to G$,

satisfying the group axioms diagrammatically. There are no elements in sight: associativity, the identity property, and the inverse property are stated as the commutativity of four squares and triangles in the category of $S$-schemes. Equivalently, by the functor-of-points philosophy, $G$ is a group scheme iff the representable functor $h_G(T) = \mathrm{Hom}_S(T, G)$ lands in $\mathbf{Grp}$ for every $S$-scheme $T$ — naturally in $T$. So a group scheme is exactly a group object in the category $\mathbf{Sch}_S$ of $S$-schemes.

Spelled out as commuting diagrams: associativity is the equality $m \circ (m \times \mathrm{id}) = m \circ (\mathrm{id} \times m)\colon G^3 \to G$. The identity axiom says that the two compositions $G \xrightarrow{(e\circ\pi,\, \mathrm{id})} G \times_S G \xrightarrow{m} G$ and $G \xrightarrow{(\mathrm{id},\, e\circ\pi)} G \times_S G \xrightarrow{m} G$ both equal $\mathrm{id}_G$. The inverse axiom says that $m \circ (\mathrm{id}, \iota) = e \circ \pi$ (and the symmetric version). These are the same four diagrams that define a group object in any category with finite products — what is special about $\mathbf{Sch}_S$ is that the product is the fibre product over $S$, and the resulting structure is rich enough to support algebraic geometry instead of just set-theoretic combinatorics.

The functor-of-points view is the workhorse. Saying "$G$ is a group scheme" is the same as giving, for each test scheme $T$, a group structure on $G(T) := \mathrm{Hom}_S(T,G)$, varying naturally with $T$. Concrete dictionary: $\mathbb{G}_m(T) = \Gamma(T, \mathcal{O}_T)^\times$ (the group of units of the ring of global sections); $\mathbb{G}_a(T) = \Gamma(T, \mathcal{O}_T)$ (additive group); $\mathrm{GL}_n(T) = \mathrm{GL}_n(\Gamma(T, \mathcal{O}_T))$; $\mu_n(T) = \{f \in \Gamma(T,\mathcal{O}_T)^\times : f^n = 1\}$. A morphism of group schemes is a morphism of schemes that respects these natural group structures — equivalently, makes the obvious squares commute.

2. Examples: $\mathbb{G}_a, \mathbb{G}_m, \mu_n, \alpha_p$

Four small affine group schemes carry most of the local theory. Each one is $\mathrm{Spec}$ of a small commutative ring, and the group structure comes from a comultiplication $\Delta$ on that ring (next section). They are the building blocks: every other affine group scheme is, locally, a successive extension of these.

  • The additive group $\mathbb{G}_a = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[t]$. Its $T$-points are $\mathbb{G}_a(T) = \Gamma(T, \mathcal{O}_T)$ under addition; the comultiplication is $\Delta(t) = t \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes t$, encoding $(a,b) \mapsto a + b$.
  • The multiplicative group $\mathbb{G}_m = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[t, t^{-1}]$. Its $T$-points are the units $\Gamma(T,\mathcal{O}_T)^\times$; comultiplication $\Delta(t) = t \otimes t$ encodes multiplication of units.
  • $n$-th roots of unity $\mu_n = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[t]/(t^n - 1)$. Its $T$-points are $\{f \in \Gamma(T,\mathcal{O}_T)^\times : f^n = 1\}$; same comultiplication $\Delta(t) = t \otimes t$ as $\mathbb{G}_m$. There is a closed immersion $\mu_n \hookrightarrow \mathbb{G}_m$ — $\mu_n$ is the kernel of the $n$-th power map $[n]\colon \mathbb{G}_m \to \mathbb{G}_m$.
  • The infinitesimal $\alpha_p$ in characteristic $p$: $\alpha_p = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[t]/(t^p)$ over a field $k$ of characteristic $p$. Comultiplication $\Delta(t) = t \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes t$ as for $\mathbb{G}_a$, but with the relation $t^p = 0$. Equivalently, $\alpha_p$ is the kernel of Frobenius $\mathrm{Frob}\colon \mathbb{G}_a \to \mathbb{G}_a$, $x \mapsto x^p$.

Over a field $k$, count the $\bar k$-points to feel the geometry. $\mathbb{G}_a(\bar k) = \bar k$ and $\mathbb{G}_m(\bar k) = \bar k^\times$ are vast. By contrast, $\mu_n$ is finite of order $n$ provided $n$ is invertible in $k$: in $\bar k$ the equation $t^n = 1$ has exactly $n$ distinct solutions, the cyclic group of $n$-th roots of unity. If $\mathrm{char}(k) = p$ and $p \mid n$, things change — e.g. over $\bar{\mathbb{F}_p}$, $\mu_p$ has only one $\bar{\mathbb{F}_p}$-point because $t^p - 1 = (t-1)^p$, but as a scheme it is still nonzero (it has nilpotents). And $\alpha_p$ has only one $\bar k$-point but is geometrically nontrivial — it lives entirely in the infinitesimal direction.

The clock toy below dramatises the analogy. Pick $n$ and a multiplier $a$. The map $k \mapsto a\cdot k \pmod n$ on the dial $\mathbb{Z}/n$ is exactly the multiplication-by-$\zeta^a$ map on $\mu_n(\mathbb{C})$ once you identify $\mathbb{Z}/n$ with $\mu_n(\mathbb{C})$ via $k \leftrightarrow \zeta^k = e^{2\pi i k/n}$. When $\gcd(a,n) = 1$, the map is a bijection — that's precisely when $\zeta^a$ is itself a primitive $n$-th root, i.e. a generator of $\mu_n$. When $\gcd(a,n) > 1$ the orbit collapses onto a proper subgroup, the same way a non-unit element of $\mu_n$ generates a proper $\mu_d$ inside it for $d = n/\gcd(a,n)$.

3. Affine group schemes ↔ commutative Hopf algebras

An affine group scheme over $k$ is one of the form $G = \mathrm{Spec}\,A$ for some commutative $k$-algebra $A$. The contravariant equivalence $\mathrm{Spec}\colon \mathbf{CAlg}_k^{\mathrm{op}} \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathbf{AffSch}_k$ then turns the structure morphisms $m, e, \iota$ into the structure maps $\Delta, \epsilon, S$ on $A$ — but with arrows reversed. The result is a Hopf algebra: a commutative $k$-algebra equipped with

  • a comultiplication $\Delta\colon A \to A \otimes_k A$ (from $m\colon G \times G \to G$),
  • a counit $\epsilon\colon A \to k$ (from $e\colon \mathrm{Spec}\,k \to G$),
  • an antipode $S\colon A \to A$ (from $\iota\colon G \to G$),

satisfying coassociativity, the counit law, and the antipode equation — the diagrams of §1 with every arrow reversed. The category of affine group schemes over $k$ is anti-equivalent to the category of commutative Hopf algebras over $k$. A morphism of group schemes corresponds to a morphism of Hopf algebras (algebra map respecting $\Delta, \epsilon, S$).

The dictionary is concrete. For $\mathbb{G}_m = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[t,t^{-1}]$, the multiplication $(a,b) \mapsto ab$ on units pulls back to $\Delta(t) = t \otimes t$; the identity element $1$ pulls back to $\epsilon(t) = 1$; and the inverse $a \mapsto a^{-1}$ pulls back to $S(t) = t^{-1}$. For $\mathbb{G}_a = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[t]$, addition $(a,b)\mapsto a+b$ pulls back to $\Delta(t) = t\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes t$; identity is $\epsilon(t) = 0$; inverse is $S(t) = -t$. The pattern is: every group-theoretic statement on points is dual to an algebraic identity in the Hopf algebra. Cocommutative Hopf algebras correspond to commutative group schemes (multiplication is symmetric).

Why insist on this duality? Because the Hopf-algebra side is where the calculation lives. To check whether a candidate affine group scheme actually satisfies the group axioms, one verifies the dual identities on $A$ — for example, coassociativity $(\Delta \otimes \mathrm{id}) \circ \Delta = (\mathrm{id} \otimes \Delta) \circ \Delta$, which is just an equality of two algebra maps $A \to A^{\otimes 3}$. To construct $\mathrm{Lie}(G)$ in §5 we'll use the augmentation ideal $I = \ker \epsilon \subset A$ and form $I/I^2$. Subgroup schemes correspond to Hopf-ideal quotients $A \twoheadrightarrow A/J$. And so on — the Hopf-algebra perspective makes the proofs algebraic.

4. Étale, connected, and infinitesimal pieces

Over a perfect field $k$, the finite commutative group schemes form an abelian category — call it $\mathbf{FCGS}_k$ — and every object decomposes canonically. Each $G$ sits in a short exact sequence

$1 \to G^\circ \to G \to G^{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}} \to 1$

where $G^\circ$ is the connected component of the identity (a connected, hence local, scheme — supported at one point) and $G^{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}} = \pi_0(G)$ is the étale quotient (a constant group scheme over $\bar k$). Over a perfect field this sequence splits canonically: $G \cong G^\circ \rtimes G^{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}$, where in the commutative case the semidirect product is just a direct product. The proof uses that $\mathcal{O}(G^\circ)$ is the local Artin component at the identity and $\mathcal{O}(G^{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}) = \mathcal{O}(G) / \mathfrak{m}_e^\infty \cdot \text{stuff}$ — concretely, $G^{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}$ is recovered as $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}(G)_{\mathrm{red}}/\text{nilpotents at non-identity components})$.

The key cases: in characteristic 0, every finite group scheme is automatically étale (no non-trivial infinitesimals, because finite étale algebras are exactly products of finite separable extensions). So $G^\circ = 1$ always, and $\mathbf{FCGS}_{\mathbb{Q}}$ collapses to the ordinary category of finite groups (with continuous Galois action). In characteristic $p$, the connected piece can be non-trivial — that is where $\alpha_p$ and $\mu_p$ live. Both have $\bar k$-point set of size 1 and are concentrated at the identity, but they are distinct group schemes (different Hopf algebras, different Lie algebras). Even more refined: the connected/étale decomposition can be applied dually too, giving Cartier-style components $G^{\circ,\circ}, G^{\circ,\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}, G^{\mathrm{\acute{e}t},\circ}, G^{\mathrm{\acute{e}t},\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}$ (the last two equal in the commutative case because Cartier duality swaps connected and étale).

A useful slogan: étale-ness is a topological/separable feature — $G$ is étale over $k$ iff it is "discrete in the étale topology," equivalently iff the augmentation ideal $\mathfrak{m}_e$ is generated by elements $f$ with $\mathrm{d}f$ generating $\mathfrak{m}_e/\mathfrak{m}_e^2$ rationally. Connectedness is an infinitesimal feature — $G^\circ$ has a single $\bar k$-point but a thick local ring filling out the tangent space at the identity. The two are orthogonal: a finite étale group scheme has trivial Lie algebra; a connected (infinitesimal) one has Lie algebra equal to its dimension.

5. Lie algebra $\mathrm{Lie}(G)$

The Lie algebra of an algebraic group $G$ over $k$ is defined functorially via dual numbers. Let $k[\epsilon] := k[\epsilon]/\epsilon^2$ be the ring of dual numbers (rank 2 over $k$, with $\epsilon$ a square-zero element); the natural projection $k[\epsilon] \to k$ killing $\epsilon$ induces a map of $k$-points $G(k[\epsilon]) \to G(k)$. The Lie algebra is its kernel as a set, equipped with a $k$-vector-space structure and a Lie bracket from the adjoint action:

$\mathrm{Lie}(G) := \ker\bigl(G(k[\epsilon]/\epsilon^2) \to G(k)\bigr).$

Why is this the right object? A $k[\epsilon]$-point of $G$ that lifts the identity is a "first-order deformation" of $\mathrm{id}_e$ — a tangent vector at $e \in G$. So set-theoretically $\mathrm{Lie}(G) = T_e G$, the Zariski tangent space at the identity. The vector-space structure is forced: addition comes from the diagonal $G \times G \to G$, scalar multiplication from $k$-linearity of dual numbers. The non-trivial part is the bracket, which comes from conjugation: for $g \in G(k[\epsilon][\epsilon'])$ (dual numbers in two variables) and $h \in G(k[\epsilon'])$, the commutator $g h g^{-1} h^{-1}$ measures the failure of $G$ to be commutative at first order, and lives in $\mathrm{Lie}(G) \otimes_k k\epsilon\epsilon'$. Extracting the $\epsilon\epsilon'$-coefficient gives the Lie bracket $[\cdot, \cdot]$ on $\mathrm{Lie}(G)$.

Concrete computations. For $G = \mathrm{GL}_n$: a $k[\epsilon]$-point is a matrix $M = I + \epsilon X$ with $X \in \mathrm{Mat}_n(k)$ arbitrary (since $\det M = 1 + \epsilon\,\mathrm{tr}(X)$ is automatically a unit in $k[\epsilon]$ — no extra condition). So $\mathrm{Lie}(\mathrm{GL}_n) = \mathrm{Mat}_n(k) =: \mathfrak{gl}_n$, with bracket the usual matrix commutator. Dimension $n^2$. For $G = \mathrm{SL}_n$ the same calculation with the constraint $\det M = 1$ forces $\mathrm{tr}(X) = 0$, so $\mathrm{Lie}(\mathrm{SL}_n) = \mathfrak{sl}_n$, dimension $n^2 - 1$. For $\mathbb{G}_m$: a point of $\mathbb{G}_m(k[\epsilon])$ is a unit $u = 1 + \epsilon a$ with $a \in k$ arbitrary, so $\mathrm{Lie}(\mathbb{G}_m) = k$ — one-dimensional, abelian (bracket is trivially zero because $\mathbb{G}_m$ is commutative). For $\mathbb{G}_a$: a point is $a \in k$ with addition law on dual numbers, so again $\mathrm{Lie}(\mathbb{G}_a) = k$, abelian.

Over $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$, when $G$ is smooth, this construction agrees with the classical Lie algebra of the Lie group $G(\mathbb{R})$ (or $G(\mathbb{C})$) as left-invariant vector fields, or as the tangent space at the identity with bracket from the differential of conjugation. The dual-number formulation has the advantage of working uniformly in any characteristic, including for non-smooth $G$ (in which case $\dim_k \mathrm{Lie}(G)$ can exceed $\dim G$ — that excess is the failure of smoothness at the identity).

6. $G$-torsors and $H^1(X, G)$

Let $G$ be a group scheme over a base $S$. A $G$-torsor (or "principal $G$-bundle") over an $S$-scheme $X$ is an $X$-scheme $\pi\colon P \to X$ equipped with a $G$-action $G \times_S P \to P$ over $X$ that is

  • free and transitive — the map $G \times_S P \to P \times_X P$, $(g, p) \mapsto (gp, p)$, is an isomorphism;
  • étale-locally trivial — there is an étale cover $\{U_i \to X\}$ such that $P|_{U_i} \cong G \times U_i$ as $G$-spaces over $U_i$.

The trivial torsor is $G \times_S X$, with $G$ acting on the first factor. Trivial-locally-but-not-globally is exactly the $H^1$-flavour of the moduli problem: torsors are twisted forms of the trivial bundle, glued by transition cocycles in $G$. The base case $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,k$ for $k$ a field recovers Galois cohomology: $H^1_{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}(\mathrm{Spec}\,k, G) = H^1(\mathrm{Gal}(\bar k/k), G(\bar k))$ for smooth commutative $G$.

The moduli-theoretic punchline is that isomorphism classes of $G$-torsors over $X$ are classified by the first étale cohomology:

$\{\,\text{$G$-torsors on $X$}\,\}/\!\cong \;=\; H^1_{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}(X, G).$

This is a vast generalisation of vector-bundle classification. For $G = \mathbb{G}_m$, a torsor is a line bundle (well, the $\mathbb{G}_m$-bundle of frames of a line bundle); the classification recovers the Picard group $\mathrm{Pic}(X) = H^1_{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}(X, \mathbb{G}_m)$ (Hilbert 90 says this agrees with $H^1$ in the Zariski topology). For $G = \mu_n$, the Kummer sequence $1 \to \mu_n \to \mathbb{G}_m \xrightarrow{[n]} \mathbb{G}_m \to 1$ gives the long exact sequence

$\cdots \to \Gamma(X,\mathcal{O}_X)^\times \xrightarrow{[n]} \Gamma(X,\mathcal{O}_X)^\times \to H^1_{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}(X, \mu_n) \to \mathrm{Pic}(X)[n] \to 0$

so $\mu_n$-torsors are Kummer-style "$n$-th roots of a unit, modulo $n$-th powers" combined with $n$-torsion in $\mathrm{Pic}$. For $G$ a finite étale group scheme, $H^1_{\mathrm{\acute{e}t}}(X, G)$ classifies $G$-Galois covers of $X$.

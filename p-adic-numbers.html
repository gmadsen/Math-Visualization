p-adic numbers: trees, ultrametrics, and Hensel

A parallel universe of "size" on the rationals, built from divisibility by a prime. The number line is replaced by a rooted tree, triangles become isoceles, and power series converge when their coefficients grow. This page keeps one image at the center: $\mathbb{Z}_p$ as a Cantor-set-like tree.

1. The other absolute value

On the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ we are used to one measure of size, the usual absolute value $|x|_\infty$. But a prime $p$ secretly supplies another one. For a nonzero integer $n$, let the $p$-adic valuation be $$v_p(n) = \max\{k\ge 0 : p^k \mid n\},$$ extended to $\mathbb{Q}^\times$ by $v_p(a/b) = v_p(a)-v_p(b)$, and set $v_p(0)=\infty$. The $p$-adic absolute value is $$|x|_p = p^{-v_p(x)}, \qquad |0|_p=0.$$ So $|p|_p = 1/p$ is small, while $1/p$ is large: divisibility by $p$ makes a number tiny.

Ostrowski's theorem. Every nontrivial absolute value on $\mathbb{Q}$ is equivalent either to $|\cdot|_\infty$ or to $|\cdot|_p$ for a unique prime $p$. So the list is complete: one Archimedean place, one place for each prime, and nothing else.

The product formula neatly ties them together: for every nonzero rational $x$, $$\prod_{v} |x|_v = 1,$$ where $v$ ranges over $\infty$ and all primes. Decrease somewhere, increase somewhere else.

2. The ultrametric inequality

The $p$-adic absolute value satisfies more than the triangle inequality. For all $x,y\in\mathbb{Q}$, $$|x+y|_p \le \max(|x|_p,|y|_p).$$ Call an absolute value with this property non-Archimedean, or an ultrametric. Three small but dramatic consequences:

  • Every triangle is isoceles. If $|x|_p \ne |y|_p$, then $|x+y|_p = \max(|x|_p,|y|_p)$. So among three pairwise distances, the two largest must be equal.
  • Every point inside a ball is its center. If $y$ lies in $\overline{B}(x,r) = \{z : |z-x|_p \le r\}$, then $\overline{B}(y,r) = \overline{B}(x,r)$.
  • Balls are clopen. A $p$-adic ball is both open and closed. The topology is totally disconnected — no nontrivial connected sets.

A short reason for ultrametricity: $v_p(x+y) \ge \min(v_p(x),v_p(y))$ (factors of $p$ survive addition), and exponentiating by $-\log p$ flips the inequality.

W4 · Ultrametric trianglethree integers in $\mathbb{Z}_p$

Totally disconnected topology is the structural punchline. In $\mathbb{R}$, connected intervals are the building blocks of analysis; in $\mathbb{Q}_p$, the only connected subsets are single points. Instead the building blocks are balls, and the collection of balls forms a basis of clopens that is nested rather than overlapping: any two balls are either disjoint or one contains the other.

3. p-adic expansions

Every $x\in \mathbb{Z}_p$ (the $p$-adic integers, to be defined precisely in a moment) has a unique expansion $$x = a_0 + a_1 p + a_2 p^2 + a_3 p^3 + \cdots, \qquad a_i \in \{0,1,\ldots,p-1\}.$$ For a non-negative integer $n$, the expansion terminates: it is exactly the base-$p$ representation. For a rational $a/b$ with $\gcd(b,p)=1$, the expansion is eventually periodic. Rationals with $p\mid b$ need a finite negative tail and live in $\mathbb{Q}_p\setminus\mathbb{Z}_p$.

The ring structure on these expansions is usual base-$p$ arithmetic with carries propagating to the right — toward higher powers of $p$. Addition and multiplication do not depend on "where the expansion ends" because $p^n\to 0$ in $|\cdot|_p$.

W2 · p-adic expansioninteger or rational $a/b$ with $\gcd(b,p)=1$

Example: in $\mathbb{Z}_5$, $-1 = 4 + 4\cdot 5 + 4\cdot 5^2 + \cdots$ because $(p-1)\sum_{i\ge 0} p^i = -1$ under $|\cdot|_p$. Every negative integer has an infinite expansion.

4. $\mathbb{Z}_p$ as an inverse limit

The cleanest definition: set $$\mathbb{Z}_p = \varprojlim_{n} \mathbb{Z}/p^n\mathbb{Z}.$$ An element is a compatible tower $(x_1,x_2,x_3,\ldots)$ with $x_n\in \mathbb{Z}/p^n$ and $x_{n+1}\equiv x_n\pmod{p^n}$. Compatibility is exactly the statement that the digits $a_0,a_1,\ldots$ are consistent across precisions.

The topology is the projective-limit topology from the discrete $\mathbb{Z}/p^n$, making $\mathbb{Z}_p$ compact, Hausdorff, and totally disconnected — homeomorphic to a Cantor set. It is also a commutative ring, integral domain, and in fact a complete DVR with residue field $\mathbb{F}_p$.

5. The p-adic tree

Here is the picture to imprint. Represent $\mathbb{Z}_p$ as a rooted tree: the root is the full ring; at level 1 the root has $p$ children, one for each residue class mod $p$; each of those has $p$ children, one for each residue mod $p^2$ refining the parent; and so on. Each infinite downward path corresponds to a unique element of $\mathbb{Z}_p$, namely the compatible tower of its residues.

Distance is shared depth. Two elements $x,y\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ are close iff they agree modulo a high power of $p$, iff their paths in the tree share a long initial segment. Explicitly, $$|x-y|_p = p^{-n} \iff x\equiv y\pmod{p^n},\ x\not\equiv y\pmod{p^{n+1}}.$$ That is why $\mathbb{Z}_p$ is homeomorphic to a Cantor set: its leaves at infinity are exactly the branches of a complete infinite tree.

Convergence becomes visual: a sequence $x_n\to x$ iff, for every $k$, eventually all $x_n$ lie in the same subtree of depth $k$ as $x$.

6. $\mathbb{Q}_p$ and completion

Define $\mathbb{Q}_p$ as the completion of $\mathbb{Q}$ with respect to $|\cdot|_p$ — the same Cauchy-sequence construction that produces $\mathbb{R}$ from $|\cdot|_\infty$. It is a field, and $\mathbb{Z}_p$ sits inside as the closed unit ball $\{x : |x|_p \le 1\}$.

Elements of $\mathbb{Q}_p$ have expansions

$$x = \sum_{i=N}^{\infty} a_i p^i, \qquad a_i \in \{0,\ldots,p-1\},\ N\in\mathbb{Z},$$

where $N = v_p(x)$ is the location of the first nonzero digit. The left-infinite part is unavailable; all the action is to the right, toward higher powers of $p$.

Structure summary. $\mathbb{Q}_p$ is a complete non-Archimedean local field with residue field $\mathbb{F}_p$, uniformizer $p$, and ring of integers $\mathbb{Z}_p = \{|x|_p\le 1\}$. Its unit group is $\mathbb{Z}_p^\times = \{|x|_p=1\}$, and $\mathbb{Q}_p^\times \cong p^{\mathbb{Z}}\times \mathbb{Z}_p^\times$.
W6 · Convergence comparisonsame series, two absolute values

Striking cases: $\sum p^n$ diverges in $\mathbb{R}$ but converges to $1/(1-p)$ in $\mathbb{Q}_p$; $\sum n!$ diverges in $\mathbb{R}$ but converges in every $\mathbb{Q}_p$ because $v_p(n!)\to\infty$.

7. Hensel's lemma

Hensel's lemma (first form). Let $f(x)\in\mathbb{Z}_p[x]$ and $x_0\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ with $f(x_0)\equiv 0\pmod p$ and $f'(x_0)\not\equiv 0\pmod p$. Then there is a unique $x\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ with $f(x)=0$ and $x\equiv x_0\pmod p$.

Think of this as Newton's method with automatic quadratic convergence. Given $x_n$ with $f(x_n)\equiv 0\pmod{p^{n+1}}$, set $$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}.$$ Since $f'(x_n)$ is a unit mod $p$, this is well-defined in $\mathbb{Z}_p$; a Taylor-expansion argument shows $f(x_{n+1})\equiv 0\pmod{p^{2n+2}}$.

Example. In $\mathbb{Z}_7$, does $\sqrt{2}$ exist? Try $f(x)=x^2-2$. We need $x_0^2\equiv 2\pmod 7$. Since $3^2=9\equiv 2\pmod 7$, take $x_0=3$. Then $f'(3)=6\not\equiv 0\pmod 7$. Hensel says a unique lift exists, and the Newton iteration gives it digit by digit.

W5 · Hensel lift step-by-step$f(x)=x^2-k$, solve $x^2=k$ in $\mathbb{Z}_p$

The take-away: in $\mathbb{Z}_p$, "approximate solution mod $p$" plus "derivative is a unit" always promotes to an exact root. This is why $p$-adic fields behave so well for deformation arguments.

8. The local–global (Hasse) principle

Having built one completion per prime plus the Archimedean place $\mathbb{R}$, a natural question is how much a global problem over $\mathbb{Q}$ is controlled by its behavior at each completion. For an equation $f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=0$ with rational coefficients, say it satisfies the local–global principle if $$f=0\ \text{has a nonzero rational solution}\iff f=0\ \text{has a nonzero solution in }\mathbb{R}\ \text{and in every }\mathbb{Q}_p.$$ The left-to-right direction is automatic; the deep content is the reverse direction, which says local solvability at every place is sufficient for global solvability.

Hasse–Minkowski. For a quadratic form $Q(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, $Q$ represents zero nontrivially over $\mathbb{Q}$ iff it does so over $\mathbb{R}$ and over every $\mathbb{Q}_p$. Checking $\mathbb{R}$ is a signature condition; checking $\mathbb{Q}_p$ reduces to finitely many Hilbert symbols, via Hensel's lemma on the non-zero-discriminant primes. So an infinite question becomes a finite calculation.

Failure for higher degree. The principle fails for cubics: Selmer's famous $3x^3+4y^3+5z^3=0$ has solutions in $\mathbb{R}$ and every $\mathbb{Q}_p$ but none in $\mathbb{Q}$. The gap between "locally solvable" and "globally solvable" is organized: for Brauer–Severi varieties it is controlled by the Brauer group of $\mathbb{Q}$, and for elliptic curves by the Tate–Shafarevich group $\Sha(E/\mathbb{Q})$. Obstruction theory turns the failure itself into arithmetic.

Heuristic. Think of solving $f=0$ at each place as asking local witnesses. For quadratic forms over $\mathbb{Q}$ the witnesses always assemble into a global solution. For higher degree they might not, and the difference lives in cohomology groups attached to $G_\mathbb{Q}$.
W7 · Local solvability check$ax^2+by^2=c$ over $\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{Q}_p$

Ostrowski told us there are no other places to check: $\mathbb{R}$ plus one $\mathbb{Q}_p$ per prime is the complete list. "Solving at every place" is therefore a finite, mechanisable check for quadratic forms.

9. Why care

$p$-adic numbers are not exotic formalism; they reorganize number theory, algebraic geometry, and representation theory.

  • Local-global (Hasse) principle. For quadratic forms over $\mathbb{Q}$, a form represents zero globally iff it does so over $\mathbb{R}$ and every $\mathbb{Q}_p$. "Check the problem at every place." It fails for higher-degree forms, but the failure itself is organized (Brauer groups, Tate–Shafarevich). See the sibling page on quadratic reciprocity.
  • Reduction of elliptic curves. An elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ reduces mod $p$ for each prime, and whether the reduction is good, multiplicative, or additive is controlled by $p$-adic geometry. Forward to elliptic curves.
  • $\ell$-adic Galois representations. The étale cohomology of a variety over $\mathbb{Q}$ produces continuous representations $\rho\colon G_\mathbb{Q}\to\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ carrying deep arithmetic information (Frobenius traces $=$ point counts). Forward-linked in the Galois and ANT pages.
  • Completion = localization + analysis. Doing analysis at a prime ideal lets you use calculus (Newton's method, Taylor series, $p$-adic $L$-functions) exactly where the combinatorics of primes lives.
Mental picture to leave with. The rationals have one "place at infinity" (Euclidean distance) and one place per prime. Each place has a completion; together they form the adèles. Every global arithmetic phenomenon factors through its behavior at each place, and the $p$-adic places are rooted trees, not lines.

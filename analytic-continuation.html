Analytic continuation — germs, monodromy, and the functional equation

A holomorphic function is a local recipe, but its global life is often richer than any one formula. Tracking a germ from disk to disk, we meet branch points, natural boundaries, and — as the flagship example — Riemann's trick that promotes $\zeta(s)$ from a half-plane series to a meromorphic function satisfying $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$.

1. Germs and the identity theorem

The local object of complex analysis is the germ: a convergent power series $\sum c_n (z-a)^n$ centered at some point $a$. Its disk of convergence has radius $$R \;=\; \frac{1}{\limsup_{n\to\infty}|c_n|^{1/n}}.$$ Inside $D(a,R)$ the germ defines a holomorphic function; on the boundary anything can happen; outside, the series diverges but the function it represents may still extend.

The identity theorem is the single algebraic fact that makes analytic continuation well-defined:

Identity theorem. If $f,g$ are holomorphic on a connected open set $U$ and agree on a subset of $U$ with an accumulation point inside $U$, then $f\equiv g$ on all of $U$.

Equivalently: a holomorphic function is rigid. Two overlapping germs that agree on any open subset of the overlap must agree wherever both are defined. In particular, if $f$ admits a holomorphic extension from an open set to a larger connected open set, the extension is unique. "The" analytic continuation is unambiguous — when it exists.

Example: the geometric germ

The germ at $0$, $$f(z) \;=\; \sum_{n\ge 0} z^n,$$ has radius $R=1$. Already inside the disk we can re-expand it around any point $a\in D(0,1)$:

$$f(z) \;=\; \frac{1}{1-z} \;=\; \frac{1}{(1-a)-(z-a)} \;=\; \frac{1}{1-a}\sum_{n\ge 0}\left(\frac{z-a}{1-a}\right)^{\!n},$$

a new germ centered at $a$ with radius $|1-a|$. As $a$ moves toward the boundary point $z=1$ the radius shrinks to zero; as $a$ moves away from $1$, the new disk can escape the original $D(0,1)$. Chaining these re-expansions covers all of $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{1\}$ with local power series — and by the identity theorem every such chain recovers the same function $1/(1-z)$.

2. Continuation along a path — disk-chaining

Given a holomorphic germ at $a$ and a path $\gamma\colon [0,1]\to\mathbb{C}$ with $\gamma(0)=a$, Weierstrass's procedure produces a continuation along $\gamma$ — whenever one exists — by disk-chaining:

  1. Start with the germ $(f_0, D_0=D(a,R_0))$.
  2. Pick $t_1 > 0$ so that $\gamma(t_1)\in D_0$. Re-expand $f_0$ at $\gamma(t_1)$ to get $(f_1, D_1)$, where $D_1=D(\gamma(t_1), R_1)$ with $R_1 \ge R_0 - |\gamma(t_1)-a|$.
  3. Iterate, choosing $t_2 < t_3 < \dots$ so each $\gamma(t_k)\in D_{k-1}$.
  4. Stop when $\gamma$ is covered, or the radii shrink to $0$ (obstruction — typically a singularity on the path).

The key point from the identity theorem: on the overlap $D_{k-1}\cap D_k$, the functions $f_{k-1}$ and $f_k$ agree (they come from the same function). The chain defines one coherent function along $\gamma$.

Two different subdivisions of the same path produce the same final germ — but two different paths with the same endpoints may not. That's the subtlety Section 3 resolves.

3. Monodromy around a branch point

Two paths from $p$ to $q$ through a region where continuation works may produce different germs at $q$ if the region is not simply connected. This is the phenomenon of monodromy. The obstructing singularities are called branch points.

The basic example is $\log z$ on $\mathbb{C}^*$. Start at $z_0=1$ with the principal value $\log 1 = 0$. Along any path $\gamma\colon[0,1]\to\mathbb{C}^*$, continuation gives $$\log\gamma(t) \;=\; \int_0^t \frac{\gamma'(s)}{\gamma(s)}\,ds,$$ which is the argument variation plus a real part. Around a loop that winds $n$ times around $0$, the imaginary part picks up $2\pi i n$. The "branches" of $\log$ differ by this additive constant.

Monodromy theorem. Let $U$ be a simply connected open set, $p\in U$. If a holomorphic germ at $p$ continues along every path in $U$, then the continuation is independent of the path and defines a single-valued holomorphic function on $U$.

Proof sketch: two paths with the same endpoints are homotopic through paths; continuation is locally constant in the path (a standard compactness argument), hence constant on homotopy classes; in a simply connected space there is only one class.

On a non-simply-connected $U$, the continuations are indexed by $\pi_1(U)$: each homotopy class of loops acts on the stalk at $p$ by a monodromy representation. For $U=\mathbb{C}^*$, $\pi_1=\mathbb{Z}$, and $\log$'s monodromy is $\mathbb{Z}\to(\mathbb{C},+)$, $n\mapsto 2\pi i n$. For $\sqrt z$ it's $\mathbb{Z}\to\{\pm 1\}$ with generator $\mapsto -1$, a two-sheeted cover.

4. Schwarz reflection principle

Schwarz reflection is a targeted analytic continuation across a real-analytic boundary. Its prototype:

Schwarz reflection (real-axis case). Let $U^+$ be an open subset of the upper half plane whose boundary contains an open interval $I\subset\mathbb{R}$. Suppose $f\colon U^+\to\mathbb{C}$ is holomorphic, extends continuously to $U^+\cup I$, and takes real values on $I$. Then the formula $$\tilde f(z) \;=\; \overline{f(\bar z)},\qquad z\in U^- := \{\bar z : z\in U^+\},$$ defines a holomorphic extension of $f$ to $U^+\cup I\cup U^-$.

The formula is forced: on $I$ both sides equal $f$ (because $f$ is real there); the map $z\mapsto \overline{f(\bar z)}$ is holomorphic because $z\mapsto\bar z$ and $w\mapsto\bar w$ are each antiholomorphic, and two antiholomorphic maps compose to a holomorphic one. Morera plus continuity across $I$ does the gluing.

Variants replace the straight boundary arc with any real-analytic arc (line, circle, more generally a real-analytic curve) — and replace "real values" with "values on another real-analytic arc." The reflection is through the unique antiholomorphic involution that fixes the boundary arc.

An application: boundary regularity

Reflection turns boundary regularity into interior regularity. If $f$ is a conformal map of $D$ onto a Jordan domain $\Omega$ and the boundary of $\Omega$ is real-analytic, then $f$ extends holomorphically across $\partial D$. Riemann surfaces are often glued out of half-disks by precisely this move.

5. Natural boundaries — lacunary series

Not every holomorphic function on a domain can be continued beyond it. Sometimes the boundary is natural: every boundary point is a singularity, and no germ on the inside extends through any arc of the boundary.

Ostrowski's gap theorem (flavor). If a power series $\sum c_n z^n$ has radius $1$ and its nonzero coefficients occur only on indices $n_k$ with $n_{k+1}/n_k \ge 1+\delta > 1$, the unit circle is a natural boundary.

The cleanest example is the theta-like lacunary series $$F(z) \;=\; \sum_{n=0}^\infty z^{n^2} \;=\; 1+z+z^4+z^9+z^{16}+\cdots$$

On $|z|<1$ the series converges absolutely, defining a holomorphic $F$. Near any root of unity $\zeta = e^{2\pi i p/q}$ on the boundary, one shows $|F(r\zeta)|\to\infty$ as $r\to 1^-$ (using the identity $F(z)^2 \approx $ number-theoretic counting of $n_1^2+n_2^2$). Roots of unity are dense in the unit circle, so $F$ cannot be continued across any boundary arc — each would contain infinitely many singular points.

This is typical: lacunary series produce functions with the unit disk as a "private universe." Another famous example: $\eta(\tau) = q^{1/24}\prod(1-q^n)$ as a function of $q=e^{2\pi i\tau}$ has the unit circle as a natural boundary (from the $q$-side).

6. The Riemann zeta functional equation

The flagship example of analytic continuation is Riemann's 1859 memoir, where he produces a meromorphic extension of $$\zeta(s) \;=\; \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s}\quad(\mathrm{Re}s>1)$$ to all of $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{1\}$ and discovers its symmetry $s\leftrightarrow 1-s$. The mechanism is a Mellin transform against the Jacobi theta function $$\theta(x) \;=\; \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} e^{-\pi n^2 x} \;=\; 1 + 2\sum_{n\ge 1}e^{-\pi n^2 x}\quad(x>0).$$

Step 1 — from Dirichlet series to Mellin integral

Start with the gamma integral: for $\mathrm{Re}s>0$, $$\Gamma(s/2)\,n^{-s}\pi^{-s/2} \;=\; \int_0^\infty e^{-\pi n^2 x}\,x^{s/2 - 1}\,dx.$$ Summing over $n\ge 1$ and interchanging (justified for $\mathrm{Re}s>1$), $$\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s) \;=\; \int_0^\infty \bigl(\tfrac{\theta(x)-1}{2}\bigr)\,x^{s/2 - 1}\,dx.$$ This is the Mellin transform of the "nontrivial" part $\tfrac{\theta(x)-1}{2} = \sum_{n\ge 1}e^{-\pi n^2 x}$.

Step 2 — split at $x=1$ and apply theta's functional equation

Poisson summation gives the theta transformation law: $$\theta(1/x) \;=\; \sqrt{x}\,\theta(x).$$ Split the integral at $x=1$: $\int_0^\infty = \int_0^1 + \int_1^\infty$. On $\int_0^1$, change variables $x\mapsto 1/x$ and apply the transformation. After the dust settles (an exercise in careful bookkeeping), $$\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s) \;=\; -\frac{1}{s(1-s)} + \int_1^\infty \bigl(\tfrac{\theta(x)-1}{2}\bigr)\bigl(x^{s/2 - 1} + x^{(1-s)/2 - 1}\bigr)\,dx.$$

Step 3 — the completed zeta is symmetric

The integral on the right converges for all $s\in\mathbb{C}$ (because $\theta(x)-1 = 2e^{-\pi x}+\cdots$ decays super-exponentially at $\infty$), and it is manifestly invariant under $s\mapsto 1-s$. Defining $$\xi(s) \;:=\; \tfrac12 s(s-1)\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s),$$ the $1/s(1-s)$ pole is cancelled and we obtain Riemann's functional equation:

Riemann's functional equation. $\xi$ extends to an entire function of $s$, and $$\boxed{\;\xi(s) \;=\; \xi(1-s)\;}$$ for all $s\in\mathbb{C}$. Equivalently, $$\zeta(s) \;=\; 2^s\pi^{s-1}\sin\!\tfrac{\pi s}{2}\,\Gamma(1-s)\,\zeta(1-s).$$ Consequences: $\zeta$ has a simple pole at $s=1$ (with residue $1$), "trivial" zeros at $s=-2,-4,-6,\dots$ (from $\sin(\pi s/2)$), and a critical strip $0<\mathrm{Re}s<1$ symmetric about $\mathrm{Re}s=1/2$ containing all nontrivial zeros.

The two pillars — Mellin continuation and theta's modularity — are exactly the two cross-links on this page: the Mellin transform ships in from Dirichlet series, and the theta transformation from theta functions. Analytic continuation is the glue that turns a divergent-looking series into a global, symmetric object.

7. Coda — where to next

Analytic continuation is the organizing principle behind three different halves of this notebook:

  • Complex analysis — the identity theorem, Laurent series, and branch points set the stage. Read complex-analysis for the machinery of amplitwists, contour integrals, and residues that makes everything on this page rigorous.
  • Riemann surfaces — the correct universe for multi-valued functions. The universal cover of $\mathbb{C}^*$ resolves $\log$ into a single-valued function on a spiral; $\sqrt z$ lives on a two-sheeted cover. Monodromy becomes deck-transformation on the cover. See riemann-surfaces.
  • Dirichlet series & $L$-functions — $\zeta$ is the first example; the same Mellin/theta trick continues Dirichlet $L$-functions, Hecke $L$-functions, and modular $L$-functions. Each has its own functional equation relating $s$ to $k-s$ for a completion level $k$. See Dirichlet series and L-functions.

The $\xi(s)=\xi(1-s)$ symmetry is the prototype for a wall of conjectural and proven functional equations covering every automorphic $L$-function. Langlands' program asserts that they all arise the same way: from a Mellin pairing with an automorphic form, whose transformation law is the grandchild of Poisson summation. Analytic continuation is not a trick — it is the organizing mechanism of modern number theory.

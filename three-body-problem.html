The three-body problem

Three masses, gravity, and the ten classical integrals — and the surprise that what's left is enough to invent dynamical systems, KAM theory, and modern celestial mechanics.

1. Newton's equations and conserved quantities

Three point masses $m_1,m_2,m_3$ at positions $r_1,r_2,r_3\in\mathbb{R}^3$, interacting only by gravity, satisfy $$\ddot r_i \;=\; G\sum_{j\ne i} m_j\,\frac{r_j - r_i}{|r_j-r_i|^3}\,, \qquad i=1,2,3.$$ Six second-order ODEs in three dimensions: $18$-dimensional phase space. Newton (1687) wrote down the equations; the next two centuries were a search for explicit solutions, and the next century after that was a forced retreat from that search.

The system is Hamiltonian with $H = \sum_i \tfrac{|p_i|^2}{2m_i} - G\sum_{ino further algebraic integrals exist — there is no closed-form solution.

2. Restricted three-body & Lagrange points

Drop the third mass to zero. The two primaries $m_1,m_2$ now decouple — they trace a Keplerian ellipse — and the test particle obeys

$$\ddot r \;=\; -G\,\frac{m_1(r-r_1(t))}{|r-r_1(t)|^3} \;-\; G\,\frac{m_2(r-r_2(t))}{|r-r_2(t)|^3}.$$

In the circular restricted case, the primaries orbit at fixed separation; passing to a frame co-rotating at angular velocity $\Omega$ pins them at constant positions and gives a time-independent effective potential

$$U_{\mathrm{eff}}(x,y) \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2}\Omega^2(x^2+y^2) \;-\; \frac{Gm_1}{r_1} \;-\; \frac{Gm_2}{r_2}.$$

Critical points $\nabla U_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0$ are equilibria of the full rotating-frame dynamics (the Coriolis force vanishes at zero velocity). They form Lagrange's five points: three collinear $L_1, L_2, L_3$ on the $m_1$–$m_2$ axis (saddles for every mass ratio), and two equilateral $L_4, L_5$ at the apexes of equilateral triangles on the two sides of that axis.

Routh (1875): $L_4$ and $L_5$ are linearly stable (centres) when $\frac{m_1}{m_2} > \frac{25 + 3\sqrt{69}}{2} \approx 24.96$. The Sun–Jupiter ratio is $\approx 1047$, so the equilateral wells trap the Trojans. Earth–Moon is $\approx 81$, also stable; Sun–Earth is $\approx 333{,}000$, comfortably so.

3. Lagrange, Euler, and the figure-eight

The full three-body problem, away from the restricted limit, admits only a thin family of explicit solutions. Three classes of relative equilibria — solutions that look stationary in some rotating frame — were known before 1900:

  • Lagrange (1772) — equilateral. Place the three masses at the vertices of an equilateral triangle with side $a$. Choose velocities so the triangle rigidly rotates about the common centre of mass. The configuration stays equilateral forever; in the planar case it can also expand and contract along a Keplerian similarity (homographic motions).
  • Euler (1767) — collinear. Three masses on a rotating line, with separations in fixed ratios determined by a quintic in $m_1,m_2,m_3$. Three orderings of masses give three Euler families.
  • Chenciner–Montgomery (2000) — figure-eight. For three equal masses, there exists a planar orbit on which all three bodies trace a single curve in the shape of a lemniscate, chasing each other with equal time-spacing. Discovered numerically by Moore (1993), proved rigorously by Chenciner and Montgomery via an action minimisation on a symmetry-constrained loop space.

All three are choreographies in a generalised sense: every mass follows a translate (or the same copy) of one curve. The figure-eight is the only currently known choreography of three equal masses on a single curve in the plane that is not a relative equilibrium.

4. Poincaré's tangle and the birth of chaos

King Oscar II of Sweden offered a prize in 1885 for an explicit series solution to the $n$-body problem. Poincaré won in 1889 with a memoir on the restricted three-body problem — and then, while preparing the published version, he discovered an error. His correction was deeper than the original work: stable and unstable manifolds of a hyperbolic periodic orbit can intersect transversally, and once they do, they must do so infinitely often.

Poincaré's own description (1899): "When one tries to depict the figure formed by these two curves and their infinitely many intersections... one is struck by the complexity of this figure, which I shall not even attempt to draw." The picture — the homoclinic tangle — is the geometric heart of chaos.

Smale (1965) extracted a topological model: take a unit square, stretch it horizontally by a factor $\lambda > 2$, compress it vertically by $\lambda^{-1}$, fold it into a horseshoe shape, and lay it back inside the original square. The invariant set $\Lambda = \bigcap_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} f^n(Q)$ is a Cantor-like set on which the dynamics is conjugate to the full shift $\sigma$ on $\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{Z}}$:

$$\bigl(\Lambda, f\bigr) \;\cong\; \bigl(\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{Z}},\, \sigma\bigr).$$

Consequences: countably many periodic orbits of every period, a dense orbit, sensitive dependence on initial conditions, positive topological entropy. Wherever a homoclinic tangle exists, a horseshoe is embedded — and so is chaos.

5. KAM and the stability of the solar system

Poincaré's tangle is a worst-case scenario: chaos is generic. But planetary orbits have been quasi-stable for billions of years — so what saves them?

The answer is the Kolmogorov–Arnold–Moser theorem. Start with an integrable Hamiltonian $H_0(I)$ on action-angle variables $(I,\theta)\in\mathbb{R}^n\times\mathbb{T}^n$ — the phase space is foliated by invariant tori $\{I = \mathrm{const}\}$, on each of which the flow is linear with frequency $\omega(I) = \partial H_0/\partial I$. Perturb:

$$H(I,\theta) \;=\; H_0(I) \;+\; \varepsilon\,H_1(I,\theta).$$

Naively, every torus could break up under arbitrarily small $\varepsilon$. KAM (Kolmogorov 1954, Arnold 1963, Moser 1962) shows otherwise: most tori survive, deformed but topologically intact. The survivors are exactly those whose frequencies $\omega$ are Diophantine: there exist $\gamma > 0,\ \tau > n-1$ with

$$|k\cdot\omega| \;\ge\; \frac{\gamma}{|k|^\tau} \quad\text{for all }0\ne k\in\mathbb{Z}^n.$$

The set of Diophantine $\omega$ has full Lebesgue measure as $\gamma\to 0$, and the proof uses a Newton-style super-convergent iteration to defeat the small-denominator divergences $1/(k\cdot\omega)$ that doomed the classical perturbation series.

Resonant tori (where $k\cdot\omega = 0$ for some $k$) generically dissolve into Birkhoff zones of alternating elliptic and hyperbolic islands. Between the surviving KAM tori, chaos can leak through (Arnold diffusion); but for $n=2$ degrees of freedom, KAM tori topologically enclose the chaotic regions and bound them forever.

6. Spacecraft, Trojans, and exoplanet stability

Three-body dynamics is not academic. The Lagrange points are real estate; the homoclinic web is a transit network; KAM bounds tell us which exoplanet architectures can persist.

The interplanetary transport network, formalised by Koon, Lo, Marsden, and Ross around 2000, exploits the same homoclinic tangle Poincaré described to design near-zero-fuel transfers between planets. Trajectories ride invariant manifolds of $L_1$/$L_2$ Lyapunov orbits; intersections of stable and unstable manifolds in different three-body subsystems splice into a multi-planet itinerary.

For exoplanets, KAM and resonance overlap criteria (Wisdom, Murray–Holman) bound which multi-planet architectures are dynamically stable on Gyr timescales. Mean-motion resonances (e.g. Trappist-1's $8\!:\!5\!:\!3\!:\!2\!:\!3\!:\!4\!:\!3$ Laplace chain) sit on the boundary between order and chaos — protected by KAM tori on one side, eaten by Arnold diffusion on the other.

Halo orbits at $L_1$/$L_2$ are 3D periodic orbits in the rotating frame; tadpole orbits at $L_4$ are large-amplitude librations. Real spacecraft halo orbits (e.g. JWST's around Sun–Earth $L_2$) have amplitudes $\sim 10^5$ km — small fractions of the Earth–Sun distance.

Connections

The three-body problem is unsolved in a precise sense: no algebraic first integrals beyond the classical ten exist (Bruns, Poincaré), and Sundman's 1912 convergent series — though formally giving a global solution — converges so slowly that no useful number ever emerges. The questions that remain open are surprising: are all bounded planar solutions either Lagrangian, Euler, or chaotic? do new choreographies with more bodies exist generically? how thick is Arnold diffusion in the real solar system, and can it explain Mercury's marginal long-term stability?

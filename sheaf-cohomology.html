Sheaf cohomology — obstructions to gluing globally

When local sections exist but no global section ties them together, the obstruction lives in a cohomology group. We compute $H^\bullet(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(d))$ by hand, identify $\mathrm{Pic}(X) = H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^\times)$, and glance toward étale cohomology.

1. Why cohomology?

In sheaves.html §7 we met sheafification: the constant presheaf $\underline{S}^{\mathrm{pre}}$ on a disconnected space is not a sheaf, and forcing the gluing axiom replaces it by the constant sheaf $\underline{S}$. But sheafification only fixes defects visible on a single cover; deeper obstructions show up when you ask whether stalkwise-surjective maps are globally surjective. Those obstructions are measured by $H^i(X, \mathcal{F})$ for $i \geq 1$.

Informally, sheaf cohomology assigns to every sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$ a tower of abelian groups $$H^0(X,\mathcal{F}) \;,\; H^1(X,\mathcal{F}) \;,\; H^2(X,\mathcal{F}) \;,\; \ldots$$ with $H^0(X,\mathcal{F}) = \Gamma(X,\mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F}(X)$ the abelian group of global sections, and $H^i$ for $i>0$ detecting "global twists" that are invisible stalk-by-stalk. Three properties pin the theory down: $H^0$ is global sections, $H^i$ is a functor in $\mathcal{F}$, and every short exact sequence of sheaves produces a long exact sequence in cohomology (§6). Any construction with these three properties is forced to agree on "nice" sheaves and "nice" spaces.

The archetype: the exponential sequence

Let $X$ be a complex manifold. On $X$ there is a short exact sequence of sheaves of abelian groups $$0 \to \underline{\mathbb{Z}} \xrightarrow{\;\cdot 2\pi i\;} \mathcal{O}_X \xrightarrow{\;\exp\;} \mathcal{O}_X^\times \to 0.$$ The map $\exp$ is surjective as a map of sheaves: any nonvanishing holomorphic function $g$ has a local holomorphic logarithm on a small enough disk. But on a space like $X = \mathbb{C}^\times$ it is not surjective on global sections: the function $g(z) = z$ has no global holomorphic logarithm. The obstruction is an integer — the winding number — and it is exactly the image of $z$ in $H^1(\mathbb{C}^\times, \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}$ under the connecting map $\delta\colon H^0(\mathcal{O}^\times) \to H^1(\underline{\mathbb{Z}})$.

So: sheaf cohomology is the bookkeeping device that turns "locally yes, globally no" into an explicit element of an abelian group. $H^1(X,\underline{\mathbb{Z}})$ counts integer-valued winding obstructions; $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^\times)$ classifies holomorphic line bundles (§8); $H^1(X, \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathcal{O}))$ classifies rank-$n$ vector bundles (nonabelian). Each is telling you that transition functions on overlaps cannot always be trivialized.

Two equivalent ways to produce this theory:

  1. Čech cohomology (§2, §3) — pick a cover $\mathcal{U}$; assemble an explicit chain complex out of sections over intersections. This is the hands-on approach; you can actually compute with it.
  2. Derived functor cohomology (§4) — the global-sections functor $\Gamma(X, -)$ is left-exact but not right-exact; its right derived functors $R^i\Gamma$ are what you want. This is the conceptual approach, and it makes the long exact sequence automatic.

For reasonable $\mathcal{F}$ and a good enough cover, the two agree (§5). That is the engine of essentially every concrete computation below.

A running motto

$H^0$ is about "what is global." $H^1$ is about "how many ways can you twist on overlaps." $H^2$ is about "how many ways can two-overlap twists themselves be twisted on triple overlaps." And so on, up the simplicial ladder of a cover.

2. Čech cohomology

Fix a sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ of abelian groups on $X$, and an open cover $\mathcal{U} = \{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ of $X$ indexed by a totally ordered set $A$. Write $U_{\alpha_0 \cdots \alpha_p} = U_{\alpha_0} \cap \cdots \cap U_{\alpha_p}$.

Definition. The Čech $p$-cochains with values in $\mathcal{F}$ are $$\check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) \;=\; \prod_{\alpha_0 < \alpha_1 < \cdots < \alpha_p} \mathcal{F}(U_{\alpha_0 \cdots \alpha_p}).$$ A $p$-cochain $\sigma$ is a section $\sigma_{\alpha_0\cdots\alpha_p} \in \mathcal{F}(U_{\alpha_0\cdots\alpha_p})$ for every strictly increasing tuple. The Čech differential $d\colon \check{C}^p \to \check{C}^{p+1}$ is the alternating-restriction $$(d\sigma)_{\alpha_0 \cdots \alpha_{p+1}} \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{p+1} (-1)^i\, \sigma_{\alpha_0 \cdots \widehat{\alpha_i} \cdots \alpha_{p+1}}\big|_{U_{\alpha_0\cdots\alpha_{p+1}}}.$$ A direct computation shows $d^2 = 0$, so $(\check{C}^\bullet(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}), d)$ is a cochain complex. Its cohomology is the Čech cohomology of $\mathcal{F}$ with respect to $\mathcal{U}$, written $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$.

Concretely:

  • Degree 0. A $0$-cochain is a family $\{s_\alpha \in \mathcal{F}(U_\alpha)\}$ of local sections. The differential is $(d s)_{\alpha \beta} = s_\beta|_{U_{\alpha\beta}} - s_\alpha|_{U_{\alpha\beta}}$. A $0$-cocycle is a collection of sections that agree on overlaps — i.e., a global section of $\mathcal{F}$. So $\check{H}^0(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F}(X)$.
  • Degree 1. A $1$-cochain is a family $\{t_{\alpha\beta} \in \mathcal{F}(U_{\alpha\beta})\}$. It is a $1$-cocycle when $t_{\beta\gamma} - t_{\alpha\gamma} + t_{\alpha\beta} = 0$ on $U_{\alpha\beta\gamma}$ (the cocycle condition). It is a coboundary when $t_{\alpha\beta} = s_\beta - s_\alpha$ for some $0$-cochain $s$ (i.e., when the twist can be absorbed into local trivializations).
  • Higher degrees. The same pattern: you record discrepancies on $p$-fold overlaps modulo discrepancies that come from lower-degree data.

Refinement and the Čech limit

$\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$ depends on $\mathcal{U}$. If $\mathcal{V}$ refines $\mathcal{U}$ there is a canonical map $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) \to \check{H}^p(\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{F})$, and we define $$\check{H}^p(X, \mathcal{F}) = \varinjlim_{\mathcal{U}} \check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$$ over open covers ordered by refinement. For paracompact Hausdorff $X$ this agrees with derived-functor cohomology (§5). For a single "good" cover, often you don't need to pass to the limit — see Leray (§5).

The nerve of a cover

The Čech complex has a direct combinatorial meaning. The nerve $N(\mathcal{U})$ of a cover is the simplicial complex whose $p$-simplices are $(p+1)$-element subsets $\{\alpha_0 < \cdots < \alpha_p\}$ with $U_{\alpha_0 \cdots \alpha_p} \neq \emptyset$. The constant sheaf $\underline{A}$ assigns $A$ to each simplex, and Čech cohomology is just simplicial cohomology of $N(\mathcal{U})$ with coefficients in $A$. For a general sheaf $\mathcal{F}$, replace the constant assignment by "$\mathcal{F}$ of the intersection" — a twisted coefficient system on the nerve. For a good cover in the topological sense, $N(\mathcal{U}) \simeq X$ homotopically (nerve theorem), which is why Čech of a good cover recovers singular cohomology of $X$.

Nonabelian Čech $H^1$

If $\mathcal{G}$ is a sheaf of possibly-nonabelian groups, the cocycle condition $g_{\beta\gamma} g_{\alpha\gamma}^{-1} g_{\alpha\beta} = e$ still makes sense (in multiplicative notation), with $g_{\alpha\beta} \sim h_\beta^{-1} g_{\alpha\beta} h_\alpha$ the equivalence from $0$-cochains. The pointed set $\check{H}^1(X, \mathcal{G})$ then classifies $\mathcal{G}$-torsors. For $\mathcal{G} = \mathcal{O}_X^\times$ abelian, this is the Picard group (§8). For $\mathcal{G} = \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathcal{O}_X)$ nonabelian, this classifies rank-$n$ vector bundles.

You can see the whole pattern in one picture: the Čech complex is built from the nerve of the cover (a simplicial complex whose $p$-simplices are $(p+1)$-fold intersections), and taking cohomology of this simplicial complex with coefficients in $\mathcal{F}$ is what you are doing. For the constant sheaf on a paracompact space, Čech cohomology reproduces ordinary singular cohomology of $X$.

Alternating vs. ordered cochains

Two conventions circulate. The ordered Čech complex uses products over strictly increasing tuples (as above). The alternating Čech complex uses all tuples but insists sections alternate under permutation and vanish on repeats. The two produce the same cohomology — you can check by a quick sign-averaging argument. Stacks Project uses the ordered version; Hartshorne uses alternating; no practical difference.

Functoriality

A morphism of sheaves $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ induces $\check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) \to \check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{G})$ by postcomposing each section. This commutes with $d$ and descends to $\check{H}^p$. A refinement of covers $\mathcal{V} \prec \mathcal{U}$ and a choice of refinement map $\tau\colon \mathrm{Ind}(\mathcal{V}) \to \mathrm{Ind}(\mathcal{U})$ induces $\check{C}^p(\mathcal{U},\mathcal{F}) \to \check{C}^p(\mathcal{V},\mathcal{F})$ by restricting sections; different choices of $\tau$ are chain-homotopic, so the map on $\check{H}^p$ is canonical. These pieces assemble into a bifunctor $\check{H}^p(-, -)$ in the cover and the sheaf separately.

3. Čech for simple spaces

Two worked computations: the circle $S^1$ with the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ (a purely topological calculation), and the projective line $\mathbb{P}^1$ with $\mathcal{O}(n)$ (the first nontrivial algebraic calculation).

The circle $S^1$ with a 2-open cover

Take $X = S^1 \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ and cover it by two overlapping arcs $U_0$ (upper arc, slightly past each pole) and $U_1$ (lower arc). Each $U_i$ is contractible; the overlap $U_0 \cap U_1$ has two connected components (a left strip and a right strip).

For the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$, sections over a connected contractible open are $\mathbb{Z}$, and sections over a space with $k$ components are $\mathbb{Z}^k$. So $$\check{C}^0(\mathcal{U}, \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \underline{\mathbb{Z}}(U_0) \oplus \underline{\mathbb{Z}}(U_1) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}, \quad \check{C}^1 = \underline{\mathbb{Z}}(U_{01}) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}.$$ The differential $d\colon \mathbb{Z}^2 \to \mathbb{Z}^2$ sends $(a, b) \mapsto (b - a, b - a)$: on each component of $U_{01}$ the difference is the same. So $d$ has matrix $\left(\begin{smallmatrix}-1 & 1\\-1&1\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, rank $1$.

Kernel: $a = b$, so $\ker d \cong \mathbb{Z}$. Image: the diagonal $\{(n,n)\} \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$. Cokernel: $\mathbb{Z}^2/\mathrm{diag} \cong \mathbb{Z}$. Therefore $$\check{H}^0(S^1, \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}, \qquad \check{H}^1(S^1, \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}.$$ The generator of $H^1$ is the cocycle $(+1, 0)$: "mismatch by $+1$ on the right overlap, by $0$ on the left." It cannot be written as $(s_1 - s_0, s_1 - s_0)$ because the two components force a single integer — but the cocycle has two different integers, so the best you can do is cohomologically nontrivial. Geometrically this is the winding-number class of the identity map $S^1 \to S^1$.

The projective line $\mathbb{P}^1$ with the standard affine cover

Take $\mathbb{P}^1 = \mathrm{Proj}\,k[X_0, X_1]$. The standard cover is $\mathcal{U} = \{U_0, U_1\}$ where $U_i = D_+(X_i) \cong \mathbb{A}^1$. On $U_0$ the coordinate is $x = X_1/X_0$; on $U_1$ the coordinate is $y = X_0/X_1 = 1/x$; the overlap $U_0 \cap U_1 \cong \mathbb{G}_m$ has coordinate ring $k[x, x^{-1}]$.

For the structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}$: $$\mathcal{O}(U_0) = k[x], \quad \mathcal{O}(U_1) = k[y] = k[x^{-1}], \quad \mathcal{O}(U_0 \cap U_1) = k[x, x^{-1}].$$ Čech: $\check{C}^0 = k[x] \oplus k[x^{-1}]$, $\check{C}^1 = k[x, x^{-1}]$, $d(f, g)(x) = g(1/x) - f(x) = g - f$ in $k[x, x^{-1}]$. The image is $\{g - f : f \in k[x], g \in k[x^{-1}]\}$, which is all of $k[x, x^{-1}]$ (any Laurent polynomial is a difference of its nonnegative and negative parts). So $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1, \mathcal{O}) = 0$. The kernel is $\{(f, g) : f(x) = g(1/x) \text{ in } k[x, x^{-1}]\}$, forcing $f, g$ both constant: $H^0 = k$, a single global constant. Good, $\mathbb{P}^1$ is connected and proper, so global regular functions are constants.

For the twisted sheaf $\mathcal{O}(n)$ we need to track the twist. Sections of $\mathcal{O}(n)$ on $U_0$ are "$\deg n$ in homogeneous notation" — in the $U_0$ chart where we've dehomogenized by dividing by $X_0^n$, they are simply $k[x]$. On $U_1$ sections look like $\deg n$ in homogeneous coords divided by $X_1^n$, giving $k[y]$. On the overlap, transitioning from $U_0$ to $U_1$ divides by $(X_1/X_0)^n = x^n$, so the gluing map sends $f(x) \in k[x]$ to $x^n f(x) \in k[x, x^{-1}]$ and $g(y) \in k[y]$ to $g(1/x) = x^0 \cdot g(x^{-1}) \in k[x, x^{-1}]$. Let's write this cleanly by embedding both into $k[x, x^{-1}]$ and taking the differential there:

$\check{C}^0(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{O}(n)) \;=\; \underbrace{\{\text{polynomials in } x\}}_{=k[x]} \;\oplus\; \underbrace{x^n \cdot \{\text{polynomials in } x^{-1}\}}_{= x^n k[x^{-1}]}$ $\;\;\xrightarrow{\;d\;}\;\; \check{C}^1 = k[x, x^{-1}].$

with $d(f, g) = g - f$ as Laurent polynomials. Kernel: $f = g$, so both sides are polynomials in $x$ and lie in $x^n k[x^{-1}]$ — i.e., degree between $0$ and $n$. $\dim_k \ker d = \max(n+1, 0)$. Image: the span of $\{x^k : k \leq -1\} \cup \{x^k : k \leq n\}$ as differences, which is $x^{-1} k[x^{-1}] + x^n k[x^{-1}] = \{x^k : k \leq n\}$ when $n \geq 0$, and is all of $\{x^k : k \leq n\}$ in general. Cokernel: $\{x^k : -n < k < 0\}$ when $n \leq -2$, dimension $|n| - 1 = -n - 1$; zero for $n \geq -1$.

Result. $\dim_k H^0(\mathbb{P}^1, \mathcal{O}(n)) = \max(n+1, 0)$, $\dim_k H^1(\mathbb{P}^1, \mathcal{O}(n)) = \max(-n-1, 0)$. Note the Serre duality symmetry $n \leftrightarrow -n - 2$: $H^0$ at $n$ has the same dimension as $H^1$ at $-n - 2$, since $\omega_{\mathbb{P}^1} = \mathcal{O}(-2)$.

The widget of §7 lets you set $n$ and see this complex explicitly, including the cocycle representatives.

Aside: sections of $\mathcal{O}(n)$ as homogeneous polynomials

The cleanest formulation — matching the $\mathrm{Proj}$ construction of $\mathbb{P}^1$ — is that $H^0(\mathbb{P}^1, \mathcal{O}(n))$ is the degree-$n$ piece of $k[X_0, X_1]$. For $n \geq 0$ this is $\mathrm{Sym}^n(k^2)$ with basis $\{X_0^n, X_0^{n-1} X_1, \ldots, X_1^n\}$, dimension $n + 1$. Dehomogenizing to the $U_0$ chart with $x = X_1/X_0$ turns this basis into $\{1, x, \ldots, x^n\}$; on the $U_1$ chart with $y = X_0/X_1$ it becomes $\{y^n, y^{n-1}, \ldots, 1\}$. The transition $y = 1/x$ times the cocycle $x^n$ is exactly the Čech differential we wrote.

For $n = -2$, the $H^1$ generator $x^{-1}$ on the overlap has a lovely geometric meaning: it is the Čech class of the rational $1$-form $dx/x$ on $\mathbb{P}^1$, via the identification $\omega_{\mathbb{P}^1} = \mathcal{O}(-2)$. Under Serre duality it pairs with the unique global section $1 \in H^0(\mathcal{O})$ — the trace map $H^1(\omega) \to k$, central to the formalism.

4. Derived functor cohomology

Čech cohomology depends on a cover, and the Čech-limit definition is clumsy to reason with abstractly. A cleaner theory declares $H^i(X, \mathcal{F}) = R^i\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$, the right derived functors of the global-sections functor. This requires the background of homological algebra — injective resolutions, derived functors, the long exact sequence.

Right derived functors, refresher

The global-sections functor $\Gamma(X, -)\colon \mathsf{Sh}(X) \to \mathsf{Ab}$ is left exact: if $0 \to \mathcal{F}' \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}''$ is exact, so is $0 \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}') \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}'')$. But it is not right exact — that is precisely the point of §1's exponential example, where $\Gamma(\exp)\colon \Gamma(\mathcal{O}) \to \Gamma(\mathcal{O}^\times)$ is generally not surjective. The failure is measured by $R^i\Gamma$.

To compute $R^i\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$:

  1. Choose an injective resolution $\mathcal{F} \hookrightarrow \mathcal{I}^0 \to \mathcal{I}^1 \to \mathcal{I}^2 \to \cdots$ (an exact sequence with each $\mathcal{I}^i$ injective as a sheaf).
  2. Apply $\Gamma(X, -)$ to get a complex $0 \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{I}^0) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{I}^1) \to \cdots$ (no longer exact).
  3. Take its cohomology: $H^i(X, \mathcal{F}) := H^i(\Gamma(X, \mathcal{I}^\bullet))$.

Classical fact: the category $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$ has enough injectives, and any two injective resolutions of $\mathcal{F}$ are homotopy-equivalent after applying $\Gamma$, so $H^i(X, \mathcal{F})$ is well-defined.

Godement: the canonical injective resolution

Injective sheaves look abstract, but there is a concrete construction that always works. Given $\mathcal{F}$, define the Godement sheaf $$\mathcal{G}(\mathcal{F})(U) \;=\; \prod_{x \in U} \mathcal{F}_x$$ (arbitrary stalk-valued "sections," no continuity required). This is flabby ( = flasque: every restriction $\mathcal{G}(\mathcal{F})(U) \to \mathcal{G}(\mathcal{F})(V)$ is surjective) and contains $\mathcal{F}$ as a subsheaf. Iterating $\mathcal{G}$ on the cokernel gives a canonical Godement resolution $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}^\bullet(\mathcal{F})$ by flabby sheaves.

Acyclic objects. A sheaf $\mathcal{A}$ is called $\Gamma$-acyclic if $H^i(X, \mathcal{A}) = 0$ for $i > 0$. Injective sheaves are acyclic; more generally, flabby sheaves are acyclic, and so are soft sheaves on paracompact spaces, and fine sheaves (those admitting partitions of unity). Any resolution by acyclic sheaves computes $H^i(X, \mathcal{F})$: just take cohomology after applying $\Gamma$. This is cheaper than finding injective resolutions.

Why derived functors?

Three payoffs:

  • Long exact sequence for free. Any short exact sequence of sheaves produces a long exact sequence in $R^i\Gamma$ — the general $\delta$-functor property (§6).
  • Base independence. The answer doesn't depend on a chosen cover; it's intrinsic to the sheaf.
  • Functoriality. Morphisms $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ induce $H^i(X, \mathcal{F}) \to H^i(X, \mathcal{G})$; continuous maps $f\colon X \to Y$ induce $f_*$ and $f^*$ with spectral-sequence relations (Leray spectral sequence).

The price is abstraction. For actual computation we fall back to Čech, via the comparison theorem.

The Leray spectral sequence

For a continuous $f\colon X \to Y$ and a sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, there is a convergent spectral sequence $$E_2^{p,q} \;=\; H^p(Y, R^q f_* \mathcal{F}) \;\Longrightarrow\; H^{p+q}(X, \mathcal{F}).$$ When $f$ is an affine morphism of schemes and $\mathcal{F}$ is quasi-coherent, $R^q f_* \mathcal{F} = 0$ for $q > 0$, so the sequence degenerates: $H^p(X, \mathcal{F}) = H^p(Y, f_*\mathcal{F})$. This is why affine covers compute cohomology — each open inclusion $U_\alpha \hookrightarrow X$ is affine, and the Čech complex arises by unrolling Leray along the entire cover. The homological-algebra background on spectral sequences lives in homological.html §8.

Acyclic resolutions in practice

You almost never actually resolve by injectives. Three flavors of acyclic resolution dominate in practice:

  • Flabby — Godement's canonical construction. Always works, canonical, opaque.
  • Soft/fine — for a paracompact Hausdorff $X$, any sheaf admits a fine resolution (tensor with smooth partitions of unity on manifolds); the de Rham complex of a smooth manifold is the canonical example. $H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(X) = H^p(X, \underline{\mathbb{R}})$ follows from this with two lines of extra argument.
  • Čech / affine — for a scheme with an affine cover, apply Leray; the Čech complex is a resolution computed through the affines.

5. Comparison: Čech = derived for Leray covers

There is a canonical map $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) \to H^p(X, \mathcal{F})$, obtained by comparing the Čech complex with an injective resolution.

Leray theorem. If every finite intersection $U_{\alpha_0 \cdots \alpha_p}$ in the cover $\mathcal{U}$ satisfies $H^i(U_{\alpha_0 \cdots \alpha_p}, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for all $i > 0$, then the canonical map $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) \to H^p(X, \mathcal{F})$ is an isomorphism for all $p \geq 0$.

Such a cover is called a Leray cover for $\mathcal{F}$, or an $\mathcal{F}$-acyclic cover. The practical corollary is enormous: for any quasi-coherent sheaf on a scheme, an affine open cover (one where each $U_\alpha$ and all intersections are affine) is Leray. This is a theorem of Serre: on an affine scheme $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$, $H^i(\mathrm{Spec}\,R, \widetilde{M}) = 0$ for all $i > 0$ and all $R$-modules $M$.

In particular, for the cover $\{U_0, U_1\}$ of $\mathbb{P}^1$ from §3, both $U_0 \cong \mathbb{A}^1$ and $U_1 \cong \mathbb{A}^1$ are affine, and so is $U_0 \cap U_1 \cong \mathbb{G}_m$. Hence this cover is Leray for any quasi-coherent $\mathcal{F}$, and the naive two-term Čech computation we did is the honest cohomology. No refinement is needed. Similarly, the cover $\{U_0, \ldots, U_n\}$ of $\mathbb{P}^n$ by standard affine charts, together with all multi-intersections, is affine and thus Leray for any quasi-coherent sheaf; this is what makes the computation of $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(d))$ in §7 feasible.

Serre's affine vanishing. If $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,R$ is affine and $\mathcal{F} = \widetilde{M}$ is quasi-coherent, then $H^i(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $i > 0$. Moreover the converse holds in reasonable generality (Serre's criterion): on a Noetherian separated scheme, affineness is equivalent to vanishing of $H^i$ for all quasi-coherent $\mathcal{F}$ and $i > 0$.

The topological analogue is: a good cover (each $U_{\alpha_0\cdots\alpha_p}$ contractible) is Leray for the constant sheaf, so Čech of a good cover equals singular cohomology. The 2-open cover of $S^1$ in §3 is not good (the overlap has two components); but a 3-open cover with three triply-overlapping arcs is good, and gives the same answer — $H^1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z}$ — via the same arithmetic.

Example: $\mathbb{P}^2$ with three affine charts

$\mathbb{P}^2$ has the standard affine cover $\{U_0, U_1, U_2\}$ with $U_i = D_+(X_i) \cong \mathbb{A}^2$. Double and triple intersections are $U_{ij} \cong \mathbb{A}^1 \times \mathbb{G}_m \cong \mathrm{Spec}\, k[x, y, y^{-1}]$ (or similar), and $U_{012} \cong \mathrm{Spec}\, k[X_0, X_0^{-1}, X_1, X_1^{-1}, X_2, X_2^{-1}]_{(0)}$ (the degree-zero part). All affine, so Leray, so the Čech complex $$\check{C}^0 \to \check{C}^1 \to \check{C}^2$$ has three, three, and one term (because $\binom{3}{1} = 3, \binom{3}{2} = 3, \binom{3}{3} = 1$). Computing $H^i(\mathbb{P}^2, \mathcal{O}(d))$ on this complex gives the dimensions of §7. You can check by hand, for instance, that $H^2(\mathbb{P}^2, \mathcal{O}(-3)) = k$ (generated by the Čech cocycle $\frac{1}{X_0 X_1 X_2}$, the unique all-negative monomial) matches $\binom{-(-3)-1}{2} = \binom{2}{2} = 1$.

Proof sketch of Leray

The argument uses a spectral sequence. Given an injective resolution $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{I}^\bullet$, form the double complex $\check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{I}^q)$. Its two spectral sequences converge to the total cohomology:

  • Row-first: by acyclicity of each $\mathcal{I}^q$ as a sheaf, the rows of the $E_1$ page collapse, and the $E_2$ page is $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$ in degree $q = 0$, zero elsewhere. So the total cohomology is $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$.
  • Column-first: by the Leray hypothesis, $\check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{I}^q)$ computes $\prod_I H^q(U_I, \mathcal{F})$ in columns, and this vanishes for $q > 0$. So the $E_1$ page has only a $q = 0$ row, which is $\check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}, \Gamma(-, \mathcal{I}^\bullet))$. Taking $p$-cohomology is $R^p\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) = H^p(X, \mathcal{F})$.

Equating the two limits gives $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}) = H^p(X, \mathcal{F})$.

Counterexample: bad covers

If the cover is not Leray, Čech can still produce the wrong answer. Take $X = S^1$ with a 2-open cover by disjoint arcs (not overlapping): $U_0 = $ open upper half, $U_1 = $ open lower half, $U_{01} = \emptyset$. Then $\check{C}^0 = \mathbb{Z}^2$, $\check{C}^1 = 0$, so $\check{H}^0 = \mathbb{Z}^2$ and $\check{H}^1 = 0$ — both wrong (should be $\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{Z}$). But the cover is not a cover! The overlap is empty, which isn't a Leray condition but does tell you the cover fails to cover. A proper cover with nonempty overlaps and contractible pieces gives the correct Čech answer; passing to the colimit over refinements always does.

6. The long exact sequence in cohomology

The single most-used fact about sheaf cohomology: a short exact sequence of sheaves produces a long exact sequence of cohomology groups.

Theorem. Given a short exact sequence $0 \to \mathcal{F}' \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}'' \to 0$ of sheaves on $X$, there is a natural long exact sequence $$\begin{aligned}0 \to\; & H^0(X, \mathcal{F}') \to H^0(X, \mathcal{F}) \to H^0(X, \mathcal{F}'') \\ \xrightarrow{\delta}\; & H^1(X, \mathcal{F}') \to H^1(X, \mathcal{F}) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{F}'') \\ \xrightarrow{\delta}\; & H^2(X, \mathcal{F}') \to H^2(X, \mathcal{F}) \to H^2(X, \mathcal{F}'') \xrightarrow{\delta} \cdots.\end{aligned}$$ This is the $\delta$-functor property of $H^\bullet(X, -)$.

Proof sketch (derived-functor viewpoint): pick injective resolutions of $\mathcal{F}'$ and $\mathcal{F}''$ and mash them into an injective resolution of $\mathcal{F}$ (horseshoe lemma). Apply $\Gamma$; the resulting short exact sequence of complexes has its associated long exact sequence in cohomology, which is the claim. See homological.html §5 for the algebraic mechanics.

The connecting homomorphism, by Čech

The $\delta\colon H^p(\mathcal{F}'') \to H^{p+1}(\mathcal{F}')$ is the "interesting" map — the one that witnesses new obstructions. In Čech terms: take a $p$-cocycle $t'' \in \check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}'')$, lift each component locally to $t \in \check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$ (possible because $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}''$ is surjective on sections over small enough opens — after refining). The Čech differential $dt$ lands in $\check{C}^{p+1}(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})$, and its image in $\check{C}^{p+1}(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}'')$ is $dt'' = 0$. So $dt$ actually lies in $\check{C}^{p+1}(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F}')$, and a diagram chase shows it is a cocycle. Its class is $\delta[t'']$.

Think of $\delta$ as "lift a local section of $\mathcal{F}''$ to a local section of $\mathcal{F}$; discrepancies on overlaps fail to live in $\mathcal{F}$ but do live in $\mathcal{F}'$."

Worked example: the connecting map for exp on $\mathbb{C}^\times$

Cover $\mathbb{C}^\times$ by two open sets $U_0 = \mathbb{C}^\times \setminus \mathbb{R}_{<0}$ (complement of negative reals) and $U_1 = \mathbb{C}^\times \setminus \mathbb{R}_{>0}$. Both are simply connected; the overlap $U_{01} = \mathbb{C}^\times \setminus \mathbb{R}$ has two components (upper and lower half-planes).

Start with $g(z) = z \in \Gamma(\mathbb{C}^\times, \mathcal{O}^\times)$. On $U_0$ choose the branch $\log z$ with $\arg z \in (-\pi, \pi)$; on $U_1$ choose $\log z$ with $\arg z \in (0, 2\pi)$. These are $\mathcal{O}$-sections on each $U_i$; their difference on $U_{01}$ is $0$ on the upper half-plane and $2\pi i$ on the lower half-plane. Dividing by $2\pi i$ gives the Čech $1$-cocycle with values $(0, 1)$ in $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}(U_{01}) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}$. This cocycle represents $1 \in H^1(\mathbb{C}^\times, \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}$ — the winding number class. So $\delta(z) = 1$, confirming that $z$ has no holomorphic logarithm on $\mathbb{C}^\times$.

Running example: Euler sequence on $\mathbb{P}^n$

The Euler sequence on $\mathbb{P}^n$ is $$0 \to \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n} \to \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}(1)^{\oplus (n+1)} \to \mathcal{T}_{\mathbb{P}^n} \to 0,$$ where $\mathcal{T}_{\mathbb{P}^n}$ is the tangent sheaf. This is an essential input to deformation theory of $\mathbb{P}^n$ — and the answer "it is rigid" comes from $H^1(\mathcal{T}) = 0$.

Five-term exact sequence

The first five terms of the LES deserve to be memorized: $$0 \to H^0(\mathcal{F}') \to H^0(\mathcal{F}) \to H^0(\mathcal{F}'') \xrightarrow{\delta} H^1(\mathcal{F}') \to H^1(\mathcal{F}).$$ Applications:

  • When $H^1(\mathcal{F}') = 0$: $\Gamma(\mathcal{F}) \to \Gamma(\mathcal{F}'')$ is surjective. (The expected local-to-global lifting.)
  • When $H^1(\mathcal{F}') \neq 0$: measure of the obstruction to lifting global sections.
  • When $\mathcal{F}' = \mathcal{F}'' = \mathcal{L}, \mathcal{F} = \mathcal{L}^{\otimes 2}$ via squaring... wait, this is multiplicative, not additive, so you can't do it with sheaves of abelian groups directly — but the Kummer sequence $0 \to \mu_n \to \mathbb{G}_m \xrightarrow{(\cdot)^n} \mathbb{G}_m \to 0$ (in étale topology!) is exactly this phenomenon.

7. Cohomology of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}(d)$

This is the first real computation and the one you will use forever. The statement:

Theorem (Serre). Let $k$ be a field, $n \geq 1$, $d \in \mathbb{Z}$. Then $$\dim_k H^i(\mathbb{P}^n_k, \mathcal{O}(d)) = \begin{cases} \binom{n+d}{n} & i = 0,\; d \geq 0 \\ \binom{-d-1}{n} & i = n,\; d \leq -n-1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$ In particular $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(d)) = 0$ for $0 < i < n$, and $H^n(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(d))$ is dual to $H^0(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(-d-n-1))$. The canonical sheaf is $\omega_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \mathcal{O}(-n-1)$, and the duality matches $H^n(\mathcal{O}(d)) = H^0(\mathcal{O}(-d) \otimes \omega)^\vee$ — Serre duality, §9.

$H^0$: homogeneous polynomials

Global sections of $\mathcal{O}(d)$ are degree-$d$ homogeneous polynomials in $X_0, \ldots, X_n$: dimension $\binom{n+d}{n}$ for $d \geq 0$, zero for $d < 0$. This is clean from the $\mathrm{Proj}$ construction.

$H^n$: the "top" cohomology, via Čech

Use the standard affine cover $\mathcal{U} = \{U_0, \ldots, U_n\}$ with $U_i = D_+(X_i)$. The top-degree Čech cochain $\check{C}^n(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{O}(d))$ is sections over $U_{0 \cdots n} = D_+(X_0 \cdots X_n)$, i.e., the localization $k[X_0, \ldots, X_n][1/X_0 \cdots X_n]_d$ (degree-$d$ piece). As a $k$-vector space this is spanned by monomials $X_0^{a_0} \cdots X_n^{a_n}$ with $a_0 + \cdots + a_n = d$, $a_i \in \mathbb{Z}$ (negatives allowed).

The image of the differential $\check{C}^{n-1} \to \check{C}^n$ is spanned by monomials with at least one $a_i \geq 0$: such a monomial lives in the restriction from some $U_{0 \cdots \widehat{i} \cdots n}$ and hence is a coboundary. The quotient $H^n$ is therefore spanned by monomials with all $a_i \leq -1$: writing $a_i = -b_i - 1$ with $b_i \geq 0$ gives $\sum b_i = -d - n - 1$, a nonnegative integer only when $d \leq -n-1$. The count is $\binom{-d-1}{n}$, matching the theorem.

Intermediate $H^i$ vanish

This is the hard part — a genuine calculation with the Čech complex. The outline: on the affine cover $\mathcal{U} = \{U_0, \ldots, U_n\}$, the cochain $\check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{O}(d))$ in degree $p$ is a direct sum over $(p+1)$-element subsets $I \subseteq \{0, \ldots, n\}$ of the degree-$d$ piece of $k[X_0, \ldots, X_n][X_i^{-1} : i \in I]$. The Čech complex decomposes by multi-degree into a direct sum of little Koszul complexes on $n+1$ variables, one per multi-degree $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{Z}^{n+1}$ with $\sum a_i = d$. Each little complex computes Koszul cohomology of the set of $i$ with $a_i < 0$ — nontrivial only when this set is empty (giving $H^0$) or is all of $\{0, \ldots, n\}$ (giving $H^n$). Intermediate degrees vanish. Full details in Hartshorne III.5.

$\mathbb{P}^1$ in full

For $\mathbb{P}^1$, let's walk through $\mathcal{O}(n)$ explicitly in the widget. The Čech complex has only two terms: $$\check{C}^0 = k[x] \oplus x^n k[x^{-1}] \;\xrightarrow{\;(f, g)\mapsto g - f\;}\; \check{C}^1 = k[x, x^{-1}].$$ For $n = 2$: $\check{C}^0 = k[x] \oplus \{x^2 k + x \cdot k + k\} = k[x] \oplus \mathrm{span}(1, x, x^2)$. Kernel is $\mathrm{span}(1, x, x^2)$, so $\dim H^0 = 3 = \binom{2+1}{1}$. Image is all of $k[x, x^{-1}]$ (you can hit every Laurent monomial), so $H^1 = 0$.

For $n = -2$: $\check{C}^0 = k[x] \oplus x^{-2} k[x^{-1}] = k[x] \oplus \mathrm{span}(x^{-2}, x^{-3}, \ldots)$. Kernel is zero (no nonzero Laurent poly is simultaneously in $k[x]$ and $x^{-2} k[x^{-1}]$). Image is $k[x] + x^{-2}k[x^{-1}] = $ everything except $\mathrm{span}(x^{-1})$, so $\dim H^1 = 1$, represented by the cocycle $x^{-1}$.

The Euler characteristic

The alternating sum $\chi(\mathcal{F}) = \sum_i (-1)^i \dim H^i(X, \mathcal{F})$ is the Euler–Poincaré characteristic. It is better-behaved than the individual $h^i$: it is additive on short exact sequences, and for $X$ projective it is a polynomial function of $d$ when $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{O}(d)$ is varied (the Hilbert polynomial). On $\mathbb{P}^n$: $$\chi(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}(d)) = \binom{n + d}{n}$$ (valid as a polynomial in $d$, hence valid for all $d \in \mathbb{Z}$, including negative $d$ where the binomial is interpreted via the factorial formula). For $d = -1, -2, \ldots, -n$, the binomial $\binom{n-1}{n}, \binom{n-2}{n}, \ldots, \binom{0}{n}$ all vanish — and indeed all the $h^i$ vanish in that range. For $d \leq -n-1$, $\chi = (-1)^n \binom{-d-1}{n} = (-1)^n h^n$, matching the theorem.

8. The Picard group as $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^\times)$

A line bundle on $X$ is an $\mathcal{O}_X$-module $\mathcal{L}$ that is locally free of rank $1$. Equivalently: an atlas of trivializations $\mathcal{L}|_{U_\alpha} \cong \mathcal{O}_{U_\alpha}$ together with transition functions $g_{\alpha\beta} \in \mathcal{O}_X^\times(U_\alpha \cap U_\beta)$ satisfying the cocycle condition $g_{\beta\gamma} g_{\alpha\gamma}^{-1} g_{\alpha\beta} = 1$. Two such trivializations differ by a $0$-cochain $\{h_\alpha \in \mathcal{O}^\times(U_\alpha)\}$ via $g_{\alpha\beta} \mapsto h_\beta^{-1} g_{\alpha\beta} h_\alpha$ — a coboundary.

Theorem. For $X$ reasonable (e.g., a scheme, or a paracompact Hausdorff space), $$\mathrm{Pic}(X) = \{\text{iso classes of line bundles on } X\} \;\cong\; H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^\times).$$ Tensor product of line bundles corresponds to multiplication in $H^1$; the trivial bundle is the identity; the dual bundle is the inverse.

This is the cleanest geometric meaning of $H^1$: transition-function obstructions to trivializing a line bundle. Note $\mathcal{O}^\times$ is a sheaf of abelian groups (multiplicative), so this $H^1$ is a genuine abelian group.

Examples

  • $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^n) = \mathbb{Z}$. Every line bundle on $\mathbb{P}^n$ is $\mathcal{O}(d)$ for a unique $d \in \mathbb{Z}$, called the degree. The isomorphism is $\mathcal{L} \mapsto \deg\mathcal{L} = \chi(\mathcal{L}) - \chi(\mathcal{O})$ (on $\mathbb{P}^1$) or more generally the first Chern class.
  • $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathbb{A}^n) = 0$. Every line bundle on affine $n$-space is trivial. (Affine, so $H^1(\mathcal{O}^\times)$ vanishes after suitable care — this is nontrivial; it uses that $k[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ is a UFD. Contrast: $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathbb{A}^n \setminus 0) = 0$ for $n \geq 2$ but nonzero for $n = 1$... actually $\mathbb{A}^1 \setminus 0 = \mathbb{G}_m$ has $\mathrm{Pic} = 0$ too.)
  • Dedekind domains. If $R$ is a Dedekind domain (1-dim Noetherian integrally closed domain — rings of integers in number fields, DVRs, coordinate rings of smooth affine curves), then $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathrm{Spec}\,R) = \mathrm{Cl}(R)$, the ideal class group. E.g. $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ — the element is the class of $\mathfrak{p} = (2, 1+\sqrt{-5})$, which is not principal. This is the original geometric interpretation of "how far $R$ is from being a UFD."
  • Elliptic curves. For $E$ a smooth projective curve of genus $1$ over an algebraically closed field, $\mathrm{Pic}(E) \cong \mathbb{Z} \oplus E(k)$: the degree, plus a point of $E$ (the Jacobian). The "$\mathrm{Pic}^0$" part has the structure of an abelian variety — in genus $1$ it is $E$ itself. This is a teaser; the general construction of the Jacobian of a curve $C$ of genus $g$ is a $g$-dimensional abelian variety $\mathrm{Pic}^0(C)$.
  • Products. $\mathrm{Pic}(X \times Y) = \mathrm{Pic}(X) \oplus \mathrm{Pic}(Y) \oplus \mathrm{Corr}(X,Y)$ with a "correspondence" term (by the Künneth-style theorem). For $X \times \mathbb{A}^1$ and $X$ a variety, $\mathrm{Pic}(X \times \mathbb{A}^1) = \mathrm{Pic}(X)$ (homotopy invariance of $\mathrm{Pic}$ under $\mathbb{A}^1$-families, for $X$ normal).
  • Blowups. If $\tilde X \to X$ is the blowup of a smooth variety at a smooth subvariety $Z$ of codimension $c$, then $\mathrm{Pic}(\tilde X) = \mathrm{Pic}(X) \oplus \mathbb{Z} \cdot [E]$ where $E$ is the exceptional divisor. So blowups strictly enlarge the Picard group by a copy of $\mathbb{Z}$.

The connecting map for the exponential sequence, revisited

For a complex manifold $X$, the exp sequence $0 \to \underline{\mathbb{Z}} \to \mathcal{O} \to \mathcal{O}^\times \to 0$ gives the piece $$\cdots \to H^1(X, \mathcal{O}) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{O}^\times) \xrightarrow{c_1} H^2(X, \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) \to H^2(X, \mathcal{O}) \to \cdots$$ The map $c_1$ is the first Chern class. For a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ with transition functions $g_{\alpha\beta}$, choose local logarithms $\log g_{\alpha\beta}$ on overlaps; their triple-overlap mismatch $\frac{1}{2\pi i}(\log g_{\beta\gamma} - \log g_{\alpha\gamma} + \log g_{\alpha\beta})$ is an integer, which is the $\delta$-image in $H^2(X, \underline{\mathbb{Z}})$. For $X = \mathbb{P}^1$ this recovers the degree as an integer; for a complex torus $X = \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ it recovers the class in $H^2(X, \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}$ of the Riemann form.

The kernel $H^1(X, \mathcal{O})/H^1(X, \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathrm{Pic}^0(X)$ is the connected component of $\mathrm{Pic}$ — it forms an abelian variety, the Picard variety. This is the decomposition $\mathrm{Pic}(X) = \mathrm{NS}(X) \oplus \mathrm{Pic}^0(X)$ of the Picard group into the Néron–Severi group (discrete) and the Picard variety (continuous).

9. Serre duality and Kodaira vanishing

Two structural theorems whose statements alone pay rent.

Serre duality

Theorem (Serre duality). Let $X$ be a smooth projective variety of dimension $n$ over an algebraically closed field $k$, with canonical sheaf $\omega_X = \Lambda^n \Omega_X^1$. Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a coherent sheaf on $X$. Then there are natural isomorphisms $$H^i(X, \mathcal{F}) \;\cong\; H^{n-i}(X, \mathcal{F}^\vee \otimes \omega_X)^\vee \qquad (0 \leq i \leq n)$$ where $\mathcal{F}^\vee = \mathrm{\mathcal{H}\!om}(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{O}_X)$ and $(-)^\vee$ on vector spaces is $k$-linear duality. For a locally free $\mathcal{F}$ of finite rank this is the statement you use most; for $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{O}(d)$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$, $\omega_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \mathcal{O}(-n-1)$ and the duality reads $H^i(\mathcal{O}(d)) \cong H^{n-i}(\mathcal{O}(-d-n-1))^\vee$, exactly the reflection visible in the §7 table.

Two-line sanity check on $\mathbb{P}^1$: $H^1(\mathcal{O}(n)) \cong H^0(\mathcal{O}(-n-2))^\vee$. For $n = -2$, $H^1(\mathcal{O}(-2)) \cong H^0(\mathcal{O}(0))^\vee = k^\vee = k$: matches our computation $\dim H^1(\mathbb{P}^1, \mathcal{O}(-2)) = 1$. For $n = -3$, $H^1(\mathcal{O}(-3)) \cong H^0(\mathcal{O}(1))^\vee = (k^2)^\vee$: matches $\dim = 2$.

The trace map

Serre duality comes bundled with a canonical trace $\mathrm{tr}\colon H^n(X, \omega_X) \to k$, the isomorphism that realizes the duality. On $\mathbb{P}^n$, $\omega = \mathcal{O}(-n-1)$ and $H^n(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(-n-1))$ is one-dimensional, generated by the Čech cocycle $\frac{1}{X_0 X_1 \cdots X_n}$ (residue at the origin in Čech coordinates); the trace sends it to $1$. For a smooth projective curve $C$, $\mathrm{tr}\colon H^1(C, \omega_C) \to k$ is literal integration of $1$-forms along cycles, well-defined modulo exact forms. The whole pairing $H^0(\mathcal{F}) \otimes H^n(\omega \otimes \mathcal{F}^\vee) \to H^n(\omega) \to k$ is "cup product plus trace" — concrete and computable in Čech terms.

Derived Serre duality

The modern formulation works in the derived category: there is a quasi-isomorphism $R\mathrm{Hom}(\mathcal{F}, \omega_X[n]) \cong R\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})^\vee$ for any coherent $\mathcal{F}$ on a smooth proper $X$ of dimension $n$. This is compact and avoids the degree-by-degree statements. For complex projective varieties the $\omega_X[n]$ is the dualizing complex $\omega_X^\bullet$; on singular varieties the dualizing complex is a more general object in $D^b(X)$, not concentrated in a single degree. This is the Grothendieck-duality generalization.

The general statement for singular or non-projective $X$ requires the dualizing complex — Grothendieck duality, a significant enhancement, expressed cleanly in derived-category language. For projective Cohen–Macaulay schemes the dualizing complex is concentrated in one degree and recovers the statement above.

Kodaira vanishing

Theorem (Kodaira). Let $X$ be a smooth projective variety over a field of characteristic $0$, and $L$ an ample line bundle on $X$. Then $$H^i(X, L \otimes \omega_X) = 0 \qquad \text{for all } i > 0.$$ Equivalently, $H^i(X, L) = 0$ for $i < \dim X$ and $L$ ample (apply Serre duality to the statement).

"Ample" means large positive powers of $L$ give projective embeddings. On $\mathbb{P}^n$ ample means $L = \mathcal{O}(d)$ with $d > 0$. The Kodaira theorem then says $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(d) \otimes \mathcal{O}(-n-1)) = H^i(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(d - n - 1)) = 0$ for $i > 0$ when $d > 0$ — which is strictly weaker than what §7 actually proves. The payoff of Kodaira is on curves and surfaces of higher genus, where you lack explicit projective coordinates.

Kodaira fails in positive characteristic (Raynaud). Replacing "ample" with "nef-and-big" recovers it (Kawamata–Viehweg).

Why Kodaira matters — a concrete corollary

Apply Kodaira vanishing to a smooth projective surface $X$ with canonical bundle $K = \omega_X$. For any ample $L$, $H^i(X, L \otimes K) = 0$ for $i > 0$. Specialize to $L = \mathcal{O}(A)$ for a very ample divisor $A$; then $h^0(K + A) = \chi(K + A)$ computes directly via Riemann–Roch, with no correction. This is the crucial input to the Kodaira embedding theorem: sufficiently ample multiples of any line bundle $L$ with $L - K$ ample give projective embeddings. It's the theorem that turns "line bundle" into "projective embedding."

Other vanishing theorems

A zoo of relatives, each useful in its own corner:

  • Grauert–Riemenschneider. For a resolution of singularities $f\colon \tilde X \to X$, $R^i f_* \omega_{\tilde X} = 0$ for $i > 0$.
  • Kawamata–Viehweg. Replaces "ample" with "nef and big."
  • Akizuki–Nakano. $H^q(X, \Omega^p \otimes L) = 0$ for $p + q > n$ and $L$ ample — a refinement that controls Hodge-theoretic cohomology.

Riemann–Roch, briefly

The other pillar: for a smooth projective curve $C$ of genus $g$ and a line bundle $L$ of degree $d$, $$\dim H^0(C, L) - \dim H^1(C, L) \;=\; d - g + 1.$$ Combine with Serre duality ($H^1(L) \cong H^0(\omega_C \otimes L^{-1})^\vee$, $\deg \omega_C = 2g - 2$) and you have a complete tool for managing line-bundle cohomology on curves. Riemann–Roch on surfaces, and Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch for arbitrary coherent sheaves, is the higher-dimensional version: $\chi(\mathcal{F}) = \int_X \mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{F}) \cdot \mathrm{td}(X)$.

Sanity: applying Serre duality to Riemann–Roch on $\mathbb{P}^1$

Take $C = \mathbb{P}^1$, $g = 0$, $L = \mathcal{O}(d)$. Riemann–Roch: $h^0 - h^1 = d + 1$. Serre duality: $h^1(\mathcal{O}(d)) = h^0(\mathcal{O}(-d-2))$. Combining:

  • $d \geq 0$: $h^0 = d+1$, $h^1 = 0$. RR: $(d+1) - 0 = d + 1$. ✓
  • $d = -1$: $h^0 = 0$, $h^1 = h^0(\mathcal{O}(-1)) = 0$. RR: $0 - 0 = 0 = -1 + 1$. ✓
  • $d \leq -2$: $h^0 = 0$, $h^1 = -d - 1$. RR: $0 - (-d-1) = d + 1$. ✓

The two sides match exactly the §3 and §7 computations.

10. Coda: toward étale cohomology

Everything above takes place in the Zariski topology (or the usual topology on a complex manifold). That is enough to do coherent sheaf cohomology, classify line bundles, and prove the geometric theorems of §9. It is not enough to do Galois-theoretic cohomology of varieties over non-algebraically-closed fields — and for arithmetic geometry this is a serious limitation.

The problem: Zariski opens of a scheme are "too big." On $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$ the only nonempty open sets are complements of finite sets of primes, so the Zariski topology is not fine enough to detect the arithmetic structure. On a smooth variety $X$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, the Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$ acts on $X(\overline{\mathbb{Q}})$, and you would like a cohomology theory on which it acts naturally. The Zariski topology doesn't see this.

Grothendieck's resolution: replace "open covers" by étale covers. An étale morphism of schemes is one that is flat and unramified — the algebraic analogue of a local diffeomorphism. The category of étale morphisms $U \to X$ with étale covers (jointly-surjective families) is a Grothendieck site, $X_{\mathrm{\acute et}}$, and sheaves on it have the same definition as before: $$\mathcal{F}(U) \;\to\; \textstyle\prod_i \mathcal{F}(U_i) \;\rightrightarrows\; \textstyle\prod_{i,j} \mathcal{F}(U_i \times_U U_j)$$ is an equalizer for every étale cover. The derived functors of $\Gamma$ on this site give étale cohomology $H^i_{\mathrm{\acute et}}(X, \mathcal{F})$.

The payoff is huge. For a smooth projective variety $X$ over $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ and a prime $\ell \neq p$, étale cohomology $H^i_{\mathrm{\acute et}}(X, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ is a finite-dimensional $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-vector space with a continuous action of $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}/\mathbb{F}_p)$. The Lefschetz trace formula then expresses $\#X(\mathbb{F}_{p^n})$ in terms of the eigenvalues of Frobenius on these groups — the gateway to the Weil conjectures, proved by Deligne. For varieties over $\mathbb{Q}$, the Galois representation on étale cohomology encodes essentially all the arithmetic, and is the object studied by the Langlands program.

The story of this page — Čech cochains, long exact sequences, Leray covers, Serre duality — all transfers to the étale site with (sometimes serious) technical modifications. The dictionary is close enough that you can think of étale cohomology as "sheaf cohomology on a better topology," and the intuitions built here will carry.

See a forthcoming etale-cohomology.html for the étale site, constructible sheaves, $\ell$-adic cohomology, and the statement of the Weil conjectures.

Why étale, concretely?

Three symptoms of Zariski's inadequacy that étale fixes:

  1. Galois cohomology as sheaf cohomology. For $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,k$ a point ($k$ a field), the Zariski cohomology is trivial for $i > 0$. But $H^i_{\mathrm{\acute et}}(\mathrm{Spec}\,k, \mathcal{F})$ becomes Galois cohomology $H^i(\mathrm{Gal}(\bar k/k), \mathcal{F}(\bar k))$ — nontrivial and arithmetically essential.
  2. Torsion constant sheaves. On a complex variety $X$, you want $H^i_{\mathrm{\acute et}}(X, \mathbb{Z}/n) = H^i_{\mathrm{sing}}(X(\mathbb{C}), \mathbb{Z}/n)$ — the "topological" cohomology, recovered via the comparison theorem. The Zariski topology cannot see this; the étale topology does.
  3. Finiteness. On a smooth projective variety over $\mathbb{F}_q$, $H^i_{\mathrm{\acute et}}(X, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ is finite-dimensional, with Frobenius eigenvalues whose absolute values are $q^{i/2}$ (Deligne's Weil II). This is the structure theorem the Zariski story cannot touch.

Relation to this page

Everything we computed above — Čech cohomology on a good cover, long exact sequences, Serre-like vanishing — transfers, modulo technical care. Affine covers of a scheme are Zariski-Leray; étale covers by "standard étale" opens are étale-Leray. Affine schemes have no higher coherent cohomology (Serre); affine schemes with finite cohomological dimension are the analogue for étale of prime-to-$p$ torsion coefficients (Artin's theorem: $H^i_{\mathrm{\acute et}}(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $i > \dim X$ and $\mathcal{F}$ torsion on an affine scheme). The techniques you just learned continue to apply — only the site changes.

A bigger picture: six functors and exceptional inverse image

Zooming out, sheaf cohomology is one slice of the six-functor formalism: to each morphism $f\colon X \to Y$ of suitable spaces one attaches six functors between derived categories — $f^*, f_*, f_!, f^!, \otimes^L, R\mathrm{Hom}$ — satisfying adjunctions, base-change, projection formulas, and the Verdier duality that generalizes Serre duality. For proper $f$, $f_! = f_*$; for open immersions, $f_! = f_!$-extension by zero. Étale cohomology with compact support, $H^i_{c,\mathrm{\acute et}}$, is $R^i f_!$ applied to the structure map $X \to \mathrm{Spec}\,k$. The whole edifice of Grothendieck's six functors is the abstract skeleton that $\ell$-adic and Galois cohomology stand on.

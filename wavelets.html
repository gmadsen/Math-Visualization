Wavelets

Bases of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ that see *where* a signal changes — not just *which frequencies* it contains. Mallat's nested-subspace recipe, the Haar prototype, Daubechies' compactly supported family, the linear-time pyramid algorithm, and what happens when you replace JPEG's blocked DCT with a global wavelet basis.

1. Multiresolution analysis

A Fourier basis spreads a localised event — a cliff, a transient — across infinitely many coefficients. Mallat's multiresolution analysis (MRA) builds a different basis, one organised by scale: at each dyadic resolution we keep a closed subspace $V_j\subset L^2(\mathbb{R})$ of "functions visible at scale $2^{-j}$", and we look at what extra detail $W_j=V_{j+1}\ominus V_j$ adds when we double the resolution.

The MRA axioms — nesting, density, separation, dyadic dilation, and a scaling function $\phi$ whose integer translates orthonormally span $V_0$ — together force $L^2(\mathbb{R})=\bigoplus_{j\in\mathbb{Z}} W_j$ and produce a single function $\psi$ (the wavelet) whose dilations and translations $\psi_{j,k}(x)=2^{j/2}\psi(2^j x-k)$ form an orthonormal basis of all of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$.

MRA tower
Slide $j$ to refine.

The picture above projects a smooth bump $f(x)=e^{-(x-0.5)^2/0.05}$ onto the Haar $V_j$: at $j=0$ the projection is one constant on $[0,1]$; each step $j\to j+1$ halves the cell width and the projection looks more like $f$. The detail subspace $W_j=V_{j+1}\ominus V_j$ is what got added. As $j\to\infty$ the projections converge in $L^2$ (and pointwise a.e. by Lebesgue differentiation) to $f$.

2. The Haar wavelet

The simplest MRA: take $V_j$ to be all $L^2$ functions that are constant on each dyadic interval $[2^{-j}k,\,2^{-j}(k+1))$. The scaling function is $\phi=\chi_{[0,1)}$ — its integer translates tile the line, orthonormally in $L^2$. The associated wavelet is the difference of two halves,

$$\psi(x) = \chi_{[0,1/2)}(x) - \chi_{[1/2,1)}(x).$$

Then $\{\psi_{j,k}\}_{j,k\in\mathbb{Z}}$ with $\psi_{j,k}(x)=2^{j/2}\psi(2^j x-k)$ is an orthonormal basis of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ — the Haar basis, discovered in 1909 a quarter-century before Fourier–Plancherel even had a name.

Haar coefficient
Pick scale $j$ and translation $k$.

Each Haar coefficient $\langle f,\psi_{j,k}\rangle$ is a (rescaled) difference of two block averages: average of $f$ on the left half of the dyadic cell minus the average on the right half. So the Haar coefficient sees local change, while a Fourier coefficient $\hat f(n)$ sees global oscillation. The price the Haar basis pays: only one vanishing moment ($\int\psi=0$, but $\int x\psi\ne 0$), so it barely sees linear trends.

3. Daubechies wavelets

The Haar wavelet is short but rough — a step function. To get a wavelet that is both compactly supported and smooth, Ingrid Daubechies (1988) ran the MRA construction backwards: start from a finite filter $h_n$, ask for the refinement equation $\phi(x)=\sqrt 2\sum_n h_n\,\phi(2x-n)$ to produce a valid scaling function, and impose enough algebraic conditions on $h_n$ to force orthonormality plus $N$ vanishing moments.

The result is a family $\mathrm{db}N$ ($N=1,2,3,\ldots$) of compactly supported orthonormal wavelets with $\operatorname{supp}\psi=[0,2N-1]$ and $N$ vanishing moments. $\mathrm{db}1$ is Haar; $\mathrm{db}2$ is the first non-trivial member with the iconic asymmetric, fractal-looking graph below.

Daubechies family
Trade-off: smoothness vs. support.

The QMF condition $|m_0(\xi)|^2+|m_0(\xi+\tfrac12)|^2=1$ on the filter symbol $m_0(\xi)=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt 2}\sum_n h_n e^{-2\pi i n\xi}$ is what guarantees the integer translates of $\phi$ stay orthonormal under refinement. Daubechies's brilliant move: spectral-factor the polynomial $P(y)=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\binom{N-1+k}{k}y^k$ to extract a minimum-phase filter $h_n$ realising $N$ vanishing moments with the shortest possible support.

4. Discrete wavelet transform

Mallat's pyramid algorithm computes wavelet coefficients faster than the FFT computes Fourier coefficients. The trick: nesting $V_j\subset V_{j+1}$ means the level-$j$ scaling coefficients $a_j$ are obtained from the level-$(j{+}1)$ scaling coefficients $a_{j+1}$ by convolution with a fixed low-pass filter $h$ then downsampling by $2$. Symmetrically, the level-$j$ wavelet (detail) coefficients $d_j$ come from convolving with the high-pass mirror filter $g$ and downsampling.

$$a_j[k] = \sum_n h[n-2k]\,a_{j+1}[n], \qquad d_j[k] = \sum_n g[n-2k]\,a_{j+1}[n].$$

Iterate on the low-pass branch only. After $\log_2 n$ levels you've replaced the original length-$n$ signal with one scalar (the global average) plus $n-1$ detail coefficients organised by scale. Total cost: $L\cdot n + L\cdot n/2 + L\cdot n/4 + \cdots = 2Ln = O(n)$ for an $L$-tap filter — strictly faster than the FFT's $O(n\log n)$.

Inverse DWT mirrors analysis: upsample $a_j,d_j$ by inserting zeros, convolve with the dual reconstruction filters, and sum. For an orthonormal wavelet the reconstruction filters are the time-reversals of the analysis filters, so the inverse pyramid is also $O(n)$. Software libraries (PyWavelets, MATLAB Wavelet Toolbox) implement this in a hundred lines of straight loops.

5. Time–frequency localisation

The Fourier transform $\hat f(\xi)$ tells you what frequencies are present in $f$ — but every frequency coefficient depends on the entire signal, so a transient at $x_0$ is invisible to $|\hat f(\xi)|$ alone. The windowed Fourier (Gabor) transform fixes this by using a window of fixed width $\sigma$, but then high-frequency events get over-resolved in space and low-frequency events under-resolved.

Wavelets adapt: the atom $\psi_{j,k}$ has spatial width $\sim 2^{-j}$ and frequency width $\sim 2^j$, saturating the Heisenberg inequality $\Delta x\,\Delta\xi\gtrsim 1$. Cells are tall-and-thin at high frequency, short-and-wide at low frequency — exactly the trade-off needed to track jumps and oscillations in the same basis.

Heisenberg tiling
Toggle Gabor vs. wavelet.

The vanishing-moment payoff is quantitative. If $f\in C^N$ in a neighbourhood of $2^{-j}k$ and $\psi$ has $N$ vanishing moments, Taylor expansion plus integration kills the first $N$ terms and leaves $|\langle f,\psi_{j,k}\rangle|=O(2^{-j(N+1/2)})$. So smooth signals concentrate their wavelet energy in a small number of large coefficients near singularities — this is the engine behind every wavelet-based compression and denoising scheme.

6. Applications

Wavelets are not just a theoretical curiosity. Three vignettes:

Image compression (JPEG2000)

Legacy JPEG quantises the DCT of each $8\times 8$ tile independently — at low bitrates, you see block boundaries because each tile rounds independently. JPEG2000 replaces the blocked DCT with a global Cohen–Daubechies–Feauveau (CDF) 9/7 biorthogonal DWT applied to the whole image, then quantises and entropy-codes the wavelet coefficients. Aggressive compression looks like overall smoothing, not mosaic artefacts.

Signal denoising (Donoho–Johnstone soft-thresholding)

Take a noisy signal $y=f+\varepsilon$, $\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I)$. Apply DWT, soft-threshold each coefficient via $\hat c=\operatorname{sgn}(c)(|c|-\lambda)_+$ with $\lambda=\sigma\sqrt{2\log n}$, invert. Why it works: the wavelet transform of white Gaussian noise is again white Gaussian (orthonormal change of basis), spread thinly over all coefficients; smooth $f$ concentrates its energy in a few large coefficients. Thresholding kills the noise floor and keeps the spikes — Donoho–Johnstone proved this estimator is asymptotically minimax over Besov balls.

Calderón–Zygmund operators

If $T$ is a Calderón–Zygmund operator (the Hilbert transform, the Riesz transforms, $-\Delta^{-1}$, and other singular-integral cousins) and $\psi$ has $N$ vanishing moments, Beylkin–Coifman–Rokhlin showed the matrix entries $\langle T\psi_{j,k},\psi_{j',k'}\rangle$ decay as $2^{-(N+1)|j-j'|}(1+|2^j k-2^{j'}k'|)^{-N-1}$. The matrix of $T$ in a wavelet basis is therefore sparse: only $O(n)$ entries are non-negligible. Applying $T$ to a signal in the wavelet basis costs $O(n)$ instead of $O(n^2)$ — the engine behind fast multipole and adaptive PDE solvers.

Connections

Wavelets sit at the junction of harmonic analysis, signal processing, and PDE numerics. The frontier is partly geometric — wavelets on graphs, manifolds, and groups (continuous wavelet transforms attached to Lie group representations) — and partly statistical: optimal-rate adaptive estimation in Besov spaces, sparse recovery via $\ell^1$ relaxations, and the deeper connection between vanishing moments and approximation theory.

  • Harmonic analysis & Fourier — Fourier transform — wavelets refine the Fourier picture by adding spatial localisation; the QMF condition lives on the Fourier side.
  • Harmonic analysis & Fourier — Heisenberg uncertainty — every wavelet atom saturates the lower bound; Gabor and wavelet are two ways to tile $(\Delta x)\times(\Delta\xi)$ space.
  • Sobolev spaces — $W^{k,p}$ and $H^s$ — wavelet coefficient decay characterises Besov and Sobolev regularity by sequence-space norms.
  • Functional analysis — orthonormal bases — the wavelet expansion theorem is a Hilbert basis statement.
  • PDE — adaptive wavelet methods solve elliptic problems with optimal $N\log N$ complexity by exploiting CZ-operator sparsity.

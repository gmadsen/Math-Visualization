Mathematical statistics

From samples to inference: estimators with bias and variance, maximum likelihood and the Fisher information bound, Neyman–Pearson tests, Bayesian posteriors with conjugate updates, and the asymptotic apparatus (delta method, Slutsky, Wilks) that makes finite-sample procedures honest as $n\to\infty$. Probability theory supplies the random variables; statistics asks what those samples tell you about the law.

1. Point estimators: bias, consistency, MSE, sufficiency

Given i.i.d. samples $X_1,\ldots,X_n$ from a parametric family $\{f(x;\theta):\theta\in\Theta\}$, an estimator is any measurable function $\hat\theta_n=T(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ taking values in $\Theta$. Two estimators of the same $\theta$ can differ wildly in their behaviour as $n$ grows — the language for comparing them is bias, variance, and risk.

The bias at $\theta$ is $b(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_\theta[\hat\theta_n]-\theta$; the variance is $\mathrm{Var}_\theta(\hat\theta_n)$; and the mean-square error decomposes: $$\mathrm{MSE}_\theta(\hat\theta_n)=\mathbb{E}_\theta[(\hat\theta_n-\theta)^2]=b(\theta)^2+\mathrm{Var}_\theta(\hat\theta_n).$$ A biased estimator with low variance can have lower MSE than an unbiased one — the statistical free lunch from accepting some bias.

Consistency. $\hat\theta_n$ is consistent if $\hat\theta_n\xrightarrow{p}\theta$ as $n\to\infty$. Strong consistency upgrades to almost-sure convergence. Consistency is essentially asking that bias and variance both go to zero.

Sufficiency. A statistic $T(X)$ is sufficient for $\theta$ when the conditional law of $X$ given $T$ is free of $\theta$. The Fisher–Neyman factorisation criterion makes this practical: $T$ is sufficient iff $$f(x;\theta)=g(T(x);\theta)\,h(x).$$ For i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$ samples, $(\sum X_i,\sum X_i^2)$ is sufficient — once you know the sample sum and sum of squares, the individual $X_i$ values carry no further information about $(\mu,\sigma^2)$.

Worked example: sample variance. Two natural variance estimators are $$\hat\sigma^2_{\mathrm{MLE}}=\tfrac{1}{n}\sum(X_i-\bar X)^2,\qquad S^2=\tfrac{1}{n-1}\sum(X_i-\bar X)^2.$$ The first has bias $-\sigma^2/n$ (downward); $S^2$ is unbiased — Bessel's correction. But $\hat\sigma^2_{\mathrm{MLE}}$ has smaller variance, so $\mathrm{MSE}(\hat\sigma^2_{\mathrm{MLE}})<\mathrm{MSE}(S^2)$ for moderate $n$. Bias is not the only thing to optimise.

2. Maximum likelihood and the score equation

Once we have an estimator framework, the next question is: which estimator do we pick? The answer for most parametric models is *the one that maximises the likelihood of the data we observed* — and the surprise is how often this strategy is asymptotically optimal.

The likelihood $L(\theta)=\prod_i f(X_i;\theta)$ is, viewed as a function of $\theta$ with the data fixed, just the joint density evaluated at the observed sample. Its log is the log-likelihood: $$\ell(\theta)=\sum_{i=1}^n\log f(X_i;\theta).$$ The maximum likelihood estimator is $\hat\theta_{\mathrm{MLE}}=\arg\max_\theta\ell(\theta)$. Differentiating and setting to zero gives the score equation: $$\partial_\theta\ell(\theta)=\sum_i\frac{\partial_\theta f(X_i;\theta)}{f(X_i;\theta)}=0.$$

Fisher information measures the curvature of the log-likelihood: $$I(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_\theta[(\partial_\theta\log f(X;\theta))^2]=-\mathbb{E}_\theta[\partial_\theta^2\log f(X;\theta)].$$ The two expressions agree under regularity (differentiating $\int f=1$ twice). The score has mean zero and variance $I(\theta)$.

Under regularity, the MLE is consistent and satisfies the asymptotic normality $$\sqrt n\bigl(\hat\theta_n-\theta\bigr)\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}\bigl(0,\,1/I(\theta)\bigr).$$ The Fisher information sets the asymptotic precision; we will see in §3 that it is also a hard floor.

MLE = empirical KL minimiser. Let $\hat p_n$ be the empirical distribution of the sample. Then $$D(\hat p_n\,\|\,p_\theta)=\mathbb{E}_{\hat p_n}[\log\hat p_n]-\tfrac{1}{n}\ell(\theta).$$ Minimising $D(\hat p_n\,\|\,p_\theta)$ in $\theta$ is the same problem as maximising $\ell(\theta)$. So MLE is the statistical shadow of KL divergence, and Sanov's theorem governs how fast the empirical law concentrates on the truth.

Worked example: exponential rate. For $X_i\sim\mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$, $\log f=\log\lambda-\lambda x$, so $\ell(\lambda)=n\log\lambda-\lambda\sum X_i$ and $\hat\lambda_{\mathrm{MLE}}=n/\sum X_i=1/\bar X$. Fisher information is $I(\lambda)=1/\lambda^2$, so $\sqrt n(\hat\lambda-\lambda)\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,\lambda^2)$.

3. Cramér–Rao bound, UMVU, Rao–Blackwell

How small can $\mathrm{Var}_\theta(\hat\theta_n)$ be made among unbiased estimators? The Cramér–Rao inequality gives a sharp floor: $$\mathrm{Var}_\theta(\hat\theta_n)\ge\frac{1}{nI(\theta)}\quad\text{whenever}\quad\mathbb{E}_\theta[\hat\theta_n]=\theta.$$ Information bounds variance — there is no free lunch on precision below what the score equation allows.

An estimator achieving the floor is efficient; uniformly across $\theta$ it is the UMVU (uniformly minimum-variance unbiased) estimator. Sample mean $\bar X$ is UMVU for $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$'s mean — its variance $\sigma^2/n$ exactly equals $1/(nI)$.

Rao–Blackwell. If $W$ is unbiased and $T$ is sufficient, then $W^*=\mathbb{E}[W\mid T]$ is also unbiased and has $\mathrm{Var}(W^*)\le\mathrm{Var}(W)$. The proof is a one-line application of the conditional Jensen inequality plus the law of total variance: $$\mathrm{Var}(W)=\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Var}(W\mid T)]+\mathrm{Var}(\mathbb{E}[W\mid T])\ge\mathrm{Var}(W^*).$$ Lehmann–Scheffé: if $T$ is also complete, $W^*$ is the unique UMVU estimator of $\theta$. Conditioning on a complete sufficient statistic projects you onto the optimal estimator.

Worked example. For $X_i\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known, $I(\mu)=1/\sigma^2$ per sample, so the CRLB on unbiased estimators of $\mu$ is $\sigma^2/n$. The sample mean achieves this exactly — $\bar X$ is UMVU and efficient. For estimating $\sigma^2$ with $\mu$ known, the CRLB is $2\sigma^4/n$, and the MLE $\tfrac{1}{n}\sum(X_i-\mu)^2$ achieves it.

4. Hypothesis testing: Neyman–Pearson and likelihood ratios

Estimation tells us where $\theta$ probably is; testing tells us whether $\theta$ is plausibly some specific value $\theta_0$. Once estimation is in place, hypothesis testing is the natural next move.

A statistical test partitions the sample space into a rejection region $R$ and its complement; we reject $H_0:\theta\in\Theta_0$ in favour of $H_1:\theta\in\Theta_1$ when $X\in R$. Two error types:

$H_0$ true$H_1$ true
reject $H_0$type I, prob $\alpha$correct, prob $1-\beta$ (power)
accept $H_0$correct, prob $1-\alpha$type II, prob $\beta$

The size $\alpha=\sup_{\theta\in\Theta_0}\mathbb{P}_\theta(X\in R)$ is the worst-case type-I error rate; the power function is $\theta\mapsto\mathbb{P}_\theta(X\in R)$, which we want large on $\Theta_1$ for fixed $\alpha$.

Neyman–Pearson lemma. For simple-vs-simple $H_0:\theta=\theta_0$ vs $H_1:\theta=\theta_1$, the level-$\alpha$ test that rejects when $$\Lambda(x)=\frac{L(\theta_1;x)}{L(\theta_0;x)}>k$$ (with $k$ chosen so $\mathbb{P}_{\theta_0}(\Lambda>k)=\alpha$) is uniformly most powerful: every other test of size $\le\alpha$ has power $\le 1-\beta_{\mathrm{NP}}$. Likelihood ratios are the optimal test statistic.

The $p$-value of an observation $x_{\mathrm{obs}}$ is $p=\mathbb{P}_{H_0}(T(X)\ge T(x_{\mathrm{obs}}))$ for the chosen test statistic $T$ — the smallest level at which $x_{\mathrm{obs}}$ would have led to rejection. It is not $\mathbb{P}(H_0\mid x)$.

Worked example: one-sample z-test. Test $H_0:\mu=0$ vs $H_1:\mu>0$ with i.i.d.\ $X_i\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu,1)$ and $n=25$. Under $H_0$, $\bar X\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1/25)$; reject at level $0.05$ when $\bar X>z_{0.95}/\sqrt n=1.645/5\approx 0.329$. Power at $\mu=0.5$: $$\mathbb{P}_{0.5}(\bar X>0.329)=1-\Phi\bigl(\tfrac{0.329-0.5}{1/5}\bigr)=1-\Phi(-0.855)\approx 0.804.$$

5. Bayesian inference: posteriors, conjugate priors, credible intervals

Frequentist statistics treats $\theta$ as a fixed unknown and the data as random. Bayesian statistics flips this — $\theta$ is random with a prior distribution, and observing data shrinks our uncertainty by Bayes' rule. The methodologies look different but agree on most asymptotic claims.

Bayesian inference treats $\theta$ as a random variable. Begin with a prior $\pi(\theta)$ encoding belief before data; observe $X$ with likelihood $L(\theta;x)=f(x;\theta)$; the posterior is $$\pi(\theta\mid x)=\frac{\pi(\theta)L(\theta;x)}{\int\pi(\theta')L(\theta';x)\,d\theta'}\propto\pi(\theta)L(\theta;x).$$ Bayes' rule on parameters — the same identity that runs the medical-test posterior in probability theory, applied to a continuous parameter.

Conjugate priors are families closed under updating: when prior and likelihood multiply to a posterior in the same family, only the hyperparameters change. The classics:

likelihoodconjugate priorposterior
Binomial$(n,\theta)$$\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)$$\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha+x,\beta+n-x)$
Poisson$(\lambda)$$\mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha,\beta)$$\mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha+\sum x_i,\beta+n)$
Normal$(\mu,\sigma^2)$, $\sigma^2$ knownNormal$(\mu_0,\tau_0^2)$Normal (precisions add)

Point summaries of the posterior:

  • MAP $\hat\theta_{\mathrm{MAP}}=\arg\max\pi(\theta\mid x)$ — posterior mode; minimises a $0$–$1$ loss.
  • Posterior mean $\mathbb{E}[\theta\mid x]$ — minimises squared-error loss.
  • Posterior median — minimises absolute-error loss.

For symmetric unimodal posteriors all three coincide. A $1-\alpha$ credible interval $C$ is any region with $\mathbb{P}(\theta\in C\mid x)=1-\alpha$; the highest posterior density interval is the shortest such region.

Frequentist vs Bayesian, in one line. A frequentist confidence interval has a coverage guarantee over resampling data with $\theta$ fixed; a Bayesian credible interval is a probability statement about $\theta$ given the observed data. They agree numerically with flat priors and Gaussian likelihoods; they diverge philosophically and at small $n$.

Worked example. Coin with $\theta\sim\mathrm{Beta}(1,1)$ (uniform prior). Flip $n=10$ times, see $k=7$ heads. Posterior is $\mathrm{Beta}(8,4)$, with mean $8/12\approx 0.667$ and mode $7/10=0.700$ (= MLE). The MLE is the MAP under the flat prior — uniform priors recover frequentist point estimates as their posterior modes.

6. Asymptotics: delta method, Slutsky, Wilks

The CLT promotes any sample statistic with finite variance to an asymptotic Gaussian. Mathematical statistics builds three workhorses on top of it.

Delta method. If $\sqrt n(\hat\theta_n-\theta)\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ and $g$ is continuously differentiable at $\theta$ with $g'(\theta)\neq 0$, then $$\sqrt n\bigl(g(\hat\theta_n)-g(\theta)\bigr)\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}\bigl(0,\,g'(\theta)^2\sigma^2\bigr).$$ Proof: Taylor expand $g(\hat\theta_n)\approx g(\theta)+g'(\theta)(\hat\theta_n-\theta)$ and apply Slutsky. The Jacobian rescales the asymptotic variance.

Slutsky's theorem. If $X_n\xrightarrow{d}X$ and $Y_n\xrightarrow{p}c$ (constant), then $X_n+Y_n\xrightarrow{d}X+c$ and $X_nY_n\xrightarrow{d}cX$. The lemma that lets you replace consistent nuisance estimates by their probability limits inside a converging sequence — for example, $\sqrt n(\bar X-\mu)/S\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ because $S\xrightarrow{p}\sigma$.

Wilks' theorem. For the LRT statistic $\Lambda_n=\sup_{\Theta_0}L/\sup_\Theta L$ with $H_0$ specifying $r=\dim\Theta-\dim\Theta_0$ constraints, under $H_0$: $$-2\log\Lambda_n\xrightarrow{d}\chi^2_r.$$ This is the asymptotic calibration of the LRT — without simulation, you can read off the rejection threshold from $\chi^2_r$ tables. The constant $-2$ comes from Taylor-expanding $\log L$ around the unrestricted MLE; Fisher information turns the quadratic into a sum of $r$ squared standard normals.

Worked example. Estimate $\theta=p^2$ for $p$ a binomial proportion. The MLE of $p$ is $\hat p=k/n$ with $\sqrt n(\hat p-p)\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,p(1-p))$. Apply the delta method with $g(p)=p^2$, $g'(p)=2p$: $$\sqrt n(\hat p^2-p^2)\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,4p^2\cdot p(1-p))=\mathcal{N}(0,4p^3(1-p)).$$ At $p=1/2$, this is $\mathcal{N}(0,1/4)$.

Connections

Mathematical statistics is the inferential face of probability — every result here treats samples drawn from a probability law and asks what those samples reveal. Live frontiers: high-dimensional asymptotics where $p/n$ does not vanish (random matrix corrections to MLE), causal inference (where conditional distributions encode counterfactuals, not just associations), and the bridge to learning theory (PAC bounds, empirical risk minimisation as MLE under model misspecification).

  • Probability theory — central limit theorem — the asymptotic engine that makes MLE, delta method, and Wilks calibrate.
  • Information theory — KL divergence — MLE is empirical-KL minimisation, and Sanov's theorem governs how fast empirical laws concentrate.
  • Large deviations — Sanov's theorem — sharp exponential tail bounds for likelihood ratios; refines Wilks beyond first order.
  • Stochastic processes — filtrations — sequential testing and martingale-based confidence sequences extend the static framework here.
  • Information theory — mutual information — Fisher information is the local quadratic form of KL, hence the second derivative of relative entropy.

Riemann surfaces

A Riemann surface is a one-complex-dimensional manifold — equivalently, a real surface with complex-analytic transition maps. The subject is where complex analysis, algebraic geometry, and low-dimensional topology become the same story told in three dialects.

1. What is a Riemann surface?

A Riemann surface $X$ is a connected Hausdorff space equipped with an atlas of charts $\varphi_\alpha\colon U_\alpha\to V_\alpha\subset\mathbb{C}$ whose transition maps $\varphi_\beta\circ\varphi_\alpha^{-1}$ are biholomorphic wherever defined. Since a biholomorphism of open sets in $\mathbb{C}\cong\mathbb{R}^2$ is in particular a smooth orientation-preserving diffeomorphism, every Riemann surface is canonically an oriented real $2$-manifold.

Two perspectives sit on top of this definition and will not go away:

  • Analytic. A Riemann surface is exactly the natural domain for multi-valued holomorphic functions: the locus where $\sqrt{z}$, $\log z$, or $\wp$-inverses become single-valued and well-behaved.
  • Algebraic. A compact Riemann surface is the same thing as a smooth projective algebraic curve over $\mathbb{C}$. The functor from one to the other is an equivalence of categories; in either direction, meromorphic functions correspond to rational functions.

The miracle that makes the subject work is that on a compact Riemann surface, the complex-analytic structure rigidifies the topology: there are only countably many Riemann-surface structures on a surface of genus $g\ge 2$ (Teichmüller's theorem gives the correct count — a $(6g-6)$-real-dimensional moduli space), but the genus itself is a pure topological invariant, computed by $\chi = 2 - 2g$.

The right mental image of a Riemann surface is a donut (or pretzel, or sphere) that knows what it means for a function on it to be holomorphic. Almost every deep theorem in the subject is about how this local notion of holomorphy pins down global data: cohomology, degrees of line bundles, dimensions of spaces of meromorphic functions.

Why the term "complex curve"?

Complex dimension $1$ is real dimension $2$, so when algebraic geometers call it a curve they mean the complex-analytic dimension. A genus-$2$ surface — a two-holed donut to a topologist — is a "curve" to an algebraic geometer. Live with the terminology; it rewards you.

2. The simplest examples: sphere and torus

The Riemann sphere $\mathbb{CP}^1$

Glue two copies of $\mathbb{C}$ along $\mathbb{C}^\times$ by the transition $w = 1/z$. The result is topologically $S^2$ and is called the Riemann sphere; with coordinates it is the complex projective line $\mathbb{CP}^1$. The point added to $\mathbb{C}$ at infinity is genuinely a point of $X$ once you do this — in the $w$-chart it is just $w = 0$.

The two charts cover $\mathbb{CP}^1$, their intersection is $\mathbb{C}^\times$ in either picture, and the transition $w = 1/z$ is visibly biholomorphic there. Stereographic projection from the north pole gives the map $z \leftrightarrow $ point of the sphere concretely; as $|z|\to\infty$ the corresponding point on the sphere approaches the north pole, which is the point $w = 0$ in the other chart.

The torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$

Pick a lattice $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 \oplus \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$ with $\omega_1,\omega_2\in\mathbb{C}$ linearly independent over $\mathbb{R}$. The quotient $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ inherits charts from $\mathbb{C}$ because translations are biholomorphic; topologically it is a torus. The conformal class of this torus depends only on the modular parameter $\tau = \omega_2/\omega_1$, and we may always arrange $\mathrm{Im}\,\tau > 0$ by swapping $\omega_1\leftrightarrow \omega_2$ if necessary. Two lattices give biholomorphic tori iff their $\tau$'s are related by the $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ action $\tau\mapsto \frac{a\tau+b}{c\tau+d}$, so the moduli space of complex tori is $\mathbb{H}/\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$.

The Weierstrass $\wp$-function supplies an explicit biholomorphism $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda \xrightarrow{\sim} E_\Lambda\subset\mathbb{CP}^2$ onto the elliptic curve $y^2 = 4x^3 - g_2 x - g_3$, matching the complex-analytic torus to a smooth plane cubic. This is the first and cleanest example of "Riemann surfaces = algebraic curves."

Why only two charts suffice for the sphere

In general an atlas on a Riemann surface can have many charts, but $\mathbb{CP}^1$ admits one with just two. That is because $\mathbb{CP}^1\smallsetminus\{\infty\}$ is biholomorphic to $\mathbb{C}$, which is itself a chart — the obstruction to having a single global chart is precisely the topology (a sphere is not contractible). The general statement is that $X$ is biholomorphic to an open subset of $\mathbb{C}$ iff $X$ is simply connected and non-compact — part of the conclusion of uniformization, §5.

3. Branched covers from $w^2 = P(z)$

Let $P(z)\in\mathbb{C}[z]$ be a squarefree polynomial of degree $d$. The equation $w^2 = P(z)$ defines a curve whose smooth model is a compact Riemann surface $X$ admitting a map $\pi\colon X\to \mathbb{CP}^1$, $(z,w)\mapsto z$. Away from the roots of $P$ (and, if $d$ is odd, also $\infty$) the map $\pi$ is a $2$-to-$1$ local biholomorphism. Over a root $z_0$ of $P$ we have $w=0$ and the two sheets come together: $z_0$ is a branch point.

The standard picture is a two-sheeted cover of $\mathbb{CP}^1$ glued along branch cuts joining pairs of branch points. A small loop around an odd number of branch points swaps sheets; a loop around an even number returns to the same sheet. This is the monodromy of the cover.

For the hyperelliptic family $w^2 = P(z)$ with $\deg P = d$, the number of branch points is $d$ (plus $\infty$ if $d$ is odd). Riemann–Hurwitz will give genus $\lceil (d-1)/2\rceil$, so: $d=2$ gives a sphere, $d=3,4$ give a torus, $d=5,6$ give genus $2$, and so on. The Legendre form $w^2 = z(z-1)(z-\lambda)$ is an elliptic curve for every $\lambda\in\mathbb{C}\smallsetminus\{0,1\}$; different $\lambda$ give different complex structures, related by the $\Gamma(2)$ action.

Monodromy, more seriously

The complement $\mathbb{CP}^1\smallsetminus B$ of the branch locus $B = \{b_1,\ldots,b_r\}$ has fundamental group freely generated by small loops $\gamma_1,\ldots,\gamma_r$ around the $b_i$, modulo the single relation $\gamma_1\cdots\gamma_r = 1$. The unramified cover $X\smallsetminus \pi^{-1}(B)\to \mathbb{CP}^1\smallsetminus B$ is classified by the monodromy representation $\rho\colon \pi_1(\mathbb{CP}^1\smallsetminus B, *) \to S_n$, where $n$ is the degree of the cover. For double covers $n=2$, so $\rho$ lands in $S_2 = \{1,\sigma\}$, and $\rho(\gamma_i) = \sigma$ at each branch point. The relation $\prod\gamma_i = 1$ becomes $\sigma^r = 1$, which forces $r$ to be even.

For higher-degree covers the local contribution is the cycle structure of $\rho(\gamma_i)$. This is the same data as specifying the partition of $n$ given by the ramification indices over $b_i$ — exactly the numbers that feed into the Riemann–Hurwitz calculator in §4.

4. Riemann–Hurwitz and genus

Let $f\colon X\to Y$ be a non-constant holomorphic map of compact Riemann surfaces, of degree $n$. At each point $p\in X$ there is a local form $z \mapsto z^{e_p}$ in suitable coordinates; the integer $e_p\ge 1$ is the ramification index at $p$. The set of $p$ with $e_p > 1$ is finite.

The Riemann–Hurwitz formula: $$ 2g_X - 2 \;=\; n\,(2g_Y - 2) \;+\; \sum_{p\in X}(e_p - 1). $$ Equivalently, $\chi(X) = n\,\chi(Y) - \sum(e_p - 1)$: each ramification eats one of the $n$ Euler-characteristic copies.

Two sanity checks:

  • Unramified cover. If $e_p \equiv 1$, the ramification sum is $0$ and $\chi(X) = n\chi(Y)$. This is just the covering-space relation: an $n$-fold cover multiplies Euler characteristic by $n$.
  • Double cover of $\mathbb{CP}^1$. $n=2$, $g_Y=0$, each ramified point has $e_p=2$ contributing $1$. With $b$ branch points we get $2g_X - 2 = 2(-2) + b$, i.e. $g_X = b/2 - 1$. So $b$ must be even (and it is, by the topology of the cover).

One of the key uses of Riemann–Hurwitz: given any compact Riemann surface $X$ of genus $g\ge 1$, a non-constant meromorphic function $f\in K(X)$ realises $X$ as a branched cover of $\mathbb{CP}^1$, and the formula constrains the possible genera and degrees.

Genus as the universal topological invariant

For a compact oriented surface, the genus $g\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ is literally the number of handles: $g=0$ is the sphere, $g=1$ the torus, $g=2$ the two-holed donut, and so on. It is a homeomorphism invariant, but on a Riemann surface it is also what every complex-analytic number is secretly counting. The standard dictionary:

  • Euler characteristic $\chi(X) = 2 - 2g$.
  • Betti numbers $b_0 = b_2 = 1$, $b_1 = 2g$, so $H_1(X;\mathbb{Z})\cong \mathbb{Z}^{2g}$.
  • Fundamental group $\pi_1(X) = \langle a_1,b_1,\ldots,a_g,b_g\mid \prod[a_i,b_i]=1\rangle$.
  • Holomorphic $1$-forms $\dim_\mathbb{C} H^0(X,\Omega^1) = g$.
  • Canonical divisor degree $\deg K_X = 2g-2$.

All of these are manifestly different-flavoured objects — two pieces of real homology, one complex-analytic dimension, one algebro-geometric degree — and yet all are rigidly determined by the single number $g$. That coincidence is the reason Riemann surfaces occupy a miraculous corner of mathematics: the topology is so restrictive that it pins down the rest.

Riemann–Hurwitz is the first incarnation of this rigidity: once $g_Y$, the degree $n$, and the ramification profile are fixed, $g_X$ is forced. In §6 we will see Riemann–Roch, which is the same rigidity dressed up as an equation for $\ell(D)$ in terms of $g$.

5. Uniformization

Every Riemann surface $X$ has a universal cover $\widetilde X$, and $\widetilde X$ inherits a Riemann-surface structure making $\widetilde X \to X$ holomorphic. The deepest theorem in the subject — the uniformization theorem of Koebe and Poincaré — identifies the list of possible $\widetilde X$ up to biholomorphism.

Every simply connected Riemann surface is biholomorphic to exactly one of $$\mathbb{CP}^1,\quad \mathbb{C},\quad \mathbb{H} = \{\tau\in\mathbb{C}:\mathrm{Im}\,\tau > 0\}.$$ Hence every Riemann surface $X$ is a quotient $\widetilde X / \Gamma$ where $\Gamma = \pi_1(X)$ acts freely and properly discontinuously by biholomorphisms of $\widetilde X$.

The three universal covers correspond to three geometric signatures:

  • $\mathbb{CP}^1$ → spherical: genus $0$, positive curvature.
  • $\mathbb{C}$ → Euclidean: genus $1$ tori $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$, flat.
  • $\mathbb{H}$ → hyperbolic: genus $\ge 2$ and most non-compact surfaces, negative curvature.

Some flavour of the hyperbolic case: a surface of genus $g\ge 2$ has universal cover $\mathbb{H}$, and the deck group $\Gamma\subset\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ is a Fuchsian group. The surface is tiled by a fundamental polygon (for genus $g$, a regular $4g$-gon with side identifications $a_1 b_1 a_1^{-1} b_1^{-1} \cdots a_g b_g a_g^{-1} b_g^{-1}$); the card above shows the $4\cdot 2 = 8$-gon for genus $2$. This is the hyperbolic-geometry face of the moduli space $\mathcal{M}_g$.

The Gauss–Bonnet check

The trichotomy is enforced by a curvature integral. If $X$ carries a metric of constant curvature $\kappa$ compatible with its complex structure, Gauss–Bonnet says $\int_X \kappa\,dA = 2\pi\chi(X) = 2\pi(2-2g)$. For $g=0$, the integral is positive and $\kappa > 0$; for $g=1$ it vanishes and $\kappa = 0$; for $g\ge 2$ it is negative and $\kappa < 0$. Uniformization is the statement that such a metric always exists and is unique in its conformal class, up to the obvious symmetries.

6. Divisors and a peek at Riemann–Roch

A divisor on a Riemann surface $X$ is a formal $\mathbb{Z}$-linear sum $D = \sum_{P\in X} n_P \cdot P$ with $n_P = 0$ for all but finitely many $P$. The degree of $D$ is $\deg D = \sum n_P\in\mathbb{Z}$. Divisors form the free abelian group $\mathrm{Div}(X)$; the subgroup of principal divisors $(f) = \sum \mathrm{ord}_P(f)\cdot P$ of meromorphic functions is the kernel of the degree (on a compact $X$) on the level of equivalence classes, giving the divisor class group (Picard group) $\mathrm{Pic}(X) = \mathrm{Div}(X)/\mathrm{princ}$.

Divisors are the algebraic bookkeeping for "orders of zero and pole" and show up everywhere: every line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on $X$ is $\mathcal{O}(D)$ for some divisor $D$, and the degree of the line bundle equals $\deg D$.

The central result relating divisors to complex analysis is:

Riemann–Roch. Let $X$ be a compact Riemann surface of genus $g$, $K$ a canonical divisor (i.e. the divisor of any meromorphic $1$-form), and $D$ any divisor on $X$. Then $$ \ell(D) - \ell(K - D) \;=\; \deg D - g + 1, $$ where $\ell(D) = \dim_\mathbb{C} H^0(X, \mathcal{O}(D))$ is the dimension of the space of meromorphic functions $f$ with $(f) + D \ge 0$.

Two quick consequences, just to feel it:

  • Genus $0$. On $\mathbb{CP}^1$, $g=0$ and $K = -2\cdot \infty$. For $D = n\cdot \infty$ we get $\ell(D) = n+1$: polynomials of degree $\le n$.
  • Genus $1$. On an elliptic curve, $\deg K = 2g-2 = 0$ and in fact $K \sim 0$. For $\deg D > 0$, $\ell(K-D)=0$ and $\ell(D) = \deg D$: this is where the theory of elliptic functions with prescribed poles lives.

The qualitative content: $\ell(D) \ge \deg D - g + 1$ always, with equality when $\deg D$ is large enough. The correction term $\ell(K-D)$ is what makes the theorem powerful — it encodes exactly how the first-order count fails, and the answer is that it fails by sections of the canonical bundle twisted by $-D$.

A worked example on a genus-$1$ surface

Fix an elliptic curve $E = \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ with origin $O$ (the identity of the group law). On $E$ we have $g=1$, $K \sim 0$, and Abel's theorem identifies $\mathrm{Pic}^0(E)$ with $E$ itself. Consider divisors of the form $D_n = n\cdot O$ for $n\ge 1$.

Row 2 is the tell-tale feature of non-zero genus: even though $\deg D = 1 > 0 = g - 1$, you do not get a section with a simple pole at a single prescribed point (such a function would be a biholomorphism $E\to\mathbb{CP}^1$). The Riemann–Roch formula records this as the case where $\ell(K-D) = \ell(-O)$ jumps — except $\ell(-O) = 0$, and the correction is hidden in the subtler statement that $1\cdot O$ is not principal (that's Abel's theorem again: $1\cdot O - 0$ is zero in $\mathrm{Pic}^0$ iff $O = O$, trivially, but not in the sense of "arising from a function"). For genus $0$ the analogous phenomenon doesn't happen: $1\cdot \infty$ is the divisor of $1/z$ (taking "negative of the zero of $z$" with the right conventions), and $\ell(1\cdot\infty) = 2$.

7. Connections

Riemann surfaces glue together the rest of the mathematics in the notebook.

Differential forms and holomorphic $1$-forms

The sheaf $\Omega^1_X$ of holomorphic $1$-forms on a compact Riemann surface $X$ of genus $g$ has $H^0(X,\Omega^1) = g$-dimensional. This is the Hodge / de Rham content: the canonical line bundle $K_X = \Omega^1_X$ has degree $2g-2$, and its global sections give Abel–Jacobi maps and the period lattice.

Sheaf cohomology and Serre duality

Riemann–Roch is the Euler-characteristic identity $\chi(\mathcal{O}(D)) = \deg D - g + 1$ together with Serre duality $H^1(X,\mathcal{O}(D))^* \cong H^0(X,\Omega^1(-D))$. The whole machinery of sheaf cohomology is forced onto you by trying to make the counting work.

Elliptic curves and modular forms

A compact Riemann surface of genus $1$ with a chosen base point is precisely an elliptic curve. The moduli space $\mathcal{M}_{1,1} = \mathbb{H}/\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ of such objects is the home of modular forms; meromorphic sections of powers of the Hodge bundle over $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ are modular forms.

Algebraic geometry and schemes

Every compact Riemann surface is the complex points of a unique smooth projective algebraic curve over $\mathbb{C}$, and this equivalence is functorial: the category of compact Riemann surfaces with non-constant holomorphic maps is equivalent to the category of smooth projective curves with non-constant morphisms. Push further: arbitrary Riemann surfaces correspond to smooth $1$-dimensional complex-analytic spaces, and the right setting to generalise over arbitrary base is the theory of arithmetic surfaces and the étale site.

Topology

The underlying topology — genus, the fundamental group $\pi_1 = \langle a_1,b_1,\ldots,a_g,b_g \mid \prod [a_i,b_i]\rangle$, the mapping class group — controls everything non-analytic. Teichmüller theory lifts the moduli space $\mathcal{M}_g$ to a contractible space $\mathcal{T}_g\cong\mathbb{R}^{6g-6}$ on which the mapping class group acts.

Where to next

A reasonable next step is to pin down any one of these faces with more care:

  • Analytic: work through Forster's Lectures on Riemann surfaces, or Donaldson's treatment — the sheaf-theoretic proof of Riemann–Roch, Abel–Jacobi, and the Jacobian.
  • Algebraic: Hartshorne IV, or Vakil's chapter on curves — the same theorems in the language of schemes, degenerations, and moduli.
  • Hyperbolic: Farb–Margalit, Primer on mapping class groups, to see how Teichmüller space, Dehn twists, and the curve complex encode the topology of $\mathcal{M}_g$.

None of the three perspectives makes the others obsolete. You need the whole triangle to do real work.

The slogan to take away: a compact Riemann surface is a single object with three faces — a complex manifold, an algebraic curve, and a hyperbolic (or Euclidean, or spherical) geometry — and the subject consists of theorems that let you translate freely between those faces.

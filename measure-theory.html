Measure theory and the Lebesgue integral

From the failure of the Riemann integral on pathological functions to a theory of integration that is closed under pointwise limits, with $L^p$ spaces as its Banach-space completion. A structured review.

1. Why measure theory?

Riemann's construction integrates a bounded function $f\colon[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ by partitioning the domain into subintervals and sandwiching the area between upper and lower step approximations. When these agree in the limit, the common value is $\int_a^b f$. This works beautifully for continuous functions, and well enough for functions with only countably many discontinuities. But it has two fatal defects.

First defect: pointwise limits break Riemann integrability. Enumerate $\mathbb{Q}\cap[0,1] = \{q_1,q_2,\dots\}$. Set $$ f_n(x) = \begin{cases} 1, & x\in\{q_1,\dots,q_n\},\\ 0,& \text{otherwise.}\end{cases}$$ Each $f_n$ is zero except on a finite set and is trivially Riemann-integrable with $\int f_n = 0$. The pointwise limit $f(x)=\lim_n f_n(x) = \mathbf{1}_{\mathbb{Q}\cap[0,1]}(x)$ — Dirichlet's indicator of the rationals — is not Riemann-integrable: in every subinterval, rationals and irrationals are dense, so the upper Riemann sum is always $1$ and the lower always $0$. A category of integrable functions that is not closed under pointwise limits is not a workable category.

Second defect: unbounded domains and unbounded integrands. Improper Riemann integrals like $\int_0^\infty \tfrac{\sin x}{x}\,dx$ exist only as conditional limits; their value depends on how one approaches the singular endpoint or $\infty$. Even such a "nice" function as $\tfrac{\sin x}{x}$ fails to be absolutely integrable. Swapping limit and integral — the move underlying Fourier analysis, probability, and PDE — requires a theory where the unsigned integral $\int |f|$ controls everything.

Desiderata for a better theory. We want a class $\mathcal{L}$ of functions and an integral $f\mapsto\int f$ such that:
(i) $\mathcal{L}$ contains the Riemann-integrable functions and assigns them the same value;
(ii) $\mathcal{L}$ is closed under a.e.\ pointwise limits (modulo a dominating hypothesis);
(iii) $\mathcal{L}$ is a Banach space under an integral norm;
(iv) integration is well-defined on $[0,\infty]\cup\mathbb{R}$ and on abstract measure spaces.
Lebesgue's idea: slice the range, not the domain. Instead of summing $f(\xi_k)\Delta x_k$ over a partition of $[a,b]$, sum $y_k\cdot m\{f\approx y_k\}$ over a partition of the range $[\min f, \max f]$. The price of admission: we must know how to measure $\{x: f(x)\in E\}$ for every reasonable $E\subset\mathbb{R}$.

This is why measure theory comes first: the integral is built on a prior notion of "size" for subsets of the domain. Once we have a measure $\mu$ — a countably additive, nonnegative set function — the Lebesgue integral is forced. The four sections that follow develop this machinery: $\sigma$-algebras and Lebesgue measure ($\S 2$); the integral of measurable functions ($\S 3$); convergence theorems that license the limit-integral swap ($\S 4$); and the $L^p$ spaces that package the result as functional analysis ($\S 5$).

2. $\sigma$-algebras and measures

A $\sigma$-algebra on a set $X$ is a collection $\mathcal{A}\subseteq\mathcal{P}(X)$ satisfying:

  • $\emptyset\in\mathcal{A}$;
  • $A\in\mathcal{A}\Rightarrow A^c\in\mathcal{A}$;
  • $(A_n)_{n\ge 1}\subset\mathcal{A} \Rightarrow \bigcup_n A_n\in\mathcal{A}$.

The three axioms are chosen to make countable operations safe. Finite additivity is easy; it is the step from finite to countable that distinguishes measure theory from its classical ancestor (Jordan content).

Given a family $\mathcal{E}\subseteq\mathcal{P}(X)$, the intersection of all $\sigma$-algebras containing $\mathcal{E}$ is itself a $\sigma$-algebra, the $\sigma$-algebra generated by $\mathcal{E}$, written $\sigma(\mathcal{E})$. Applied to the open sets of a topological space, this yields the Borel $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}(X)$.

A measure on $(X,\mathcal{A})$ is a function $\mu\colon\mathcal{A}\to[0,\infty]$ with $\mu(\emptyset)=0$ and countable additivity: for pairwise disjoint $(A_n)\subset\mathcal{A}$, $$ \mu\!\left(\bigsqcup_n A_n\right) = \sum_n \mu(A_n). $$ This single property — that the measure of a disjoint countable union is the sum of the measures — is the engine of the entire theory.

3. Outer measure and Carathéodory's criterion

Given any nonempty family $\mathcal{E}\subset\mathcal{P}(X)$ equipped with a nonnegative set function $\rho\colon\mathcal{E}\to[0,\infty]$ (typically a premeasure on an algebra), one builds an outer measure $$ \mu^*(E) \;=\; \inf\!\Bigl\{\sum_n \rho(E_n) \,:\, E\subseteq\bigcup_n E_n,\ E_n\in\mathcal{E}\Bigr\}. $$ For Lebesgue on $\mathbb{R}$ the generators are open intervals and $\rho((a,b))=b-a$. The functional $\mu^*$ is defined on all subsets of $X$, is countably subadditive, monotone, and assigns $0$ to $\varnothing$ — but it is not countably additive. (Vitali's construction is the obstruction.)

Carathéodory's criterion. Declare $E\subseteq X$ to be measurable when it splits every test set $A$ additively against $\mu^*$: $$ \mu^*(A) \;=\; \mu^*(A\cap E) + \mu^*(A\cap E^c) \quad\text{for all } A\subseteq X. $$ The $\le$ direction is free (subadditivity); the $\ge$ direction is the content of the criterion.

Carathéodory's theorem. The measurable sets $\mathcal{M}$ form a $\sigma$-algebra, and $\mu:=\mu^*|_{\mathcal{M}}$ is a complete measure. If $\rho$ was a $\sigma$-finite premeasure on an algebra $\mathcal{A}_0$, then $\mathcal{A}_0\subseteq\mathcal{M}$, $\mu|_{\mathcal{A}_0}=\rho$, and the extension to $\sigma(\mathcal{A}_0)$ is unique.

Proof sketch: algebra closure is elementary bookkeeping on the additivity identity. The $\sigma$-algebra step truncates any countable union by monotonicity. Completeness is automatic — a subset of a $\mu^*$-null set trivially splits any $A$ additively (both pieces have $\mu^*=0$).

Why the Carathéodory condition? We want a family on which subadditivity upgrades to equality. A set $E$ that already splits every test set additively cannot be combined with others to break additivity. That closure property is what promotes $\mu^*$ from an outer measure to an honest measure — on exactly the $\sigma$-algebra it picks out.

4. Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}$

Applying Carathéodory to the length premeasure on the algebra of finite disjoint unions of intervals yields Lebesgue measure $m$, a complete, translation-invariant, $\sigma$-finite measure on a $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{M}$ strictly larger than the Borel $\sigma$-algebra. On any interval $m([a,b])=b-a$, and $m$ is the unique translation-invariant Radon measure on $\mathbb{R}$ with $m([0,1])=1$ (Haar measure on $(\mathbb{R},+)$).

Regularity. For every Lebesgue-measurable $E$, $$ m(E) \;=\; \inf\{m(U):U\supseteq E,\ U\text{ open}\} \;=\; \sup\{m(K):K\subseteq E,\ K\text{ compact}\}. $$ Outer regularity on the left is straightforward from the cover-infimum definition; inner regularity (on the right) is the substantive statement.

The Cantor set: uncountable, measure zero

Iteratively remove middle thirds. Start with $C_0=[0,1]$, and at stage $k$ delete the open middle third of each surviving interval, obtaining $C_k$ as a disjoint union of $2^k$ intervals each of length $3^{-k}$. The Cantor set is $C = \bigcap_k C_k$.

By countable additivity (or monotonicity), $m(C) = \lim_k m(C_k) = \lim_k (2/3)^k = 0$. Yet $C$ is uncountable — bijective to $\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$ via ternary expansions. This is the prototype of a Lebesgue-null set with the cardinality of the continuum. Its Hausdorff dimension is $\log 2/\log 3$ (see §13).

Non-measurable sets (Vitali)

Define $x\sim y$ iff $x-y\in\mathbb{Q}$ on $[0,1]$. The equivalence classes partition $[0,1]$ into uncountably many countable classes. Choose (AC) one representative from each: call the resulting set $V$. The translates $V_q = V+q$ for $q\in\mathbb{Q}\cap[-1,1]$ are pairwise disjoint, and $$ [0,1]\subseteq \bigsqcup_{q\in\mathbb{Q}\cap[-1,1]} V_q \subseteq [-1,2].$$ If $V$ were measurable, translation invariance gives $m(V_q)=m(V)$; countable additivity then forces $$ 1 \le \sum_{q} m(V) \le 3,$$ which is impossible whether $m(V)=0$ (sum = 0) or $m(V)>0$ (sum = $\infty$). Hence $V\notin\mathcal{M}$. Without some choice axiom Vitali's construction fails, and in models of ZF with Dependent Choice + "every set is measurable" (Solovay) the theory is consistent.

5. Measurable functions

A function $f\colon(X,\mathcal A)\to(\mathbb R,\mathcal B)$ is measurable when $f^{-1}(B)\in\mathcal A$ for every Borel set $B$. Equivalent tests use half-lines: $$ f \text{ measurable } \iff \{x\in X:f(x)>t\}\in\mathcal A \ \text{for all } t\in\mathbb R. $$ (Equivalence: the family of $B\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ with $f^{-1}(B)\in\mathcal{A}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra; it contains every $(t,\infty)$; hence it contains $\sigma$ of those, which is $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$.)

Closure properties. Sums, products, and pointwise suprema/infima of measurable functions are measurable; pointwise limits (where defined) are measurable; compositions $g\circ f$ are measurable for Borel $g$. The closure under pointwise limits is a decisive advantage over the continuous functions.

Simple function approximation. A simple function $\varphi=\sum_{k=1}^N c_k\,\mathbf{1}_{E_k}$ ($E_k\in\mathcal{A}$, $c_k\ge 0$) is measurable by construction. For any measurable $f\colon X\to[0,\infty]$ define the dyadic floor $$ \varphi_n(x) \;=\; \min\!\bigl(n,\ 2^{-n}\lfloor 2^n f(x)\rfloor\bigr), $$ so $\varphi_n$ is simple, $0\le\varphi_n\nearrow f$ pointwise, and uniformly on $\{f\le M\}$ for any $M$. Every integration theorem below bootstraps up from simple functions along this ladder.

6. The Lebesgue integral

With Lebesgue measure in hand, the integral is built in three steps: (i) for simple functions (finite-range measurable functions); (ii) extended to nonnegative measurable functions by monotone limits; (iii) extended to signed/complex measurable functions by decomposition. A function $f\colon X\to\overline{\mathbb{R}}$ is measurable if $f^{-1}((\alpha,\infty])\in\mathcal{A}$ for every $\alpha\in\mathbb{R}$ — equivalently, preimages of Borel sets land in $\mathcal{A}$.

Simple functions

A simple function is a finite measurable linear combination $$ \varphi = \sum_{k=1}^N c_k\,\mathbf{1}_{E_k}, \qquad E_k\in\mathcal{A}, $$ with the $E_k$ pairwise disjoint and $c_k\ge 0$. Its integral is defined in the obvious way: $$ \int \varphi\,d\mu := \sum_{k=1}^N c_k\,\mu(E_k), $$ with the convention $0\cdot\infty=0$. The definition is independent of representation, linear, monotone, and agrees on common domains.

Monotone extension to $[0,\infty]$-valued measurable functions

For $f\colon X\to[0,\infty]$ measurable, set $$ \int f\,d\mu := \sup\!\left\{\int \varphi\,d\mu\;:\; 0\le\varphi\le f,\ \varphi\text{ simple}\right\}. $$ The key fact is that every such $f$ is the pointwise increasing limit of simple functions $\varphi_n\nearrow f$. Explicitly, for each $n$ define $$ \varphi_n(x) = \begin{cases} k\,2^{-n} & \text{if } k\,2^{-n} \le f(x) < (k+1)\,2^{-n},\ 0\le k < n\,2^n \\ n & \text{if } f(x)\ge n. \end{cases}$$ These $\varphi_n$ are simple, $0\le\varphi_n\nearrow f$, and the monotone convergence theorem (next section) will confirm $\int\varphi_n\to\int f$. Slicing the range into dyadic bands is exactly Lebesgue's original idea.

Signed/complex: Jordan decomposition

For general measurable $f$, write $f = f^+ - f^-$ with $f^\pm := \max(\pm f,0)\ge 0$. Say $f$ is integrable ($f\in L^1$) if $\int f^+ + \int f^- < \infty$ — equivalently, $\int|f| < \infty$ — and define $$ \int f\,d\mu := \int f^+\,d\mu - \int f^-\,d\mu. $$ Linearity, monotonicity, and the triangle inequality $\bigl|\int f\bigr|\le\int |f|$ follow. For complex-valued functions take real and imaginary parts.

Compatibility with Riemann. If $f\colon[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ is bounded, then $f$ is Riemann-integrable iff the set of discontinuities has Lebesgue measure zero (Lebesgue's criterion), and the Riemann and Lebesgue integrals coincide. Every Riemann-integrable function is Lebesgue-integrable; the new theory is a strict extension.

7. Convergence theorems

The appeal of Lebesgue integration is that it plays well with limits. Three theorems — each a step weaker in hypothesis, each a direct consequence of the last — cover the main cases.

Monotone convergence (Beppo Levi)

Theorem (MCT). Let $f_n\colon X\to[0,\infty]$ be measurable with $f_n\nearrow f$ pointwise a.e. Then $$ \int f_n\,d\mu \ \nearrow\ \int f\,d\mu. $$

This is the foundational theorem: both Fatou and dominated convergence reduce to it. It is almost a tautology, given the definition of $\int f$ for nonnegative measurable $f$ as a supremum over simple minorants.

Fatou's lemma

Fatou. For any sequence $(f_n)$ of nonnegative measurable functions, $$ \int \liminf_n f_n\,d\mu \ \le\ \liminf_n \int f_n\,d\mu. $$

Proof: apply MCT to $g_n = \inf_{k\ge n} f_k$ (which $\nearrow \liminf f_n$) and note $\int g_n\le\int f_k$ for all $k\ge n$. Fatou does not give equality — only an inequality, and in the wrong direction.

Dominated convergence (DCT)

DCT. Let $f_n\to f$ pointwise a.e.\ and suppose there is an integrable $g\ge 0$ with $|f_n|\le g$ a.e.\ for all $n$. Then $f\in L^1$ and $$ \lim_n \int f_n\,d\mu = \int f\,d\mu, \qquad \lim_n \int |f_n - f|\,d\mu = 0. $$

Proof: apply Fatou to $g + f_n$ and to $g - f_n$; the two inequalities pinch $\int f_n$ to $\int f$. The domination hypothesis is what allows the swap. Without an integrable dominator every conclusion can fail.

Failure modes

Counterexamples clarify the hypotheses. Define on $[0,2]$ with Lebesgue measure:

  • Spike escaping to infinity: $f_n(x) = n\cdot\mathbf{1}_{[0,1/n]}(x)$. Then $f_n(x)\to 0$ for every $x>0$, yet $\int f_n = 1$ for every $n$. The mass concentrates at $0$ and is invisible pointwise. No integrable dominator exists (the pointwise supremum $\sup_n f_n$ behaves like $1/x$ near $0$, which is not integrable).
  • Drifting spike: $f_n(x) = \mathbf{1}_{[n,n+1]}(x)$ on $\mathbb{R}$. Again $f_n\to 0$ pointwise but $\int f_n = 1$. Mass escapes to $+\infty$. No integrable dominator.
  • DCT does apply: $g_n(x) = \tfrac{\sin(n x)}{n}$ on $[0,2\pi]$. Then $|g_n|\le 1/n \le 1$ (the constant $1$ is integrable on a finite-measure space) and $g_n\to 0$ uniformly, hence $\int g_n\to 0$.

8. $L^p$ spaces

Fix a measure space $(X,\mathcal{A},\mu)$ and a real parameter $1\le p<\infty$. The space $$ \mathcal{L}^p(\mu) := \Bigl\{ f\colon X\to\mathbb{C} \text{ measurable} : \int |f|^p\,d\mu < \infty\Bigr\} $$ is a vector space. The functional $\|f\|_p := (\int |f|^p)^{1/p}$ is a seminorm — it vanishes on the subspace $\mathcal{N}$ of functions zero a.e. — and passing to the quotient yields a genuine normed space $$ L^p(\mu) := \mathcal{L}^p(\mu)/\mathcal{N}. $$ In practice we work with $f$ "defined a.e." and suppress the quotient.

For $p=\infty$ use the essential supremum $\|f\|_\infty = \inf\{M : |f|\le M\text{ a.e.}\}$, giving $L^\infty(\mu)$.

Hölder, Minkowski

Hölder's inequality. If $1\le p,q\le\infty$ satisfy $1/p + 1/q = 1$, then $$ \int |fg|\,d\mu \ \le\ \|f\|_p\,\|g\|_q. $$

Proof: the Young inequality $ab\le a^p/p + b^q/q$ (follows from the convexity of $\exp$). Apply with $a = |f|/\|f\|_p$, $b=|g|/\|g\|_q$, and integrate.

Minkowski. For $1\le p \le \infty$, $\|f+g\|_p \le \|f\|_p + \|g\|_p$.

Minkowski follows from Hölder by expanding $|f+g|^p \le |f+g|^{p-1}(|f|+|g|)$ and conjugating the exponents. Together, Hölder and Minkowski make $L^p$ a normed space. The non-trivial fact is that this norm is complete:

Riesz–Fischer

Riesz–Fischer. For $1\le p\le\infty$, $L^p(\mu)$ is a Banach space.

Proof sketch ($1\le p <\infty$): a Cauchy sequence has a rapidly convergent subsequence with $\sum\|f_{n_{k+1}}-f_{n_k}\|_p < \infty$; by Minkowski the telescoping series $\sum|f_{n_{k+1}}-f_{n_k}|$ lies in $L^p$, so it converges a.e.; the limit is the candidate; DCT finishes.

$L^2$ as a Hilbert space

The exponent $p=2$ is self-conjugate: $1/2+1/2=1$. The resulting inner product $$ \langle f,g\rangle := \int f\,\overline{g}\,d\mu $$ makes $L^2(\mu)$ a Hilbert space. Orthogonality, projections, and the spectral theorem all become available. The Riesz representation theorem identifies $(L^2)^* = L^2$ via $g\mapsto\langle\,\cdot\,,g\rangle$. Fourier analysis on $\mathbb{R}^n$, quantum mechanics, and stochastic calculus are all $L^2$ theories.

Duality $L^p/L^q$

Geometry of $L^p$: unit balls

The unit ball of $\ell^p_2 = (\mathbb{R}^2, \|\cdot\|_p)$ is $\{(x,y): |x|^p + |y|^p \le 1\}$. For $p=1$ it is the $45°$-rotated square (the $\ell^1$ ball); for $p=2$ it is the Euclidean disk; for $p=\infty$ it is the axis-aligned square; for $p\in(1,\infty)$ it interpolates smoothly. For $p<1$ the "unit ball" is concave, so $(|x|^p+|y|^p)^{1/p}$ is not a norm — it fails the triangle inequality.

9. Product measures & $\sigma$-finiteness

Given $\sigma$-finite measure spaces $(X,\mathcal{A},\mu)$ and $(Y,\mathcal{B},\nu)$, the product $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{A}\otimes\mathcal{B}$ is generated by the measurable rectangles $A\times B$, $A\in\mathcal{A}, B\in\mathcal{B}$. On the algebra of finite disjoint unions of rectangles, the set function $$ \rho(A_1\times B_1 \sqcup \cdots \sqcup A_N\times B_N) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^N \mu(A_k)\,\nu(B_k) $$ is a $\sigma$-finite premeasure, and Carathéodory extension produces the product measure $\mu\otimes\nu$ on $\mathcal{A}\otimes\mathcal{B}$ characterised by $$ (\mu\otimes\nu)(A\times B) \;=\; \mu(A)\,\nu(B). $$ Uniqueness on $\mathcal{A}\otimes\mathcal{B}$ uses Carathéodory's uniqueness half — this is exactly where $\sigma$-finiteness does its work.

Where $\sigma$-finiteness bites. Without $\sigma$-finiteness, two different measures can agree on rectangles but diverge on the full product $\sigma$-algebra. Standard counterexample: take $X=Y=[0,1]$ with $\mu$ Lebesgue and $\nu$ counting measure. Counting measure is not $\sigma$-finite, and the diagonal $\Delta=\{(x,x)\}$ has $\int\int\mathbf{1}_\Delta\,d\mu\,d\nu = 0$ but $\int\int\mathbf{1}_\Delta\,d\nu\,d\mu = 1$. Product-measure uniqueness fails here.

Sections. For $E\in\mathcal{A}\otimes\mathcal{B}$ and $x\in X$, the $x$-section $E_x:=\{y\in Y:(x,y)\in E\}$ belongs to $\mathcal{B}$; similarly for $y$-sections. In the $\sigma$-finite case, $x\mapsto\nu(E_x)$ is $\mathcal{A}$-measurable and $$ (\mu\otimes\nu)(E) \;=\; \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu(x) \;=\; \int_Y \mu(E^y)\,d\nu(y). $$ This measurability-of-sections statement is the combinatorial heart of Tonelli–Fubini.

10. Fubini & Tonelli

Tonelli. Let $(X,\mathcal{A},\mu)$, $(Y,\mathcal{B},\nu)$ be $\sigma$-finite and $f\colon X\times Y\to[0,\infty]$ measurable. Then all three of $$ \int_{X\times Y} f\,d(\mu\otimes\nu),\qquad \int_X\!\int_Y f(x,y)\,d\nu(y)\,d\mu(x),\qquad \int_Y\!\int_X f(x,y)\,d\mu(x)\,d\nu(y) $$ exist in $[0,\infty]$ and are equal. No integrability hypothesis.
Fubini. For $f\colon X\times Y\to\mathbb{C}$ with $\int|f|\,d(\mu\otimes\nu)<\infty$ (equivalently, one of the unsigned iterated integrals of $|f|$ is finite, by Tonelli), the iterated integrals exist for a.e.\ section, are equal, and agree with the double integral.

The standard proof climbs the measurable ladder: indicators of rectangles (elementary) $\to$ simple nonneg (linearity) $\to$ nonneg measurable (MCT, giving Tonelli) $\to$ complex integrable (split $f=f^+-f^-$, finite integrability avoids $\infty-\infty$, giving Fubini).

Failure when integrability fails

On $X=Y=\mathbb{N}$ with counting measure, consider $$ a_{m,n} = \begin{cases} 1 & n=m, \\ -1 & n=m+1, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$ Then $\sum_n a_{m,n} = 0$ for each $m$, so $\sum_m\sum_n a_{m,n} = 0$. But $\sum_m a_{m,n}$ equals $1$ for $n=0$ (from the diagonal) and telescopes to $0$ elsewhere, giving $\sum_n\sum_m a_{m,n} = 1$. The two iterated sums disagree because $\sum_{m,n}|a_{m,n}|=\infty$.

A continuous version: on $[0,1]^2$ with Lebesgue measure, let $$ f(x,y) = \frac{x^2-y^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}, \qquad (x,y)\ne (0,0).$$ Then $\int_0^1\!\int_0^1 f\,dx\,dy = \pi/4$ while $\int_0^1\!\int_0^1 f\,dy\,dx = -\pi/4$. The function is not absolutely integrable near the origin, so Fubini's hypothesis is violated and the iterated integrals genuinely disagree.

Operational protocol. Before swapping order of integration on a signed $f$, compute $\int\int|f|$ in either order (Tonelli makes the unsigned iterated integrals automatically equal). If that is finite, $f\in L^1(\mu\otimes\nu)$ and Fubini licenses the swap. Otherwise: don't.

11. Signed measures & Radon–Nikodym

A signed measure on $(X,\mathcal{A})$ is a countably additive $\nu\colon\mathcal{A}\to[-\infty,\infty]$ (taking at most one of the two infinities) with $\nu(\varnothing)=0$. Examples: $\nu(E) = \int_E f\,d\mu$ for a $\mu$-measurable $f$ with $\int f^+\,d\mu < \infty$ or $\int f^-\,d\mu < \infty$.

Hahn–Jordan decomposition. There is a measurable partition $X = P \sqcup N$ such that $\nu(E\cap P)\ge 0$ and $\nu(E\cap N)\le 0$ for every measurable $E$. Setting $\nu^+(E)=\nu(E\cap P)$ and $\nu^-(E)=-\nu(E\cap N)$ yields two mutually singular positive measures with $\nu = \nu^+ - \nu^-$, unique up to null-set adjustments. The total variation is $|\nu|=\nu^++\nu^-$.

Absolute continuity. Write $\nu\ll\mu$ (or "$\nu$ is absolutely continuous with respect to $\mu$") when $\mu(E)=0 \Rightarrow \nu(E)=0$ for every $E\in\mathcal{A}$. Write $\nu\perp\mu$ ("mutually singular") when there is a measurable partition $X=A\sqcup B$ with $\nu(A)=\mu(B)=0$.

Radon–Nikodym. Let $\mu$ be $\sigma$-finite and $\nu$ a $\sigma$-finite signed measure with $\nu\ll\mu$. Then there exists a $\mu$-measurable $f\colon X\to\mathbb{R}$, $\mu$-a.e. unique, such that $$ \nu(E) \;=\; \int_E f\,d\mu \qquad\text{for every } E\in\mathcal{A}. $$ One writes $f = \dfrac{d\nu}{d\mu}$ — the Radon–Nikodym density.

Lebesgue decomposition. For $\sigma$-finite $\mu,\nu$ there is a unique split $\nu = \nu_{ac} + \nu_s$ with $\nu_{ac}\ll\mu$ and $\nu_s\perp\mu$. Radon–Nikodym gives a density for $\nu_{ac}$; the singular part $\nu_s$ lives on a $\mu$-null set (a Dirac mass, or the Cantor–Lebesgue measure on $C\subset[0,1]$).

Proof idea (Hilbert-space route). On the finite-measure case, define $L\colon L^2(\mu+\nu)\to\mathbb{R}$ by $Lg=\int g\,d\nu$. By Riesz representation on $L^2(\mu+\nu)$, $Lg = \int gh\,d(\mu+\nu)$ for some $h\in L^2(\mu+\nu)$ with $0\le h\le 1$. Algebra on indicators gives $d\nu = h/(1-h)\,d\mu$ on $\{h<1\}$, and $\{h=1\}$ is $\mu$-null — exactly the Lebesgue decomposition. This von-Neumann-style argument avoids transfinite induction entirely.

12. Riesz representation (measure form)

Let $X$ be a locally compact Hausdorff space and $C_c(X)$ the continuous real-valued functions with compact support. A linear functional $\Lambda\colon C_c(X)\to\mathbb{R}$ is positive if $f\ge 0 \Rightarrow \Lambda f\ge 0$.

Riesz–Markov–Kakutani. Every positive linear functional $\Lambda$ on $C_c(X)$ is integration against a unique regular Borel measure $\mu$ on $X$: $$ \Lambda f \;=\; \int_X f\,d\mu, \qquad f\in C_c(X). $$ Here "regular" means:
  • $\mu(K) < \infty$ for every compact $K\subset X$ (finiteness on compacta);
  • $\mu(U) = \sup\{\mu(K) : K\subset U \text{ compact}\}$ for every open $U$ (inner regularity on opens);
  • $\mu(E) = \inf\{\mu(U) : E\subset U \text{ open}\}$ for every Borel $E$ (outer regularity).

This is how measure theory and functional analysis meet: any positive linear functional on continuous test-functions is a measure. Dirac deltas $\Lambda f = f(p)$ correspond to $\mu = \delta_p$; Lebesgue integration $\Lambda f = \int f\,dm$ corresponds to $\mu = m$; summing at a sequence $\Lambda f = \sum_n \alpha_n f(x_n)$ corresponds to $\mu = \sum_n \alpha_n \delta_{x_n}$ when $\sum|\alpha_n|<\infty$ on each compact.

Why the regularity conditions? Without regularity, two different Borel measures can give the same functional on $C_c(X)$. Regularity pins $\mu$ down uniquely: knowing $\mu(U)$ on opens determines $\mu$ on Borel sets via outer regularity, and inner regularity recovers $\mu(U)$ from the compact-set values $\mu(K)$, which are computable from $\Lambda$ via Urysohn's lemma (sandwich $\mathbf{1}_K\le f\le \mathbf{1}_U$).

13. Hausdorff measure & dimension

Lebesgue measure counts 1-dim length on $\mathbb{R}$, 2-dim area on $\mathbb{R}^2$, and so on — but only for integer dimensions. Fractals (the Cantor set, the Koch snowflake) have no natural "integer-dimensional" size; we need a size-functional that interpolates smoothly.

The $s$-dimensional Hausdorff outer measure. For $s\ge 0$, $\delta>0$, $E\subset\mathbb{R}^n$, define $$ \mathcal{H}^s_\delta(E) \;=\; \inf\!\Bigl\{\sum_i (\mathrm{diam}\,U_i)^s \,:\, E\subseteq\bigcup_i U_i,\ \mathrm{diam}\,U_i\le\delta\Bigr\}. $$ $\mathcal{H}^s_\delta$ is monotone decreasing in $\delta$, and the Hausdorff outer measure is the limit $$ \mathcal{H}^s(E) \;=\; \lim_{\delta\downarrow 0}\mathcal{H}^s_\delta(E) \;=\; \sup_{\delta>0}\mathcal{H}^s_\delta(E). $$ Applying Carathéodory's criterion to $\mathcal{H}^s$ produces a $\sigma$-algebra containing the Borel sets; the restriction is a Borel-regular measure (the $s$-dimensional Hausdorff measure).

Dimension. For each $E$ the map $s\mapsto\mathcal{H}^s(E)$ is $\infty$ for $ss^*$, with a unique jump-point $s^* =: \dim_H(E)$ (the Hausdorff dimension). At $s=s^*$ itself, $\mathcal{H}^{s^*}(E)\in[0,\infty]$ is information beyond just the dimension.

Self-similar sets & Moran's equation. If $E = \bigcup_{i=1}^N T_i(E)$ with $T_i$ contractions of ratios $r_i\in(0,1)$ satisfying the Open Set Condition, then $$ \sum_{i=1}^N r_i^{\,s} = 1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad s = \dim_H(E), $$ and $0<\mathcal{H}^s(E)<\infty$. The middle-thirds Cantor set has $N=2$, $r=1/3$: $2\cdot(1/3)^s=1\Rightarrow s=\log 2/\log 3 \approx 0.6309$. The Koch curve has $N=4$, $r=1/3$, giving $\log 4/\log 3 \approx 1.2619$. The Sierpiński gasket: $N=3$, $r=1/2$, giving $\log 3/\log 2\approx 1.585$.

Consistency with Lebesgue. Up to a normalisation constant depending on $n$ and $s=n$, $\mathcal{H}^n$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ agrees with Lebesgue measure $m_n$. (Translation-invariance + agreement on a basis of cubes forces equality up to a Haar-measure scalar.) Hausdorff measure is the natural extension of "length/area/volume" to non-integer $s$.

14. Connections

Measure theory is not an end in itself. It is the substrate on which several further theories are built; each contributes back motivation for why the definitions of $\S 2$ were the right ones.

Functional analysis

$L^p$ spaces are the first examples of Banach spaces that are not finite-dimensional and not obviously sequence spaces. The Hahn–Banach theorem, weak-$*$ topologies, Banach–Alaoglu, and the open-mapping/closed-graph theorems all find their first applications here. $L^2$ theory, with Fourier-basis expansions, is the bridge to spectral theory and PDE. A companion notebook page (coming) will develop Hilbert spaces, Riesz representation, bounded operators, and the spectral theorem for compact/self-adjoint operators with the $L^2$ theory above as the running example.

Probability

A probability space is a triple $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},P)$ with $P(\Omega)=1$. A random variable is a measurable function; an expectation is a Lebesgue integral. The convergence theorems reappear: almost-sure convergence vs convergence in probability vs $L^p$-convergence, with DCT/MCT the workhorses. Conditional expectation is an $L^2$ orthogonal projection. Martingales, ergodic theory, stochastic calculus — none of it works without measure theory first.

Fourier analysis

The Fourier transform $\hat f(\xi) = \int f(x)e^{-2\pi i x\xi}\,dx$ is a $L^2(\mathbb{R})\to L^2(\mathbb{R})$ unitary operator (Plancherel) extended by density from the Schwartz class. Convolutions in $L^1$, the $L^p$ exchange properties (Hausdorff–Young), and the role of the Fourier transform in PDE all require $L^p$-theory.

Harmonic analysis

Maximal functions, singular integrals (Calderón–Zygmund), Hardy spaces, BMO, Littlewood–Paley theory: each of these lives on $L^p$ and exploits measure-theoretic tools at the boundary of Fourier analysis and real analysis. The Hardy–Littlewood maximal theorem is itself a Lebesgue-differentiation statement.

PDE

Sobolev spaces $W^{k,p}$ are $L^p$-spaces of weak derivatives; well-posedness of elliptic, parabolic, and hyperbolic PDE is stated and proved in these spaces. Distributions (Schwartz) are continuous linear functionals; their duality with test-functions generalises the $L^p/L^q$ pairing.

Geometric measure theory

Hausdorff measures $\mathcal{H}^s$ measure $s$-dimensional sets even when $s$ is not an integer. Rectifiable sets, currents, varifolds, the Plateau problem — a substantial industry built on the same $\sigma$-algebra + countable additivity foundation, tuned to geometry.

Prerequisites throughout: basic set theory, undergraduate real analysis (Riemann integral, metric spaces), linear algebra. The graduate "big theorem" list — MCT, Fatou, DCT, Riesz–Fischer, Radon–Nikodym, Fubini–Tonelli — is roughly what is assumed elsewhere in this notebook.

ZFC and ordinals

Cantor's naive set theory, made bulletproof. Nine axioms tell you which collections deserve to be sets; once that's settled, every infinite size — countable or not — is named by a von Neumann ordinal, and the continuum hypothesis turns out to be a question the axioms simply do not answer.

Naive set theory has a leak: unrestricted comprehension $\{x : \varphi(x)\}$ admits $R = \{x : x\notin x\}$, and $R\in R \iff R\notin R$ (Russell, 1901). The fix, due to Zermelo (1908) and Fraenkel/Skolem (1922), is to declare a short list of set-construction axioms and forbid everything else. Add the axiom of choice and you have ZFC, the working foundation of essentially all modern mathematics.

This page does three things. First, it lists the nine axioms and points at what each one buys you. Second, it builds the von Neumann ordinals — a canonical set-theoretic name for every well-ordered length, finite or transfinite — and shows their non-commutative arithmetic. Third, it does cardinals, the axiom of choice, the continuum hypothesis, and a glance at large cardinals. The widgets are mostly diagrams: ordinals are visual objects, and once you've seen $\omega^2$ as a tower of $\omega$-pickets you remember it.

1. The ZFC axioms

Everything below is a first-order sentence in the language $\{\in\}$. The axioms divide into three groups: identity (extensionality, regularity), construction (pairing, union, power set, infinity), and comprehension (separation, replacement, choice).

Why Separation, not Comprehension. The naive axiom $\exists B\,\forall x(x\in B\leftrightarrow\varphi(x))$ — for arbitrary $\varphi$ — gives Russell's paradox by taking $\varphi(x)\equiv x\notin x$. Separation requires an ambient set $A$, so $\{x\in A: x\notin x\}$ exists harmlessly (it is a subset of $A$), but the universal $R=\{x:x\notin x\}$ is never formed.

The cumulative hierarchy $V$ is built by transfinite recursion on the ordinals: $V_0=\emptyset$, $V_{\alpha+1}=\mathcal P(V_\alpha)$, $V_\lambda=\bigcup_{\alpha<\lambda}V_\alpha$ at limits. 2. Von Neumann ordinals

An ordinal is a transitive set well-ordered by $\in$. Concretely, von Neumann's recipe is:

\[ 0 = \emptyset,\qquad \alpha + 1 = \alpha \cup \{\alpha\},\qquad \lambda = \bigcup_{\beta < \lambda} \beta \text{ at limits}. \]

So $\alpha = \{\beta : \beta < \alpha\}$ — every ordinal is the set of smaller ordinals. The first few:

\[ 0=\emptyset,\quad 1=\{\emptyset\},\quad 2=\{0,1\},\quad 3=\{0,1,2\},\quad \dots,\quad \omega = \{0,1,2,\dots\},\quad \omega+1=\omega\cup\{\omega\}. \]
Why this encoding? Three things fall out for free. (i) $\alpha < \beta \iff \alpha \in \beta \iff \alpha \subsetneq \beta$ — order, membership, and subset coincide. (ii) The "next ordinal" is just $\alpha\cup\{\alpha\}$, no new machinery needed. (iii) $|\alpha|$ for finite $\alpha$ literally equals the integer $\alpha$.

Transfinite induction and recursion

Once $\in$ well-orders the ordinals, you get two principles for free:

Transfinite induction. If $\varphi(\alpha)$ holds whenever $\varphi(\beta)$ holds for all $\beta<\alpha$, then $\varphi(\alpha)$ holds for every ordinal $\alpha$.
Transfinite recursion. Given a class function $G$, there is a unique class function $F$ with $F(\alpha) = G(F\!\restriction\!\alpha)$. This is how $V_\alpha$, ordinal arithmetic, and Gödel's $L$ are all defined.

Induction lets you prove things layer by layer, even past $\omega$. Recursion lets you build things layer by layer; the price is replacement, which is what guarantees the recursion ranges over a set at each stage.

3. Ordinal arithmetic

Sum, product, and exponentiation of ordinals are defined by transfinite recursion on the second argument:

\[ \alpha + 0 = \alpha,\quad \alpha + (\beta+1) = (\alpha+\beta)+1,\quad \alpha+\lambda = \sup_{\beta<\lambda}(\alpha+\beta). \] \[ \alpha\cdot 0 = 0,\quad \alpha\cdot(\beta+1) = \alpha\cdot\beta + \alpha,\quad \alpha\cdot\lambda = \sup_{\beta<\lambda}\alpha\cdot\beta. \] \[ \alpha^0 = 1,\quad \alpha^{\beta+1} = \alpha^\beta\cdot\alpha,\quad \alpha^\lambda = \sup_{\beta<\lambda}\alpha^\beta. \]
Warning — non-commutativity. Both addition and multiplication of ordinals fail to commute: $1 + \omega = \omega \ne \omega + 1$, and $2\cdot\omega = \omega \ne \omega\cdot 2$. Think of $\alpha+\beta$ as "place a copy of $\beta$ after $\alpha$": adding a single point to the front of $\omega$ doesn't change its order type, but adding one to the back makes a new top element.

The tower up to $\varepsilon_0$

Every ordinal $\alpha < \varepsilon_0$ has a unique Cantor normal form \[ \alpha = \omega^{\beta_1}\cdot n_1 + \omega^{\beta_2}\cdot n_2 + \cdots + \omega^{\beta_k}\cdot n_k \] with $\beta_1>\beta_2>\cdots>\beta_k$ and $n_i\in\mathbb N_{>0}$. The first ordinal not of this form is $\varepsilon_0 = \sup\{\omega,\omega^\omega,\omega^{\omega^\omega},\dots\}$ — the least fixed point of $\alpha\mapsto\omega^\alpha$. Despite the towering iterated exponentials, $\varepsilon_0$ is still countable; the smallest uncountable ordinal $\omega_1$ is wildly larger.

Gentzen's theorem. Peano arithmetic cannot prove that $\varepsilon_0$ is well-founded. Adding well-foundedness of $\varepsilon_0$ as an extra principle (Gentzen, 1936) yields a consistency proof of PA. So $\varepsilon_0$ is, quantitatively, where the strength of PA runs out.

4. Cardinals and cofinality

Two sets have the same cardinality if there is a bijection between them. The cardinal $|X|$ is, in ZFC, the least ordinal in bijection with $X$ (this uses the well-ordering theorem, hence AC). For finite $X$ this is just the integer $|X|$; for infinite $X$ the cardinals are exactly the initial ordinals — those not in bijection with any smaller ordinal.

The aleph hierarchy. \[ \aleph_0 = \omega = |\mathbb N|,\qquad \aleph_{\alpha+1} = (\aleph_\alpha)^+ = \text{least cardinal} > \aleph_\alpha,\qquad \aleph_\lambda = \sup_{\beta<\lambda}\aleph_\beta. \] Every infinite cardinal is some $\aleph_\alpha$. The ordinal index $\alpha$ ranges over all ordinals — the alephs form a proper class.

Cofinality

The cofinality $\operatorname{cf}(\alpha)$ of a limit ordinal $\alpha$ is the least order type of an unbounded sequence in $\alpha$:

\[ \operatorname{cf}(\alpha) = \min\{\,|\,S\,| : S \subseteq \alpha,\ \sup S = \alpha\,\}. \]

A cardinal $\kappa$ is regular if $\operatorname{cf}(\kappa)=\kappa$, otherwise singular. In ZFC, every successor cardinal $\aleph_{\alpha+1}$ is regular. Limit cardinals can be singular: $\operatorname{cf}(\aleph_\omega)=\omega=\aleph_0$, because $\aleph_\omega = \sup_n\aleph_n$ is reached by a countable sequence.

König's theorem. $\kappa^{\operatorname{cf}(\kappa)} > \kappa$, so e.g. $2^{\aleph_0}\ne\aleph_\omega$ — the continuum cannot have countable cofinality, eliminating one popular guess for its size.

5. The axiom of choice

Often phrased: for every family of nonempty sets $\{A_i\}_{i\in I}$, the product $\prod_i A_i$ is nonempty. Equivalently, there is a choice function $f\colon I\to\bigcup A_i$ with $f(i)\in A_i$ for all $i$.

Equivalent formulations (all over ZF):
  • Zorn's lemma. Every nonempty poset in which every chain has an upper bound has a maximal element.
  • Well-ordering theorem. Every set can be well-ordered.
  • Tychonoff. Any product of compact topological spaces is compact.
  • Vector space basis. Every vector space over a field has a basis.
  • Hahn–Banach. Bounded linear functionals on subspaces extend to the whole space (this is strictly weaker than full AC).

For finite or countable index sets you don't need AC — pick by induction. The full strength shows up for uncountable products. Solovay (1970) showed that ZF + DC (dependent choice) is consistent with "every set of reals is Lebesgue measurable", so any non-measurable set (Vitali, Banach–Tarski) genuinely requires more than DC.

6. The continuum hypothesis

Cantor proved $|\mathbb N| < |\mathbb R|$ in 1874, and conjectured the continuum hypothesis:

\[ \text{(CH)}\qquad 2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1. \]

That is: there is no cardinal strictly between $\aleph_0$ and $|\mathbb R|$. He could not prove it. Hilbert listed it as Problem 1 in 1900. The resolution came in two halves:

yearwhowhat
1940Gödelbuilds the constructible universe $L \subseteq V$, an inner model of ZFC where CH (and GCH) hold. So ZFC + CH is consistent (assuming ZFC is).
1963Coheninvents forcing; constructs a model of ZFC where $2^{\aleph_0}=\aleph_2$. So ZFC + ¬CH is consistent (assuming ZFC is).
Independence. CH is neither provable nor refutable from ZFC. The axioms simply do not pin the size of the continuum down beyond König's $\operatorname{cf}(2^{\aleph_0})>\omega$. Modern research (Woodin, Foreman, …) studies which extensions of ZFC settle CH and how.

7. Inaccessible cardinals (and Grothendieck universes)

An uncountable cardinal $\kappa$ is (strongly) inaccessible if it is

  • regular: $\operatorname{cf}(\kappa)=\kappa$, and
  • a strong limit: $\lambda < \kappa \Rightarrow 2^\lambda < \kappa$.

The point: $V_\kappa$ is then closed under all the ZFC set-construction operations (pairing, union, power-set, replacement bounded by $\kappa$). So $V_\kappa$ itself is a model of ZFC.

Why this is "more than ZFC". If ZFC + (∃ inaccessible) proves Con(ZFC), then by Gödel's second incompleteness theorem ZFC alone cannot prove the existence of an inaccessible (else ZFC would prove its own consistency). Inaccessibles are the bottom rung of the large cardinal hierarchy; above them sit Mahlo, weakly compact, measurable, …, supercompact, Woodin, and Reinhardt.

Grothendieck universes

A Grothendieck universe $\mathcal U$ is a transitive set with

  • $x\in\mathcal U \Rightarrow \mathcal P(x)\in\mathcal U$,
  • $x\in\mathcal U,\ y\in x \Rightarrow y\in\mathcal U$ (transitive),
  • $\{x_i\}_{i\in I}$ a family with $I\in\mathcal U$ and each $x_i\in\mathcal U$ implies $\bigcup_i x_i \in\mathcal U$.

Theorem (Grothendieck): a set $\mathcal U$ with these closure properties is exactly $V_\kappa$ for $\kappa$ an inaccessible cardinal (in the uncountable case). The "universe axiom" — every set lies in some Grothendieck universe — is what SGA assumes to make category-theoretic constructions like $\mathbf{Set}$, $\mathbf{Cat}$, sheaf categories, derived functors all live coherently as actual sets rather than proper classes. It is equivalent to the existence of a proper class of inaccessibles.

Practical translation. When a category theorist says "let $\mathcal U$ be a universe and consider $\mathcal U$-small categories", they are implicitly assuming (at least) one inaccessible cardinal beyond ZFC. Most working mathematicians never notice; the few who care are usually doing higher-categorical foundations or set-theoretic universes.

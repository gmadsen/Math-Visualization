Waring's problem and the circle method

Lagrange said four squares always suffice. Waring asked: and cubes? And fourth powers? The answer — finitely many always do — is Hilbert's theorem; the modern proof runs through a Fourier integral over the unit circle that splits into "major" and "minor" arcs. It is the prototype of additive number theory.

1. The statement

Waring conjectured in 1770 that for every exponent $k\ge 1$ there is a finite constant $g(k)$ such that every positive integer is a sum of at most $g(k)$ non-negative $k$-th powers: $$n = x_1^k + x_2^k + \cdots + x_{g(k)}^k,\qquad x_i\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}.$$ Lagrange had just proved $g(2)=4$; the extension to $g(3)$ and $g(4)$ was folklore. The constants turned out to be:

2. $g(k)$ versus $G(k)$

The constant $g(k)$ is hostage to a few small "bad" integers — $7$, $23$, $79$, $223$ — that are inefficient because they are too small to use large powers. For the large-$n$ behaviour it is more natural to define the asymptotic Waring constant:

$G(k)$ = the smallest $s$ such that every sufficiently large integer $n$ is a sum of $s$ $k$-th powers.

Trivially $G(k)\le g(k)$, with the gap caused exactly by the small exceptional $n$.

The huge gap $G(3)\le 7 \ll 9 = g(3)$ shows that nine cubes are only required for three or four specific integers. The asymptotic behaviour is genuinely linear:

Theorem (Vinogradov / Wooley). $G(k) \le k\log k + k\log\log k + O(k)$ as $k\to\infty$.

This is within a $\log k$ factor of the trivial lower bound $G(k)\ge k+1$ (since $n$ has $\asymp n^{s/k}/s!$ representations as an $s$-tuple, which must be $\ge 1$, giving $s\ge k$). The conjectural truth $G(k) = k+1$ for $k\ne 2^j$ is wide open.

3. Hilbert–Waring: finiteness

For 140 years after Waring no one could prove even that $g(k)<\infty$ in general. Then:

Theorem (Hilbert, 1909). For every $k\ge 1$, $g(k)$ is finite.

Hilbert's proof was an algebraic identity, not an analytic estimate. The crucial input is a polynomial identity of the form $$\Bigl(x_1^2+x_2^2+\cdots+x_r^2\Bigr)^k = \sum_{j=1}^{M} c_j\bigl(a_{j,1} x_1 + \cdots + a_{j,r} x_r\bigr)^{2k}$$ with $c_j>0$ rationals and $a_{j,i}\in\mathbb{Z}$. (Such an identity, later made constructive by Hausdorff, exists for every $k\ge 1$; the proof uses a lattice-point counting argument in $\mathbb{R}^r$ for $r$ sufficiently large.) Given this, take $r=4$ and use Lagrange: every $n$ is $x_1^2+\cdots+x_4^2$ for some integers, so $$n^k = \sum_{j=1}^M c_j L_j^{2k},\qquad L_j\in\mathbb{Z}.$$ Clearing denominators (replace $n$ by a large multiple), one gets every sufficiently large integer of the form $c\, n^k$ as a sum of boundedly many $(2k)$-th powers. An induction on $k$ then descends to every integer.

The idea in one slogan. Lagrange handles squares. An algebraic identity converts $k$-th powers of squares into sums of $(2k)$-th powers. Iterating, every integer becomes a bounded sum of $(2^m k)$-th powers for every $m$ — enough to cover every exponent inductively.

Hilbert's method is non-constructive: the implicit $g(k)$ is astronomically large. For example, the first explicit bounds of the form $g(k)\le (2k+1)\cdot 2^k$ came only decades later. The circle method of Hardy–Littlewood (1920–25), which replaced algebra with analysis, brought the estimates down to essentially optimal.

4. The circle method

Fix $k\ge 2$ and a target integer $N$, and consider the generating function $$f(\alpha) = \sum_{n=1}^{\lfloor N^{1/k}\rfloor} e(\alpha\, n^k),\qquad e(x):=e^{2\pi i x},\ \alpha\in\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}.$$ Raising to the $s$-th power, $$f(\alpha)^s = \sum_m r_{s,k}(m)\, e(\alpha m),$$ where $r_{s,k}(m)$ is the number of ordered representations $$m = x_1^k + x_2^k + \cdots + x_s^k,\qquad 1\le x_i\le N^{1/k}.$$ By Fourier orthogonality on $\mathbb{Z}$, $\int_0^1 e(\alpha m)\,d\alpha = [m=0]$, hence

Hardy–Littlewood, 1920. $$r_{s,k}(N) = \int_0^1 f(\alpha)^s\, e(-\alpha N)\, d\alpha.$$

That reduces counting representations of $N$ to evaluating an integral. The success of the method depends entirely on understanding $f(\alpha)$ as $\alpha$ varies across $[0,1)$:

  • When $\alpha$ is close to $0$ (or close to a rational $a/q$ with $q$ small), the summands $e(\alpha n^k)$ are in near-arithmetic progression, and $f(\alpha)$ is large, of order $N^{1/k}$.
  • When $\alpha$ is "badly approximable" by small-denominator rationals, $f(\alpha)$ is a highly oscillatory sum, and Weyl differencing gives a nontrivial bound $|f(\alpha)| \ll N^{1/k - \sigma_k}$.

The unit interval splits into two complementary sets: the major arcs $\mathfrak{M}$ (small neighbourhoods of rationals with small denominator) and the minor arcs $\mathfrak{m}=[0,1)\setminus\mathfrak{M}$. On $\mathfrak{M}$ the integrand is concentrated around a computable main term; on $\mathfrak{m}$ Weyl's estimates force the integrand to be tiny.

5. Major arcs: the main term

Pick $\alpha$ close to a reduced fraction $a/q$: write $\alpha = a/q + \beta$ with $|\beta|$ small. Then $$f(\alpha) = \sum_{n\le M} e\bigl((a/q)n^k\bigr)\, e(\beta n^k),\qquad M:=\lfloor N^{1/k}\rfloor.$$ The first factor depends only on $n \bmod q$, so split the sum by residue class: $$f(\alpha) = \sum_{r=1}^{q} e(a r^k/q)\sum_{\substack{n\le M\\ n\equiv r\pmod q}} e(\beta n^k).$$ The inner sum is (for $|\beta|\cdot M^k$ bounded) well approximated by the continuous integral $$\frac{1}{q}\int_0^M e(\beta t^k)\, dt.$$ Summing over $r$ identifies the Gauss-type sum $$S(a,q) := \sum_{r=1}^{q} e(a r^k/q)$$ as the leading weight. Putting it together, $$f(a/q + \beta) \approx q^{-1}\, S(a,q)\, v(\beta),\qquad v(\beta):=\int_0^M e(\beta t^k)\,dt.$$ Raise to the $s$-th power and integrate $\beta$ over a small window around $0$: the contribution of all major arcs is

$$\mathfrak{S}(N)\cdot J(N) + \text{(error)},\qquad \mathfrak{S}(N) = \sum_{q=1}^{\infty} \sum_{\substack{a=1\\ (a,q)=1}}^{q} \Bigl(\tfrac{S(a,q)}{q}\Bigr)^{\!s} e(-aN/q),\quad J(N) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} v(\beta)^s\, e(-\beta N)\, d\beta.$$

The quantity $\mathfrak{S}(N)$ is the singular series and $J(N)$ the singular integral. Each factor is an Euler product over primes times an archimedean factor — a Hasse-style local-to-global product, see section 6.

On the minor arcs Weyl's inequality does the rest:

Weyl's inequality. If $|\alpha - a/q| \le 1/q^2$ and $\gcd(a,q)=1$, then $$|f(\alpha)| \ll M^{1+\varepsilon}\Bigl(\tfrac{1}{q} + \tfrac{1}{M} + \tfrac{q}{M^k}\Bigr)^{\!2^{1-k}}.$$ In particular for $q$ in the "minor-arc range" $M \le q \le M^{k-1}$, $|f(\alpha)| \ll M^{1-\sigma_k+\varepsilon}$ with $\sigma_k = 2^{1-k}$.

Integrating $|f(\alpha)|^s$ on the minor arcs and requiring the estimate to be smaller than the major-arc main term gives the Hardy–Littlewood asymptotic:

Hardy–Littlewood (1920–25). For $k\ge 2$ and $s\ge s_0(k)$ (sufficiently large), $$r_{s,k}(N) = \mathfrak{S}(N)\cdot \frac{\Gamma(1+1/k)^s}{\Gamma(s/k)}\, N^{s/k-1} + o(N^{s/k-1}).$$ The leading term $N^{s/k-1}$ is the geometric expectation: the volume of the box $\{x\in\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^s : x_1^k+\cdots+x_s^k = N\}$.

The clean upshot: for $s$ large enough, $r_{s,k}(N) \to \infty$ as $N\to\infty$, hence $r_{s,k}(N)\ge 1$ for all sufficiently large $N$, hence $G(k)\le s$. Taking $s=s_0(k)\sim k\log k$ proves the Vinogradov bound. The same machinery recovers Hilbert's finiteness of $g(k)$ with explicit constants.

6. Local densities — Hasse principle for powers

The singular series $\mathfrak{S}(N)$ is more than a bookkeeping device: it factors as a product over primes, $$\mathfrak{S}(N) = \prod_{p\text{ prime}} \sigma_p(N),\qquad \sigma_p(N) = \lim_{h\to\infty} \frac{\#\{(x_1,\ldots,x_s) \in (\mathbb{Z}/p^h\mathbb{Z})^s : \sum x_i^k \equiv N\pmod{p^h}\}}{p^{h(s-1)}}.$$ That is, $\sigma_p(N)$ is the $p$-adic density of solutions to $\sum x_i^k = N$ modulo every power of $p$. Likewise the singular integral $J(N)$ is the archimedean density at the real place.

Hasse-style criterion. For $s$ sufficiently large depending on $k$, Waring's equation $$N = x_1^k + \cdots + x_s^k$$ has integer solutions iff it has solutions modulo every $p^h$ and solutions in $\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$.

The archimedean condition is trivial for $N\ge 0$ (just take any $x$). The prime conditions are the interesting ones:

  • Odd primes $p$. For $s$ large (concretely $s\ge k+1$ usually suffices, and always $s\ge 2k$), Hensel's lemma reduces to solvability mod $p$, which is automatic for any $N$ by a pigeonhole ("every residue is a sum of $k$-th powers mod $p$ for $s$ large enough").
  • $p = 2$. This is the obstruction source. For $k=2$ the excluded set is $\{4^a(8b+7)\}$ (Legendre). For $k=4$ one has the classical congruence $n^4 \equiv 0$ or $1 \pmod{16}$, so $s\ge 15$ fourth powers are needed just to hit every residue class mod $16$ — hence $G(4) \ge 16$, achieved exactly.
Analogy to Dirichlet series. The singular series is an Euler product indexed by primes, exactly like a Dirichlet $L$-function. On the Dirichlet series page the Euler product encodes multiplicative arithmetic; here it encodes additive arithmetic, but the local-to-global slogan is the same: a global solution exists iff every local solution exists and the infinite product of local densities converges to a positive constant.
Cross-link: sums of squares. The $k=2$ case specializes to Lagrange, Jacobi, and the three-squares theorem of Legendre — developed in full on sums-of-squares.html. Jacobi's exact formula $r_4(N) = 8\sum_{d\mid N,\ 4\nmid d} d$ is the circle-method asymptotic made exact, because the minor arcs vanish (the Eisenstein series $\theta^4$ is a finite sum, not a limit). That is the miracle of the modular-form picture developed on the theta-functions page: for $k=2$, the generating function is itself modular, and the whole singular series collapses to a divisor sum.

7. Forward

Waring's problem is the prototype; the circle method has many descendants:

  • Goldbach. Vinogradov (1937): every sufficiently large odd $N$ is a sum of three primes, by the same machinery with $f(\alpha)=\sum_{p\le N} e(\alpha p)$ (the exponential sum over primes). Weyl's inequality is replaced by Vinogradov's bilinear-form estimate.
  • Waring–Goldbach. Every sufficiently large $N$ satisfying natural congruence conditions is a sum of $s$ $k$-th powers of primes (Hua, Vinogradov).
  • Vinogradov's mean-value theorem and Wooley's efficient-congruencing method. Sharp bounds $G(k)\le k\log k + k\log\log k + O(k)$, implying Waring's problem has the "right" asymptotic count for $s$ just slightly above $k\log k$.
  • The modular-forms alternative. For quadratic forms of rank $\ge 4$ the singular series is a sum over a congruence subgroup, and the generating function is a modular form — the Siegel mass formula is a direct consequence. See theta-functions.html and modular-forms.html.

Two threads recur throughout: the splitting of a unit-circle integral into major and minor arcs, and the Euler-product structure of the main term — the additive cousin of the Euler product for $L$-functions.

Further reading: Vaughan, The Hardy–Littlewood Method, 2nd ed. (Cambridge Tracts, 1997). Wooley, Vinogradov's mean value theorem via efficient congruencing, Ann. Math. 175 (2012).

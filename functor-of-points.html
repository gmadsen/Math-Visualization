The functor of points

A scheme is what it does to rings. Instead of gluing local affines, think of a scheme $X$ as the rule $R \mapsto X(R)$ sending a commutative ring to the set of its $R$-points. This is the change of posture that makes moduli problems natural — and points directly at stacks.

1. The reframe

You already have two pictures of a scheme $X$:

  • a locally ringed space $(|X|, \mathcal{O}_X)$ glued from spectra;
  • the category object $X \in \mathsf{Sch}$, determined up to isomorphism by its morphisms in and out.

The second picture, pushed hard, becomes the functor of points. The Yoneda lemma says the hom-functor $$h_X = \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathsf{Sch}}(-, X) : \mathsf{Sch}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}$$ determines $X$ up to unique isomorphism. Equivalently, by adjunction $\operatorname{Spec} \dashv \Gamma$, we may restrict this functor to affine test objects and regard $X$ as a covariant functor on commutative rings:

$$X \ \leftrightarrow\ h_X\big|_{\mathrm{CRing}} : \mathrm{CRing} \to \mathsf{Set}, \qquad R \mapsto X(R) := \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathsf{Sch}}(\operatorname{Spec} R,\, X).$$
Why this is a reframe, not a repackaging. The usual picture asks "what are the solutions of these equations in a fixed ring $k$?" The functor-of-points picture asks "what is the rule that takes any ring $R$ and returns the set of $R$-solutions?" The solution rule — with its naturality in $R$ — is the scheme.

A morphism of schemes $X \to Y$ is, in this language, a natural transformation $h_X \Rightarrow h_Y$, i.e. a family of set maps $X(R) \to Y(R)$ compatible with ring homs $R \to R'$.

Two reasons to bother:

  1. Moduli problems arrive as functors. "Elliptic curves over $R$," "vector bundles on $S$ of rank $n$," "subschemes of $\mathbb{P}^n$ of Hilbert polynomial $P$" — each is naturally a functor $\mathrm{CRing} \to \mathsf{Set}$ or into groupoids. Asking whether it's a scheme means asking whether the functor is representable.
  2. Constructions become algebra. Fibered products, group schemes, affine bundles, loop spaces — each is trivial to describe at the level of $R$-points, then lifted by representability.

2. Worked example: an affine cubic

Let $X = \operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{Z}[x,y]/(y^2 - x^3 - 1)$. Because $X$ is affine, its $R$-points are spectacularly concrete:

$$X(R) \;=\; \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathrm{CRing}}(\mathbb{Z}[x,y]/(y^2-x^3-1),\, R) \;=\; \{(a,b) \in R^2 : b^2 = a^3 + 1\}.$$

So the functor $X$ is the solution-set functor of the affine equation $y^2 = x^3 + 1$. Two examples you can verify by hand:

  • $X(\mathbb{F}_2) = \{(0,1),(1,0)\}$. Two points.
  • $X(\mathbb{F}_3) = \{(0,1),(0,2),(2,0),(2,1),(2,2)\}$. Five points. Wait, check $(2,1)$: $1 = 8 + 1 = 9 \equiv 0 \not= 1$. Retract that. The widget below does the brute-force properly.
The point. As the ring $R$ varies, $X(R)$ is literally the set of solutions in $R$. There is no hidden infrastructure: the scheme is this rule, and a morphism $X \to Y$ is a rule that turns $R$-solutions of one equation into $R$-solutions of the other, naturally in $R$.

3. Representable functors

A functor $F : \mathrm{CRing} \to \mathsf{Set}$ is representable if there is a scheme $X$ with a natural isomorphism $F \cong h_X$. When $X$ is affine, $X = \operatorname{Spec} A$ for some ring $A$, and Yoneda plus the $\operatorname{Spec} \dashv \Gamma$ adjunction collapses to:

$$F \text{ representable by } \operatorname{Spec} A \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad F(R) \cong \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathrm{CRing}}(A, R) \text{ naturally in } R.$$

That is: representing a set-valued functor on rings means writing its value on $R$ as ring homs out of a fixed ring $A$. The universal element of $F$ is the image of $\mathrm{id}_A$; every other element of $F(R)$ is obtained by "plugging" the universal element into the map $A \to R$.

Example. The affine line $\mathbb{A}^1$. As a functor, $\mathbb{A}^1(R) = R$ (the underlying set of $R$). Representable by $\mathbb{Z}[t]$: a homomorphism $\mathbb{Z}[t] \to R$ is just a choice of element of $R$.
Example. The multiplicative group $\mathbb{G}_m$. $\mathbb{G}_m(R) = R^\times$. Representable by $\mathbb{Z}[t, t^{-1}]$: forcing $t$ to become a unit.

Representability is a theorem, not a definition: you exhibit the pair $(A, \xi)$ where $\xi \in F(A)$ is universal, and then verify that "map out of $A$" is really the same as "element of $F(R)$."

Yoneda uniqueness. If both $X$ and $Y$ represent $F$, then $h_X \cong h_Y$, hence $X \cong Y$ in $\mathsf{Sch}$ by Yoneda. The representing scheme is unique up to unique isomorphism.

4. $\mathrm{GL}_n$ as a functor, first

The cleanest advertisement for the functor-of-points posture is $\mathrm{GL}_n$. Define it by what it does to rings:

$$\mathrm{GL}_n(R) \;=\; \{\,n\times n \text{ matrices } M \text{ with entries in } R \text{ such that } \det(M) \in R^\times\,\}.$$

This is clearly a functor (ring maps induce matrix maps preserving units). Is it representable? Yes — by

$$\mathrm{GL}_n \;\cong\; \operatorname{Spec}\mathbb{Z}[x_{11}, \ldots, x_{nn}, t] \big/ (t \cdot \det(X) - 1).$$

The variable $t$ is a formal inverse of the determinant; forcing $t \cdot \det = 1$ makes $\det$ a unit. Note how much less painful this is than trying to describe $\mathrm{GL}_n$ as a locally ringed space first.

5. Moduli problems as functors

The functor-of-points language is most useful when the functor is natural but the space isn't yet constructed. A moduli problem is exactly this: define a functor whose $R$-points are "families of objects of type $T$ over $\operatorname{Spec} R$, up to isomorphism," then ask whether it's representable.

The testbed is the moduli of elliptic curves $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$:

$$\mathcal{M}_{1,1}(R) \;=\; \left\{ \begin{array}{c} \text{elliptic curves } E/R \\ \text{(smooth proper genus-1 with a section)} \end{array} \right\} \Big/ \cong_R.$$

Over an algebraically closed field, elliptic curves are classified up to isomorphism by the $j$-invariant, so one hopes $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ should "be" $\mathbb{A}^1_j$. Over a general ring the situation is subtler, but it is irresistible to slide a parameter and watch a family of curves.

Imagine repeating this for every commutative ring $R$. The functor $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ sends $R$ to isomorphism classes of elliptic curves over $R$. This looks good: sending a parameter family over $\operatorname{Spec} R$ to its isomorphism class is clearly functorial in $R$. The shock is that $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ is not representable as a scheme.

6. Why representability fails

The obstruction is automorphisms. A generic elliptic curve has only the automorphism $[-1]$ (order 2). But certain curves have extra automorphisms:

  • $y^2 = x^3 + x$ has $\mathrm{Aut} \cong \mathbb{Z}/4$ (coming from $x \mapsto -x$, $y \mapsto \pm i y$), over any field containing $i$.
  • $y^2 = x^3 + 1$ has $\mathrm{Aut} \cong \mathbb{Z}/6$ (via $x \mapsto \zeta_3 x$), over any field containing $\zeta_3$.

Here is the precise form of the obstruction. Suppose $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ were representable, say $\mathcal{M}_{1,1} \cong h_M$ for a scheme $M$. Then for any scheme $S$ and any family of elliptic curves $\mathcal{E} \to S$, there would be a unique classifying morphism $S \to M$. In particular, given an isomorphism class of family $[\mathcal{E}]$, the family itself would be reconstructible up to isomorphism from its classifying map.

It isn't. You can glue two families along different isomorphisms of their fibers and get non-isomorphic total spaces with the same "pointwise" classifying data — a non-trivial twist by a $\mathrm{Aut}$-valued cocycle.

W6 · the automorphism twist
two families over a 2-point base
The sheaf-of-sets argument also fails. One might hope to fix this by sheafifying the presheaf of isomorphism classes. But the isomorphism-class assignment is not a sheaf: for a cover $\{U_i \to S\}$, specifying a compatible family of classes on each $U_i$ does not uniquely assemble to a class on $S$ — the gluing requires remembering the automorphisms you used to identify overlaps. Sheafification literally forgets this information, producing a functor that no longer captures the moduli problem.

This is the content of the slogan "moduli of objects with automorphisms is not a scheme." The presence of $\mathrm{Aut} \neq 1$ on some fibers is not a nuisance to be quotiented away; it is structural data that the functor-of-points-to-SETS cannot remember.

Contrast with a rigidified problem: elliptic curves with full level-$N$ structure (a basis for the $N$-torsion) for $N \ge 3$ has no automorphisms (over a base where $N$ is invertible), and the corresponding moduli functor is representable — by the modular curve $Y(N)$. The automorphism obstruction has been eliminated by adding structure.

7. Preview: the fix is to target groupoids

The moral: if your objects have automorphisms, sets are the wrong target. The answer is to replace

$$\mathrm{CRing} \longrightarrow \mathsf{Set}$$

by

$$\mathrm{CRing} \longrightarrow \mathsf{Grpd},$$

sending $R$ to the groupoid of elliptic curves over $R$ with their isomorphisms. A lax-functorial, sheafy version of this is an algebraic stack. The scheme / functor-of-points story is the special case where every object has trivial automorphism groupoid.

Sketch. An algebraic stack is a category fibered in groupoids $\mathcal{X} \to \mathsf{Sch}$ satisfying a descent condition plus a representability / smoothness condition on the diagonal. Morphisms $S \to \mathcal{X}$ are not "elements of a set" but objects of the groupoid fiber $\mathcal{X}(S)$, and two such morphisms are equal when they are isomorphic. The isomorphisms are now encoded, so the sheaf condition returns.

The twist widget above is the key picture: the "twisted family" and the "product family" had the same classes at each point, but they are distinct objects of the groupoid fiber, because gluing by $[-1]$ is a different 2-arrow from gluing by $\mathrm{id}$. Upgrading the target category captures exactly that information.

The functor-of-points perspective is then the base camp from which stacks become a modest and natural extension — not a new abstraction, but the same game played in a 2-category.

8. The Yoneda embedding for schemes

The functor-of-points reframe of §1 is justified by a single theorem: the assignment

$$h_{(-)}: \mathsf{Sch} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Fun}(\mathsf{Sch}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathsf{Set}), \qquad X \longmapsto h_X = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathsf{Sch}}(-, X)$$

is fully faithful. This is the Yoneda lemma in its representable form: for any presheaf $F$ on $\mathsf{Sch}$, $\mathrm{Nat}(h_X, F) \cong F(X)$ naturally in $X$, with the isomorphism sending a natural transformation $\eta$ to $\eta_X(\mathrm{id}_X) \in F(X)$. Taking $F = h_Y$ yields $\mathrm{Nat}(h_X, h_Y) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathsf{Sch}}(X,Y)$, so a natural transformation between representable functors is the same data as a scheme morphism — no more, no less.

Two consequences make this workhorse. First, to define a morphism $X \to Y$ you may equivalently give a rule that to each test ring $R$ assigns a function $X(R) \to Y(R)$ natural in $R$; second, to define a scheme at all it is sometimes cleaner to write down the functor $R \mapsto X(R)$ directly and then prove representability by exhibiting a representing scheme. The identity $\mathrm{id}_X \in h_X(X)$ is the universal element: every $R$-point $x \in X(R)$ is the pushforward of $\mathrm{id}_X$ along the classifying map $\mathrm{Spec}\,R \to X$, and every natural transformation out of $h_X$ is determined by its value there.

Why "embedding". Full faithfulness means $\mathsf{Sch}$ sits inside presheaves on rings as a reflective subcategory. Presheaves that land in that subcategory are exactly the representable ones — equivalently, those satisfying Zariski (or fppf, fpqc, …) descent plus a local representability condition. Objects that satisfy descent but are not representable are precisely the algebraic spaces and stacks from §7: the embedding picture tells you where the generalization lives.

9. Base change as pullback of functors

Representable functors preserve limits, so fiber products of schemes are computed pointwise on their functors of points:

$$(X \times_S Y)(T) \;=\; X(T) \times_{S(T)} Y(T).$$

Nothing about this equality is subtle — both sides are sets and it is an identity of sets, natural in the test scheme $T$. But the consequences for base change are immediate. Given $X \to S$ and a morphism $S' \to S$, the base-changed scheme $X_{S'} = X \times_S S'$ has functor of points $X_{S'}(T) = X(T) \times_{S(T)} S'(T)$; for affine $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,R$ and $S = \mathrm{Spec}\,k$ this simplifies to $X_k(R) = X(R)$ where $R$ is regarded with its given $k$-algebra structure. Extending scalars is literally restricting the functor to a slice category.

Three practical payoffs. (i) Computing fibers. The fiber $X_s$ of $X \to S$ over a point $s: \mathrm{Spec}\,\kappa(s) \to S$ has $X_s(R) = \{x \in X(R) : x \text{ maps to } s\}$ — exactly solving the original equations over the residue field. (ii) Weil restriction. If $L/k$ is a finite extension and $X$ is an $L$-scheme, the Weil restriction $\mathrm{Res}_{L/k} X$ represents the functor $R \mapsto X(R \otimes_k L)$; the functor of points makes this definition, not a theorem. (iii) Twists and descent. Base-change agreement between two schemes over $L$ that become isomorphic over $\bar k$ is exactly equality of their functors restricted to $\bar k$-algebras, with Galois descent controlling when the common functor descends to a $k$-scheme.

Worked fiber. Let $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2 - p)$ with the obvious map to $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$. For a prime $\ell \ne p$ with $p$ a square mod $\ell$, the fiber $X_{\mathbb{F}_\ell}$ has $X_{\mathbb{F}_\ell}(\mathbb{F}_\ell) = \{\pm\sqrt{p}\} \subset \mathbb{F}_\ell$, two points; for $\ell = p$ it has the single fat point $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_p[x]/(x^2)$. The functor of points reads these off without setting up any sheaves.

Symplectic manifolds

A symplectic manifold is a smooth even-dimensional space carrying a closed non-degenerate 2-form $\omega$. That single piece of structure encodes all of Hamiltonian mechanics: vector fields from energy functions, conservation laws, canonical transformations, and — through Floer's loop-space construction — a hands-on homology theory whose ranks bound the periodic orbits of any Hamiltonian.

1. Symplectic form

Fix a smooth manifold $M$. A symplectic form on $M$ is a 2-form $\omega\in\Omega^2(M)$ satisfying two conditions:

  • Closed: $d\omega = 0$.
  • Non-degenerate: for every $p\in M$, the bilinear form $\omega_p\colon T_pM\times T_pM\to\mathbb{R}$ is non-degenerate. Equivalently, $v\mapsto \iota_v\omega_p$ is an isomorphism $T_pM\to T_p^*M$.

Non-degeneracy of an alternating bilinear form forces even dimension: an antisymmetric matrix in odd size has zero determinant. So $\dim M = 2n$, and the top exterior power $\omega^n = \omega\wedge\cdots\wedge\omega$ is a nowhere-vanishing $2n$-form — a canonical volume form. In particular every symplectic manifold is orientable.

Examples to keep in mind.
  • $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ with $\omega_0 = \sum_i dp_i\wedge dq_i$ (the model).
  • The cotangent bundle $T^*N$ with $\omega = -d\lambda$, where $\lambda = \sum p_i\,dq_i$ is the Liouville 1-form. This is the symplectic stage of classical mechanics on configuration space $N$.
  • $S^2$ with its area form. Any orientable surface is symplectic.
  • $\mathbb{C}P^n$ with the Fubini–Study form (it's Kähler).
  • Counterexample: $S^4$ admits no symplectic form (any closed $\omega$ on $S^4$ is exact, and exact forms can't have non-vanishing $\omega^2$).

2. Darboux's theorem

The first thing to ask about any geometric structure is: what are its local invariants? For Riemannian metrics it's curvature; for complex structures it's nothing in the integrable case (Newlander–Nirenberg) but plenty in general. For symplectic forms the answer is nothing at all.

Darboux's theorem. Let $(M^{2n},\omega)$ be symplectic. Around every point $p\in M$ there is a chart $(p_1,\dots,p_n,q_1,\dots,q_n)$ with $p\mapsto 0$ in which $$\omega \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n dp_i\wedge dq_i.$$ Equivalently, every symplectic $2n$-manifold is locally symplectomorphic to $(\mathbb{R}^{2n},\omega_0)$.

The proof is a Moser-trick deformation. Pick a chart taking $\omega_p$ to $\omega_0$ at the origin (linear algebra: any non-degenerate alternating form on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ is $\omega_0$ in suitable coordinates). Define $\omega_t = (1-t)\omega_0 + t\omega$ for $t\in[0,1]$. Both endpoints are symplectic at the origin; by closedness write $\omega - \omega_0 = d\alpha$ on a small ball, then solve $\iota_{X_t}\omega_t = -\alpha$ for a time-dependent vector field $X_t$. Its flow is a diffeomorphism $\Phi$ with $\Phi^*\omega = \omega_0$. Done.

3. Hamiltonian vector fields and flows

Non-degeneracy of $\omega$ is the bridge from functions to dynamics. Given a smooth $H\colon M\to\mathbb{R}$, its differential $dH$ is a 1-form. Apply the inverse of $v\mapsto\iota_v\omega$:

Definition. The Hamiltonian vector field $X_H$ associated to $H$ is the unique vector field satisfying $$\iota_{X_H}\,\omega \;=\; dH.$$

In Darboux coordinates $\omega = \sum dp_i\wedge dq_i$, the equation unwraps to Hamilton's equations: $$\dot q_i \;=\; \tfrac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \qquad \dot p_i \;=\; -\tfrac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}.$$ Two facts come for free:

  • Energy conservation. $\dot H = X_H(H) = dH(X_H) = \omega(X_H,X_H) = 0$ by antisymmetry. $H$ is constant along trajectories.
  • Symplectic flow. By Cartan's formula $\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\omega = d\iota_{X_H}\omega + \iota_{X_H}d\omega = d(dH) + 0 = 0$. So the time-$t$ flow $\Phi_t^{X_H}$ is a symplectomorphism — Liouville's theorem.

4. Poisson bracket

Hamiltonian vector fields close under the Lie bracket, but the right way to see this is via a bracket on functions.

Definition. The Poisson bracket of $f,g\in C^\infty(M)$ is $$\{f,g\} \;=\; \omega(X_f, X_g) \;=\; X_f(g) \;=\; -X_g(f).$$

In Darboux coordinates this is $\{f,g\} = \sum_i \big(\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\tfrac{\partial g}{\partial q_i} - \tfrac{\partial f}{\partial q_i}\tfrac{\partial g}{\partial p_i}\big)$ — the formula from classical mechanics textbooks. Three structural identities:

  • Antisymmetry: $\{f,g\} = -\{g,f\}$.
  • Leibniz: $\{f,gh\} = \{f,g\}h + g\{f,h\}$.
  • Jacobi: $\{f,\{g,h\}\} + \{g,\{h,f\}\} + \{h,\{f,g\}\} = 0$.

Antisymmetry is automatic; Leibniz follows because $X_f$ is a derivation; Jacobi is the deepest — it depends on $d\omega = 0$. So $C^\infty(M)$ becomes a Poisson algebra (commutative under pointwise product, Lie under $\{\cdot,\cdot\}$, with the two operations linked by Leibniz).

Quantisation. Dirac's correspondence sends $f\mapsto \hat f$ with $$\{f,g\} \;\leadsto\; \frac{[\hat f,\hat g]}{i\hbar}.$$ The quantum commutator is the classical bracket plus $O(\hbar)$ corrections. This is why classical mechanics is the limit of quantum mechanics — the algebraic structures are the same, with $\hbar\to 0$ flattening the operators back to functions.

5. Lagrangian submanifolds

The right "subobjects" of a symplectic manifold are not symplectic submanifolds (those exist but are rare and rigid) but Lagrangian submanifolds — half-dimensional and totally isotropic.

Definition. A submanifold $L\subset (M^{2n},\omega)$ is Lagrangian if $\dim L = n$ and $\omega|_L = 0$ (i.e. $\omega(u,v)=0$ for all $u,v\in T_pL$ and all $p\in L$).

Half-dimensionality is forced by maximality: an isotropic subspace of $(T_pM,\omega_p)$ has dimension at most $n$, and equality is the Lagrangian condition. Two key examples:

  1. Cotangent bundle. In $T^*N$ with canonical form $\omega = -d\lambda$, the zero section $\{(x,0):x\in N\}$ is Lagrangian. More generally the graph $\Gamma_\alpha = \{(x,\alpha_x)\}$ of a 1-form $\alpha\in\Omega^1(N)$ is Lagrangian iff $d\alpha = 0$, i.e. iff $\alpha$ is closed. So Lagrangians near the zero section correspond to closed 1-forms — the link to de Rham theory.
  2. Graphs of symplectomorphisms. Equip $M_1\times M_2$ with $\omega_1\ominus\omega_2 := \pi_1^*\omega_1 - \pi_2^*\omega_2$. The graph $\Gamma_f = \{(x,f(x))\}\subset M_1\times M_2$ is Lagrangian iff $f\colon M_1\to M_2$ is a symplectomorphism. Canonical relations (general Lagrangians of $M_1\times M_2$) compose to give a symplectic category.

Weinstein's slogan: "everything is Lagrangian." Periodic orbits are intersections with a Lagrangian; integrable systems foliate $M$ by Lagrangian tori (Liouville–Arnold); generating functions describe symplectomorphisms by the Lagrangian graph in a cotangent bundle. Mirror symmetry trades Lagrangians on one side for coherent sheaves on the other.

Generic non-closed forms like $f(\theta)d\theta$ with $f$ non-constant give Lagrangian graphs only when $f$ is a constant — that's the closedness obstruction.

6. Floer homology — a preview

Symplectic topology contains rigid theorems that look impossible from the differential-topology side. The cleanest example is the Arnold conjecture:

Arnold conjecture (Floer's theorem). Let $(M,\omega)$ be a closed symplectic manifold and $H_t$ a non-degenerate time-1-periodic Hamiltonian. The number of 1-periodic orbits of $X_{H_t}$ is at least $\sum_k b_k(M)$.

This is a Morse inequality on an infinite-dimensional space. Floer made it rigorous as follows.

Define the symplectic action functional on the free loop space $\mathcal{L}M$ (loops $\gamma\colon S^1\to M$): $$\mathcal{A}_H(\gamma) \;=\; -\int_{D^2}\bar u^*\omega \;-\; \int_0^1 H(t,\gamma(t))\,dt,$$ where $\bar u\colon D^2\to M$ is any extension of $\gamma$ (the multivaluedness of the first term is mild and controlled). The critical points of $\mathcal{A}_H$ are exactly the 1-periodic orbits of $X_{H_t}$. The "$L^2$-gradient" of $\mathcal{A}_H$ with respect to a compatible almost-complex structure $J$ is the Cauchy–Riemann operator: gradient flow lines from orbit $x$ to orbit $y$ are pseudo-holomorphic cylinders $$\partial_s u + J(u)(\partial_t u - X_{H_t}) \;=\; 0$$ joining $x$ at $s=-\infty$ to $y$ at $s=+\infty$. Counting them mod the obvious $\mathbb{R}$-action gives a chain complex $$\partial x \;=\; \sum_{\mu(x,y)=1} \#\widehat{\mathcal{M}}(x,y)\cdot y,$$ and Floer proved $\partial^2 = 0$ and $HF_*(M;H,J) \cong H_*(M)$.

The Lagrangian variant works between two Lagrangians $L_0,L_1\subset M$. Generators: transverse intersections $L_0\cap L_1$. Differentials: pseudo-holomorphic strips with boundary on $L_0$ and $L_1$. The resulting $HF_*(L_0,L_1)$ is the morphism space in the Fukaya category — the symplectic side of homological mirror symmetry.

7. Connections

Symplectic geometry sits at a crossroads. Locally trivial (Darboux) but globally rigid (Gromov non-squeezing, Floer-theoretic obstructions), it is the natural language for both classical mechanics and a swathe of modern topology — and the open problems are dense. The symplectic isotopy question, the existence of exotic symplectic structures on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, the Weinstein conjecture on Reeb orbits, and homological mirror symmetry are all live research frontiers.

  • Differential forms — de Rham cohomology — closedness of $\omega$ gives a class $[\omega]\in H^2(M;\mathbb{R})$; its powers detect symplectic obstructions in cohomology.
  • Smooth manifolds — integral curves and flows — Hamiltonian flows are the worked example that motivates the whole apparatus of vector-field flows.
  • Morse theory — applications — Floer homology is "Morse theory on the loop space"; the parallel is exact down to chain-complex structure.
  • Differential geometry — almost-complex structures, the Levi-Civita connection on the loop space, and the analytic estimates underlying Floer.
  • Lie groups — coadjoint orbits are symplectic (Kirillov–Kostant–Souriau), giving the geometric input to the orbit method in representation theory.

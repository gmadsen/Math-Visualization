K-theory

Vector bundles modulo direct sum, formally inverted: a generalised cohomology theory whose values are stable equivalence classes of bundles. Bott periodicity makes it $2$-periodic for complex theories and $8$-periodic for real ones; the Chern character converts it rationally back to ordinary cohomology; Quillen's $+$-construction extends it from rings to a full hierarchy of higher invariants $K_n(R)$.

1. Topological $K^0(X)$ from vector bundles

Let $X$ be a compact Hausdorff space and $\mathrm{Vect}_\mathbb{C}(X)$ the set of isomorphism classes of complex vector bundles on $X$. Direct sum $\oplus$ makes it an abelian monoid, but cancellation can fail: there exist bundles $E,F,G$ with $E\oplus G\cong F\oplus G$ yet $E\not\cong F$ (this is "stable but not unstable equivalence" — the failure is genuine on $S^7$, for instance).

The Grothendieck group $K^0(X)$ is the universal abelian group receiving a monoid map from $\mathrm{Vect}_\mathbb{C}(X)$: $$K^0(X) \;=\; \{\,[E]-[F]\,\}/\!\sim, \qquad [E]-[F]\sim[E']-[F'] \iff E\oplus F'\oplus G \cong E'\oplus F\oplus G \text{ for some }G.$$ Concretely every element is a formal difference of bundle classes; two differences agree iff they become equal after stabilising by a common bundle.

$K^0$ inherits much more than the abelian group law:

  • Ring structure. Tensor product distributes over direct sum: $(E\oplus E')\otimes F\cong (E\otimes F)\oplus(E'\otimes F)$. Setting $[E]\cdot[F]=[E\otimes F]$ makes $K^0(X)$ a commutative ring with unit $[\mathbf 1]$ (the trivial line bundle).
  • Functoriality. A continuous $f\colon Y\to X$ induces the ring homomorphism $f^*\colon K^0(X)\to K^0(Y)$, $f^*[E]=[f^*E]$.
  • Augmentation. The rank gives a ring map $\dim\colon K^0(X)\to\mathbb{Z}$; its kernel is the reduced K-theory $\widetilde K^0(X)$.
Computation. $K^0(\mathrm{pt})=\mathbb{Z}$ (bundles on a point are vector spaces, classified by dimension). $K^0(S^2)=\mathbb{Z}\oplus\mathbb{Z}$, generated by $[\mathbf 1]$ and the Bott class $[\mathcal{O}(1)]-[\mathbf 1]$. 2. Grothendieck group of an exact category

Quillen's elegance: replace "monoid + group completion" with the more flexible exact category picture. An exact category $\mathcal{C}$ is an additive category equipped with a class of distinguished short exact sequences $0\to A\to B\to C\to 0$ (e.g. all SES in an abelian category, or split SES of vector bundles).

The Grothendieck group $K_0(\mathcal{C})$ is then $$K_0(\mathcal{C}) \;=\; \mathbb{Z}\langle\,[X] : X\in\mathcal{C}\,\rangle \,/\, \big([B]-[A]-[C]\;\text{for every distinguished SES}\big).$$ This recovers the bundle picture and many more:

category $\mathcal{C}$$K_0(\mathcal{C})$by what
$\mathrm{Vect}_\mathbb{C}(X)$, split SES$K^0(X)$SES of bundles split (orthogonal complement)
$\mathbf{Proj}(R)$, fin. gen. projective $R$-modules$K_0(R)$algebraic $K_0$ of a ring
$\mathrm{Coh}(X)$ on a scheme $X$$G_0(X)$K-theory with coefficients in coherent sheaves
$\mathbf{Mod}^{\mathrm{fg}}(\mathbb{Z})$$\mathbb{Z}$$[\mathbb{Z}/n]=0$ from $0\to\mathbb{Z}\xrightarrow{n}\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{Z}/n\to 0$
The vanishing of $[\mathbb{Z}/n]$. In $K_0(\mathbf{Mod}^{\mathrm{fg}}(\mathbb{Z}))$ the SES $0\to\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{Z}/n\to 0$ forces $[\mathbb{Z}/n]=[\mathbb{Z}]-[\mathbb{Z}]=0$. So torsion modules contribute nothing — $K_0$ sees only the rank, and the map "rank" $\colon K_0\to\mathbb{Z}$ is an iso.

The price of allowing all SES (not just split) is exactly that extensions kill differences. For projective $R$-modules every SES splits, so $K_0(R)$ counts projectives up to stable equivalence — the projective class group, $\widetilde K_0(R)=K_0(R)/\mathbb{Z}[R]$, captures non-free projectives. For Dedekind domains $\widetilde K_0(\mathcal{O}_F)=\mathrm{Cl}(F)$, the ideal class group.

3. Bott periodicity

The deep theorem of complex K-theory, due to Bott (1959) and reformulated by Atiyah–Bott (1964): there is a natural isomorphism $$\widetilde K^0(X) \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} \widetilde K^0(\Sigma^2 X), \qquad \alpha \mapsto \beta\boxtimes\alpha,$$ where $\beta=[\mathcal{O}(1)]-[\mathbf 1]\in\widetilde K^0(S^2)$ is the Bott class (the reduced class of the Hopf line bundle on $\mathbb{CP}^1$) and $\boxtimes$ is the external product. Equivalently $K^n(X)\cong K^{n+2}(X)$ for all $n$.

Three immediate consequences:

  • $\mathbb{Z}/2$-graded cohomology theory. $K^*(X)$ is determined by just two groups: $K^0(X)$ and $K^1(X)\cong\widetilde K^0(SX)$. For a finite CW complex these admit a Mayer–Vietoris exact hexagon.
  • Computation of spheres. $\widetilde K^0(S^{2k})\cong\mathbb{Z}$ generated by $\beta^k$; $\widetilde K^0(S^{2k+1})=0$. Compare with ordinary cohomology, $\widetilde H^*(S^n;\mathbb{Z})=\mathbb{Z}$ in degree $n$, zero elsewhere — K-theory packages all the even spheres into one periodic pattern.
  • Real version. Real K-theory $KO$ has period $8$ — the surprise that ultimately drove Adams to settle the Hopf invariant one problem.
Bott's homotopical statement. Let $U=\varinjlim U(n)$ be the infinite unitary group. Then $$\Omega U \;\simeq\; \mathbb{Z}\times BU, \qquad \Omega^2 U \;\simeq\; U.$$ The homotopy groups $\pi_n(U)$ thus satisfy $\pi_n(U)\cong\pi_{n+2}(U)$, with values $0,\mathbb{Z},0,\mathbb{Z},\ldots$ starting in degree $0$.

4. Chern character

K-theory is finer than cohomology integrally, but rationally they agree. The bridge is the Chern character, the unique natural ring homomorphism $$\mathrm{ch}\colon K^0(X)\to H^{\mathrm{even}}(X;\mathbb{Q})$$ extending $\mathrm{ch}([L])=e^{c_1(L)}=1+c_1(L)+\tfrac{1}{2}c_1(L)^2+\cdots$ for line bundles. By the splitting principle, on a bundle $E$ with formal Chern roots $x_1,\ldots,x_n$: $$\mathrm{ch}(E) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n e^{x_i} \;=\; n + c_1(E) + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(c_1(E)^2 - 2c_2(E)\right) + \tfrac{1}{6}\!\left(c_1^3 - 3c_1c_2 + 3c_3\right) + \cdots.$$

The exponential is forced by the two structural compatibilities:

  • Additivity: $\mathrm{ch}(E\oplus F)=\mathrm{ch}(E)+\mathrm{ch}(F)$, since the roots of $E\oplus F$ are the union of roots.
  • Multiplicativity: $\mathrm{ch}(E\otimes F)=\mathrm{ch}(E)\smile\mathrm{ch}(F)$, since $E\otimes F$ has roots $x_i+y_j$ and $e^{x_i+y_j}=e^{x_i}e^{y_j}$.
Atiyah–Hirzebruch. Tensoring with $\mathbb{Q}$, the Chern character becomes an isomorphism $$\mathrm{ch}\otimes\mathbb{Q}\colon K^0(X)\otimes\mathbb{Q} \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; H^{\mathrm{even}}(X;\mathbb{Q}) \qquad (\text{finite CW }X).$$ The Atiyah–Hirzebruch spectral sequence $E_2^{p,q}=H^p(X;K^q(\mathrm{pt}))\Rightarrow K^{p+q}(X)$ collapses rationally — over $\mathbb{Q}$, K-theory is no fancier than cohomology. The genuinely new content of K-theory is its integral torsion.

For line bundles $\mathrm{ch}(L)=e^{c_1(L)}$, so on $\mathbb{CP}^n$ with $c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))=H$ the generator, $$\mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{O}(d)) \;=\; e^{dH} \;=\; 1 + dH + \tfrac{d^2}{2}H^2 + \cdots + \tfrac{d^n}{n!}H^n.$$ The coefficients $d^k/k!$ are not integers — the Chern character genuinely lives in $H^*(\mathbb{CP}^n;\mathbb{Q})$, not $\mathbb{Z}$.

5. Algebraic K-theory $K_n(R)$

For a ring $R$, the low K-groups have hands-on descriptions:

grouppresentationexample
$K_0(R)$Grothendieck group of fin. gen. projective modules$K_0(\mathbb{Z})=\mathbb{Z}$, $K_0(\mathcal{O}_F)=\mathbb{Z}\oplus\mathrm{Cl}(F)$
$K_1(R)$$GL_\infty(R)^{\mathrm{ab}} = GL_\infty(R)/E(R)$$K_1(\mathbb{Z})=\{\pm 1\}$; $K_1(\mathbb{F}_q)=\mathbb{F}_q^\times$
$K_2(R)$$\ker\bigl(\mathrm{St}(R)\twoheadrightarrow E(R)\bigr)$$K_2(\mathbb{Z})=\mathbb{Z}/2$; $K_2(F)=F^\times\!\otimes\!F^\times/\langle a\otimes(1-a)\rangle$ (Matsumoto)

These three were defined separately and unified by Quillen (1972) via the $+$-construction: $$K_n(R) \;=\; \pi_n\bigl(BGL(R)^+\bigr), \qquad n\ge 1,$$ together with $K_0(R)=\pi_0$. Here $BGL(R)$ is the classifying space of the infinite general linear group, and $X\mapsto X^+$ is a homotopy modification that abelianises $\pi_1$ by killing a perfect normal subgroup (here $E(R)\subset GL_\infty(R)$, the elementary subgroup) without changing integral homology.

Whitehead's lemma. $E(R)$ — the subgroup generated by elementary matrices $e_{ij}(r)=I+r\,E_{ij}$ — equals the commutator subgroup $[GL_\infty(R),GL_\infty(R)]$. So $\pi_1(BGL(R)^+)=GL_\infty(R)/E(R)=K_1(R)$, recovering the original Bass definition.

The Steinberg group $\mathrm{St}(R)$ is the universal central extension of $E(R)$. The kernel of $\mathrm{St}(R)\twoheadrightarrow E(R)$ is $K_2(R)$ — Milnor's definition — and equals $\pi_2(BGL(R)^+)$ in Quillen's framework.

Quillen's calculation of $K_*(\mathbb{F}_q)$. A virtuoso application of Brauer lifting and the homology of finite groups of Lie type: $$K_{2k}(\mathbb{F}_q) = 0, \qquad K_{2k-1}(\mathbb{F}_q) = \mathbb{Z}/(q^k-1) \quad (k\ge 1).$$ This is the input that combines, via localisation, with class field theory to produce Borel's calculation of $K_n(\mathcal{O}_F)\otimes\mathbb{Q}$.

6. Applications: index theorem and $K_*(\mathcal{O}_F)$

K-theory's two cardinal applications come from its two faces — topological and algebraic.

The Atiyah–Singer index theorem

Let $D\colon\Gamma(E)\to\Gamma(F)$ be an elliptic differential operator between vector bundles on a closed manifold $M$. Two integers attach to $D$:

  • The analytic index $\ind(D)=\dim\ker D - \dim\mathrm{coker}\, D$, finite by ellipticity.
  • The topological index: the symbol $\sigma(D)\colon\pi^*E\to\pi^*F$ (a bundle map on $T^*M\setminus 0$, an iso outside the zero section) defines a class $$[\sigma(D)] \;\in\; K^0(T^*M).$$

Atiyah–Singer (1963): $$\boxed{\;\ind(D) \;=\; \bigl\langle\,\mathrm{ch}([\sigma(D)])\smile\mathrm{Td}(TM\otimes\mathbb{C}),\,[T^*M]\,\bigr\rangle.\;}$$ The Chern character and Todd class together translate the K-class into an integral cohomology pairing — and the answer is the analytical invariant. Special cases recover Gauss–Bonnet ($D=d+d^*$, even forms vs odd), Hirzebruch signature ($D$ on self-dual vs anti-self-dual forms), and Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch ($D=\bar\partial$ on a holomorphic bundle).

K-theory of rings of integers

For $F$ a number field with $r_1$ real and $r_2$ complex places, Borel (1974) computed: $$\mathrm{rk}_\mathbb{Z}\, K_n(\mathcal{O}_F) \;=\; \begin{cases} 1 & n=0 \\ r_1+r_2-1 & n=1 \\ 0 & n\ge 2,\;n\text{ even} \\ r_1+r_2 & n\equiv 1\pmod 4,\;n\ge 5 \\ r_2 & n\equiv 3\pmod 4,\;n\ge 3.\end{cases}$$ The $n=1$ entry is Dirichlet's unit theorem; the higher-degree entries refine it. The proof routes through Quillen's $K_*(\mathbb{F}_q)$ calculation, the localisation sequence $\cdots\to K_n(\mathbb{F}_p)\to K_n(\mathbb{Z}_p)\to K_n(\mathbb{Q}_p)\to\cdots$, and stable cohomology of arithmetic groups.

Connections

K-theory is the prototype of a generalised cohomology theory, and the place where bundle topology, index theory, and the arithmetic of rings of integers all meet a single algebraic invariant. The frontier is broad: motivic K-theory and algebraic cycles (Bloch–Beilinson), $K(1)$- and $K(n)$-local stable homotopy (chromatic), trace methods (TC, THH, cyclotomic spectra by Nikolaus–Scholze), and equivariant / twisted K-theory in mathematical physics.

  • Characteristic classes — Chern classes — the splitting principle and Chern roots are how the Chern character is computed.
  • Algebraic topology — singular cohomology — the receiver of $\mathrm{ch}$, and the host of the Atiyah–Hirzebruch spectral sequence.
  • Homological algebra — exact sequences — the relations defining $K_0$ of an exact category, and the localisation long exact sequence in algebraic K-theory.
  • Differential geometry — connections, curvature, and Chern–Weil supply the analytic representatives entering the index theorem.
  • Class field theory — feeds into Borel's calculation of higher $K_n(\mathcal{O}_F)$ and the regulators conjecturally controlling special values of $\zeta_F$.

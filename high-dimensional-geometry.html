High-dimensional geometry

In high dimension, geometry stops feeling spatial and starts behaving like probability. Lipschitz functions on $S^{n-1}$ concentrate near a single value; convex bodies hide $(\log n)$-dimensional almost-Euclidean sections; random projections preserve distances; and the Gaussian measure is sharper than the sphere. Concentration of measure is the geometric backbone of modern data analysis.

1. Concentration on the sphere

Let $\sigma$ be the uniform probability measure on the unit sphere $S^{n-1}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}$. Lévy's lemma says that for any $1$-Lipschitz $f:S^{n-1}\to\mathbb{R}$ with median $M_{f}=\mathrm{med}\,f$, $$\sigma\bigl(\{x:|f(x)-M_{f}|>t\}\bigr)\le 2\,e^{-(n-1)t^{2}/2}.$$ The deviation scale is $1/\sqrt n$ — Lipschitz functionals are essentially constant on most of the sphere, and the higher the dimension, the more concentrated they get. This is the blessing of dimensionality: random inputs produce predictable outputs.

Generic vectors are nearly orthogonal. If $u,v\sim\sigma$ are independent, $\mathbb{E}[\langle u,v\rangle^{2}]=1/n$, so $|\langle u,v\rangle|\lesssim 1/\sqrt n$ with overwhelming probability. Pick $N=e^{c n}$ random unit vectors and they form an almost-orthonormal system — the foundation of randomised dimension reduction.

Where the constant comes from. Maurey's proof uses the spherical isoperimetric inequality (Schmidt): among Borel sets on $S^{n-1}$ of fixed measure, spherical caps minimise the boundary measure. A $1$-Lipschitz $f$ maps the $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood of $\{f\le M_{f}\}$ inside $\{f\le M_{f}+\varepsilon\}$, and cap volumes obey $\sigma(\text{cap of angular radius }\theta)\le e^{-(n-1)\sin^{2}\theta/2}$.

2. Dvoretzky's theorem

Let $K\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}$ be a centred convex body with associated norm $\|\cdot\|_{K}$. Dvoretzky's theorem (1961, sharpened by Milman 1971) asserts the existence of a subspace $E\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}$ of dimension $$k=k(K,\varepsilon)\ge c\,\varepsilon^{2}\log n$$ such that, for some $r>0$, every $x\in E$ satisfies $$r\,|x|\le\|x\|_{K}\le (1+\varepsilon)\,r\,|x|.$$ That is, every centred convex body has an almost-Euclidean section of logarithmic dimension. The body $\ell^{\infty}_{n}$ shows the $\log n$ scaling is sharp.

Milman's proof in one paragraph. Concentration on $S^{n-1}$ shows the random variable $\|X\|_{K}$ (for $X\sim\sigma$) is concentrated within $1/\sqrt n$ of its mean $M$. So on $S^{n-1}$ the level sets $\{x:|\|x\|_{K}-M|<\varepsilon M\}$ have measure exponentially close to $1$. An $\varepsilon$-net argument over the Grassmannian $\mathrm{Gr}(k,n)$ — whose entropy is $\sim k\,n$ — combined with a union bound produces a single $k$-dim subspace $E$ on which the deviation is uniform.

Constant for $\ell^{p}$. For $K=B_{\ell^{p}}$, the critical dimension is $k(\ell^{p},\varepsilon)\asymp\varepsilon^{2}n^{2/p}$ for $1\le p\le 2$ and $\asymp\varepsilon^{2}\log n$ for $p\ge 2$. The phase transition at $p=2$ is the boundary between "Hilbertian-already" and "non-Hilbertian-but-rescuable".

3. Johnson–Lindenstrauss

Johnson–Lindenstrauss lemma (1984). Given $N$ points $x_{1},\ldots,x_{N}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}$ and $\varepsilon\in(0,1)$, there is a linear map $\Phi:\mathbb{R}^{n}\to\mathbb{R}^{k}$ with $$k=O\!\left(\frac{\log N}{\varepsilon^{2}}\right)$$ such that for every pair $i,j$, $$(1-\varepsilon)\,\|x_{i}-x_{j}\|^{2}\le\|\Phi x_{i}-\Phi x_{j}\|^{2}\le(1+\varepsilon)\,\|x_{i}-x_{j}\|^{2}.$$ The target dimension is independent of the ambient dimension $n$. A random Gaussian matrix $\Phi=\frac{1}{\sqrt k}G$ with $G_{ij}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ does the job with high probability.

Proof sketch. For a fixed unit vector $x$, $\|\Phi x\|^{2}=\frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k}(g_{i}\cdot x)^{2}$ is a $\chi^{2}_{k}/k$ variable; concentration gives $$\mathbb{P}\bigl(|\,\|\Phi x\|^{2}-1\,|>\varepsilon\bigr)\le 2e^{-c\varepsilon^{2}k}.$$ Apply to all $\binom{N}{2}$ difference vectors $x_{i}-x_{j}$ and union-bound: choosing $k\gtrsim\log(N^{2})/\varepsilon^{2}$ makes the failure probability $<1$, so a fixed projection works.

4. Talagrand's inequality

Let $\Omega=\Omega_{1}\times\cdots\times\Omega_{n}$ be a product probability space and $A\subset\Omega$. The convex distance from $x$ to $A$ is $$d_{T}(x,A)=\sup_{\|\alpha\|_{2}=1}\inf_{y\in A}\sum_{i:\,x_{i}\ne y_{i}}\alpha_{i}.$$ Talagrand's inequality (1995) says $$\mathbb{P}(A)\cdot\mathbb{P}(d_{T}(\cdot,A)>t)\le e^{-t^{2}/4}.$$ For a convex $1$-Lipschitz $f:[0,1]^{n}\to\mathbb{R}$ this implies the dimension-free tail $$\mathbb{P}\bigl(|f(X)-\mathrm{med}\,f|>t\bigr)\le 4e^{-t^{2}/4},$$ with no $n$ on the right-hand side. The $L^{p}$-Lipschitz cousin gives sub-Gaussian tails with constant determined by the Lipschitz norm.

Why convex distance is the right yardstick. On the Hamming cube the function $f(x)=\sum_{i}x_{i}$ is $1$-Lipschitz (in Hamming) but its standard deviation is $\sqrt n/2$, so the deviation cannot be dimension-free in Hamming. The convex distance is much smaller for "spread-out" disagreements — it absorbs the coordinatewise variability that the Hamming metric misses, and that's exactly what convex Lipschitz functionals see.

5. Gaussian isoperimetry

Let $\gamma_{n}$ be the standard Gaussian measure on $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. The Gaussian isoperimetric inequality (Borell, Sudakov–Tsirelson 1975) asserts: among Borel sets of equal Gaussian measure, halfspaces $H=\{x:\langle x,v\rangle\le c\}$ minimise the Gaussian boundary measure. Equivalently, for any Borel $A$ with $\gamma_{n}(A)=\Phi(a)$, $$\gamma_{n}(A_{t})\ge\Phi(a+t),\qquad A_{t}=\{x:d(x,A)\le t\},$$ where $\Phi$ is the standard Gaussian CDF.

Tail consequence. If $A\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}$ has $\gamma_{n}(A)\ge 1/2$, then $\gamma_{n}(A_{t})\ge\Phi(t)\ge 1-e^{-t^{2}/2}$. Applied to $A=\{f\le M_{f}\}$ for a $1$-Lipschitz $f$: $\gamma_{n}(f>M_{f}+t)\le e^{-t^{2}/2}$ — Gaussian concentration is dimension-free, sharper than the sphere.

Sphere $\to$ Gauss limit. Project the uniform measure on $S^{n-1}(\sqrt n)$ onto the first $k$ coordinates: as $n\to\infty$, the marginal converges to $\mathcal{N}(0,I_{k})$ (Maxwell–Poincaré). Lévy's $(n-1)t^{2}/2$ tail rescales to $t^{2}/2$ — Gaussian concentration is the high-dimensional shadow of the sphere.

Log-Sobolev (Gross 1975). For $f$ in the Gaussian Sobolev space, $\mathrm{Ent}_{\gamma}(f^{2})\le 2\int|\nabla f|^{2}\,d\gamma$. This functional isoperimetric inequality is dimension-free, tensorises perfectly across products, and is equivalent to hypercontractivity of the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck semigroup. Bobkov's inequality $I(\mathbb{E}f)\le\mathbb{E}\sqrt{I(f)^{2}+|\nabla f|^{2}}$ (where $I=\varphi\circ\Phi^{-1}$) is the sharp functional refinement that recovers Gaussian isoperimetry directly.

6. Applications

Concentration of measure is the geometric backbone of high-dimensional data analysis. Four signature applications:

Compressed sensing. Candès–Romberg–Tao (2006): a $k$-sparse signal $x\in\mathbb{R}^{n}$ can be recovered exactly from $m=O(k\log(n/k))$ random linear measurements via $\ell^{1}$-minimisation. The proof rests on the Restricted Isometry Property: for every $k$-sparse $x$, $(1-\delta)\|x\|^{2}\le\|Ax\|^{2}\le(1+\delta)\|x\|^{2}$. JL-type concentration applied to all $\binom{n}{k}$ coordinate subspaces gives RIP with high probability.

Random matrix theory. The empirical spectral distribution of $\frac{1}{n}WW^{*}$ for $W$ an $m\times n$ matrix with i.i.d.\ centred entries (variance $1$) converges, as $m,n\to\infty$ with $m/n\to\lambda$, to the Marchenko–Pastur law on $\bigl[(1-\sqrt\lambda)^{2},(1+\sqrt\lambda)^{2}\bigr]$. Wigner's semicircle and Tracy–Widom edge fluctuations are companion phenomena. Concentration controls the operator norm: $\|W\|_{op}\le 2\sqrt n+O(1)$ for $n\times n$ Wigner.

Statistical learning (PAC). For a hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ of VC dimension $d$, the empirical-risk minimiser $\hat h$ on $m$ samples satisfies $$\mathbb{P}\!\left(\sup_{h\in\mathcal{H}}|R_{\mathrm{emp}}(h)-R(h)|>\varepsilon\right)\le 8\,m^{d}e^{-m\varepsilon^{2}/32}.$$ The proof uses uniform concentration over $\mathcal{H}$ — Talagrand's inequality and chaining are the modern tools. Sample complexity $m=O(d/\varepsilon^{2})$.

Random tensors. An $n\times n\times n$ tensor with i.i.d.\ Gaussian entries has injective norm $\sim\sqrt{n\log n}$ — concentration at order $\sqrt n$ is much harder to establish than for matrices and is an active research frontier (Bandeira, Boedihardjo, van Handel).

Connections

High-dimensional geometry is where probability, geometry, and analysis meet on equal terms. The active frontier sits at structured high-dimensional models: matrix concentration with dependent entries (van Handel), free probability for non-commutative limits, and concentration on manifolds with Ricci curvature lower bounds (Sturm–Lott–Villani). Random tensors and the geometry of overparametrised neural-network losses are open at the research level.

  • Probability theory — expectation & moments — concentration is a quantitative law of large numbers.
  • Large deviations — Cramér — exponential tail rates packaged as a Legendre transform; Gaussian isoperimetry is the geometric face.
  • Functional analysis — Banach & Hilbert spaces — Dvoretzky says every Banach space hides Hilbert geometry in $\log n$ dimensions.
  • Information theory — KL divergence is the Sanov rate function and the Gaussian log-Sobolev constant.
  • Harmonic analysis — hypercontractivity of the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck semigroup is dual to Gaussian log-Sobolev.

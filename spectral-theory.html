Spectral theory

From eigenvalues of matrices to projection-valued measures: the analytic dictionary that turns operators into multiplications.

1. Spectrum of a bounded operator

For a bounded operator $T$ on a Hilbert space $H$, the spectrum is $$\sigma(T) = \{\lambda \in \mathbb{C} : T-\lambda I \text{ is not invertible in } B(H)\}.$$ On finite-dimensional $H$ this is just the set of eigenvalues. In infinite dimensions $T-\lambda I$ can fail to be invertible in three different ways, and the spectrum decomposes accordingly:

  • Point spectrum $\sigma_p(T)$: $T-\lambda I$ has non-trivial kernel — $\lambda$ is a genuine eigenvalue.
  • Continuous spectrum $\sigma_c(T)$: $T-\lambda I$ is injective and its range is dense but not closed.
  • Residual spectrum $\sigma_r(T)$: $T-\lambda I$ is injective but its range is not dense.

Three theorems anchor the picture: $\sigma(T)$ is a non-empty compact subset of $\overline{B(0,\|T\|)}\subset\mathbb{C}$ (Liouville for the resolvent), and the spectral radius formula $r(T)=\lim_n \|T^n\|^{1/n}$ recovers it from the norm.

2. Self-adjoint spectrum lives on $\mathbb{R}$

If $T = T^*$ on a Hilbert space, two strong constraints follow:

  • $\sigma(T) \subset \mathbb{R}$. The proof: for $\lambda = a + ib$ with $b \ne 0$, $\|(T-\lambda)\varphi\|^2 = \|(T-a)\varphi\|^2 + b^2\|\varphi\|^2 \ge b^2\|\varphi\|^2$, so $T-\lambda$ is bounded below — and the same inequality for $T-\bar\lambda = (T-\lambda)^*$ shows the range is dense, giving invertibility.
  • $r(T) = \|T\|$. Because $\|T^2\| = \|T^*T\| = \|T\|^2$ for self-adjoint $T$, induction gives $\|T^{2^k}\| = \|T\|^{2^k}$, and the spectral-radius limit equals $\|T\|$.

The residual spectrum is also empty: if $T-\lambda$ has range not dense for $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$, then $\overline{\mathrm{Ran}(T-\lambda)}^{\perp}=\ker(T-\lambda)^*=\ker(T-\lambda)\ne 0$, so $\lambda$ is actually an eigenvalue — contradiction. Self-adjoint spectra split into point + continuous parts only.

3. Spectral theorem (compact self-adjoint)

The cleanest spectral statement: a compact self-adjoint operator $K$ on a separable Hilbert space admits an orthonormal basis $(\varphi_n)$ of eigenvectors with real eigenvalues $\lambda_n \to 0$. Concretely: $$K = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \lambda_n \langle \varphi_n, \cdot\rangle\, \varphi_n.$$ Each non-zero eigenvalue has a finite-dimensional eigenspace (compactness rules out infinite-dimensional ones). The kernel of $K$ may still be infinite-dimensional.

The canonical example: the Hilbert–Schmidt integral operator $(Kf)(x)=\int_0^1 k(x,y)f(y)\,dy$ with $k\in L^2([0,1]^2)$ symmetric ($k(x,y)=k(y,x)$). It produces a compact self-adjoint $K$, and the eigenfunction expansion is the Mercer-type series for $k$.

4. Spectral theorem (bounded self-adjoint)

When the spectrum is no longer discrete, summation $\sum \lambda_n P_n$ must be replaced by integration. The bounded spectral theorem produces a unique projection-valued measure (PVM) $E$ on the Borel sets of $\sigma(T)\subset\mathbb{R}$ such that $$T = \int_{\sigma(T)} \lambda\, dE_\lambda, \qquad f(T) = \int_{\sigma(T)} f(\lambda)\, dE_\lambda \text{ for } f\in C(\sigma(T)).$$ $E_\Omega$ is an orthogonal projection for every Borel $\Omega$, and $E_\Omega E_{\Omega'} = E_{\Omega\cap\Omega'}$. The map $f\mapsto f(T)$ is an isometric *-homomorphism $C(\sigma(T)) \to C^*(T,1)$ — Gelfand–Naimark applied to a single self-adjoint generator.

Equivalent formulations: every bounded self-adjoint operator is unitarily equivalent to a multiplication operator $M_g\colon L^2(X,\mu)\to L^2(X,\mu)$ for some bounded measurable $g$ on a measure space $(X,\mu)$.

5. Unbounded operators and Stone's theorem

The most important operators in physics are unbounded: position $X$, momentum $P=-i\partial_x$, the Laplacian $-\Delta$, the harmonic-oscillator Hamiltonian. They cannot be elements of $B(H)$, so the bounded spectral theorem does not directly apply. The fix: work with densely defined closed operators on a domain $\mathrm{Dom}(T)\subset H$.

An operator is symmetric if $\langle T\varphi,\psi\rangle = \langle\varphi, T\psi\rangle$ for $\varphi,\psi\in\mathrm{Dom}(T)$, and self-adjoint if additionally $\mathrm{Dom}(T) = \mathrm{Dom}(T^*)$. The two conditions diverge once you leave bounded operators: symmetric is too weak. Self-adjoint operators admit a spectral resolution $T = \int_\mathbb{R} \lambda\, dE_\lambda$ on their natural domain.

Stone's theorem upgrades this to dynamics: strongly continuous one-parameter unitary groups $\{U(t)\}_{t\in\mathbb{R}}$ are in bijection with self-adjoint operators $A$ via $U(t)=e^{itA}$, with $A\varphi = -i\,\frac{d}{dt}U(t)\varphi|_{t=0}$ on the natural domain. Quantum dynamics, heat flow, and wave propagation are all instances.

6. Applications: quantum mechanics, PDE, harmonic analysis

Spectral theory's reach is wide:

  • Quantum mechanics. Observables are self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space; their spectra are the possible measured values, and the spectral measure is the Born rule. Hamiltonians generate unitary time evolution $U(t)=e^{-itH/\hbar}$.
  • PDE. Elliptic operators (Laplacian, Schrödinger) on bounded domains have compact resolvent; eigenfunctions diagonalize the heat and wave semigroups. On unbounded domains, the continuous spectrum carries scattering states.
  • Harmonic analysis. Translation-invariant operators on $L^2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ are diagonalized by the Fourier transform: $\widehat{Tf}(\xi) = m(\xi)\hat f(\xi)$, the symbol $m$ being the spectral multiplier. Pontryagin duality generalizes this to all locally compact abelian groups.

Computational number theory

Algorithms that turn the structural results of number theory into running code — primality, factoring, lattice reduction, point counting, and class-group computation, with their complexity classes attached.

1. Primality testing

How do you decide whether a 1024-bit integer is prime? Trial division costs $O(\sqrt n)$ — exponential in $\log n$ and hopeless. The path forward is Fermat's little theorem: if $p$ is prime and $\gcd(a,p)=1$, then $a^{p-1}\equiv 1\pmod p$. Compute $a^{n-1}\bmod n$ by fast exponentiation in $O(\log n)$ multiplies; if it isn't $1$, then $n$ is composite — and you've produced a witness.

The trouble: Carmichael numbers like $561 = 3\cdot 11\cdot 17$ pass Fermat for every base coprime to $n$. Miller–Rabin patches the leak. Write $n-1 = 2^s \cdot d$ with $d$ odd. If $n$ is prime, then for any $a$ coprime to $n$, either $a^d\equiv 1$ or $a^{2^r d}\equiv -1$ for some $0\le r

2. Integer factoring

Primality is in $P$. Factoring is conjecturally not. The fastest known classical algorithm — the general number-field sieve — runs in subexponential time $L_n[1/3, (64/9)^{1/3}]$. RSA's security rests on this gap.

Pollard's $\rho$ is the entry-level method: iterate $x_{i+1} = x_i^2 + c \bmod n$ starting from $x_0=2$. Reduced modulo a prime divisor $p$ of $n$, the sequence enters a cycle in $O(\sqrt p)$ steps by the birthday paradox. Floyd's tortoise–hare detects the cycle without storing the whole sequence, and $\gcd(x_i - x_j, n)$ extracts a nontrivial factor.

For RSA-sized targets one needs Pollard's $p-1$ (works when $p-1$ is smooth), the quadratic sieve (subexp $L_n[1/2, 1]$, good up to ~120 digits), and finally the number-field sieve. All three use the same recipe: collect relations of the form "smooth product equals smooth product modulo $n$" in a factor base, find a $\mathbb F_2$-linear dependence, extract a square root, take a $\gcd$.

$L_n[\alpha, c] = \exp\!\Big(c\,(\log n)^\alpha (\log\log n)^{1-\alpha}\Big)$ interpolates between polynomial ($\alpha=0$) and exponential ($\alpha=1$). The factoring records sit at $\alpha = 1/3$.

3. LLL — short bases in polynomial time

A lattice $L\subset\mathbb R^n$ is the $\mathbb Z$-span of $n$ linearly independent vectors $b_1,\dots,b_n$. The shortest-vector problem (SVP) is NP-hard under randomized reductions. Lenstra–Lenstra–Lovász (1982) finds, in polynomial time, a basis whose first vector is at most $2^{(n-1)/2}$ times the true shortest — exponentially loose, yet enough to break a remarkable amount of cryptography.

The algorithm alternates two moves on the Gram–Schmidt orthogonalization $b_i^*$:

  • Size reduction: replace $b_i$ by $b_i - \lfloor \mu_{ij}\rceil b_j$ for $j
  • Lovász swap: if $\|b_{i}^*\|^2 < (\delta - \mu_{i,i-1}^2)\|b_{i-1}^*\|^2$ for some $\delta\in(1/4, 1)$, swap $b_{i-1}$ and $b_i$.

4. Modular arithmetic algorithms

Every higher-level routine in this notebook calls a handful of primitives. They look elementary; making them fast is what enables 2048-bit RSA and 256-bit ECC.

Fast modular exponentiation

Compute $a^k \bmod n$ in $O(\log k)$ multiplies by repeated squaring. Read the binary expansion $k = \sum k_i 2^i$ from the top down; at each step square, and multiply by $a$ when $k_i=1$.

Tonelli–Shanks: square roots mod $p$

Given $a$ with $\left(\tfrac{a}{p}\right) = 1$, find $r$ with $r^2 \equiv a \pmod p$. The easy case $p\equiv 3\pmod 4$: take $r = a^{(p+1)/4}$, since $r^2 = a \cdot a^{(p-1)/2} \equiv a$. For $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$, factor $p-1 = 2^s Q$ with $Q$ odd, pick a non-residue $z$, and chase $r$ down through powers of a $2^s$-th root of unity.

Chinese remainder theorem

To reconstruct $x$ from $x \bmod m_1, \dots, x \bmod m_k$ for pairwise coprime $m_i$, Garner's algorithm runs in $O(k^2)$ multiplies in the small moduli — far faster than working modulo the full product. CRT splits modular exponentiation in RSA: compute $a^d \bmod p$ and $a^d \bmod q$, then glue. This is a 4× speedup in practice.

5. Schoof's algorithm — counting points on elliptic curves

Given $E/\mathbb F_p$, Hasse's bound says $\#E(\mathbb F_p) = p+1-t$ with $|t|\le 2\sqrt p$. Brute-force counting is $O(p)$ — exponential in $\log p$. Schoof's algorithm (1985) computes $t$ in time polynomial in $\log p$, and was the first deep result of the kind. It is the algorithmic engine behind every standardized elliptic curve.

The trick: determine $t \bmod \ell$ for many small primes $\ell$, then reconstruct $t$ by CRT once $\prod \ell > 4\sqrt p$. To find $t \bmod \ell$, work on the $\ell$-torsion subgroup $E[\ell] \cong (\mathbb Z/\ell)^2$, represented by the $\ell$-th division polynomial $\psi_\ell(x)$. Frobenius $\phi_p(x,y) = (x^p, y^p)$ satisfies its characteristic equation

$\phi_p^2 - t\,\phi_p + p \equiv 0 \quad \text{on } E[\ell].$

For each candidate $t' \in \{0,1,\dots,\ell-1\}$, test whether the equation holds in $\mathbb F_p[x,y] / (\psi_\ell, E)$; the unique $t'$ that works is $t \bmod \ell$.

SEA (Schoof–Elkies–Atkin) speeds Schoof up by a factor of about $\ell$ when $\ell$ is an Elkies prime — when the modular polynomial $\Phi_\ell(j(E), Y)$ has a root in $\mathbb F_p$, meaning Frobenius has rational eigenvalues on $E[\ell]$. SEA is the algorithm that ECDSA-2.0 implementations actually run.

6. Computing class groups & units

For a number field $K = \mathbb Q(\alpha)$ with ring of integers $\mathcal O_K$, two finiteness theorems govern the arithmetic:

  • The class group $\mathrm{Cl}(K) = \mathcal I(\mathcal O_K) / P(\mathcal O_K)$ is finite — its order $h_K$ is the class number.
  • The unit group $\mathcal O_K^\times \cong \mu(K) \oplus \mathbb Z^{r_1+r_2-1}$ is finitely generated (Dirichlet); the regulator $R_K$ is the covolume of the log-embedding lattice.

Buchmann's algorithm (1990) computes $h_K$, $R_K$, and a system of fundamental units in subexponential time — provided the generalized Riemann hypothesis for Hecke $L$-functions of $K$ holds. The recipe:

  1. Fix a factor base. Take all prime ideals $\mathfrak p$ of $\mathcal O_K$ with norm below a bound $B \approx (\log|\Delta_K|)^2$. Under GRH, these generate $\mathrm{Cl}(K)$.
  2. Collect relations. Random elements $\alpha\in\mathcal O_K$ whose principal ideal $(\alpha)$ factors over the factor base give a relation vector in $\mathbb Z^{|S|}$, plus a logarithm vector recording $\log|\sigma_i(\alpha)|$ for each archimedean place.
  3. Reduce the lattice. Apply LLL to the combined relation/log lattice; its Smith normal form yields the class-group invariants and a basis of fundamental units.

The classical small example: $K = \mathbb Q(\sqrt{-5})$, $\mathcal O_K = \mathbb Z[\sqrt{-5}]$. The famous non-unique factorization $6 = 2\cdot 3 = (1+\sqrt{-5})(1-\sqrt{-5})$ is a relation in the factor base $S = \{\mathfrak p_2, \mathfrak p_3, \mathfrak p_3'\}$; after reduction the class group is $\mathbb Z/2\mathbb Z$, $h=2$.

Connections

Computational number theory is where structural theorems become engineering. Open frontiers cluster around two questions: can the subexponential factoring barrier be broken classically (currently no candidate algorithm), and can post-quantum lattice schemes survive cryptanalytic improvements that chip away at LLL's gap to exact SVP?

  • RSA — depends entirely on the conjectured hardness of factoring.
  • ECC — needs Schoof–SEA point counting to certify curve order.
  • Algebraic number theory — class numbers and regulators above small discriminant are computed, not proved by hand.
  • Complexity theory — primality is in $P$, factoring conjecturally is not; the gap is what cryptography monetizes.
  • $p$-adic methods — Hensel lifting underlies many efficient root-finding routines used inside these algorithms.

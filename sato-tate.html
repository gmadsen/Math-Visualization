Sato–Tate: semicircles from elliptic curves

For a non-CM elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$, the Frobenius traces $a_p$ fill the Hasse interval $[-2\sqrt{p},2\sqrt{p}]$ by a universal law — the semicircle density $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta\,d\theta$. It is the arithmetic shadow of Haar measure on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$.

Story →  long-form narrative: from Sato's histograms to the automorphy of every $\mathrm{Sym}^n$.

1. The Frobenius angle

Fix a prime $p$ of good reduction for $E$. The trace of Frobenius $a_p = p+1-\#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is an integer, and by Hasse's theorem $|a_p|\le 2\sqrt{p}$. The characteristic polynomial of Frobenius on the Tate module factors as $$T^2 - a_p T + p = (T-\alpha_p)(T-\beta_p),\qquad \alpha_p\beta_p=p,\ \alpha_p+\beta_p=a_p,$$ and Hasse is equivalent to $|\alpha_p|=|\beta_p|=\sqrt{p}$.

Thus $\alpha_p=\sqrt{p}\,e^{i\theta_p}$, $\beta_p=\sqrt{p}\,e^{-i\theta_p}$ for a unique Frobenius angle $$\theta_p\in[0,\pi],\qquad a_p=2\sqrt{p}\cos\theta_p.$$ After rescaling, Frobenius is a conjugacy class in $\mathrm{SU}(2)$.

Hasse bound, geometric form. The map $\theta\mapsto 2\sqrt{p}\cos\theta$ is a bijection $[0,\pi]\to[-2\sqrt{p},2\sqrt{p}]$. The Hasse bound is the exact statement that Frobenius eigenvalues sit on the circle $|z|=\sqrt{p}$.

At $\theta_p=0$ or $\pi$ the trace saturates Hasse ($a_p=\pm 2\sqrt{p}$) — impossible at good primes because $\sqrt{p}\notin\mathbb{Z}$. At $\theta_p=\pi/2$ we have $a_p=0$, eigenvalues $\pm i\sqrt{p}$: $p$ is supersingular.

2. Equidistribution and Weyl's criterion

Before specializing to $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, remember what equidistribution means on a compact space $K$ equipped with a probability measure $\mu$. A sequence $(x_n)\subset K$ equidistributes with respect to $\mu$ if $$\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n\le N} f(x_n) = \int_K f\,d\mu\qquad\text{for every } f\in C(K).$$ By Stone–Weierstrass, a dense subset of $C(K)$ is enough. When $K$ is a compact Lie group (or a space of conjugacy classes in one), the Peter–Weyl theorem hands us a preferred dense family: the characters of irreducible unitary representations.

Weyl's criterion (group form). Let $K$ be a compact Hausdorff group with normalized Haar measure $\mu$. A sequence $(x_n)$ equidistributes with respect to $\mu$ iff for every non-trivial irreducible character $\chi$ of $K$, $$\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n\le N}\chi(x_n)=0.$$

For Sato–Tate the "sequence" is indexed by primes, not integers, so the relevant statistic is a prime average $\pi(X)^{-1}\sum_{p\le X}\chi(\operatorname{Frob}_p)$. This is the same flavor of statement as Chebotarev's density theorem — for a finite Galois extension, Frobenius classes equidistribute in $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ against counting measure. Sato–Tate lifts this from a finite group to a compact Lie group via the $\ell$-adic Galois representation of $E$.

The semicircle density $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta\,d\theta$ is not chosen by hand: it is the pushforward of Haar on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ to conjugacy classes. The irreducible characters of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ are the Chebyshev polynomials $U_n(\cos\theta)$; Weyl's criterion for Frobenius angles is $$\lim_{X\to\infty}\frac{1}{\pi(X)}\sum_{p\le X}U_n(\cos\theta_p)=\delta_{n,0}\qquad(n\ge 0),$$ the exact input to the symmetric-power $L$-function argument of §5.

3. The Sato–Tate measure

Every prime $p$ of good reduction hands us an angle $\theta_p\in[0,\pi]$. The Sato–Tate conjecture — proved for non-CM elliptic curves over totally real fields by Clozel, Harris, Shepherd-Barron, Taylor, Barnet-Lamb and Geraghty (2008–11) — specifies how they distribute.

Sato–Tate (theorem, non-CM case). For $E/\mathbb{Q}$ non-CM elliptic and any continuous $f\colon[0,\pi]\to\mathbb{R}$, $$\lim_{X\to\infty}\frac{1}{\pi(X)}\sum_{p\le X}f(\theta_p)=\int_0^\pi f\,d\mu_{ST},\qquad d\mu_{ST}=\frac{2}{\pi}\sin^2\theta\,d\theta.$$

Why that density? The Haar probability measure on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, pushed to conjugacy classes $\operatorname{diag}(e^{i\theta},e^{-i\theta})$, is $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta\,d\theta$. Sato–Tate is Chebotarev for a compact Lie group: rescaled Frobenius classes equidistribute with respect to Haar measure on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$.

The density vanishes quadratically at the endpoints (reflecting Hasse saturation) and peaks at $\theta=\pi/2$ — supersingular angles are the modal event even though $a_p=0$ has measure zero on the real line.

CM breaks universality. For $E:y^2=x^3-x$ (CM by $\mathbb{Z}[i]$), roughly half of primes are supersingular (a Dirac mass at $\pi/2$) and the rest equidistribute uniformly as $(1/\pi)\,d\theta$ — the Haar measure of the normalizer of a torus, not the full $\mathrm{SU}(2)$.

4. CM vs. non-CM: two equidistribution laws

The semicircle density governs only the non-CM case. When $E$ has complex multiplication by an order in an imaginary quadratic field $K$, the Frobenius $\alpha_p$ is a Hecke Grössencharacter value, and the $\ell$-adic Galois representation lands in the normalizer $N(T)\subset\mathrm{SU}(2)$ of a maximal torus $T$ instead of being Zariski-dense. Sato–Tate still holds — but against Haar measure on $N(T)$, not on all of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$.

CM Sato–Tate (Hecke, Deuring, 1950s). Let $E/\mathbb{Q}$ have CM by an order in $K=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$. Then:
• for primes $p$ inert in $K$ (density $\tfrac12$), $a_p=0$ — $p$ is supersingular, $\theta_p=\pi/2$;
• for primes $p$ split in $K$ (density $\tfrac12$), the angles $\theta_p$ equidistribute uniformly on $[0,\pi]$ with density $1/\pi$.
The combined Frobenius distribution is therefore $\tfrac12\delta_{\pi/2} + \tfrac12\cdot\frac{1}{\pi}\,d\theta$, i.e. Haar measure on $N(T)/T$-conjugacy classes.

The split-prime story is genuinely different: for $p=\mathfrak{p}\bar{\mathfrak{p}}$ with $\mathfrak{p}\in K$ above $p$, the Grössencharacter $\psi$ satisfies $\psi(\mathfrak{p})\bar\psi(\mathfrak{p})=p$, and $a_p=\psi(\mathfrak{p})+\bar\psi(\mathfrak{p})$. Equidistribution of the arguments $\arg\psi(\mathfrak{p})$ in $\mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}$ is classical — it follows from equidistribution of the arguments of ideals in an ideal class via Hecke $L$-functions, and predates the full Sato–Tate conjecture by half a century.

The inert-prime story is algebraic. For $p$ inert, reducing $E\bmod p$ lands in the supersingular locus; Frobenius has trace $0$ because $\mathrm{End}(E_{\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}})$ is a quaternion order and the CM embedding forces $a_p=0$. There is no distribution to speak of: every such prime contributes the atom at $\pi/2$.

This is why the Sato–Tate group of $E$ is the right invariant: it is $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ for non-CM, and $N(T)\cong U(1)\rtimes\mathbb{Z}/2$ for CM. Both project to the "conjugacy space" with a measure that matches the observed Frobenius angles.

5. Symmetric powers and the $L$-function strategy

Proof of equidistribution flows through harmonic analysis on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$. Its irreducibles are the symmetric powers $\mathrm{Sym}^n\mathbb{C}^2$ ($n\ge 0$), with characters $$\chi_n(\theta)=U_n(\cos\theta)=\sum_{k=0}^{n}e^{i(n-2k)\theta}=\frac{\sin((n+1)\theta)}{\sin\theta},$$ the Chebyshev polynomials of the second kind. They are an orthonormal basis of $L^2([0,\pi],d\mu_{ST})$: $$\int_0^\pi U_m(\cos\theta)\,U_n(\cos\theta)\,\tfrac{2}{\pi}\sin^2\theta\,d\theta=\delta_{m,n}.$$

Symmetric-power $L$-function. At good primes, $$L_p(\mathrm{Sym}^n E,s)=\prod_{k=0}^{n}\bigl(1-\alpha_p^{n-k}\beta_p^{k}\,p^{-s}\bigr)^{-1},$$ collecting the $n{+}1$ Frobenius eigenvalues on $\mathrm{Sym}^n$. Globally $L(\mathrm{Sym}^n E,s)=\prod_p L_p$.

The trace of $\mathrm{Sym}^n\operatorname{Frob}_p$ is $\sum_k\alpha_p^{n-k}\beta_p^{k}=p^{n/2}U_n(\cos\theta_p)$. By Wiener–Ikehara, equidistribution with respect to $\mu_{ST}$ is equivalent to $$\lim_{X\to\infty}\frac{1}{\pi(X)}\sum_{p\le X}U_n(\cos\theta_p)=\delta_{n,0},\qquad n\ge 0,$$ which follows from analytic continuation and non-vanishing of each $L(\mathrm{Sym}^n E,s)$ on $\operatorname{Re}s=1$.

Sato–Tate ⇔ symmetric-power analyticity. For non-CM $E/\mathbb{Q}$, Sato–Tate holds iff $L(\mathrm{Sym}^n E,s)$ is holomorphic and non-vanishing on $\operatorname{Re}s\ge 1$ for every $n\ge 1$. The cited 2008–11 theorems supply exactly this.

Slide $\theta$ and $n$ to watch $U_n(\cos\theta)$ oscillate; its $n$ zeros are evenly spaced, and $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta$ weights the curve so that $\int U_n^2\,d\mu_{ST}=1$.

Moments are a blunt instrument — they test a single statistic at a time. The sharper object is the $\mathrm{Sym}^n$ $L$-function itself. Fix $n\ge 1$ and a non-CM curve $E$. For each good prime $p$ with Frobenius eigenvalues $\alpha_p=\sqrt{p}\,e^{i\theta_p}$ and $\beta_p=\sqrt{p}\,e^{-i\theta_p}$, the local Euler factor at $s$ is $$L_p(\mathrm{Sym}^n E, s) = \prod_{k=0}^{n}\bigl(1 - \alpha_p^{n-k}\beta_p^{k}\,p^{-s}\bigr)^{-1} = \prod_{k=0}^{n}\bigl(1 - p^{n/2 - s}\,e^{i(n-2k)\theta_p}\bigr)^{-1}.$$ Sato–Tate for $E$ is equivalent to $L(\mathrm{Sym}^n E,s)$ being holomorphic and non-vanishing on $\operatorname{Re}(s)\ge 1$ for all $n\ge 1$, where the natural normalization places the edge of the critical strip at $\operatorname{Re}(s)=1$ (the analytic conductor absorbs the $p^{n/2}$ factor; the widget plots $L_p$ with this centered normalization, putting the expected non-vanishing edge at $\sigma=1$).

The next toy treats this schematically: it samples Sato–Tate angles for a prime cap, builds the partial Euler product $\prod_{p\le P} L_p(\mathrm{Sym}^n E, \sigma + i\tau)$ along a horizontal strip $\sigma\in[0.5, 1.5]$ at fixed imaginary height $\tau$, and plots $\log|L|$ against $\sigma$. The crossing $\sigma=1$ is highlighted — a conjectured non-vanishing line. For small $n$ the partial product should stay smooth across $\sigma=1$; as $n$ grows, the cumulative Dirichlet partial sum $\sum_{p\le P}\log L_p$ accumulates variance and the "signal" becomes noisier even though the true $L$ remains non-vanishing.

Coda: open horizons

Sato–Tate for a single non-CM elliptic curve is the $\mathrm{GL}_2$ slice of a larger picture. The generalized Sato–Tate conjecture predicts Satake parameters of any cuspidal automorphic representation equidistribute against Haar measure on a compact form of the associated $L$-group; for $\mathrm{GL}_n$ forms and higher-rank abelian varieties most cases remain open. The dual Katz–Sarnak heuristic matches zeros of $L$-function families to unitary/orthogonal/symplectic random-matrix ensembles, binding Sato–Tate to the spectral side of analytic number theory.

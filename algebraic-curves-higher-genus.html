Algebraic curves: higher genus

Genus $g \ge 2$: Riemann–Roch, the canonical embedding, and $\mathcal{M}_g$.

1. Smooth projective curves and their genus

A smooth projective curve over an algebraically closed field $k$ is a smooth projective integral $k$-scheme of dimension $1$. Concretely: a one-dimensional scheme that locally looks like $\Spec\, A$ with $A$ a regular ring of Krull dimension $1$, no embedded points, no nodes, no cusps. Over $k = \mathbb{C}$ the GAGA equivalence identifies smooth projective curves with compact Riemann surfaces — the same data wears two coats, algebraic and analytic.

The single most important invariant is the genus $g = \dim_k H^0(C, \Omega^1_C)$, the dimension of the space of regular (= holomorphic) $1$-forms. Three other quantities give the same number, by deep theorems each worth its own name: the topological genus of $C(\mathbb{C})$ (number of handles), the arithmetic genus $1 - \chi(\mathcal{O}_C) = 1 - h^0(\mathcal{O}_C) + h^1(\mathcal{O}_C) = h^1(\mathcal{O}_C)$, and $1 - \tfrac12\chi_{\mathrm{top}}$. That all four agree on a smooth projective curve is the curve case of the Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch theorem; in higher dimensions arithmetic and geometric genera diverge.

Genus partitions curves into ladders of qualitatively different worlds. Genus $0$ is rigid: the unique smooth projective curve of genus $0$ over $\bar k$ is $\mathbb{P}^1$; there is no moduli. Genus $1$ is the territory of elliptic curves — a one-parameter family classified by the $j$-invariant, every such curve a complex torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$. From genus $2$ onward the moduli space $\mathcal{M}_g$ has dimension $3g - 3$ and the geometry stratifies further: hyperelliptic vs. non-hyperelliptic, generic vs. special Weierstrass points, $\theta$-divisor pathologies. Everything below is the catalogue of the new structure that appears once we cross the $g \ge 2$ threshold.

2. Divisors on curves

A divisor on a smooth projective curve $C$ is a finite formal $\mathbb{Z}$-sum $D = \sum_p n_p [p]$ over closed points. Divisors form a free abelian group $\mathrm{Div}(C)$; the degree map $\deg\colon \mathrm{Div}(C) \to \mathbb{Z}$, $\deg D = \sum n_p \deg(p/k)$, is a homomorphism. The principal divisors $\mathrm{div}(f) = \sum_p \mathrm{ord}_p(f) \cdot [p]$ for $f \in k(C)^\times$ form a subgroup of degree $0$, and the Picard group $\mathrm{Pic}(C) = \mathrm{Div}(C)/\mathrm{Princ}(C)$ classifies isomorphism classes of line bundles on $C$ — equivalently, divisors up to linear equivalence.

The degree map descends to $\deg\colon \mathrm{Pic}(C) \to \mathbb{Z}$, splitting the Picard group into degree slices: $\mathrm{Pic}(C) = \bigsqcup_d \mathrm{Pic}^d(C)$. Each slice is a torsor over the kernel $\mathrm{Pic}^0(C)$, the Jacobian. Over $\mathbb{C}$ a stunning fact: $\mathrm{Pic}^0(C) \cong \mathbb{C}^g / \Lambda_C$, a $g$-dimensional complex torus, where $\Lambda_C \subset \mathbb{C}^g$ is the period lattice of integrals of holomorphic $1$-forms over a basis of $H_1(C, \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}^{2g}$. For $g = 1$ this lattice is exactly the lattice of $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ from the elliptic-curves topic; the elliptic curve is its own Jacobian, the only genus where this happens.

For $g \ge 2$ the Jacobian acquires extra structure: a principal polarization (the $\theta$-divisor), an Abel–Jacobi map $\mathrm{AJ}\colon C \to \mathrm{Jac}(C)$, $p \mapsto \int_{p_0}^p \omega$, and via the symmetric powers $\mathrm{Sym}^d C \to \mathrm{Pic}^d(C)$ a description of effective divisors as fibres of a proper map. The widget below is a 2D slice of the period lattice — a faithful picture only for $g = 1$, but a useful ladder for visualising what "complex torus of dimension $g$" means.

What you see is one $\mathbb{C}/\langle v_1, v_2\rangle$ factor; for genus $g$ the Jacobian is $g$ such complex-dimensional factors glued together with non-trivial periods $\Omega \in M_{g \times 2g}(\mathbb{C})$. The full Jacobian's $g$-dimensional volume is $|\det(\mathrm{Im}\, \tau)|$ for a Riemann period matrix $\tau$ in the Siegel upper half-space.

3. Riemann–Roch on curves

For a divisor $D$ on a smooth projective curve $C$ of genus $g$, the Riemann–Roch theorem equates two dimension counts: $$h^0(D) - h^0(K - D) = \deg D - g + 1,$$ where $K = K_C$ is the canonical divisor (any divisor of a regular $1$-form $\omega \in H^0(C, \Omega^1_C)$). The first term $h^0(D) = \dim_k H^0(C, \mathcal{O}_C(D))$ counts global sections of $\mathcal{O}_C(D)$ — equivalently, rational functions $f$ with $\mathrm{div}(f) + D \ge 0$. The second term, $h^0(K - D) = h^1(D)$ by Serre duality, is the obstruction term.

The reason genus controls everything: Riemann–Roch is exactly the statement $\chi(\mathcal{O}_C(D)) = \deg D + 1 - g$, that is, the Euler characteristic of any line bundle is degree-shifted-by-genus. Three immediate consequences are worth keeping at the front of the mind:

  • Vanishing. If $\deg D > 2g - 2$, then $\deg(K - D) < 0$ so $h^0(K - D) = 0$ and Riemann–Roch becomes the equality $h^0(D) = \deg D - g + 1$.
  • Canonical bundle. Setting $D = K$ gives $h^0(K) - h^0(0) = (2g - 2) - g + 1$, i.e. $h^0(K) = g$ — recovering the definition of genus.
  • Inequality. Always $h^0(D) \ge \deg D - g + 1$, useful for proving sections exist when degrees are large.

Worked example: for $D = nq$ with $q \in C$ a fixed point on a genus-$g$ curve, the dimensions $h^0(nq)$ as $n$ grows are determined by the Weierstrass gap sequence: there are exactly $g$ "gap" values $1 \le n_1 < \cdots < n_g \le 2g - 1$ where $h^0(nq) = h^0((n-1)q)$, and at all other $n$ the dimension goes up by $1$. For a generic point $q$ the gaps are $\{1, 2, \ldots, g\}$; for a Weierstrass point they jump (e.g. on a hyperelliptic curve the gaps at a Weierstrass point are $\{1, 3, 5, \ldots, 2g-1\}$). The worked $g = 2$, $D = 3[p]$ case in the scrubber above puts us at $n = 3 = 2g - 1$, the last possible gap value.

4. The canonical embedding

On a curve $C$ of genus $g \ge 2$ the canonical linear system $|K|$ is base-point-free (Riemann–Roch + a short argument), and it has dimension $g - 1$ since $h^0(K) = g$. Choose a basis $\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_g$ of $H^0(C, \Omega^1_C)$. The canonical map $$\varphi_K\colon C \longrightarrow \mathbb{P}^{g-1}, \qquad p \longmapsto [\omega_1(p) : \cdots : \omega_g(p)]$$ is a morphism whose image is intrinsic to $C$ up to projective isomorphism — different basis choices differ by elements of $\mathrm{PGL}_g$.

Max Noether's theorem (or "Enriques–Babbage") says: $\varphi_K$ is an embedding if and only if $C$ is non-hyperelliptic, in which case the image is a smooth projective curve of degree $\deg K = 2g - 2$ in $\mathbb{P}^{g-1}$. When $C$ is hyperelliptic, $\varphi_K$ is the composition $C \xrightarrow{2:1} \mathbb{P}^1 \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^{g-1}$ where the second map is the rational normal curve of degree $g - 1$; the canonical map factors through the hyperelliptic involution and is no longer injective.

The canonical model is the most natural way to put an abstract curve into projective space: it is functorial in the sense that any automorphism of $C$ extends to a projective automorphism of the canonical image. For low genus the canonical image is concrete:

  • $g = 2$: $\varphi_K\colon C \to \mathbb{P}^1$ is the unique $g^1_2$ — every genus-$2$ curve is hyperelliptic, no non-hyperelliptic case exists.
  • $g = 3$: non-hyperelliptic ⇒ $\varphi_K(C)$ is a smooth quartic in $\mathbb{P}^2$ ($\deg = 4$, $\dim = 1$); hyperelliptic ⇒ image is a smooth conic $= \mathbb{P}^1$ in $\mathbb{P}^2$, mapped $2:1$.
  • $g = 4$: non-hyperelliptic ⇒ $\varphi_K(C) \subset \mathbb{P}^3$ is the intersection of a unique quadric and a cubic (Petri's theorem in low genus).
  • $g \ge 4$ generic: the canonical image is the intersection of all the quadrics through it (Noether–Enriques–Petri).

5. Hyperelliptic curves

A smooth projective curve $C$ of genus $g \ge 2$ is hyperelliptic if it admits a degree-$2$ map $\pi\colon C \to \mathbb{P}^1$. Equivalently — by Riemann–Hurwitz with $d = 2$ and $b = 2g + 2$ branch points, each with simple ramification — every hyperelliptic curve is birational to an affine model $$y^2 = f(x), \qquad f(x) \in k[x] \text{ separable of degree } 2g+1 \text{ or } 2g+2.$$ The map $\pi$ is the projection $(x, y) \mapsto x$, the involution $\sigma\colon (x, y) \mapsto (x, -y)$ (the "hyperelliptic involution") generates $\Gal(C/\mathbb{P}^1) = \mathbb{Z}/2$, and the $2g+2$ branch points are the roots of $f$ together with $\infty$ (if $\deg f$ is odd).

Three different characterisations agree, and switching between them is the recurring move:

  • Geometric: $\exists$ a degree-$2$ map $C \to \mathbb{P}^1$ — equivalently, $C$ carries a $g^1_2$ (a $1$-dimensional linear system of degree $2$).
  • Canonical: the canonical map $\varphi_K\colon C \to \mathbb{P}^{g-1}$ is not an embedding — it factors through $\mathbb{P}^1$.
  • Riemann–Roch: there exist two distinct points $p, q$ with $h^0(p + q) = 2$, i.e. $|p + q|$ separates points up to the hyperelliptic involution.

Every genus-$2$ curve is hyperelliptic — its canonical map is exactly the unique $g^1_2$. From genus $3$ onward "generic" curves are non-hyperelliptic; hyperellipticity is a closed codimension-$(g-2)$ condition in $\mathcal{M}_g$. So in $\mathcal{M}_3$ hyperelliptic curves form a divisor (codim $1$); in $\mathcal{M}_4$ they form a codim-$2$ surface; in higher genus they are increasingly special.

Each branch point of the cover is a Weierstrass point of $C$: a point where the gap sequence of $|np|$ deviates from the generic $\{1, 2, \ldots, g\}$. On a hyperelliptic curve the gap sequence at any branch point is $\{1, 3, 5, \ldots, 2g-1\}$, the maximum-jump pattern allowed. Generic curves have only finitely many Weierstrass points (with multiplicity, the count is $g^3 - g$); hyperelliptic curves concentrate them at the $2g+2$ ramification points.

6. Moduli of higher-genus curves $\mathcal{M}_g$

$\mathcal{M}_g$ is the moduli space (or rather, the moduli stack — Deligne–Mumford for $g \ge 2$) parametrising smooth projective curves of genus $g$ up to isomorphism. For $g \ge 2$ it has dimension $3g - 3$ as an algebraic stack over any base; over $\mathbb{C}$ it is a complex orbifold of complex dimension $3g - 3$ and $\mathrm{Teich}_g \to \mathcal{M}_g$ is its universal cover by Teichmüller space.

The dimension count is the deformation-theoretic statement $\dim T_C \mathcal{M}_g = h^1(C, T_C) = 3g - 3$ for $g \ge 2$. Three little tables of low-genus dimensions and structure:

The Deligne–Mumford compactification $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g$ adjoins stable nodal curves at the boundary: connected genus-$g$ curves with at worst nodal singularities and finite automorphism group. The boundary $\partial\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g = \overline{\mathcal{M}}_g \setminus \mathcal{M}_g$ decomposes as a union of boundary divisors $\Delta_0, \Delta_1, \ldots, \Delta_{\lfloor g/2\rfloor}$:

  • $\Delta_0$: irreducible curves with one self-node (pinching a non-separating cycle in $C$ — geometric genus drops by $1$).
  • $\Delta_i$ for $1 \le i \le \lfloor g/2 \rfloor$: nodal unions $C_1 \cup_p C_2$ with $g(C_1) = i$, $g(C_2) = g - i$ (pinching a separating cycle).

Each boundary divisor has codimension $1$ in $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g$, so $\dim \Delta_i = 3g - 4$. The combinatorics of how boundary strata intersect — pinching $k$ disjoint cycles produces a stratum of codimension $k$, parametrised by a stable graph — make $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g$ a textbook example of a stratified Deligne–Mumford stack. The boundary classes $\delta_0, \delta_1, \ldots, \delta_{\lfloor g/2 \rfloor} \in \mathrm{Pic}(\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g)$ together with the Hodge class $\lambda$ generate a finite-rank piece of the Picard group; the relations between them (Mumford, Cornalba–Harris) are the engine of intersection theory on the moduli of curves.

7. Connections

Higher-genus curves sit between elliptic curves and the full theory of moduli — the elementary side has long been understood, the global theory remains active.

Elliptic curves as the genus-1 case

Elliptic curves are the genus-1 anchor; everything in this page generalises away the abelian-group structure (no longer present), the rational uniformisation (replaced by Riemann–Roch), and the modular-curve story (becomes the full $\mathcal{M}_g$).

Riemann surfaces and the analytic picture

Riemann surfaces develops the complex-analytic side: a smooth projective curve over $\mathbb{C}$ is a compact Riemann surface, and the genus matches the topological genus. Period maps, Torelli, uniformisation by $\mathbb{H}/\Gamma$ all live there.

Moduli of curves

Moduli spaces $\mathcal{M}_g$ and their compactifications $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g$ are central objects of modern algebraic geometry; the canonical class, intersection numbers (Mumford’s $\kappa$ classes), and tautological rings are the language in which their geometry is studied.

Sheaf cohomology and Riemann–Roch

Sheaf cohomology in dimension 1 — Serre duality, the structure of $H^0$ and $H^1$ — is exactly Riemann–Roch in its modern formulation, and the engine behind the Brill–Noether theory of special divisors.

Open frontiers: tropical curves and Berkovich analytifications; arithmetic of curves over number fields (Mordell, Faltings, the conjecture of Vojta); the Tate module and rational points on Jacobians as a bridge to the BSD circle of ideas.

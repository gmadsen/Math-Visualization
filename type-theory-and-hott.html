Type theory and HoTT

A foundation that replaces 'membership in a set' with 'derivation of a typing judgment', then discovers that its identity types behave like spaces. From Π/Σ and the Curry–Howard correspondence to univalence, higher inductive types, and the simplicial-set model.

Set theory says 'mathematics is the study of what's in collections'. Type theory says 'mathematics is the study of what we can derive', and replaces the global $\in$ with a structured deduction system over typing judgments. Every term carries its type with it; well-formed expressions are the ones with a derivation. Layered on top of this — first by Martin-Löf, then by Voevodsky and the HoTT community — sits a startling discovery: the identity type $\mathsf{Id}_A(a,b)$ behaves like the space of paths from $a$ to $b$, with all the higher-cell coherences of homotopy theory. The univalence axiom then forces 'isomorphism = identity' as a theorem.

Plan. Sections 1–2 are the apparatus of dependent type theory: judgments, contexts, the Π/Σ duo and Curry–Howard. Section 3 introduces identity types and the path-space picture. Sections 4–5 are HoTT proper: univalence and higher inductive types. Section 6 sketches Voevodsky's simplicial-set model — what makes the syntax a coherent foundation.

1. Judgments and contexts

The primitive notion of type theory is the judgment. There are four basic forms:

judgmentread as
$\Gamma\vdash A\;\mathsf{type}$'in context $\Gamma$, the expression $A$ is a type'
$\Gamma\vdash a:A$'in context $\Gamma$, the term $a$ has type $A$'
$\Gamma\vdash A\equiv B\;\mathsf{type}$'$A$ and $B$ are definitionally equal types'
$\Gamma\vdash a\equiv b:A$'$a$ and $b$ are definitionally equal terms of $A$'

A context $\Gamma$ is a finite list $(x_1:A_1,\,x_2:A_2(x_1),\,\ldots,\,x_n:A_n(x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1}))$ where each $A_i$ is a type in the previous context. Variables in a context are distinct, and later types may depend on earlier ones — that's where the 'dependent' in dependent type theory enters. Well-formedness is itself a judgment, $\Gamma\;\mathsf{ctx}$, derivable by an obvious recursion.

Why judgments instead of "$a\in A$". In set theory, $\in$ is a global relation: any term can in principle be tested for membership in any set. In type theory, '$a:A$' is meaningful only as the conclusion of a derivation. You can't ask 'is $\pi:\mathbb{N}$?' as a yes/no question — there is simply no derivation. This makes type-checking decidable in nice cases, and it makes the foundation computational: every well-typed term reduces.

2. Π and Σ types

The two type-formers that bring dependence to life are Π (dependent function) and Σ (dependent pair).

$\Pi_{x:A} B(x)$$\Sigma_{x:A} B(x)$
introduce$\lambda x.\,b(x)$ where $b(x):B(x)$$(a,b)$ where $a:A,\ b:B(a)$
eliminateapply: $f(a):B(a)$project: $\mathrm{pr}_1(p):A$, $\mathrm{pr}_2(p):B(\mathrm{pr}_1 p)$
compute$(\lambda x.b)(a)\equiv b[a/x]$$\mathrm{pr}_1(a,b)\equiv a$, $\mathrm{pr}_2(a,b)\equiv b$
logic side$\forall x.\,B(x)$$\exists x.\,B(x)$
set-theory sidesection of the bundle $B\to A$total space of the bundle $B\to A$

When $B$ does not actually mention $x$, $\Pi_{x:A} B$ collapses to the function type $A\to B$ and $\Sigma_{x:A} B$ collapses to the product $A\times B$. The dependent versions are how type theory talks about vectors of length $n$, polynomials of degree $\le d$, or matrices of shape $(m,n)$ without escaping into a metatheory.

Curry–Howard slogan. $\forall \leftrightarrow \Pi$, $\exists \leftrightarrow \Sigma$, $\Rightarrow \leftrightarrow \to$, $\land \leftrightarrow \times$, $\lor \leftrightarrow +$, $\neg P \leftrightarrow P\to\bot$. A program of type $T$ is a constructive proof of the proposition $T$.

3. Identity types

For every type $A$ and every $a,b:A$ we form the identity type $\mathsf{Id}_A(a,b)$. It has exactly one constructor:

$$\mathsf{refl}_a : \mathsf{Id}_A(a,a)$$

and one elimination rule, the J-rule (path induction): to define a function out of $\mathsf{Id}_A(a,b)$ for arbitrary $a,b$, it suffices to specify what it does on $\mathsf{refl}_a$ for arbitrary $a$. From J alone one derives:

  • concatenation $\mathsf{Id}_A(a,b)\to \mathsf{Id}_A(b,c)\to \mathsf{Id}_A(a,c)$, written $p\cdot q$;
  • inversion $\mathsf{Id}_A(a,b)\to \mathsf{Id}_A(b,a)$, written $p^{-1}$;
  • action of any function $f:A\to B$ on identifications: $\mathsf{ap}_f:\mathsf{Id}_A(a,b)\to \mathsf{Id}_B(f(a),f(b))$;
  • transport along $p$: if $P:A\to\mathsf{Type}$ and $u:P(a)$, then $p_*(u):P(b)$.
Path-space picture. Read $A$ as a topological space, $a,b:A$ as points, $p:\mathsf{Id}_A(a,b)$ as a path, $\mathsf{refl}_a$ as the constant path. Concatenation, inversion, and $\mathsf{ap}_f$ are then literally path concatenation, path reversal, and post-composition with a continuous map. This is the picture HoTT takes seriously.

4. The univalence axiom

For any two types $A,B$ in a universe $\mathcal{U}$, there is a canonical map

$$\mathsf{idtoeqv}: (A=B) \longrightarrow (A\simeq B)$$

built from path induction: an identification $p:A=B$ transports any element of $A$ to one of $B$, and the inverse operation transports back. Voevodsky's univalence axiom says this canonical map is itself an equivalence:

$$ \mathsf{ua}: (A=B) \;\simeq\; (A\simeq B). $$

Consequences are immediate and dramatic:

  • If $A\simeq B$, then there is an identification $A=B$. Isomorphism is identity.
  • Every property invariant under equivalence is automatically invariant under identification — and vice versa. So you can transport a theorem from a structure to anything equivalent.
  • It contradicts Streicher's axiom K (uniqueness of identity proofs): the universe at $(\mathbb{B},\mathbb{B})$ has the swap equivalence as a non-reflexive identification.
  • It implies function extensionality ($\Pi\,x.\,f(x)=g(x)$ implies $f=g$).
Use case. If $A$ is the type of finite groups and $B$ the type of bijections of $A$ with itself, univalence says proving a property of finite groups up to isomorphism really does prove it 'on the nose' — no boilerplate to transport along a chosen isomorphism. The bookkeeping vanishes.

5. Higher inductive types

An ordinary inductive type — like $\mathbb{N}$ generated by $0$ and $\mathsf{succ}$ — specifies point constructors. A higher inductive type (HIT) additionally takes path constructors, and recursively higher-cell constructors. The canonical example is the circle:

$$ S^1 \;\;\text{generated by}\;\; \mathsf{base}:S^1, \quad \mathsf{loop}:\mathsf{Id}_{S^1}(\mathsf{base},\mathsf{base}). $$

That is the entire definition. From it one can reconstruct, inside the type theory, the fact that $\pi_1(S^1)\simeq\mathbb{Z}$ — the generator is $\mathsf{loop}$, and iterating it produces every integer winding number. Other examples:

Why it works. An ordinary inductive type's elimination rule says 'to map out, define what to do on each constructor'. For HITs the same recipe extends: to map $S^1\to X$, give a point $x:X$ and a loop $\ell:\mathsf{Id}(x,x)$. The recursion principle then computes coherently with the path constructor.

6. Models of HoTT

Syntax must mean something. A model of HoTT is a categorical structure that interprets every type, term, and judgment, validating all the rules. The flagship model is Voevodsky's:

  • Types ↦ Kan complexes (fibrant simplicial sets, presenting $\infty$-groupoids).
  • Dependent type $B$ over $A$ ↦ a Kan fibration $B\to A$.
  • $\Sigma_{x:A} B(x)$ ↦ total space of the fibration.
  • $\Pi_{x:A} B(x)$ ↦ space of sections (fibred mapping space).
  • Identity type $\mathsf{Id}_A(a,b)$ ↦ path object $A^I$ at the pair $(a,b)$.
  • Universe $\mathcal{U}$ ↦ a univalent universe classifying small Kan fibrations.

Univalence holds because, by construction, the path space of the universe at $(A,B)$ matches the space of equivalences $A\simeq B$. Higher inductive types have models too: $S^1$ in the model is a Kan complex weakly equivalent to the topological circle.

Why a model matters. A consistent model proves the theory consistent. Voevodsky's construction is what tells us HoTT is a coherent foundation, not a syntax that might secretly prove $0=1$. Subsequently other models (cubical, Bezem–Coquand, Orton–Pitts) made constructive sense of univalence — leading to cubical type theory with computational univalence.

7. Connections

Homotopy type theory replaces sets with $\infty$-groupoids and props with paths. The theorems above are the moves; the bridges below are how the new foundation sits next to its neighbours.

First-order logic via Curry–Howard

First-order logic with proofs becomes type theory with terms — the Curry–Howard correspondence makes "$\varphi$ is provable" the same as "type $\varphi$ is inhabited". HoTT is the homotopy-coherent enrichment.

Category theory and topoi

Internal type theory of an $\infty$-topos is HoTT (modulo strictness). Category theory + $\infty$-topoi are the semantic counterpart; univalence is the property that "internal logic = external structure".

Set theory as alternative foundation

ZFC proposes a different bedrock — material set theory. Conservativity over ZFC, and the role of large cardinals in HoTT, are active research questions.

Computer-verified mathematics

Coq, Lean, Agda all implement variants of dependent type theory; mathlib (Lean) and the Coq HoTT library have formalised significant chunks of analysis, algebra, and topology.

Open frontiers: cubical type theory (computational univalence); synthetic homotopy theory (proving real homotopy theorems inside HoTT); modal HoTT and shape theory; cohesive type theory and synthetic differential geometry.

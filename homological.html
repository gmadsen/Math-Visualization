Homological algebra, from chain complexes to spectral sequences

The machinery that measures failure of exactness. Chain complexes, exact sequences, the snake lemma, Ext and Tor, resolutions, and a gentle taste of spectral sequences.

1. Chain complexes and homology

A chain complex of abelian groups (or $A$-modules) is a sequence

$\cdots \to C_{n+1} \xrightarrow{\partial_{n+1}} C_n \xrightarrow{\partial_n} C_{n-1} \to \cdots$

such that $\partial_n\circ\partial_{n+1}=0$ for every $n$. Equivalently, $\operatorname{im}\partial_{n+1}\subseteq\ker\partial_n$. The $n$-th homology

$H_n(C_\bullet) \;=\; \ker\partial_n\,/\,\operatorname{im}\partial_{n+1}$

measures exactly how far this containment is from being equality. Elements of $\ker\partial$ are cycles; elements of $\operatorname{im}\partial$ are boundaries. A cycle that is not a boundary records a "real" hole.

The archetype: given a simplicial complex $X$, let $C_n$ be the free abelian group on the $n$-simplices of $X$ and let $\partial$ send an $n$-simplex to the alternating sum of its codimension-$1$ faces. Then $\partial^2=0$ is a combinatorial calculation, and the resulting $H_n(X;\mathbb{Z})$ are the simplicial homology groups of $X$. Over $\mathbb{Q}$ (which is what the widget below uses, so that everything reduces to linear algebra) the Betti numbers are $b_n = \dim_\mathbb{Q} H_n$.

Try the sphere: it has $H_0=\mathbb{Q}$ (connected), $H_1=0$ (no 1-dimensional holes), $H_2=\mathbb{Q}$ (one enclosed void). The torus famously has $H_0=\mathbb{Q},\ H_1=\mathbb{Q}^2,\ H_2=\mathbb{Q}$ — two independent $1$-cycles (the two generating loops) and one $2$-cycle (the whole surface). Ranks of homology are topological invariants; they do not depend on the triangulation.

2. Exact sequences

A sequence of abelian groups and homomorphisms

$\cdots \to A \xrightarrow{f} B \xrightarrow{g} C \to \cdots$

is exact at $B$ if $\operatorname{im} f = \ker g$. Exactness is a strengthening of "$g\circ f = 0$" (which says $\operatorname{im} f \subseteq \ker g$): the other inclusion is the content.

The two fundamental special cases:

  • Short exact sequence. $0 \to A \xrightarrow{f} B \xrightarrow{g} C \to 0$ — i.e. $f$ is injective, $g$ is surjective, and $\operatorname{im} f = \ker g$. Equivalently: $A\cong\ker g \subseteq B$ and $C\cong B/A$.
  • Long exact sequence. An exact sequence stretching in both directions. We'll see in §3 how a short exact sequence of complexes produces a long exact sequence in homology — the single most frequently used tool in the subject.
Classic example: for any $n\ge 1$, $$0 \;\to\; \mathbb{Z} \;\xrightarrow{\;\cdot n\;}\; \mathbb{Z} \;\twoheadrightarrow\; \mathbb{Z}/n \;\to\; 0.$$ Multiplication by $n$ is injective (no torsion in $\mathbb{Z}$), and its cokernel is $\mathbb{Z}/n$. Apply a left-exact functor like $\Hom(-,\mathbb{Z})$ and you discover that $\operatorname{Ext}^1(\mathbb{Z}/n,\mathbb{Z})\cong\mathbb{Z}/n$ — a failure of exactness precisely measured by $\operatorname{Ext}$.

3. The snake lemma

Everyone meets the snake lemma once and then uses it forever. Suppose we have a commutative diagram of abelian groups with exact rows:

$\begin{array}{ccccccccc} 0 & \to & A & \xrightarrow{\;i\;} & B & \xrightarrow{\;p\;} & C & \to & 0 \\ & & \downarrow\alpha & & \downarrow\beta & & \downarrow\gamma & & \\ 0 & \to & A' & \xrightarrow{\;i'\;} & B' & \xrightarrow{\;p'\;} & C' & \to & 0. \end{array}$

Snake lemma. There is a natural connecting homomorphism $\delta:\ker\gamma\to\operatorname{coker}\alpha$ making the following sequence exact: $$0 \to \ker\alpha \to \ker\beta \to \ker\gamma \xrightarrow{\;\delta\;} \operatorname{coker}\alpha \to \operatorname{coker}\beta \to \operatorname{coker}\gamma \to 0.$$

The point is that $\delta$ exists at all. Given $c\in\ker\gamma\subseteq C$, you lift to $b\in B$ with $p(b)=c$, apply $\beta$ to get $b'\in B'$; commutativity forces $p'(b') = \gamma(c) = 0$, so $b'$ lies in $\ker p' = \operatorname{im} i'$, and we can pull back to a unique $a'\in A'$. Well-defined modulo $\operatorname{im}\alpha$ by construction. The "snake" is the path your finger draws through the diagram as you follow the recipe: right, down, left. That is exactly the connecting morphism.

Specializing the snake lemma to a short exact sequence of chain complexes — interpret the top and bottom rows as the same complex at two consecutive degrees and iterate — gives the long exact sequence in homology. This is the single most important consequence.

Trace the connecting morphism δ
concrete example: the SES $0\to\mathbb{Z}/2\to\mathbb{Z}/4\to\mathbb{Z}/2\to 0$ mapping to itself

In the snake lemma's diagram, the rows are the SES at homological degree $n$ (top) and degree $n-1$ (bottom); the vertical maps are the differentials. Then $\ker\gamma/\operatorname{im}\alpha$ pieces are precisely $H_n(C)$-parts, and $\delta$ becomes the boundary in the long exact sequence in homology.

4. The five lemma

The snake lemma's close cousin, used constantly to recognize isomorphisms inside big diagrams. Suppose we have a commutative diagram of abelian groups (or objects of any abelian category) with exact rows:

$\begin{array}{ccccccccc} A_1 & \to & A_2 & \to & A_3 & \to & A_4 & \to & A_5 \\ \downarrow f_1 & & \downarrow f_2 & & \downarrow f_3 & & \downarrow f_4 & & \downarrow f_5 \\ B_1 & \to & B_2 & \to & B_3 & \to & B_4 & \to & B_5. \end{array}$

Five lemma. If $f_1, f_2, f_4, f_5$ are isomorphisms then $f_3$ is also an isomorphism. More precisely, it splits into two halves: $f_2$ surjective + $f_4$ injective $\Rightarrow$ $f_3$ surjective; $f_2$ injective + $f_4$ surjective $\Rightarrow$ $f_3$ injective.

The proof is the archetypal diagram chase. To see $f_3$ is injective, suppose $f_3(a_3) = 0$. Push $a_3$ one step to the right; by commutativity its image under $f_4$ is also zero, and since $f_4$ is injective, the pushed element is zero. Exactness then writes $a_3$ as the image of some $a_2$; push $a_2$ down and use commutativity; exactness on the bottom row backs up $f_2(a_2)$ to a $b_1$; the surjectivity of $f_1$ lifts $b_1$ to an $a_1$; finally commutativity forces the image to match. Trace out the tree of implications — it looks like the whale on a Moby-Dick cover.

Five-lemma chase
click step to watch the chase; pick which half to trace

Weakening any hypothesis in the five lemma breaks it: standard counterexamples drop $f_1$ surjective (so $f_3$ can fail to be injective even if everything else is an isomorphism), etc. The four outer hypotheses are each doing work — this is why the lemma is "tight."

5. The long exact sequence in homology

Suppose $0 \to A_\bullet \xrightarrow{f} B_\bullet \xrightarrow{g} C_\bullet \to 0$ is a short exact sequence of chain complexes — a triple of complexes with a chain map of each, so that in every degree $n$ the rows $0\to A_n \to B_n \to C_n\to 0$ are exact and all squares commute with the differentials. Applying the snake lemma to two consecutive degrees extracts a connecting homomorphism $\partial : H_n(C) \to H_{n-1}(A)$, and iterating over every $n$ splices the pieces into one infinite exact sequence:

$\cdots \to H_n(A) \xrightarrow{f_*} H_n(B) \xrightarrow{g_*} H_n(C) \xrightarrow{\;\partial\;} H_{n-1}(A) \to \cdots$

This is the tool you reach for first. For a pair $(X,Y)\hookrightarrow X$ of spaces the short exact sequence of singular-chain complexes $0\to C_\bullet(Y)\to C_\bullet(X)\to C_\bullet(X,Y)\to 0$ produces the relative homology long exact sequence; for $\Hom(-,N)$ applied to a short exact sequence $0\to A\to B\to C\to 0$ the analogous long exact sequence threads $\mathrm{Ext}^i$ together. Every time you see $\operatorname{Ext}^1$ measuring the failure of some $\Hom$ to be surjective, a long exact sequence is doing the bookkeeping.

Mayer–Vietoris is the long exact sequence attached to the SES $0\to C_\bullet(U\cap V)\to C_\bullet(U)\oplus C_\bullet(V)\to C_\bullet(U\cup V)\to 0$. It computes $H_*(S^n)$ in one line: gluing two $n$-disks along an equatorial $S^{n-1}$ gives $H_n(S^n)\cong H_{n-1}(S^{n-1})$, hence inductively $H_n(S^n)\cong\mathbb{Z}$.

6. Projective modules

A module $P$ over a ring $R$ is projective if every surjection $M\twoheadrightarrow P$ splits — equivalently, if for every surjection $\varphi\colon M\twoheadrightarrow N$ and every map $f\colon P\to N$, there exists a lift $\tilde f\colon P\to M$ with $\varphi\circ\tilde f = f$:

$\begin{array}{ccc} & & P \\ & \swarrow_{\tilde f} & \downarrow f \\ M & \xrightarrow{\;\varphi\;\;} & N. \end{array}$

Equivalent characterizations of $P$ projective:
  • $P$ is a direct summand of a free module;
  • $\Hom(P, -)$ is an exact functor (not merely left exact);
  • every short exact sequence $0\to A\to B\to P\to 0$ splits.
Over a PID (including $\mathbb{Z}$), projective $\iff$ free. Over a Dedekind domain, projective rank-$1$ modules are classified by the ideal class group.

Why care? Projectives are exactly the modules whose $\Hom$ functor preserves exactness — they are the "good" source side for derived functors. Free modules are visibly projective (the lift is computed on a basis), and the step from free to projective is exactly "direct summand of free."

7. Injective modules

Dual to projective: a module $I$ is injective if every injection $A\hookrightarrow B$ and every map $f\colon A\to I$ extends to a map $\tilde f\colon B\to I$:

$\begin{array}{ccc} A & \hookrightarrow & B \\ \downarrow f & \swarrow_{\tilde f} & \\ I. & & \end{array}$

Baer's criterion. $I$ is injective over a ring $R$ iff for every ideal $\mathfrak{a}\subseteq R$, every homomorphism $\mathfrak{a}\to I$ extends to $R\to I$. This reduces an a priori huge check to a tractable one — you only have to test against ideals, not all modules.

Over $\mathbb{Z}$, Baer's criterion gives a concrete characterization: a $\mathbb{Z}$-module $I$ is injective iff $I$ is divisible — for every $a\in I$ and every $n\ge 1$, there exists $x\in I$ with $nx=a$. The proof is one line: an ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ is $(n)$; extending $(n)\to I$ sending $n\mapsto a$ to $\mathbb{Z}\to I$ means finding $x\in I$ with $nx=a$. Examples of injective abelian groups: $\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$, the Prüfer groups $\mathbb{Z}/p^\infty$. Non-examples: $\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Z}/n$.

8. Flat modules

A module $M$ is flat if the functor $-\otimes_R M$ is exact — equivalently (since $-\otimes M$ is always right exact), if it takes injective maps to injective maps. Flatness is strictly between "free" and nothing: every projective module is flat, but many flat modules are not projective.

The canonical flat modules.
  • Free / projective. $R$, $R^n$, and direct summands of free modules are all flat.
  • Localizations. For any multiplicative set $S\subseteq R$, the localization $S^{-1}R$ is flat over $R$ — tensoring is just localizing on the nose, and localization is exact. Concretely, $\mathbb{Q}$ is flat (but not projective) over $\mathbb{Z}$.
  • Over a PID, flat $\iff$ torsion-free. So $\mathbb{Z}[1/p]$, $\mathbb{Q}$, and $\mathbb{Z}$ are flat; $\mathbb{Z}/n$ is not.

Flatness is detected by $\operatorname{Tor}$: $M$ is flat iff $\operatorname{Tor}_1^R(M,N) = 0$ for every $N$ (and then all higher Tor's vanish too). So "how non-flat is $M$?" is measured by its $\operatorname{Tor}_1$ with all test modules — a quantitative refinement of a qualitative property.

9. Derived functors, Ext and Tor

Many constructions in algebra are left or right exact but fail to be exact on both sides. $\Hom(M,-)$ is left exact; $-\otimes_A N$ is right exact; $(-)^G$ (invariants under a group action) is left exact; $\Gamma$ (global sections of a sheaf) is left exact. Derived functors package the failure into a sequence of functors $R^iF$ or $L_iF$ forming, for each short exact sequence in the source, a long exact sequence of derived values.

The recipe: to compute $R^iF(M)$ (for $F$ left exact) choose an injective resolution $0\to M\to I^0\to I^1\to\cdots$, apply $F$, and take cohomology of the resulting complex $F(I^\bullet)$. Dually, for $L_iF$ (with $F$ right exact), use a projective resolution. The miracle: the answer is independent of the resolution.

The two stars:

$\operatorname{Ext}^i_A(M,N)$ is the $i$-th right derived functor of $\Hom_A(-,N)$ applied to $M$ (or equivalently of $\Hom_A(M,-)$ applied to $N$). Compute it as $H^i(\Hom_A(P_\bullet, N))$ for any projective resolution $P_\bullet\to M$.

$\operatorname{Tor}_i^A(M,N)$ is the $i$-th left derived functor of $-\otimes_A N$ applied to $M$. Compute it as $H_i(P_\bullet \otimes_A N)$ for any projective (equivalently flat) resolution $P_\bullet\to M$.

Over $A=\mathbb{Z}$ everything is especially clean. Every finitely generated abelian group $M$ has a two-step free resolution $0\to\mathbb{Z}^r\to\mathbb{Z}^s\to M\to 0$, because the subgroup $\ker$ of a finitely generated free abelian group is again free. Consequently $\mathbb{Z}$ has global dimension $1$: $\operatorname{Ext}^i = \operatorname{Tor}_i = 0$ for $i\ge 2$.

10. Resolutions: projective, injective, flat

A projective resolution of an $A$-module $M$ is an exact sequence

$\cdots \to P_2 \to P_1 \to P_0 \twoheadrightarrow M \to 0$

in which every $P_i$ is projective (a direct summand of a free module). An injective resolution dually has $M \hookrightarrow I^0 \to I^1 \to \cdots$ exact with each $I^i$ injective. Flat resolutions use modules that are flat (tensor product with them preserves exact sequences). Free ⇒ projective ⇒ flat; over nice rings all three are distinct.

Why do resolutions always exist? In a category with enough projectives (every object is the quotient of a projective) you can iterate: $M$ is a quotient of some projective $P_0$; call the kernel $K_0$; it is a quotient of some projective $P_1$; continue. Same construction, dualized, works if there are enough injectives. The category $\mathsf{Ab}$ of abelian groups has both: projective = free (from a generating set) and injective = divisible (every abelian group embeds into a divisible one like $\prod \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$).

Over $\mathbb{Z}$, every finitely generated abelian group $M$ has a two-step free resolution $0 \to \mathbb{Z}^r \to \mathbb{Z}^s \to M \to 0$. The map $\mathbb{Z}^r \to \mathbb{Z}^s$ is a presentation matrix; its Smith normal form reads off the invariant factor decomposition $M \cong \mathbb{Z}^{s-r} \oplus \bigoplus \mathbb{Z}/d_i$.

The finitely generated case over $\mathbb{Z}$ is misleadingly clean. Over $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ a free resolution of $\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2)$ already has length $1$ but is infinite; over $\mathbb{Z}[x,y]$ things can get arbitrarily long. The global dimension of a ring is the supremum of projective-resolution lengths over all modules. For fields it is $0$; for PIDs it is $\le 1$; for polynomial rings in $n$ variables over a field it is $n$ (Hilbert syzygy theorem).

11. Spectral sequences (a gentle taste)

A spectral sequence is a sequence of "pages" $E_0, E_1, E_2, \dots$ where each page is a bigraded abelian group with a differential $d_r: E_r^{p,q} \to E_r^{p-r, q+r-1}$ (or with indices reversed, depending on convention) satisfying $d_r^2 = 0$, and the next page is the homology of the current one: $E_{r+1}^{p,q} = H(E_r^{p,q}, d_r)$. Under reasonable finiteness conditions, the differentials eventually vanish and the pages stabilize at an $E_\infty$ page, which is (up to a filtration) the answer you were after.

Two canonical examples:

  1. Filtered complex. Given a filtration $\cdots\subseteq F_p C \subseteq F_{p+1}C\subseteq \cdots$ on a chain complex, there is a spectral sequence with $E_0^{p,q} = F_pC_{p+q}/F_{p-1}C_{p+q}$ converging to $H_*(C)$.
  2. Double complex. A bigraded object $C^{p,q}$ with horizontal and vertical differentials $d_h, d_v$ satisfying $d_h^2 = d_v^2 = 0$ and $d_hd_v + d_vd_h = 0$ has total complex $\mathrm{Tot}^n = \bigoplus_{p+q=n} C^{p,q}$ with differential $d_h + d_v$. The two filtrations (by rows vs. by columns) give two spectral sequences, both converging to $H_*(\mathrm{Tot})$.

The double complex spectral sequence is the main application of "spectral sequences are easy." Example: for right-derived bifunctors, a Grothendieck spectral sequence of the form $E_2^{p,q} = R^p F(R^q G (X)) \Rightarrow R^{p+q}(FG)(X)$ organizes iterated derived functors. The Leray spectral sequence for $Y\to X$ and the Lyndon–Hochschild–Serre spectral sequence for $1\to N\to G\to Q\to 1$ are both special cases.

The spectral sequence of a double complex is an essential bookkeeping device, but its computational power is modest: it tells you that the totalization's homology is some extension of the limit entries by each other, not exactly what that extension is. Extension problems are the frustration tax of the subject.

12. Abelian categories and a triangulated preview

Every diagram chase in §§1–11 secretly takes place in an abelian category: a category with a zero object, all finite biproducts, all kernels and cokernels, and in which every monomorphism is a kernel and every epimorphism is a cokernel (the "every mono/epi is normal" axiom). In an abelian category, short exact sequences, the snake lemma, the five lemma, Ext/Tor, and all the tools we've built work verbatim.

Freyd–Mitchell embedding. Every small abelian category $\mathcal{A}$ admits a fully faithful, exact embedding $\mathcal{A}\hookrightarrow R\text{-Mod}$ for some ring $R$. Upshot: any element-level diagram chase in $\mathsf{Ab}$ transfers immediately to any abelian category — "you can do homological algebra without ever leaving modules."

Canonical examples of abelian categories:

  • $R$-Mod for any ring $R$ (left or right modules). The prototypical example.
  • Sheaves of abelian groups on a topological space (kernels computed sectionwise; cokernels require sheafification).
  • Chain complexes $\operatorname{Ch}(\mathcal{A})$ in an abelian category $\mathcal{A}$ — itself abelian, with pointwise kernels/cokernels.
  • Finitely generated modules over a Noetherian ring (abelian; closedness under kernel/cokernel uses Noetherian-ness).

The next conceptual leap is the derived category $D(\mathcal{A})$: take $\operatorname{Ch}(\mathcal{A})$, formally invert all quasi-isomorphisms, and work in the resulting category. It is not abelian — it is a triangulated category. Short exact sequences of complexes lift to distinguished triangles $X\to Y\to Z\xrightarrow{+1} X[1]$, and rotation/long-exact-sequence relations now follow from the triangulated axioms directly.

The fundamental identification: for $M,N\in\mathcal{A}$ viewed as complexes in degree $0$, $$\Hom_{D(\mathcal{A})}(M, N[i]) \;\cong\; \operatorname{Ext}^i_\mathcal{A}(M, N).$$ Every Ext group appears as a hom in the derived category after a shift. This is why modern homological algebra is written in derived/$\infty$-categorical language.

Where this goes next

What we've skipped or only previewed, in order of increasing sophistication:

  • The derived category, fleshed out. Instead of computing $\operatorname{Ext}$ and $\operatorname{Tor}$ one degree at a time, invert all quasi-isomorphisms of complexes and work in the resulting triangulated category $D(\mathsf{Ab})$. A map in $D(\mathsf{Ab})$ between two objects $M, N$ placed in degree zero is precisely $\operatorname{Ext}^0(M,N)$; a degree-$i$ map is $\operatorname{Ext}^i(M,N)$. Long exact sequences become distinguished triangles.
  • Sheaf cohomology. Apply the same machine to sheaves of abelian groups on a topological space. The global sections functor is left exact; its right derived functors $H^i(X,\mathcal{F}) := R^i\Gamma(\mathcal{F})$ are sheaf cohomology. Čech cohomology is a concrete model for this.
  • Group cohomology. $H^n(G,M) := \operatorname{Ext}^n_{\mathbb{Z}G}(\mathbb{Z}, M)$. It governs extensions of $G$ by $M$, obstructions to lifting actions, and the characteristic classes of central extensions.
  • Koszul duality and non-abelian derived functors. Replace $\mathsf{Ab}$ by more exotic categories — DG categories, $\infty$-categories, $\mathbb{E}_\infty$-algebras — and derived functors become Quillen-type functors between model categories. This is the setting for modern derived algebraic geometry and the work of Lurie and others.

The strange thing about homological algebra is that it started as a computation technique — Poincaré counting holes in surfaces — and ended up as the grammar in which nearly all modern algebraic geometry, representation theory, and algebraic topology is written. The derived functor is the word "and," everywhere.

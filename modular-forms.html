Modular forms on the upper half-plane

Functions on $\mathcal{H}$ that transform in a specific, rigid way under $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$. Their spaces are finite-dimensional, their Fourier coefficients encode arithmetic, and a single one — the $j$-invariant — parametrizes elliptic curves over $\mathbb{C}$.

Prerequisites. The geometric setup lives in the upper half-plane. Holomorphy in the sense needed here is complex analysis. The global viewpoint — a modular form is a section of a line bundle on a Riemann surface — is sketched in passing, and the arithmetic payoff (Eichler–Shimura, Taniyama) links back to elliptic curves.

1. Lattices in $\mathbb{C}$

A lattice in $\mathbb{C}$ is a discrete additive subgroup of rank $2$: a set $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$ where $\omega_1, \omega_2 \in \mathbb{C}$ are $\mathbb{R}$-linearly independent (equivalently, $\omega_2/\omega_1 \notin \mathbb{R}$). Geometrically it is the set of integer combinations of two non-parallel arrows; the four points $0, \omega_1, \omega_1+\omega_2, \omega_2$ outline a fundamental parallelogram whose area $|\det[\omega_1\,\omega_2]|$ is called the covolume of $\Lambda$ — it depends only on $\Lambda$, not on the chosen basis.

That last clause is the whole point. Any lattice has infinitely many ordered bases, and two ordered bases $(\omega_1,\omega_2)$ and $(\omega_1',\omega_2')$ generate the same $\Lambda$ iff they are related by a matrix in $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$; restricting to oriented bases (those with $\mathrm{Im}(\omega_2/\omega_1) > 0$) cuts this down to $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$. So the modular group is the basis-change group of a $2$-dimensional lattice. Sending an oriented basis to its ratio $\tau = \omega_2/\omega_1 \in \mathcal{H}$ identifies the space of similarity classes of lattices with $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) \backslash \mathcal{H}$, which is why a modular form — at first a function on lattices that scales by a fixed power under $\Lambda \mapsto c\Lambda$ — is the same data as an $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$-equivariant function of $\tau$.

The fundamental parallelogram with corners $0,\,v_1,\,v_1+v_2,\,v_2$ is a fundamental domain for $\Lambda$ acting on $\mathbb{C}$ by translation; its Euclidean area is the covolume $|\det[v_1\ v_2]|$, an $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$-invariant of $\Lambda$. The pink overlay shows a sublattice $\Lambda' = M \cdot \Lambda$ with $M = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}2&1\\0&2\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$; the index $[\Lambda:\Lambda'] = |\det M| = 4$ is the number of cosets, and equivalently the ratio of covolumes. Slide $v_1, v_2$ and watch both invariants stay rigid as the lattice deforms.

2. The group $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ and its action on $\mathcal{H}$

The modular group is $$\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) \;=\; \Bigl\{ \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} : a,b,c,d \in \mathbb{Z},\; ad-bc = 1 \Bigr\}.$$ It acts on the upper half-plane $\mathcal{H} = \{\tau \in \mathbb{C} : \mathrm{Im}\,\tau > 0\}$ by Möbius transformations, $$\gamma \cdot \tau \;=\; \frac{a\tau + b}{c\tau + d}.$$ The identity matrix and $-I$ act trivially, so the effective action is by $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) = \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})/\{\pm I\}$.

Two generators do all the work

A remarkable classical fact: every element of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ is a word in $$S \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad T \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.$$ On the upper half-plane $S$ is the inversion $\tau \mapsto -1/\tau$, and $T$ is the unit translation $\tau \mapsto \tau + 1$. They satisfy $S^2 = -I$ and $(ST)^3 = -I$; in $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ these become $S^2 = 1$ and $(ST)^3 = 1$, and there are no further relations — we will say more about this presentation when it matters.

The action is geometric: $T$ slides the whole half-plane horizontally, and $S$ is the "conformal flip" that interchanges the inside and outside of the unit circle, upper half preserved. Compose them and you can carry any point anywhere — but in an extremely constrained way, because determinant one and integer entries together force a Euclidean-algorithm rigidity.

3. The fundamental domain $\mathcal{F}$

If a group acts, it is natural to ask for a set of representatives for the orbits. For $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ on $\mathcal{H}$ the answer is beautiful and explicit: $$\mathcal{F} \;=\; \Bigl\{\, \tau \in \mathcal{H} \,:\, |\mathrm{Re}\,\tau| \le \tfrac{1}{2} \text{ and } |\tau| \ge 1 \,\Bigr\}.$$ Every orbit hits $\mathcal{F}$ at least once; two points of $\mathcal{F}$ lie in the same orbit only through the boundary identifications $\tau \sim \tau+1$ on the vertical sides and $\tau \sim -1/\tau$ on the bottom arc.

The darker tile is the canonical $\mathcal{F}$. Every other tile is $\gamma \mathcal{F}$ for some $\gamma \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$.

Area, cusp, and elliptic points

The hyperbolic area of $\mathcal{F}$ is $\pi/3$ — a famous finite answer that seeds every volume computation for arithmetic quotients. Three kinds of points in $\mathcal{F}$ play a special structural role:

  • $i$ is fixed by $S$ — stabilizer of order $2$;
  • $\rho = e^{2\pi i/3}$ is fixed by $ST$ — stabilizer of order $3$;
  • the cusp $i\infty$ (a single boundary point after the identification) — stabilizer $\langle T \rangle$.

These are exactly the points where the quotient $\mathcal{H}/\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ fails to be a smooth manifold in the naive sense; adding the cusp compactifies the quotient to a Riemann surface of genus $0$, the modular curve $X(1)$.

4. What is a modular form?

Fix an integer $k \ge 0$. A modular form of weight $k$ for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ is a holomorphic function $f \colon \mathcal{H} \to \mathbb{C}$ satisfying the transformation law $$f\!\left(\frac{a\tau + b}{c\tau + d}\right) \;=\; (c\tau + d)^k \, f(\tau) \qquad \text{for all }\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}),$$ together with a holomorphicity condition at the cusp $i\infty$: the function $$\tilde f(q) \;=\; f(\tau), \qquad q = e^{2\pi i \tau}$$ extends to a holomorphic function on a neighborhood of $q=0$. Applying the law to $T$ gives $f(\tau+1) = f(\tau)$, which is exactly the periodicity that lets us Fourier-expand in $q$.

If $k$ is odd the transformation law applied to $-I \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ forces $f = (-1)^k f$, so $f \equiv 0$. Hence only even weights give anything interesting.

The two minimal identities

Because $S$ and $T$ generate, $f$ is modular of weight $k$ if and only if it is holomorphic, holomorphic at $\infty$, and satisfies both $$f(\tau + 1) = f(\tau), \qquad f(-1/\tau) = \tau^k f(\tau).$$ Almost every proof about modular forms is, at heart, a clever use of these two identities at once.

That is why only even $k$ can have nonzero invariants: odd powers of the cotangent bundle pick up a sign under $-I$.

5. Eisenstein series: the first examples

The cleanest way to produce a modular form is to average. For even $k \ge 4$, define the Eisenstein series $$E_k(\tau) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{\substack{(m,n) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 \\ (m,n)\ne(0,0)}} \frac{1}{(m\tau + n)^k}.$$ The half is for normalization ($(m,n)$ and $(-m,-n)$ give the same term when $k$ is even). The series converges absolutely for $k \ge 4$ and defines a holomorphic function on $\mathcal{H}$.

Why it transforms correctly

For $\gamma = \begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ apply the change of variable $(m,n) \mapsto (m,n)\gamma^{-1}$ in the sum: the lattice $\mathbb{Z}^2 \setminus \{0\}$ is stable under $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$, and a direct computation gives $m(\gamma\tau) + n = (c\tau + d)^{-1} \cdot (m'\tau + n')$ for the relabeled indices. Summing over the permuted lattice yields $E_k(\gamma\tau) = (c\tau+d)^k E_k(\tau)$.

The $q$-expansion and divisor sums

Every modular form for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ is, by $T$-periodicity, a power series in $q = e^{2\pi i \tau}$. The $q$-expansion of $E_k$ (with a standard normalization where the constant term is $1$) is $$E_k(\tau) \;=\; 1 - \frac{2k}{B_k} \sum_{n\ge 1} \sigma_{k-1}(n)\, q^n,$$ where $B_k$ is the $k$-th Bernoulli number and $\sigma_{s}(n) = \sum_{d \mid n} d^s$. Two features of this picture are hard to overstate. First: a single transcendentally-defined function on a complex domain is really a sequence of integers — in the $E_4$ case, the integers $240\,\sigma_3(n)$ counting divisor sums. Second: the same integers arise from counting lattice points on spheres (the theta functions used in sums of squares), which is why modular forms encode arithmetic.

6. The discriminant $\Delta$ and the $j$-invariant

Define $$\Delta(\tau) \;=\; \frac{E_4(\tau)^3 - E_6(\tau)^2}{1728}.$$ This is a modular form of weight $12$: subtract the right multiple of $E_4^3$ from $E_6^2$ and the constant term cancels, leaving a $q$-series that starts with $q$. Its $q$-expansion is the famous Ramanujan tau function: $$\Delta(\tau) \;=\; q \prod_{n=1}^{\infty}(1-q^n)^{24} \;=\; \sum_{n\ge 1} \tau(n)\, q^n, \qquad \tau(1)=1,\,\tau(2)=-24,\,\tau(3)=252,\,\ldots$$ $\Delta$ has no zeros on $\mathcal{H}$ — it vanishes only to first order at the cusp. This makes the quotient $$j(\tau) \;=\; \frac{E_4(\tau)^3}{\Delta(\tau)} \;=\; \frac{1}{q} + 744 + 196884\, q + 21493760\, q^2 + \cdots$$ well-defined and holomorphic on all of $\mathcal{H}$, with a simple pole at the cusp. It has weight $4 \cdot 3 - 12 = 0$ — it is modular-invariant, a genuine function on $\mathcal{H}/\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$.

The one-line theorem

Theorem. $j \colon \mathcal{H}/\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) \to \mathbb{C}$ is a bijection, and the map extends to an isomorphism of Riemann surfaces $X(1) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathbb{P}^1_\mathbb{C}$.

In particular, two points $\tau_1, \tau_2 \in \mathcal{H}$ give isomorphic complex elliptic curves $\mathbb{C}/(\mathbb{Z} + \tau_i \mathbb{Z})$ iff $j(\tau_1) = j(\tau_2)$. The $j$-invariant is the moduli-space coordinate — see moduli spaces for the general story.

7. Spaces of modular forms and cusp forms

Fix even $k \ge 0$. Let $M_k$ denote the $\mathbb{C}$-vector space of weight-$k$ modular forms for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$, and $S_k \subset M_k$ the subspace of cusp forms — those vanishing at $i\infty$, i.e.\ with $q$-expansion starting at $q^1$.

The dimension formula

A contour-integral / valence argument (Serre's valence formula) gives the clean dimension count:

Widget 6 · Dimensions of $M_k$ and $S_k$$\dim M_k = \lfloor k/12 \rfloor + \epsilon_k$ with a single correction at $k \equiv 2 \pmod{12}$
k ≤ 24

The key structural facts one reads off:

  • $\dim M_k = 0$ for $k < 0$ or $k$ odd;
  • $\dim M_k = 1$ for $k \in \{0,4,6,8,10,14\}$ — these are spanned by $1, E_4, E_6, E_8 = E_4^2, E_{10} = E_4 E_6, E_{14} = E_4^2 E_6$;
  • $\dim M_{12} = 2$, spanned by $E_{12}$ and $\Delta$;
  • $\dim S_k = \dim M_k - 1$ for $k \ge 12$: one constraint to kill the value at the cusp.

The ring structure

Collecting all weights, $M_* = \bigoplus_k M_k$ is a graded $\mathbb{C}$-algebra. The finite-dimensional data above is, miraculously, one fact:

Structure theorem. $M_*(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})) \;=\; \mathbb{C}[E_4, E_6]$, a polynomial ring in two algebraically independent generators, with $\deg E_4 = 4$ and $\deg E_6 = 6$.

A one-variable polynomial ring has a predictable dimension growth; the two-variable graded piece gives exactly the "floor of $k/12$" shape. From this one gets $\dim S_k = \dim M_{k-12}$ and the multiplication-by-$\Delta$ isomorphism $M_{k-12} \xrightarrow{\sim} S_k$.

8. $q$-expansions and the shadow of Hecke theory

The structure so far is elegant but static. The arithmetic really begins when we ask: what operators act on the finite-dimensional spaces $M_k$ and $S_k$?

The $q$-expansion principle

Two modular forms of the same weight with the same $q$-expansion are equal — holomorphy at the cusp plus the identity theorem force it. This is why a finite initial segment of Fourier coefficients is the form.

Hecke operators — a first look

For each prime $p$ there is a linear operator $T_p$ on $M_k$ defined on $q$-expansions by the curious-looking formula $$(T_p f)(\tau) \;=\; \sum_{n\ge 0} \left( a_{pn} + p^{k-1} a_{n/p} \right) q^n,$$ where $a_{n/p} = 0$ if $p \nmid n$. The $T_p$ commute, are self-adjoint for the Petersson inner product, and preserve $S_k$. This forces $S_k$ to decompose into simultaneous eigenspaces — the Hecke eigenforms — and each eigenform has Fourier coefficients that form a multiplicative sequence. In the one-dimensional case $S_{12} = \mathbb{C}\cdot \Delta$, the eigenvalue of $T_p$ on $\Delta$ is precisely Ramanujan's $\tau(p)$, and the multiplicativity says $$\tau(mn) = \tau(m)\tau(n) \text{ when } (m,n)=1, \qquad \tau(p^{r+1}) = \tau(p)\tau(p^r) - p^{11}\tau(p^{r-1}).$$ These relations encode the arithmetic content of $\Delta$.

Looking forward. The Hecke algebra $\mathbb{T}_k \subset \mathrm{End}(S_k)$ and its eigenforms are the link to Galois representations: to each eigenform one attaches a $2$-dimensional $\ell$-adic representation of $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$ whose trace on Frobenius at $p$ equals $a_p$. That correspondence, in the weight-$2$ case, is the modularity theorem for elliptic curves. The full action of $\mathbb{T}$, its structure as a commutative ring, and the Petersson product live in a next chapter of their own.

Where this page ends

Everything here has been at level $1$ — full $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ invariance. The next moves are:

  • replace $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ by a congruence subgroup $\Gamma_0(N)$, producing modular forms of level $N$ and a richer compactified modular curve $X_0(N)$;
  • add characters (nebentype) to get a Dirichlet-twisted version of the theory;
  • attach an $L$-function $L(f,s) = \sum a_n n^{-s}$ to each eigenform, a Dirichlet series with Euler product expressing the Hecke relations analytically;
  • tie these $L$-functions to Galois representations and to the $L$-functions of elliptic curves.

But the engine is already in place: a finite-dimensional space, a canonical basis of Hecke eigenforms, and a $q$-expansion that turns a transcendental function into an arithmetic sequence.

9. The Petersson inner product

The spaces $S_k$ of cusp forms are finite-dimensional, but so far we have no geometry on them — no notion of angle, length, or orthogonality. Petersson's observation is that the hyperbolic measure $d\mu = y^{-2}\,dx\,dy$ on the upper half-plane, combined with the weight-$k$ factor, produces a genuinely $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$-invariant pairing. For $f, g \in S_k$ define

$$\langle f, g\rangle \;=\; \int_{\Gamma\backslash \mathbb{H}} f(\tau)\,\overline{g(\tau)}\; y^{k-2}\,dx\,dy, \qquad \tau = x + iy.$$

The integrand is well-defined on the fundamental domain because under $\gamma = \begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}\in\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ the ratio $|c\tau+d|^{-2k}$ in $|f|^2$ is exactly compensated by the transformation $y \mapsto y / |c\tau+d|^2$ of the imaginary part, leaving $|f|^2 y^{k}$ invariant and the measure $y^{-2}dx\,dy$ invariant on its own.

Why the product converges on $S_k$

Near the cusp $i\infty$, a cusp form $f$ decays like $|f(\tau)| \ll e^{-2\pi y}$, so $|f|^2 y^{k-2}$ is integrable even though $y^{k-2}$ blows up; the exponential wins. At the elliptic points $\rho, i$ of the fundamental domain the integrand is smooth. For non-cuspidal forms the integral diverges at $i\infty$ — orthogonality is a phenomenon of $S_k$, and this is exactly why the Eisenstein subspace $E_k$ appears as its orthogonal complement in

$$M_k \;=\; S_k \oplus E_k.$$

Hecke eigenforms are orthogonal

Hecke operators $T_n$ with $(n, N)=1$ are self-adjoint for the Petersson product: $\langle T_n f, g\rangle = \langle f, T_n g\rangle$. Because the $T_n$ commute and are simultaneously diagonalizable, the spectral theorem gives an orthogonal basis of $S_k$ consisting of simultaneous Hecke eigenforms. If $f, g$ are two normalized eigenforms with distinct eigenvalue systems $\{a_p\}\ne\{b_p\}$, then

$$\langle f, g\rangle \;=\; 0.$$

This is the input that makes the Euler-product decomposition of $L(f,s)$ a canonical factorization: eigenforms are not just convenient, they are the orthonormal frame picked out by the geometry.

Looking forward. The Petersson norm $\langle f, f\rangle$ of a normalized eigenform appears in the completed $L$-function's functional equation constant and in period formulas; for a weight-$2$ newform attached to an elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$, it is a modular analogue of the real period. These "Petersson periods" are the bridge from the Hilbert-space picture to the arithmetic of $L$-values.

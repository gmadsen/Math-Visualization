Elementary topos theory

A finitely complete cartesian closed category with a subobject classifier $\Omega$.

1. What is a topos?

An elementary topos is a category $\mathcal{E}$ that is (i) finitely complete, (ii) cartesian closed, and (iii) equipped with a subobject classifier $\Omega$. Equivalently, by a theorem of Mikkelsen, finitely complete + power objects $P(A)$ — the other two axioms follow. Mac Lane and Moerdijk's Sheaves in Geometry and Logic develops the theory in this order; Johnstone's Elephant takes the power-object route.

Each axiom does work. Finite limits give us pullbacks, and so subobjects, equalisers, and fibre products — the bones of any "category of structured sets." Cartesian closure gives an internal hom: an object $B^A$ such that maps $X \to B^A$ correspond naturally to maps $X \times A \to B$. This lets the category internalise function spaces, and it's exactly the adjunction $(-)\times A \dashv (-)^A$. Together with the terminal object $1$ this already makes $\mathcal{E}$ a model of the simply-typed lambda calculus.

The third axiom — a subobject classifier $\Omega$ — is what makes $\mathcal{E}$ truly set-like rather than merely cartesian-closed. It says that for every object $X$ the partially ordered class $\mathrm{Sub}(X)$ of subobjects is representable: $\mathrm{Sub}(X) \cong \mathrm{Hom}(X, \Omega)$ naturally in $X$. Equivalently, the contravariant functor $\mathrm{Sub}\colon \mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$ is representable — and the representing object $\Omega$ inherits enough internal structure to model first-order intuitionist logic. Given $(\mathcal{E}, \Omega)$ one recovers exponentials $\Omega^A = P(A)$ as the "power object" classifying subobjects of $A$ — so the three axioms triangulate.

Examples to keep in mind throughout: $\mathbf{Set}$ itself (with $\Omega = \{\bot, \top\}$), every presheaf category $[C^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}]$, every category $\mathrm{Sh}(X)$ of sheaves on a topological space, and (with mild hypotheses) the category $G\text{-}\mathbf{Set}$ of left $G$-sets for a group $G$. Non-examples worth flagging: $\mathbf{Top}$ (no exponentials in general — function spaces aren't always topologisable) and $\mathbf{Ab}$ (an abelian category never has a subobject classifier unless it is trivial, since $\Omega$ is rarely additive).

2. The subobject classifier $\Omega$

A subobject classifier in $\mathcal{E}$ is a monomorphism $\mathrm{true}\colon 1 \hookrightarrow \Omega$ with the universal property that every monomorphism $S \hookrightarrow X$ in $\mathcal{E}$ fits into a unique pullback square

$$\begin{array}{ccc} S & \to & 1 \\ \downarrow & & \downarrow \mathrm{true} \\ X & \xrightarrow{\chi_S} & \Omega \end{array}$$

The map $\chi_S\colon X \to \Omega$ is the characteristic map (or classifying map) of $S$. Universality says $\chi$ is unique: $\mathrm{Sub}(X) \to \mathrm{Hom}(X, \Omega)$, $S \mapsto \chi_S$, is a bijection natural in $X$. By Yoneda this means $\Omega$ represents the contravariant subobject functor $\mathrm{Sub}\colon \mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$.

In $\mathbf{Set}$ the classifier is $\Omega = \{\bot, \top\}$ with $\mathrm{true}\colon \{*\} \to \{\bot,\top\}$ picking out $\top$. The characteristic map of $S \subseteq X$ is the indicator function $\chi_S(x) = \top$ iff $x \in S$. The pullback of $\{*\}$ along $\chi_S$ is exactly $\chi_S^{-1}(\top) = S$. Two-valued, classical, sharp.

In other toposes $\Omega$ has more shape. In a presheaf topos $\hat{C}$ the elements of $\Omega(c)$ are the sieves on $c$ — the downward-closed subsets of $C/c$ — and $\mathrm{true}_c$ is the maximal sieve. In sheaves on a space $X$, sections of $\Omega$ over an open $U$ are open subsets of $U$. In each case $\Omega$ inherits an internal Heyting-algebra structure from intersections of subobjects: $\Omega$ is the topos's "object of truth values," and that lattice is generally not Boolean — which is why topos logic is intuitionist by default.

3. Characteristic maps as truth values

Once we have $\Omega$, every subobject $S \hookrightarrow X$ is named by a unique map $\chi_S\colon X \to \Omega$, and conversely every $X \to \Omega$ comes from a subobject by pulling back $\mathrm{true}$. So $\Omega$ doubles as the topos's object of truth values: a global section $1 \to \Omega$ is a propositional truth value, and a generalised element $X \to \Omega$ is a "predicate on $X$."

The reason this is useful is that logical operations on predicates become morphisms in $\mathcal{E}$. The internal conjunction $\wedge\colon \Omega \times \Omega \to \Omega$ is the characteristic map of the diagonal $\langle \mathrm{true}, \mathrm{true}\rangle\colon 1 \hookrightarrow \Omega \times \Omega$. The internal implication $\Rightarrow\colon \Omega \times \Omega \to \Omega$ classifies $\{(p,q) : p \le q\} \hookrightarrow \Omega \times \Omega$. From these one builds an internal Heyting algebra structure on every $\mathrm{Sub}(X)$: $\chi_{S \cap T} = \chi_S \wedge \chi_T$, and so on. In $\mathbf{Set}$ this is just the boolean algebra of subsets; in a non-Boolean topos negation is genuinely weaker than excluded middle.

Concretely: classifying $S \cap T$ as a subobject of $X$ amounts to forming the pullback of $\mathrm{true}\colon 1 \to \Omega$ along $X \xrightarrow{\langle \chi_S, \chi_T\rangle} \Omega \times \Omega \xrightarrow{\wedge} \Omega$. Every Boolean / Heyting reasoning step you'd write on the board for $\mathbf{Set}$ has an exact diagrammatic counterpart inside $\mathcal{E}$. This is the bridge to the Mitchell–Bénabou internal language: a topos is internally a model of higher-order intuitionist logic.

4. Power objects $P(A)$

The power object of $A$ is $P(A) = \Omega^A$ — the exponential of $\Omega$ by $A$. It represents the "subobjects of $A$, parameterised by $X$" functor: there is a natural bijection

$$\mathrm{Sub}(X \times A) \;\cong\; \mathrm{Hom}(X \times A, \Omega) \;\cong\; \mathrm{Hom}(X, \Omega^A) \;=\; \mathrm{Hom}(X, P(A)).$$

The first iso is the universal property of $\Omega$; the second is currying via cartesian closure. Together they say that giving an $X$-indexed family of subobjects of $A$ is the same as giving a single map $X \to P(A)$. Setting $X = 1$ recovers $\mathrm{Sub}(A) \cong \mathrm{Hom}(1, P(A))$, the global elements of $P(A)$ — the "actual subsets" of $A$ that the topos sees.

In $\mathbf{Set}$ the power object $P(A)$ is the usual power set $\mathcal{P}(A)$, and $|P(A)| = 2^{|A|}$. The element-of relation $\in_A \subseteq A \times P(A)$ is classified by the evaluation map $\mathrm{ev}\colon A \times P(A) \to \Omega$. In a presheaf topos $\hat{C}$ one computes $P(A)(c) = \{\text{subpresheaves of } A \times \mathrm{Hom}(-,c)\}$ — markedly bigger than $2^{A(c)}$ in general, because subpresheaves can be more refined than pointwise subsets.

The Mikkelsen / Paré / Tierney theorem says power objects are essentially what defines a topos: a finitely complete category with power objects automatically has exponentials and a subobject classifier. So $P(A)$ is not just a derived gadget — it can stand in for the entire definition.

5. Presheaf toposes $\hat{C} = [C^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}]$

For any small category $C$, the presheaf category $\hat{C} = [C^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}]$ is an elementary topos. Limits and colimits are computed pointwise. Exponentials are given by the formula $G^F(c) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\hat C}(F \times \mathrm{Hom}(-, c), G)$ — Yoneda is doing the work.

The subobject classifier of $\hat{C}$ is the presheaf $\Omega$ defined by $\Omega(c) = \{\text{sieves on } c\}$. A sieve on $c$ is a set $S$ of morphisms with codomain $c$ that is downward closed under precomposition: $f \in S$ and $g$ composable ⇒ $f \circ g \in S$. Equivalently, a sieve is a subpresheaf of the representable $\mathrm{Hom}(-, c) = よ(c)$. The "true" arrow $\mathrm{true}_c\colon 1 \to \Omega(c)$ picks out the maximal sieve (all of $C/c$); restriction along $f\colon c' \to c$ pulls back $S$ to $f^*S = \{g : f\circ g \in S\}$.

The poset case is most concrete. If $C$ is a poset (viewed as a category with at most one arrow between any two objects), then a sieve on $c$ is exactly a downward-closed subset of $\{c' \in C : c' \le c\}$, i.e. a down-set in the principal filter at $c$. The lattice of sieves on $c$ is then the Heyting algebra of down-sets — generally not Boolean, which is why presheaf toposes are intuitionist.

Presheaf toposes are the workhorse non-classical examples in topos theory. Special cases: $\mathbf{Set}^{\to}$ — the arrow category (presheaves on the walking arrow $\bullet \to \bullet$) — has $\Omega$ a 3-element set; the topos of $G$-sets is $[\mathbf{B}G^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}]$; simplicial sets are $[\Delta^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}]$. Every Grothendieck topos arises as a left-exact reflective subcategory of some $\hat{C}$, so presheaf toposes are universal in a precise sense.

6. The topos of $G$-sets

For a group $G$, let $\mathbf{B}G$ denote the one-object groupoid with $\mathrm{Hom}(*,*) = G$. The category of left $G$-sets — sets $X$ with a left $G$-action $G \times X \to X$ — is exactly the presheaf topos $[\mathbf{B}G^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}] = G\text{-}\mathbf{Set}$. (The $\mathrm{op}$ swaps left/right; for a group it makes no essential difference because $g \mapsto g^{-1}$ is an isomorphism.) So everything from the presheaf section applies: $G\text{-}\mathbf{Set}$ has all limits and colimits computed underlyingly in $\mathbf{Set}$ with the $G$-action carried along, and it is cartesian closed with $Y^X$ the set of $G$-equivariant maps $X \to Y$ on which $G$ acts by conjugation.

Its subobjects of a transitive $G$-set $G/H$ are particularly clean: a subobject of $G/H$ is determined by an intermediate subgroup $H \le K \le G$, namely the image of $G/H \to G/K$. Equivariant maps $G/H \to G/K$ correspond to $H$-fixed cosets $K\setminus G^H$, which by Frobenius reciprocity is non-empty iff $H \le K$ up to conjugation. So the lattice of subobjects of $G/H$ inside $G\text{-}\mathbf{Set}$ records the subgroup structure between $H$ and $G$.

The subobject classifier $\Omega$ in $G\text{-}\mathbf{Set}$ is the set of subgroups of $G$ (more precisely: its underlying set is the set of subgroups, with $G$ acting by conjugation $g\cdot H = gHg^{-1}$). The arrow $\mathrm{true}\colon 1 \to \Omega$ picks out the full subgroup $G$ itself. Given a $G$-equivariant inclusion $S \hookrightarrow X$, the classifying map $\chi_S\colon X \to \Omega$ sends $x$ to the stabiliser-of-membership $\{g \in G : gx \in S\}$ — a subgroup of $G$, hence an element of $\Omega$. When $G$ is the trivial group this collapses to $\{\bot, \top\}$ and we recover ordinary $\mathbf{Set}$.

7. Geometric morphisms

A geometric morphism $f\colon \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{E}$ between toposes is the data of an adjoint pair $f^* \dashv f_*$, where $f^*\colon \mathcal{E} \to \mathcal{F}$ is the inverse image functor and $f_*\colon \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{E}$ is the direct image, subject to the constraint that $f^*$ is left exact — preserves all finite limits. The direction is the topology direction: $f^*$ goes the same way as the geometric morphism's named arrow $f$, while $f_*$ goes the other way. (One symbol; two functors; the asymmetry is real.)

The motivating example is a continuous map $f\colon X \to Y$ between topological spaces. It induces $f^{-1}\colon \mathrm{Open}(Y) \to \mathrm{Open}(X)$ and the corresponding inverse-image / direct-image pair on sheaves: $f^*\colon \mathrm{Sh}(Y) \to \mathrm{Sh}(X)$ takes the sheaf-pullback (and is left exact because $f^{-1}$ preserves finite intersections), while $f_*$ takes pushforward sheaves. Continuous maps of spaces $\rightleftarrows$ geometric morphisms of toposes is functorial up to canonical 2-iso, with the topos $\mathrm{Sh}(X)$ remembering all the topological information of a sober space.

Geometric morphisms also encode "points." A point of a topos $\mathcal{E}$ is a geometric morphism $\mathbf{Set} \to \mathcal{E}$, mirroring the fact that points of a space $X$ correspond to continuous maps from a one-point space. For a sheaf topos $\mathrm{Sh}(X)$ this gives the points of $X$ (when $X$ is sober). In the algebraic-geometric direction, a ring homomorphism $A \to B$ induces a geometric morphism $\mathrm{Sh}(\mathrm{Spec}\, B) \to \mathrm{Sh}(\mathrm{Spec}\, A)$ — same direction as the spec map.

Special families: a geometric morphism is an embedding when $f_*$ is full and faithful (think: closed/open subspace inclusion); a surjection when $f^*$ is faithful (covers); and an essential geometric morphism additionally has a left adjoint $f_!$ to $f^*$ (so $f_! \dashv f^* \dashv f_*$). The Grothendieck programme then unfolds: morphisms of sites induce geometric morphisms, the inclusion $\mathrm{Sh}(C, J) \hookrightarrow \hat{C}$ of sheaves into presheaves is a geometric morphism with $f^* = $ sheafification, and so on.

Modularity and Fermat's Last Theorem

Three ideas, one proof. A phantom solution $a^p + b^p = c^p$ conjures an elliptic curve so peculiar that modularity and level-lowering cannot both accommodate it. The contradiction that closes the tile is Wiles' theorem; the lever that flips the key is Ribet's.

Prerequisites: elliptic curves, modular forms, Galois representations, $L$-functions.

1. The Frey curve

Suppose, for contradiction, that there are pairwise coprime nonzero integers $a, b, c$ and a prime $p \ge 5$ with $$a^p + b^p = c^p.$$ Gerhard Frey's 1985 insight was that this hypothetical triple gives rise to a very concrete elliptic curve: $$E_{a,b,c}: \ y^2 \ =\ x\,(x - a^p)\,(x + b^p).$$ If $a^p + b^p = c^p$, then the three roots $0,\, a^p,\, -b^p$ of the right-hand side are three integers that sum to $0$ — a hidden piece of additive structure baked into a multiplicative equation.

The curve's discriminant is the square of the product of root differences (times a standard factor). Here the differences are $a^p,\, b^p,\, a^p + b^p = c^p$, giving $$\Delta(E_{a,b,c}) \ =\ 16\,(a^p \, b^p \, c^p)^2 \ =\ 16\,(abc)^{2p}.$$ Passing to a minimal Weierstrass model absorbs a factor of $2^8$, leaving the cleaner formula $$\Delta \ =\ \frac{(abc)^{2p}}{2^{8}}.$$

Frey's key observation. At every odd prime $\ell \mid abc$, the discriminant has multiplicity $2p$ — a perfect square, in fact a perfect $2p$-th power. Tate's algorithm then says $E$ has multiplicative reduction at $\ell$: the reduction mod $\ell$ is a nodal cubic, not a cusp. So $E$ is semistable away from $2$, and its conductor $N_E$ is squarefree outside $2$.

Semistability is a very strong regularity condition on an elliptic curve. It means the $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\overline\rho_{E,p}: G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is unramified at every odd $\ell \nmid N_E$ and tamely ramified with a very simple shape at the bad primes. This tame ramification is the hook that Ribet's theorem in §3 will exploit.

There is a second piece of structure: the three 2-torsion points $(0,0),\ (a^p, 0),\ (-b^p, 0)$ are all rational, so $E[2](\mathbb{Q}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2)^2$. The mod-2 Galois representation is already trivial, and the mod-$p$ representation $\overline\rho_{E,p}$ is the interesting one.

2. The modularity theorem

The punchline of the second half of the 20th century in arithmetic:

Modularity theorem (Wiles + Taylor–Wiles 1995, semistable; Breuil–Conrad–Diamond–Taylor 2001, general). Let $E/\mathbb{Q}$ be an elliptic curve of conductor $N_E$. Then there exists a weight-2 newform $f_E \in S_2(\Gamma_0(N_E))$ such that $$a_p(E) \ =\ a_p(f_E) \qquad \text{for every prime } p \nmid N_E,$$ where $a_p(E) = p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is the Frobenius trace and $a_p(f_E)$ is the $p$-th Fourier coefficient of $f_E$.

The correspondence is extraordinary: on the left, an algebraic object (points on a cubic mod $p$); on the right, a transcendental one (a holomorphic function on the upper half-plane). Modularity says the two bookkeepers agree — and more than agree, they are two descriptions of a single Galois representation $$\rho_{E,\ell}: G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$$ with the same characteristic polynomial of Frobenius on both sides.

The simplest nontrivial example is the curve $E: y^2 + y = x^3 - x^2$ of conductor $N_E = 11$ (Cremona label 11a1). Its newform is the eta quotient $$f_E(\tau) \ =\ \eta(\tau)^2\,\eta(11\tau)^2 \ =\ q\prod_{n\ge 1}(1-q^n)^2(1-q^{11n})^2,$$ whose $q$-expansion we can match term-by-term with Frobenius traces computed by counting points on $E \bmod p$.

3. Ribet's level lowering and the FLT chain

By §1, a Fermat solution forces an elliptic curve $E_{a,b,c}$ that is semistable with conductor $N_E = 2 \cdot \!\!\!\prod_{\ell \mid abc,\ \ell\text{ odd}}\!\!\! \ell$. By §2, $E_{a,b,c}$ is modular: its mod-$p$ Galois representation $\overline\rho_{E,p}$ comes from a weight-2 newform in $S_2(\Gamma_0(N_E))$.

Now Ribet's theorem strips the big primes out of the level:

Ribet's level-lowering theorem (1990). If $\overline\rho$ is an irreducible modular mod-$p$ Galois representation arising from a newform of level $Np$ (with $p \nmid N$), and $\overline\rho$ is unramified at every prime dividing the "extra" $p$, then $\overline\rho$ already arises from a newform of level $N$. For Frey's curve, the extra ramification at every odd prime $\ell \mid abc$ is carried entirely by a $p$-th power — it becomes invisible mod $p$, and Ribet lets us remove that $\ell$.

Iterating, Ribet lowers the level of $\overline\rho_{E_{a,b,c},p}$ all the way from $N_E$ down to $N = 2$. The representation must now come from a weight-2 newform in $S_2(\Gamma_0(2))$.

But the modular curve $X_0(2)$ has genus $0$, so $$\dim S_2(\Gamma_0(2)) \ =\ \mathrm{genus}\,X_0(2) \ =\ 0.$$ There is no weight-2 newform of level 2 for $\overline\rho$ to come from. The chain terminates in a vacancy, and the original assumption $a^p + b^p = c^p$ must have been false.

Fermat's Last Theorem. For every integer $n \ge 3$, the equation $x^n + y^n = z^n$ has no solutions in positive integers. (Reduction to prime exponents $p \ge 5$ is elementary; the cases $p=3,4$ are classical.)

Coda — beyond semistable, toward Langlands

Wiles' 1995 proof covered semistable elliptic curves (enough for FLT). In 2001 Breuil, Conrad, Diamond, and Taylor extended modularity to every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$, completing the Shimura–Taniyama conjecture. Since then the picture has generalized spectacularly:

  • Serre's conjecture (Khare–Wintenberger 2009): every odd, irreducible, 2-dimensional mod-$p$ Galois representation of $G_\mathbb{Q}$ is modular.
  • Potential modularity (Taylor and collaborators): many Galois representations become modular after restriction to a suitable totally real extension — enough for the Sato–Tate conjecture.
  • Modularity lifting: the Wiles–Taylor technology has matured into a general method for proving modularity of $n$-dimensional Galois representations, feeding the broader Langlands program.

Each of these is, at heart, a tiny echo of the same bet Wiles and Ribet won: that an arithmetic object's Galois representation is controlled by an automorphic form, and that that single identification is powerful enough to settle Diophantine questions that integer-by-integer chase cannot touch.

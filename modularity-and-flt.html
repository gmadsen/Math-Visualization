Modularity and Fermat's Last Theorem

Three ideas, one proof. A phantom solution $a^p + b^p = c^p$ conjures an elliptic curve so peculiar that modularity and level-lowering cannot both accommodate it. The contradiction that closes the tile is Wiles' theorem; the lever that flips the key is Ribet's.  Story ã

Prerequisites: elliptic curves, modular forms, Galois representations, $L$-functions.

1. The Frey curve

Suppose, for contradiction, that there are pairwise coprime nonzero integers $a, b, c$ and a prime $p \ge 5$ with $$a^p + b^p = c^p.$$ Gerhard Frey's 1985 insight was that this hypothetical triple gives rise to a very concrete elliptic curve: $$E_{a,b,c}: \ y^2 \ =\ x\,(x - a^p)\,(x + b^p).$$ If $a^p + b^p = c^p$, then the three roots $0,\, a^p,\, -b^p$ of the right-hand side are three integers that sum to $0$ ã a hidden piece of additive structure baked into a multiplicative equation.

The curve's discriminant is the square of the product of root differences (times a standard factor). Here the differences are $a^p,\, b^p,\, a^p + b^p = c^p$, giving $$\Delta(E_{a,b,c}) \ =\ 16\,(a^p \, b^p \, c^p)^2 \ =\ 16\,(abc)^{2p}.$$ Passing to a minimal Weierstrass model absorbs a factor of $2^8$, leaving the cleaner formula $$\Delta \ =\ \frac{(abc)^{2p}}{2^{8}}.$$

Frey's key observation. At every odd prime $\ell \mid abc$, the discriminant has multiplicity $2p$ ã a perfect square, in fact a perfect $2p$-th power. Tate's algorithm then says $E$ has multiplicative reduction at $\ell$: the reduction mod $\ell$ is a nodal cubic, not a cusp. So $E$ is semistable away from $2$, and its conductor $N_E$ is squarefree outside $2$.

Semistability is a very strong regularity condition on an elliptic curve. It means the $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\overline\rho_{E,p}: G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is unramified at every odd $\ell \nmid N_E$ and tamely ramified with a very simple shape at the bad primes. This tame ramification is the hook that Ribet's theorem in ôÏ3 will exploit.

There is a second piece of structure: the three 2-torsion points $(0,0),\ (a^p, 0),\ (-b^p, 0)$ are all rational, so $E[2](\mathbb{Q}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2)^2$. The mod-2 Galois representation is already trivial, and the mod-$p$ representation $\overline\rho_{E,p}$ is the interesting one.

The discriminant $\Delta = (abc)^{2p}/2^{8}$ stores the arithmetic of the hypothetical triple. Each odd prime $\ell \mid abc$ appears in $\Delta$ with exponent $2p$: a perfect square (even, so multiplicative reduction) and a perfect $p$-th power (hence invisible to $\overline\rho_{E,p}$ away from its $\ell$-adic inertia). These two facts together are what makes the Frey curve a fixed point of Ribet's knife: each odd bad prime is both present in the conductor and removable in the level. 2. The modularity theorem

The punchline of the second half of the 20th century in arithmetic:

Modularity theorem (Wiles + TaylorãWiles 1995, semistable; BreuilãConradãDiamondãTaylor 2001, general). Let $E/\mathbb{Q}$ be an elliptic curve of conductor $N_E$. Then there exists a weight-2 newform $f_E \in S_2(\Gamma_0(N_E))$ such that $$a_p(E) \ =\ a_p(f_E) \qquad \text{for every prime } p \nmid N_E,$$ where $a_p(E) = p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is the Frobenius trace and $a_p(f_E)$ is the $p$-th Fourier coefficient of $f_E$.

The correspondence is extraordinary: on the left, an algebraic object (points on a cubic mod $p$); on the right, a transcendental one (a holomorphic function on the upper half-plane). Modularity says the two bookkeepers agree ã and more than agree, they are two descriptions of a single Galois representation $$\rho_{E,\ell}: G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$$ with the same characteristic polynomial of Frobenius on both sides.

The simplest nontrivial example is the curve $E: y^2 + y = x^3 - x^2$ of conductor $N_E = 11$ (Cremona label 11a1). Its newform is the eta quotient $$f_E(\tau) \ =\ \eta(\tau)^2\,\eta(11\tau)^2 \ =\ q\prod_{n\ge 1}(1-q^n)^2(1-q^{11n})^2,$$ whose $q$-expansion we can match term-by-term with Frobenius traces computed by counting points on $E \bmod p$.

3. Ribet's level lowering and the FLT chain

By ôÏ1, a Fermat solution forces an elliptic curve $E_{a,b,c}$ that is semistable with conductor $N_E = 2 \cdot \!\!\!\prod_{\ell \mid abc,\ \ell\text{ odd}}\!\!\! \ell$. By ôÏ2, $E_{a,b,c}$ is modular: its mod-$p$ Galois representation $\overline\rho_{E,p}$ comes from a weight-2 newform in $S_2(\Gamma_0(N_E))$.

Now Ribet's theorem strips the big primes out of the level:

Ribet's level-lowering theorem (1990). If $\overline\rho$ is an irreducible modular mod-$p$ Galois representation arising from a newform of level $Np$ (with $p \nmid N$), and $\overline\rho$ is unramified at every prime dividing the "extra" $p$, then $\overline\rho$ already arises from a newform of level $N$. For Frey's curve, the extra ramification at every odd prime $\ell \mid abc$ is carried entirely by a $p$-th power ã it becomes invisible mod $p$, and Ribet lets us remove that $\ell$.

Iterating, Ribet lowers the level of $\overline\rho_{E_{a,b,c},p}$ all the way from $N_E$ down to $N = 2$. The representation must now come from a weight-2 newform in $S_2(\Gamma_0(2))$.

But the modular curve $X_0(2)$ has genus $0$, so $$\dim S_2(\Gamma_0(2)) \ =\ \mathrm{genus}\,X_0(2) \ =\ 0.$$ There is no weight-2 newform of level 2 for $\overline\rho$ to come from. The chain terminates in a vacancy, and the original assumption $a^p + b^p = c^p$ must have been false.

Fermat's Last Theorem. For every integer $n \ge 3$, the equation $x^n + y^n = z^n$ has no solutions in positive integers. (Reduction to prime exponents $p \ge 5$ is elementary; the cases $p=3,4$ are classical.)

Coda ã beyond semistable, toward Langlands

Wiles' 1995 proof covered semistable elliptic curves (enough for FLT). In 2001 Breuil, Conrad, Diamond, and Taylor extended modularity to every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$, completing the ShimuraãTaniyama conjecture. Since then the picture has generalized spectacularly:

  • Serre's conjecture (KhareãWintenberger 2009): every odd, irreducible, 2-dimensional mod-$p$ Galois representation of $G_\mathbb{Q}$ is modular.
  • Potential modularity (Taylor and collaborators): many Galois representations become modular after restriction to a suitable totally real extension ã enough for the SatoãTate conjecture.
  • Modularity lifting: the WilesãTaylor technology has matured into a general method for proving modularity of $n$-dimensional Galois representations, feeding the broader Langlands program.

Each of these is, at heart, a tiny echo of the same bet Wiles and Ribet won: that an arithmetic object's Galois representation is controlled by an automorphic form, and that that single identification is powerful enough to settle Diophantine questions that integer-by-integer chase cannot touch.

4. Deformation rings

The engine that drives modularity lifting is Mazur's theory of deformations of Galois representations. Fix a residual representation $$\overline\rho : G_\mathbb{Q} \longrightarrow \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$$ (thought of as the mod-$p$ reduction of some unknown $p$-adic lift). A deformation of $\overline\rho$ to a complete Noetherian local $\mathbb{Z}_p$-algebra $(A, \mathfrak{m}_A)$ with residue field $\mathbb{F}_p$ is a continuous lift $$\rho : G_\mathbb{Q} \longrightarrow \mathrm{GL}_2(A), \qquad \rho \bmod \mathfrak{m}_A \ =\ \overline\rho,$$ taken up to conjugation by $\ker(\mathrm{GL}_2(A) \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p))$.

Mazur (1989) proved that ã assuming $\overline\rho$ is absolutely irreducible ã this functor $A \mapsto \{\text{deformations of }\overline\rho\text{ to }A\}$ is representable. There is a universal complete Noetherian local ring $R^{\mathrm{univ}}$ and a universal deformation $$\rho^{\mathrm{univ}} : G_\mathbb{Q} \longrightarrow \mathrm{GL}_2(R^{\mathrm{univ}})$$ from which every other deformation arises by unique base change along a ring map $R^{\mathrm{univ}} \to A$. The ring $R^{\mathrm{univ}}$ is the formal moduli space of $p$-adic lifts of $\overline\rho$.

Cutting down by ramification data. In practice one fixes a finite set of primes $\Sigma$ (containing $p$ and the ramification primes of $\overline\rho$) and imposes local conditions at each $\ell \in \Sigma$: unramified outside $\Sigma$, ordinary at $p$, Steinberg at a Steinberg prime, etc. These cut out a quotient $R_\Sigma$ of $R^{\mathrm{univ}}$ ã still a complete Noetherian local ring, but now with tangent space computed by a Selmer group $$\dim_{\mathbb{F}_p} \mathfrak{m}_{R_\Sigma}/(\mathfrak{m}_{R_\Sigma}^2, p) \ =\ \dim_{\mathbb{F}_p} H^1_\Sigma(G_\mathbb{Q}, \mathrm{Ad}\,\overline\rho).$$ Selmer groups are the bookkeeping device: they count the infinitesimal directions $\overline\rho$ can deform subject to the chosen local conditions.

Why is $R_\Sigma$ the right object? Because modularity is, at its core, the assertion that this formal moduli space of Galois lifts is not bigger than the space of lifts coming from modular forms. The modular side of the equation is a Hecke algebra ã and ôÏ5 forces the two rings to coincide.

5. The R = T theorem

Fix the same $\Sigma$ as in ôÏ4, and consider the Hecke algebra $$\mathbb{T}_\Sigma \ \subseteq\ \mathrm{End}_{\mathbb{Z}_p}\!\big( S_2(\Gamma_0(N_\Sigma); \mathbb{Z}_p)_\mathfrak{m} \big)$$ generated by the operators $T_\ell$ for $\ell \notin \Sigma$ acting on the localization at a maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$ corresponding to $\overline\rho$. Each system of Hecke eigenvalues in $\mathbb{T}_\Sigma$ gives, via EichlerãShimura and Deligne, a Galois lift of $\overline\rho$ ã so modular lifts of $\overline\rho$ are parametrized by $\mathbb{T}_\Sigma$.

The universal property of $R_\Sigma$ then produces a canonical surjection of complete Noetherian local rings $$\varphi : R_\Sigma \ \twoheadrightarrow\ \mathbb{T}_\Sigma.$$ Surjectivity is easy (the modular Galois representations visibly land in $R_\Sigma$). The miracle is:

Modularity lifting (Wiles 1995, TaylorãWiles 1995). Under mild hypotheses on $\overline\rho$ (irreducible, modular, odd), the map $\varphi: R_\Sigma \twoheadrightarrow \mathbb{T}_\Sigma$ is an isomorphism. Equivalently, every deformation of $\overline\rho$ with ramification data $\Sigma$ is modular.

This is the celebrated "$R = T$" theorem. It is proved by the TaylorãWiles patching argument: one chooses an infinite system of auxiliary sets of "TaylorãWiles primes" $Q_n$ where, at each level, the augmented rings $R_{\Sigma \cup Q_n}$ and $\mathbb{T}_{\Sigma \cup Q_n}$ acquire enough room to be isomorphic to a power series ring over $\mathbb{Z}_p$ modulo a regular sequence of the same length. Passing to a compatible limit collapses the auxiliary primes and forces $R_\Sigma \cong \mathbb{T}_\Sigma$.

R = T closes the circle. The Frey curve's mod-$p$ representation $\overline\rho_{E,p}$ lands inside some $\mathbb{T}_\Sigma$ because $E$ is modular (ôÏ2); $R = T$ says all its $p$-adic deformations ã the whole formal family around $\overline\rho_{E,p}$ ã must also be modular; and Ribet's level lowering (ôÏ3) then forces that modular source down to a space known to be empty. The three ideas interlock, and Fermat's 350-year equation has nowhere left to hide.

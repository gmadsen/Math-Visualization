Categories, functors, and the Yoneda lemma

A disciplined way to say "arrows between things behave like the things themselves." From objects and morphisms to the universal property of the pullback and the adjunction that constructs free groups.

1. What is a category?

A category $\mathcal{C}$ consists of:

  • a class of objects $\operatorname{ob}\mathcal{C}$;
  • for every ordered pair $(A,B)$, a set $\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B)$ of morphisms (or arrows) $f\colon A\to B$;
  • a composition law $\operatorname{Hom}(B,C)\times\operatorname{Hom}(A,B)\to\operatorname{Hom}(A,C)$, $(g,f)\mapsto g\circ f$, that is associative;
  • for each $A$, an identity $\mathrm{id}_A\in\operatorname{Hom}(A,A)$ satisfying $f\circ\mathrm{id}_A=f$ and $\mathrm{id}_B\circ f=f$.

That is all. The data is so sparse that almost anything is a category, and the discipline is to see which statements about mathematical objects are really statements about the arrows between them.

More surprising are the cases where a category is a single structured object:

A poset is a category. Given a partially ordered set $(P,\le)$, take $\operatorname{ob}=P$ and $\operatorname{Hom}(x,y)$ is a singleton if $x\le y$, empty otherwise. Composition is forced by transitivity; identities by reflexivity.
A group is a one-object category. Let $G$ be a group. Define $\mathcal{B}G$ with one object $*$ and $\operatorname{Hom}(*,*)=G$, composition = group multiplication. Associativity and identities come for free. A functor $\mathcal{B}G\to\mathsf{Set}$ is a $G$-set; a functor $\mathcal{B}G\to\mathsf{Vect}_k$ is a representation.
A monoid is a one-object category (same construction, drop "inverses"). A groupoid is a category in which every morphism is invertible.

An isomorphism is a morphism $f\colon A\to B$ with a two-sided inverse. In $\mathsf{Set}$: bijections. In $\mathsf{Top}$: homeomorphisms. In $\mathsf{Grp}$: group isomorphisms. The point is that "isomorphism" is a notion purely of the category — you never unpack it into elements.

Opposite category. $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ has the same objects as $\mathcal{C}$ but $\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}}(A,B)=\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,A)$; composition is reversed. Every definition about $\mathcal{C}$ has a dual got by flipping all arrows.

2. Functors

A functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ is a homomorphism of categories: it sends objects to objects, morphisms to morphisms, preserving sources, targets, identities, and composition:

$F(g\circ f)=F(g)\circ F(f),\qquad F(\mathrm{id}_A)=\mathrm{id}_{F(A)}.$

This is the covariant case. A contravariant functor $\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ (equivalently a covariant functor $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathcal{D}$) reverses direction: $F(g\circ f)=F(f)\circ F(g)$.

Classic functors

  • Forgetful: $U\colon\mathsf{Grp}\to\mathsf{Set}$ sends a group to its underlying set. It drops structure; it is faithful (injective on $\operatorname{Hom}$s) but not full.
  • Free: $F\colon\mathsf{Set}\to\mathsf{Grp}$ sends a set $X$ to the free group on $X$. It is left-adjoint to $U$ (§6).
  • Fundamental group: $\pi_1\colon\mathsf{Top}_*\to\mathsf{Grp}$ is a functor from pointed topological spaces to groups. Continuous maps of pointed spaces induce group homomorphisms; composition is preserved. Functoriality is the entire point — it is what lets you prove $S^1$ is not contractible.
  • Hom-functors: for fixed $A\in\mathcal{C}$, $\operatorname{Hom}(A,-)\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathsf{Set}$ is covariant; $\operatorname{Hom}(-,A)\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathsf{Set}$ is contravariant. The most important functors in the subject.
  • Abelianization: $G\mapsto G/[G,G]$ is a functor $\mathsf{Grp}\to\mathsf{Ab}$, left-adjoint to the inclusion $\mathsf{Ab}\hookrightarrow\mathsf{Grp}$.
  • $\operatorname{Spec}$: a contravariant functor $\mathsf{CRing}\to\mathsf{Sch}$. You know this one.

A functor from the walking arrow category $\mathbf{2}=(\bullet\to\bullet)$ to $\mathcal{D}$ is exactly a choice of morphism in $\mathcal{D}$: two objects $F(0), F(1)$ and an arrow $F(0\to 1)$. The widget below builds one into $\mathsf{Set}$.

The point of the walking arrow is that a functor out of any "shape" category is just a diagram of that shape. Functors out of $\mathbf{2}$ are arrows; functors out of $\mathbf{3}=(\bullet\to\bullet\to\bullet)$ are composable pairs; functors out of the span $(\bullet\leftarrow\bullet\to\bullet)$ are the data defining a pullback (§5).

3. Natural transformations

Functors $F,G\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ are the objects of a category; morphisms between them are natural transformations $\eta\colon F\Rightarrow G$. The data: for each $A\in\mathcal{C}$ a morphism $\eta_A\colon F(A)\to G(A)$ in $\mathcal{D}$ (the component at $A$). The constraint: for each $f\colon A\to B$ in $\mathcal{C}$, the square

$\eta_B\circ F(f) \;=\; G(f)\circ \eta_A$

commutes. Naturality says the components "vary with the category $\mathcal{C}$ in a way that is built out of the arrows of $\mathcal{C}$ alone" — no arbitrary choices per object.

The canonical example: $V\to V^{**}$

For each $k$-vector space $V$ there is a map $\mathrm{ev}_V\colon V\to V^{**}$, $v\mapsto (\varphi\mapsto\varphi(v))$. It is an isomorphism on finite-dimensional $V$. Crucially, for every linear $f\colon V\to W$ the square

$\mathrm{ev}_W \circ f = f^{**} \circ \mathrm{ev}_V$

commutes. So $\mathrm{ev}$ is a natural transformation $\mathrm{id}_{\mathsf{Vect}_k}\Rightarrow (-)^{**}$. By contrast, there is no natural isomorphism $\mathrm{id}\Rightarrow(-)^{*}$: any isomorphism $V\cong V^*$ requires choosing a basis, and the choice can't be made naturally (in the precise sense above).

Natural transformations organize functors into a category: $[\mathcal{C},\mathcal{D}]$ or $\mathsf{Fun}(\mathcal{C},\mathcal{D})$. This is where the Yoneda magic happens.

4. The Yoneda lemma

Fix a locally small category $\mathcal{C}$. For each $X\in\mathcal{C}$ we have the contravariant representable functor

$h_X = \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,X)\;\colon\;\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathsf{Set}.$

On objects: $h_X(C)=\operatorname{Hom}(C,X)$. On a morphism $g\colon C'\to C$: $h_X(g)=g^*\colon \operatorname{Hom}(C,X)\to\operatorname{Hom}(C',X)$, $\varphi\mapsto\varphi\circ g$.

Yoneda lemma. For any contravariant $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathsf{Set}$ and any $X\in\mathcal{C}$, $$\operatorname{Nat}(h_X, F) \;\;\xrightarrow{\;\;\cong\;\;}\;\; F(X),\qquad \eta\longmapsto \eta_X(\mathrm{id}_X).$$ The inverse sends $x\in F(X)$ to the natural transformation whose $C$-component sends $\varphi\colon C\to X$ to $F(\varphi)(x)$. The bijection is natural in both $X$ and $F$.

Three immediate consequences:

  1. Yoneda embedding. $h_{(-)}\colon\mathcal{C}\hookrightarrow\mathsf{Fun}(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathsf{Set})$ is fully faithful. Taking $F=h_Y$ in the lemma gives $\operatorname{Nat}(h_X,h_Y)\cong\operatorname{Hom}(X,Y)$.
  2. Identifying objects. $X\cong Y$ in $\mathcal{C}$ iff $h_X\cong h_Y$ as functors. An object is determined up to iso by its functor of points.
  3. Representability = universal property. A functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathsf{Set}$ is representable if $F\cong h_X$ for some $X$; this $X$ is unique. Most universal constructions (products, limits, free objects) are "find an object representing this functor."

Consequence 2 is how algebraic geometers think about schemes: a scheme $X$ is determined by its functor of points $R\mapsto\operatorname{Hom}(\operatorname{Spec}R, X)$ on commutative rings. The moduli interpretation of a scheme is nothing but its $h_X$.

Why the map $\eta\mapsto\eta_X(\mathrm{id}_X)$ is a bijection

Given $\eta\colon h_X\Rightarrow F$, and any $\varphi\colon C\to X$, naturality against $\varphi\colon C\to X$ in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ gives $\eta_C(\varphi)=\eta_C(\mathrm{id}_X\circ\varphi)=F(\varphi)(\eta_X(\mathrm{id}_X))$. So $\eta$ is determined by the single element $\eta_X(\mathrm{id}_X)\in F(X)$. Conversely any $x\in F(X)$ defines components by this formula; functoriality of $F$ checks naturality.

5. Limits and colimits

A diagram in $\mathcal{C}$ of shape $\mathcal{J}$ is a functor $D\colon\mathcal{J}\to\mathcal{C}$. A cone on $D$ with apex $L$ is a collection of morphisms $\pi_j\colon L\to D(j)$ commuting with every $D(\alpha)$, $\alpha\in\operatorname{Mor}\mathcal{J}$. The limit $\varprojlim D$ is a cone through which every other cone factors uniquely — i.e. $L$ represents the functor $A\mapsto\{\text{cones on }D\text{ with apex }A\}$.

In $\mathsf{Set}$ the pullback is completely concrete:

$X\times_Z Y \;=\; \{(x,y)\in X\times Y \;:\; f(x)=g(y)\}.$

This is the single most important diagram to internalize. In $\mathsf{Sch}$ the same universal property gives the fiber product $X\times_Z Y$ — the scheme whose $R$-points are pairs of $R$-points of $X$ and $Y$ that agree after the map to $Z$. Everything from base change to fibers of morphisms to intersections of subvarieties is a pullback.

6. Adjoint functors

Two functors $F\colon\mathcal{C}\leftrightarrows\mathcal{D}:G$ are adjoint ($F$ is left-adjoint to $G$, written $F\dashv G$) when there is an isomorphism, natural in $A\in\mathcal{C}$ and $B\in\mathcal{D}$,

$\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(A),B) \;\xrightarrow{\;\cong\;}\; \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,G(B)).$

An equivalent packaging: a unit $\eta\colon \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{C}}\Rightarrow G\circ F$ and a counit $\varepsilon\colon F\circ G\Rightarrow\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{D}}$ satisfying the triangle identities $(\varepsilon F)\circ(F\eta)=\mathrm{id}_F$ and $(G\varepsilon)\circ(\eta G)=\mathrm{id}_G$.

Slogan. $F\dashv G$ means $F$ is the "freest way" to build a $\mathcal{D}$-object out of a $\mathcal{C}$-object; equivalently $G$ is forgetful; equivalently $A\to G(B)$ is the same data as $F(A)\to B$.

Free groups, concretely

Given a set $X=\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}$, the free group $F(X)$ consists of all reduced words in the symbols $x_1,\dots,x_n,x_1^{-1},\dots,x_n^{-1}$ — formal products with no adjacent cancellation $x_i x_i^{-1}$ or $x_i^{-1}x_i$ — with concatenation as multiplication. The unit $\eta_X\colon X\to U(F(X))$ is the obvious inclusion $x_i\mapsto x_i$.

The adjunction $\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathsf{Grp}}(F(X),G)\cong\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathsf{Set}}(X,U(G))$ says: a group map out of $F(X)$ is determined by a choice of image for each generator, and any such choice extends uniquely. The extension $\tilde f\colon F(X)\to G$ of $f\colon X\to U(G)$ sends a word $w=x_{i_1}^{\epsilon_1}\cdots x_{i_k}^{\epsilon_k}$ to $f(x_{i_1})^{\epsilon_1}\cdots f(x_{i_k})^{\epsilon_k}$.

Why adjoints are everywhere. Any "structure-adding" construction worth its salt (freely generate, localize, sheafify, complete, Stone–Čech compactify, tensor, abelianize, $\ldots$) is a left adjoint to a forgetful functor. Left adjoints preserve colimits; right adjoints preserve limits. That single fact replaces a thousand ad-hoc verifications.

Universal properties = representability

Combining §4 and §6: an adjunction $F\dashv G$ is the same data as a natural iso $\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(-), -)\cong\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, G(-))$, which by Yoneda is equivalent to specifying, for each $A$, the object $F(A)$ representing the functor $B\mapsto\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,G(B))$. So:

left adjoint = initial object of a comma category = representation of a Hom functor.

This is the machine behind "the free $X$ on a $Y$ is whatever represents $\operatorname{Hom}(Y, U(-))$." Any universal property you've seen — direct sum, completion, sheafification, localization of rings, fundamental group — is an instance.

Categories, functors, and natural transformations were invented by Eilenberg and Mac Lane in 1945 — originally as the language in which to state the naturality of the double-dual, and to separate honest "canonical" isomorphisms from artifacts of basis choice. The Yoneda lemma was first stated in a paper by Saunders Mac Lane attributing it to a private communication from Nobuo Yoneda; Yoneda himself never wrote it down. It took another fifteen years for it to be recognized as the beating heart of the subject.

8. Universal properties and representability

A recurring miracle of category theory is that almost every construction worth naming — products, coproducts, kernels, free groups, tensor products, localizations, completions, Stone–Čech compactifications — can be characterized by the same four-word formula: "initial (or terminal) object in an appropriate comma category." That characterization is called a universal property, and Yoneda turns it into a computational tool.

Fix a functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathsf{Set}$. We say $F$ is representable if there exist $X\in\mathcal{C}$ and a natural iso $F\cong h_X=\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-,X)$. By Yoneda, the pair $(X,\,\text{iso})$ is unique up to unique isomorphism whenever it exists. So to say "$X$ satisfies the universal property defined by $F$" means exactly $F\cong h_X$, and the "uniqueness up to unique iso" everyone quotes is just Yoneda.

Universal elements. A representation $F\cong h_X$ corresponds, by Yoneda, to a single element $u\in F(X)$ — the universal element — such that every $y\in F(C)$ is of the form $F(\varphi)(u)$ for a unique $\varphi\colon C\to X$. For the product $A\times B$, $u$ is the pair of projections $(\pi_A,\pi_B)\in\operatorname{Hom}(A\times B,A)\times\operatorname{Hom}(A\times B,B)$, and "every pair factors uniquely through $(\pi_A,\pi_B)$" is the usual universal property of the product.

Three concrete instances to pin down the pattern. The free group $F(S)$ on a set $S$ represents $\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathsf{Grp}}(-,\,-)\circ(\text{underlying set})$ restricted along $S$: precisely, $\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathsf{Set}}(S, U(G))\cong\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathsf{Grp}}(F(S), G)$, which is the $F\dashv U$ adjunction of §6. The tensor product $M\otimes_R N$ represents the functor $P\mapsto\operatorname{Bilin}_R(M\times N, P)$. The sheafification $a(\mathcal{F})$ of a presheaf $\mathcal{F}$ represents $\mathcal{G}\mapsto\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathsf{PSh}}(\mathcal{F},\iota\mathcal{G})$ on sheaves $\mathcal{G}$. In each case, the construction's defining property is "we represent this Hom-set-valued functor."

9. Monoidal categories

A monoidal category $(\mathcal{C}, \otimes, I, \alpha, \lambda, \rho)$ has a bifunctor $\otimes\colon\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{C}$, a unit object $I\in\mathcal{C}$, and natural isomorphisms $$\alpha_{A,B,C}\colon (A\otimes B)\otimes C \xrightarrow{\sim} A\otimes(B\otimes C),\qquad \lambda_A\colon I\otimes A\xrightarrow{\sim} A,\qquad \rho_A\colon A\otimes I\xrightarrow{\sim} A$$ satisfying two coherence diagrams — Mac Lane's pentagon for $\alpha$ and the triangle relating $\alpha$ to $\lambda,\rho$. The pentagon says the two ways of re-associating $((A\otimes B)\otimes C)\otimes D$ to $A\otimes(B\otimes(C\otimes D))$ agree, and Mac Lane's coherence theorem promotes this to: every diagram built from $\alpha,\lambda,\rho$ and identities commutes.

You have met dozens of examples: $(\mathsf{Set},\times,\{\ast\})$; $(\mathsf{Vect}_k,\otimes_k, k)$; $(R\mathsf{-Mod},\otimes_R, R)$; $(\mathsf{Top},\times,\ast)$; $(\mathsf{Ch}(R), \otimes, R[0])$; the endomorphism category $(\operatorname{End}(\mathcal{C}),\circ,\mathrm{id})$ of any category. A monoidal category is symmetric if it additionally carries a natural iso $\sigma_{A,B}\colon A\otimes B\xrightarrow{\sim} B\otimes A$ with $\sigma_{B,A}\circ\sigma_{A,B}=\mathrm{id}$ (and a hexagon coherence). It is braided if $\sigma$ satisfies only the hexagons — $\sigma^2\ne\mathrm{id}$ in general, which is how the category of representations of a quantum group gets its knot invariants.

Why monoidal matters. A monoidal category is the ambient setting for "algebraic structures internal to a category." A monoid in $(\mathcal{C},\otimes,I)$ is an object $M$ with $\mu\colon M\otimes M\to M$ and $\eta\colon I\to M$ satisfying associativity and unit axioms stated as commuting diagrams. A monoid in $(\mathsf{Set},\times)$ is an ordinary monoid; in $(\mathsf{Ab},\otimes_{\mathbb Z})$ it is a ring; in $(\mathsf{Vect}_k,\otimes_k)$ it is a $k$-algebra; in $(\operatorname{End}(\mathcal{C}),\circ)$ it is a monad. Monoidal structure is the syntax, and "monoid object" is the semantics.

Mac Lane's coherence theorem often licenses mathematicians to pretend $\otimes$ is strictly associative. That is legitimate — one can always replace a monoidal category by a strict monoidal one that is monoidally equivalent — but the non-trivial coherence data becomes essential in the braided and higher-categorical settings, where an elided $\alpha$ quietly encodes a piece of the braid group. The categorified version of monoidal categories — tricategories, $(\infty,1)$-categories with tensor product — are the setting for topological quantum field theory, Lurie's formalization of the cobordism hypothesis, and modern derived algebraic geometry.

Harmonic functions

Solutions of $\Delta u = 0$ — averaging, rigidity, and the Dirichlet problem. The bridge between complex analysis, electrostatics, and elliptic PDE.

1. Laplace's equation

A function $u\colon \Omega \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ of class $C^2$ is called harmonic when

$$\Delta u \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x_i^2} \;=\; 0.$$

This is the simplest elliptic PDE, and it shows up everywhere: the steady-state heat distribution on a body whose boundary temperature is fixed; the electrostatic potential outside the supports of charges; the gravitational potential outside a mass distribution; the velocity potential of an incompressible irrotational fluid.

In two dimensions there is a magic bridge to complex analysis: if $f = u + iv$ is holomorphic on an open $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}$, then differentiating the Cauchy–Riemann equations $u_x = v_y$, $u_y = -v_x$ once more gives $u_{xx} + u_{yy} = v_{yx} - v_{xy} = 0$. Both the real and imaginary part of any holomorphic function are harmonic. Conversely, on a simply connected $\Omega$, every harmonic $u$ has a harmonic conjugate $v$ — the unique-up-to-constant real function making $u + iv$ holomorphic.

Examples. Some staple harmonic functions on $\mathbb{R}^2$: linear $ax + by + c$; the real and imaginary parts of $z^n$ (so $\mathrm{Re}\,z^2 = x^2 - y^2$, $\mathrm{Im}\,z^2 = 2xy$, etc.); $\log|z|$ on $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}$; and $\arg z$ on any simply connected slit domain. Linear combinations are harmonic too. Polynomial harmonic functions of degree $\le n$ form a finite-dimensional space — the spherical harmonics in disguise.

2. The mean value property

Here is the single property that distils what "harmonic" really means. If $u$ is harmonic on $\Omega$ and the closed ball $\overline{B(x_0, r)} \subset \Omega$, then

$$u(x_0) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\partial B(x_0,r)|}\int_{\partial B(x_0,r)} u\,dS \;=\; \frac{1}{|B(x_0,r)|}\int_{B(x_0,r)} u\,dV.$$

The value at the center equals the spherical average — and equivalently the volume average over the ball. Harmonic = balanced.

The proof is one application of the divergence theorem: differentiate the average $\overline u(r) = \tfrac{1}{|\partial B(x_0,r)|}\int_{\partial B(x_0,r)} u\,dS$ with respect to $r$, get $\overline u'(r) = \tfrac{r}{n} \cdot \overline{\Delta u}(r) = 0$, so the average is independent of $r$. Sending $r \to 0$ recovers $u(x_0)$.

Converse — Koebe's theorem. The MVP characterises harmonicity. If $u$ is continuous on $\Omega$ and satisfies $u(x) = $ ball average for every admissible ball, then $u$ is harmonic — and in particular $C^\infty$. So a single integral identity, applied at every scale, forces all derivatives to exist and a PDE to hold. This is the cleanest example of elliptic regularity.

Two consequences fall out for free:

(i) Smoothing. Convolution with a radial bump, or even just averaging on small balls, takes a continuous harmonic function to itself — but for a generic $u$ it strictly improves regularity. Iterated averaging is therefore a route to constructing harmonic approximations.

(ii) Real analyticity. Going further: every harmonic function on a connected open set is real-analytic. Knowing $u$ on a tiny open ball pins it down everywhere, exactly as for holomorphic functions.

3. The maximum principle

From the mean value property the maximum principle drops out almost for free. If $u$ takes a value at $x_0$ which is its supremum on $\Omega$, then $u(x_0) = $ the average over any small ball around $x_0$, but the average can equal the maximum only if $u$ is constant on that ball. A connectedness argument propagates the constancy.

Weak maximum principle. Let $u$ be harmonic on a bounded $\Omega$ and continuous on $\overline\Omega$. Then

$$\max_{\overline\Omega} u \;=\; \max_{\partial\Omega} u, \qquad \min_{\overline\Omega} u \;=\; \min_{\partial\Omega} u.$$

Strong maximum principle. If $u$ attains its supremum at any interior point of a connected $\Omega$, then $u$ is constant on $\Omega$.

The strong form sharpens the weak: not only does the boundary realise the extremes, the interior can never do so unless $u$ is trivial.

Uniqueness for the Dirichlet problem. Suppose $u_1, u_2$ are both harmonic on $\Omega$ and continuous up to $\partial\Omega$, with $u_1 = u_2$ on $\partial\Omega$. The difference $w = u_1 - u_2$ is harmonic and zero on $\partial\Omega$. The weak maximum principle, applied to $\pm w$, forces $|w| \le 0$ on $\overline\Omega$. So $u_1 = u_2$. Boundary values determine harmonic functions.

4. The Poisson kernel and the disk Dirichlet problem

For the unit disk $\mathbb{D} = \{|z| < 1\}$ the Dirichlet problem has an explicit, beautiful solution. Given continuous boundary data $g\colon \partial \mathbb{D} \to \mathbb{R}$, the unique harmonic extension is

$$u(re^{i\theta}) \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} P_r(\theta - \varphi)\,g(\varphi)\,d\varphi, \qquad P_r(\theta) \;=\; \frac{1 - r^2}{1 - 2r\cos\theta + r^2}.$$

The kernel $P_r$ is the Poisson kernel. Three facts make it useful:

(i) Positivity. $1 - 2r\cos\theta + r^2 = |1 - re^{i\theta}|^2 > 0$, so $P_r > 0$.
(ii) Mass one. $\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} P_r(\theta)\,d\theta = 1$ for every $r \in [0,1)$.
(iii) Concentration. As $r \to 1^-$, $P_r$ concentrates a unit mass at $\theta = 0$ — it is an approximate identity.

So $u = P_r * g$ is convolution against an approximate identity: the values of $u$ deep in the disk are weighted averages of $g$ over the whole circle, while values close to the boundary look at nearby boundary points only.

Boundary recovery. Convergence to the boundary data has different flavours depending on how regular $g$ is:
• If $g$ is continuous, $u(re^{i\theta}) \to g(\theta)$ uniformly as $r \to 1^-$.
• If $g \in L^p$ for $1 \le p < \infty$, the radial limit equals $g$ almost everywhere — this is Fatou's theorem, the cleanest example of a measure-theoretic boundary value.
The MVP is the special case $r = 0$: $u(0) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int g$, the simplest boundary integral.

5. Harnack's inequality

The Poisson formula gives an uniform control on the oscillation of positive harmonic functions. Suppose $u \ge 0$ is harmonic on $B(0, R)$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Then for every $|x| = r < R$,

$$\frac{R - r}{R + r}\cdot\Big(\frac{R}{R+r}\Big)^{\!n-2} u(0) \;\le\; u(x) \;\le\; \frac{R + r}{R - r}\cdot\Big(\frac{R}{R-r}\Big)^{\!n-2} u(0).$$

In two dimensions this collapses to the textbook form

$$\frac{R - r}{R + r}\,u(0) \;\le\; u(x) \;\le\; \frac{R + r}{R - r}\,u(0).$$

The proof on the disk is one line from the Poisson kernel. For $|y| = R$ and $|x| = r$,

$$P_{r/R}(\theta) \cdot \frac{1}{R^{n-2}} \;\le\; \text{Poisson kernel}(x, y) \;\le\; \text{(upper)},$$

and the positivity of $u$ on the boundary lets us pull the bound through the integral. Without positivity the bound is meaningless: the Poisson kernel at one point can be huge, and a sign-changing $u$ can have $u(0) = 0$ while $u$ is large elsewhere.

Liouville for harmonic functions. If $u \ge 0$ is harmonic on all of $\mathbb{R}^n$, apply Harnack on $B(0, R)$ with $r$ fixed and let $R \to \infty$. The factor $\tfrac{R-r}{R+r}$ tends to $1$, and the upper bound's $\tfrac{R+r}{R-r}$ also tends to $1$. Both bounds pinch to $u(0)$, so $u(x) = u(0)$ for every $x$ — a positive harmonic function on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is constant. The complex-analysis Liouville (bounded entire ⇒ constant) is the analogous statement for holomorphic functions; both are theorems about rigidity from a one-sided bound.

6. Subharmonic functions and Perron's method

Replace $\Delta v = 0$ with the inequality $\Delta v \ge 0$ — equivalently, replace the MVP equation $v(x) = $ ball average by the inequality $v(x) \le $ ball average. The resulting class of upper semicontinuous functions is the class of subharmonic functions. (Sign convention is a coin-flip; we follow the standard "sub" = below convention, so $v$ is subharmonic when its values sit below the harmonic majorant.)

Examples:

• If $f$ is holomorphic, $\log|f|$ is subharmonic — the elementary proof is the Jensen formula.
• Any convex function of a harmonic function is subharmonic (Jensen's inequality at the level of averages).
• Maxima of finitely many subharmonic functions are subharmonic.

The maximum principle still holds for subharmonic functions: $\sup_{\overline\Omega} v = \sup_{\partial\Omega} v$. This is the property that makes them useful — they let us build harmonic functions from below.

Perron's method

Given continuous boundary data $g$ on $\partial \Omega$, define the Perron family

$$\mathcal{S}_g \;=\; \{v\colon\Omega\to\mathbb{R} \;:\; v \text{ subharmonic},\ \limsup_{y\to\zeta} v(y) \le g(\zeta)\ \forall\zeta\in\partial\Omega\}.$$

and set

$$u(x) \;=\; \sup\,\{v(x) \;:\; v \in \mathcal{S}_g\}.$$

Two near-miraculous facts:

(i) $u$ is harmonic on $\Omega$. The proof is "harmonic lifting": on any small ball $B \subset\Omega$, replace $v$ by its Poisson integral on $B$, get a larger element of $\mathcal{S}_g$, and conclude that $u$ equals the Poisson integral of itself on every small ball.
(ii) $u$ achieves $g$ on $\partial\Omega$ at every regular boundary point. A boundary point $\zeta$ is regular when there is a barrier — a subharmonic function near $\zeta$ that vanishes at $\zeta$ and is strictly negative everywhere else. For "nice" domains (e.g. bounded with $C^1$ boundary in $\mathbb{R}^n$, or simply connected planar domains with more than one boundary point) every boundary point is regular.

The classical bad point: Lebesgue spine. In $\mathbb{R}^3$ remove a thin spike pointing into a region. The tip of the spike is a boundary point that admits no barrier — Perron's $u$ exists, but it does not achieve the boundary value at the tip. Pathologies of this kind delineate the boundary regularity theory and motivate Wiener's criterion.

Mathematics and cryptography

A guided tour from (\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times through RSA, Diffie–Hellman, elliptic curves, and the post-quantum lattice frontier — with a coda on zero-knowledge proofs.

1. Modular arithmetic and Euler's theorem

Public-key cryptography lives inside the multiplicative group $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ — the units modulo $n$, i.e. integers coprime to $n$ under multiplication. Its order is Euler's totient $$\phi(n) = \#\{a : 1\le a\le n,\ \gcd(a,n)=1\}.$$ For a prime power, $\phi(p^k) = p^{k-1}(p-1)$, and $\phi$ is multiplicative on coprime arguments. The key identity, the Fermat–Euler theorem, falls out of Lagrange's theorem applied to the unit group:

$$\gcd(a,n)=1 \implies a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n.$$

When $n=p$ is prime, $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ is cyclic of order $p-1$, and a generator is called a primitive root. Powers of a primitive root enumerate every nonzero residue exactly once — this is the algebraic substrate every classical cryptosystem will rent.

2. RSA

Rivest–Shamir–Adleman (1977) is the original public-key cryptosystem. Key generation: pick two large primes $p, q$ and set $$n = pq, \qquad \phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1).$$ Choose a public exponent $e$ with $\gcd(e,\phi(n))=1$ (commonly $e=65537$), and compute the private exponent $$d \equiv e^{-1} \pmod{\phi(n)}.$$ Encryption raises a message $m$ to the public exponent, decryption raises ciphertext to the private:

$$c \equiv m^e \pmod n, \qquad m \equiv c^d \pmod n.$$

Why does this work? By construction $ed = 1 + k\phi(n)$ for some integer $k$, so for any $m$ coprime to $n$ Fermat–Euler gives

$$c^d = m^{ed} = m^{1+k\phi(n)} = m \cdot (m^{\phi(n)})^k \equiv m \cdot 1^k = m \pmod n.$$

(A short CRT argument extends correctness to all residues, even when $\gcd(m,n)>1$.)

Security rests on the conjectured hardness of factoring $n$. If you can factor, you recover $\phi(n)$ and hence $d$. The converse is also true: knowing $d$ lets you (probabilistically) factor $n$, so the private key and the factorization are essentially the same secret.

RSA assumption. Strictly, RSA security needs not just factoring-hardness but the RSA problem — given $(n,e,c)$, find $m$ with $m^e\equiv c$. No separation from factoring is known, but textbook RSA is broken in practice (deterministic, malleable, leaky), and real deployments use OAEP or PSS padding.

3. Discrete logarithm and Diffie–Hellman

Fix a prime $p$ and a primitive root $g$ of $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$. Every $h \in (\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ has a unique exponent $$x \in \{0,1,\dots,p-2\} \quad\text{with}\quad g^x \equiv h \pmod p.$$ The discrete logarithm $\log_g h$ is that exponent. Computing it is conjecturally hard; verifying $g^x=h$ is one fast exponentiation. This one-way function is the engine of Diffie–Hellman key exchange (1976):

  1. Public parameters: a prime $p$ and primitive root $g$.
  2. Alice picks secret $a$, sends $A = g^a \bmod p$.
  3. Bob picks secret $b$, sends $B = g^b \bmod p$.
  4. Both compute $K = g^{ab} \bmod p$: Alice as $B^a$, Bob as $A^b$.

The eavesdropper sees $g, p, g^a, g^b$ and must compute $g^{ab}$ — the computational Diffie–Hellman problem. The best known classical algorithm is index calculus, sub-exponential of complexity $L_p[1/3,c]$, which is why DH key sizes are pushed to $\ge 3072$ bits.

4. Elliptic-curve cryptography

Replace the multiplicative group $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ with the group of points $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ of an elliptic curve over a prime field. The chord-tangent construction equips $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ with an abelian group law, neutral element the point at infinity. Hasse's theorem bounds its order:

$$\#E(\mathbb{F}_p) = p + 1 - a_p, \qquad |a_p| \le 2\sqrt p.$$

The elliptic-curve discrete logarithm problem (ECDLP) asks, given $P, Q\in E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ with $Q = [k]P$, to recover $k$. On a generic curve no index-calculus attack is known — the only known classical attack is Pollard's $\rho$ in time $O(\sqrt{|E|})$. So 256-bit ECC delivers what 3072-bit RSA gives, and signed transactions on Bitcoin's secp256k1 stay short.

Pitfalls. Two attack families pull the ECDLP into easier groups:

  • MOV (Menezes–Okamoto–Vanstone). The Weil pairing embeds $E[\ell]\hookrightarrow \mathbb{F}_{p^k}^\times$ where $k$ is the embedding degree. Small $k$ ⇒ index calculus on $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}^\times$ wins.
  • Smart / anomalous curves. If $\#E(\mathbb{F}_p)=p$, the lift to $\mathbb{Z}_p$ linearises the DLP into addition.

Standard hardened curves (NIST P-256, Curve25519, secp256k1) avoid both. ECDH and ECDSA are the elliptic analogues of Diffie–Hellman and DSA — same protocol skeleton, different group.

5. Lattice-based and post-quantum cryptography

In 1994 Peter Shor exhibited polynomial-time quantum algorithms for integer factorization and discrete logarithms in any abelian group. A sufficiently large fault-tolerant quantum computer therefore breaks RSA, Diffie–Hellman, and ECC simultaneously. The hunt for replacement primitives — post-quantum cryptography — has settled, for now, on lattices.

A lattice $\Lambda \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ is the integer span of $n$ linearly independent vectors $\mathbf{b}_1,\dots,\mathbf{b}_n$. Two notorious problems:

  • SVP — find a shortest nonzero vector of $\Lambda$.
  • CVP — given a target $\mathbf{t}\in\mathbb{R}^n$, find the lattice point closest to it.

Both are NP-hard in worst case (CVP exactly; SVP modulo a small approximation factor under randomized reductions). Even approximating them is hard for sub-polynomial factors.

Regev's Learning With Errors (LWE) (2005) bridges worst-case lattice hardness to an average-case key exchange. Pick a secret $\mathbf{s}\in\mathbb{Z}_q^n$. Samples are pairs $$(\mathbf{a}_i,\ \langle \mathbf{a}_i, \mathbf{s}\rangle + e_i \bmod q),$$ with $\mathbf{a}_i$ uniform and $e_i$ small Gaussian noise. Without the noise, Gaussian elimination recovers $\mathbf{s}$ from $n$ samples; with noise, the problem is conjectured (and quantum-reduces from worst-case approximate-SVP / GapSVP) to be hard.

CRYSTALS-Kyber — a Module-LWE KEM — became NIST FIPS 203 in 2024, with the related Dilithium signature scheme as FIPS 204. The construction reads as: generate noisy linear samples, encode a session key in the high bits of a lattice point, recover via the secret short vector.

6. Zero-knowledge proofs

Goldwasser, Micali, and Rackoff (1985) introduced interactive proofs: a randomized protocol between a prover $P$ and a verifier $V$, with three properties for a language $L$:

  • Completeness. $x \in L$ ⇒ honest $P$ convinces $V$ with probability $\ge 2/3$.
  • Soundness. $x \notin L$ ⇒ no cheating prover $P^*$ convinces $V$ with probability $> 1/3$.
  • Zero-knowledge. The verifier's transcript can be simulated from $x$ alone — $V$ learns only the truth of the statement.

The canonical example is Schnorr's protocol (1989) for proving knowledge of a discrete log $x$ with $h = g^x$:

  1. Prover picks random $r$, sends commitment $t = g^r$.
  2. Verifier sends random challenge $c$.
  3. Prover sends response $s = r + cx$.
  4. Verifier accepts iff $g^s = t \cdot h^c$.

Soundness: a prover who can answer two distinct challenges $c \ne c'$ for the same $t$ leaks $x = (s - s') / (c - c')$. Zero-knowledge: a simulator chooses $s$ and $c$ first, then sets $t = g^s / h^c$ — the resulting transcript is indistinguishable from a real one.

Modern zk-SNARKs (Groth16 (2016), PLONK (2019), STARKs) compile any NP statement into a few-kilobyte non-interactive proof verifiable in milliseconds. They are the engine of Zcash's shielded transactions, Ethereum zk-rollups, and verifiable computation services. The Fiat–Shamir transform turns any honest-verifier interactive ZK protocol into a non-interactive one by replacing the verifier's random challenge with a hash of the transcript so far — a structural bridge from Schnorr to SNARK.

Connections

Cryptography is one of the few places where unproved hardness conjectures cash out as deployed engineering. The settled-looking landscape — RSA, ECC, lattices — sits on a small pile of conjectured separations: factoring is conjecturally not in $\mathsf{P}$, the discrete log is conjecturally hard on $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$, average-case LWE conjecturally reduces from worst-case lattice problems. Open frontiers: a fault-tolerant quantum computer large enough to run Shor end-to-end on $2048$-bit RSA, post-quantum signature standardisation beyond Dilithium, succinct proofs without trusted setup, and obfuscation built on plausible primitives.

  • Abstract algebra — groups, rings, fields — the unit group $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ and Lagrange's theorem are the foundation under Fermat–Euler.
  • Elliptic curves — the chord-tangent group law is the algebraic substrate of ECDH and ECDSA.
  • Complexity theory — P vs NP — every cryptographic hardness assumption is a refusal to believe a polynomial-time algorithm exists.
  • NP-completeness — lattice problems join the hardness web through Karp reductions; the Cook–Levin paradigm underwrites SNARK soundness via reductions to circuit-SAT.
  • p-adics — local-global — the Hasse principle organises the same "study a Diophantine problem prime-by-prime" mindset that index-calculus attacks exploit.

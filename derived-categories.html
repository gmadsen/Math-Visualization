Derived categories

Inverting quasi-isomorphisms: triangulated structure, exact triangles, and derived functors $RF, LF$.

1. The homotopy category $K(\mathcal{A})$

Fix an abelian category $\mathcal{A}$ — concretely, $\mathcal{A} = \mathrm{Mod}\,R$ for a ring $R$, or $\mathcal{A} = \mathrm{Sh}(X)$ for sheaves of abelian groups on a space. The category of chain complexes $\mathrm{Ch}(\mathcal{A})$ has, as objects, $\mathbb{Z}$-graded sequences $\dots \to X^{n-1} \xrightarrow{d} X^n \xrightarrow{d} X^{n+1} \to \dots$ with $d \circ d = 0$, and as morphisms chain maps $f^\bullet$ commuting with the differentials. The cohomology functor $H^n(X^\bullet) = \ker d^n / \mathrm{im}\,d^{n-1}$ takes a complex to a graded object of $\mathcal{A}$.

Two chain maps $f, g \colon X^\bullet \to Y^\bullet$ are chain homotopic, written $f \simeq g$, if there is a degree $-1$ map $s = (s^n \colon X^n \to Y^{n-1})$ with $f - g = ds + sd$. The geometric content: $s$ is a "chain-level nullhomotopy" of the difference $f - g$. Its key consequence is that homotopic maps induce the same map on cohomology — so cohomology is an invariant of the homotopy class.

The homotopy category $K(\mathcal{A})$ has the same objects as $\mathrm{Ch}(\mathcal{A})$ but morphisms $\mathrm{Hom}_K(X, Y) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{Ch}}(X, Y) / (\simeq)$. Composition is well-defined because chain homotopy is preserved by pre- and post-composition. Equivalently $K(\mathcal{A}) = \mathrm{Ch}(\mathcal{A})[\text{nullhomotopic maps}^{-1}]$ — we have inverted chain maps homotopic to zero, which is enough to make $H^n$ a representable functor on $K(\mathcal{A})$ but not yet enough to identify quasi-isomorphic complexes (that comes in §2).

Why $K$ before $D$? Quasi-isomorphisms in $\mathrm{Ch}(\mathcal{A})$ don't admit a calculus of fractions — but their image in $K(\mathcal{A})$ does, because identifying homotopic maps is exactly what's needed to make the localization manageable. $K(\mathcal{A})$ already carries the shift functor $X[1]^n = X^{n+1}$ (with $d_{X[1]} = -d_X$) and the mapping cone construction; the localization map $K(\mathcal{A}) \to D(\mathcal{A})$ will preserve both.

2. Quasi-isomorphisms and localization

A chain map $f \colon X^\bullet \to Y^\bullet$ is a quasi-isomorphism if the induced map $H^n(f) \colon H^n(X) \to H^n(Y)$ is an isomorphism for every $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. Quasi-isomorphisms generalize the relation between a complex and its cohomology: an injective resolution $M \hookrightarrow I^\bullet$ is the prototype example, with the inclusion $M[0] \to I^\bullet$ a quasi-iso even though $M$ and $I^\bullet$ are very different complexes.

Quasi-isomorphisms are not isomorphisms in $\mathrm{Ch}(\mathcal{A})$ or even in $K(\mathcal{A})$ in general. Standard counterexample in $\mathcal{A} = \mathrm{Ab}$: the chain map $\mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{2} \mathbb{Z}$ (in degrees $0, 1$) maps to $0 \to \mathbb{Z}/2$ (concentrated in degree $1$) by reduction mod $2$ — a quasi-iso, but no chain map back exists, so it has no inverse in $K(\mathcal{A})$.

The fix is localization: invert quasi-isos formally. The derived category $D(\mathcal{A}) := K(\mathcal{A})[\mathrm{qis}^{-1}]$ has the same objects, and morphisms $X \to Y$ are roofs $X \xleftarrow{s} Z \xrightarrow{f} Y$ with $s$ a quasi-iso, modulo a calculus-of-fractions equivalence. The quasi-iso class satisfies the Ore conditions in $K(\mathcal{A})$ (closed under composition, the "two-out-of-three" property, and a roof-completion property), so the localization is well-behaved and not merely abstract nonsense — every $D$-morphism has a roof representative.

The universal property: any functor $F\colon K(\mathcal{A}) \to \mathcal{D}$ sending quasi-isos to isomorphisms factors uniquely through $D(\mathcal{A})$. So $D(\mathcal{A})$ is what you build when you accept "having the same cohomology" as the working definition of "isomorphic complex."

3. The derived category $D(\mathcal{A})$

The derived category $D(\mathcal{A})$ is characterized by a universal property: it is a category equipped with a functor $Q\colon K(\mathcal{A}) \to D(\mathcal{A})$ that inverts every quasi-isomorphism, and is initial among such — any functor $F\colon K(\mathcal{A}) \to \mathcal{D}$ that sends quasi-isos to isomorphisms factors uniquely through $Q$. By the same trick used in commutative algebra, $D(\mathcal{A})$ exists: morphisms are computed via the Ore calculus on roofs, modulo a chain-homotopy-and-equivalence relation.

Concretely, a $D$-morphism $X \to Y$ is an equivalence class of roofs: $$X \xleftarrow{\;s\;} Z \xrightarrow{\;f\;} Y, \qquad s\text{ a quasi-iso},$$ with two roofs equivalent if they admit a common refinement. Composition of roofs $X \xleftarrow{s} Z \xrightarrow{f} Y$ and $Y \xleftarrow{t} W \xrightarrow{g} V$ produces a third roof via a chosen pullback-style completion: pick $Z'$ with $Z' \to Z$ a quasi-iso whose composition $Z' \to Z \to Y$ lifts to $W$. The Ore conditions guarantee such a $Z'$ exists.

Standard sub-categories cut $D(\mathcal{A})$ down by support:

  • $D^+(\mathcal{A})$: complexes bounded below — $X^n = 0$ for $n \ll 0$. The natural target of right derived functors.
  • $D^-(\mathcal{A})$: complexes bounded above — $X^n = 0$ for $n \gg 0$. The target of left derived functors.
  • $D^b(\mathcal{A}) = D^+ \cap D^-$: bounded complexes — the most-used variant in algebraic geometry, since $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,X)$ for a smooth projective $X$ is all you need.

Worked example: for $R$ a ring, $D(\mathrm{Mod}\,R)$ contains $\mathrm{Mod}\,R$ as a full subcategory via $M \mapsto M[0]$, and $\mathrm{Hom}_{D}(M[0], N[i]) \cong \mathrm{Ext}_R^i(M, N)$. The classical Ext groups are recovered as Hom sets in $D$ between shifted modules — derived functors become representable.

4. Triangulated structure and exact triangles

$D(\mathcal{A})$ is the standard example of a triangulated category. The data: a self-equivalence $[1]\colon D \to D$ (the shift, $X[1]^n = X^{n+1}$ with $d \mapsto -d$) and a class of distinguished triangles $$X \xrightarrow{u} Y \xrightarrow{v} Z \xrightarrow{w} X[1].$$ A triangle is distinguished iff it is isomorphic in $D$ to a triangle of the form $X \xrightarrow{f} Y \to \mathrm{Cone}(f) \to X[1]$, where the mapping cone $\mathrm{Cone}(f)$ has $\mathrm{Cone}(f)^n = X^{n+1} \oplus Y^n$ with differential $\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}-d_X & 0 \\ f & d_Y\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$.

Verdier's axioms (TR1–TR4) capture how triangles compose. The crucial one is TR3 (rotation): rotating a distinguished triangle gives another distinguished triangle, $Y \xrightarrow{v} Z \xrightarrow{w} X[1] \xrightarrow{-u[1]} Y[1]$, and dually you can rotate backwards. This makes "the third vertex of the triangle" a functor $X \to Y \mapsto Z = \mathrm{Cone}(u)$, replacing the cokernel of an abelian-category morphism. In an abelian category, a short exact sequence $0 \to A \to B \to C \to 0$ becomes a distinguished triangle $A \to B \to C \to A[1]$ via the connecting map.

The flagship payoff: the long exact cohomology sequence. Applying $H^0\colon D(\mathcal{A}) \to \mathcal{A}$ to a distinguished triangle yields $$\dots \to H^{-1}(Z) \to H^0(X) \to H^0(Y) \to H^0(Z) \to H^1(X) \to \dots$$ — this is exact, an axiom of any triangulated category equipped with a cohomological functor. So the "snake lemma / connecting map" machinery of homological algebra is the shadow of triangle rotation.

Beware: the cone is unique up to non-canonical isomorphism, so $D(\mathcal{A})$ is not an abelian category — there is no functorial cokernel. This is what we trade for the triangulated structure; it is the reason "the derived category" is genuinely different from "an abelian category of complexes."

5. Derived functors $RF, LF$

Let $F\colon \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}$ be a left-exact additive functor between abelian categories — $F = \mathrm{Hom}_R(M, -)$ or $F = \Gamma(X, -)$ on sheaves, say. The classical right derived functors $R^i F$ are computed by choosing an injective resolution $X \hookrightarrow I^\bullet$ and setting $R^i F(X) = H^i(F(I^\bullet))$. The construction depends on a choice but the answer doesn't.

The triangulated viewpoint upgrades this: define the total right derived functor $$RF\colon D^+(\mathcal{A}) \to D^+(\mathcal{B}), \qquad RF(X^\bullet) = F(I^\bullet),$$ where $X^\bullet \to I^\bullet$ is a quasi-iso to a complex of injectives. $RF$ is a triangulated functor — it commutes with shifts and sends distinguished triangles to distinguished triangles — and recovers the classical groups via $R^i F(X) = H^i(RF(X))$. Existence requires enough injectives in $\mathcal{A}$; left derived functors $LF$ are dual, requiring projective resolutions and bounded-above complexes.

Two payoffs:

  • Ext as Hom in the derived category. $\mathrm{Ext}^i_R(M, N) = R^i \mathrm{Hom}_R(M, -)(N) = \mathrm{Hom}_{D(\mathrm{Mod}\,R)}(M[0], N[i])$. The classical Yoneda Ext groups become genuine Hom sets between shifts.
  • Composition. If $F\colon \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}$ and $G\colon \mathcal{B} \to \mathcal{C}$ are left exact and $F$ sends injectives in $\mathcal{A}$ to $G$-acyclics in $\mathcal{B}$, then $R(G \circ F) \cong RG \circ RF$ as functors on $D^+$. This is the abstract source of the Grothendieck spectral sequence: $E_2^{p,q} = R^p G(R^q F(X)) \Rightarrow R^{p+q}(GF)(X)$.

Worked example: for $R$ a Noetherian ring, $L \otimes^L_R\colon D^-(\mathrm{Mod}\,R) \to D^-(\mathrm{Mod}\,R)$ is the left derived tensor, and $H^{-i}(M \otimes^L N) = \mathrm{Tor}_i^R(M, N)$. The classical Tor groups package into a single triangulated functor — the lone object $M \otimes^L N$ knows everything the family $\mathrm{Tor}_*^R(M,N)$ knows.

6. $t$-structures and hearts

A $t$-structure on a triangulated category $\mathcal{T}$ is a pair of full subcategories $\mathcal{T}^{\le 0}, \mathcal{T}^{\ge 0} \subset \mathcal{T}$, with $\mathcal{T}^{\le n} := \mathcal{T}^{\le 0}[-n]$ etc., satisfying:

  1. Inclusion: $\mathcal{T}^{\le -1} \subset \mathcal{T}^{\le 0}$ and $\mathcal{T}^{\ge 1} \subset \mathcal{T}^{\ge 0}$.
  2. Orthogonality: $\mathrm{Hom}(\mathcal{T}^{\le 0}, \mathcal{T}^{\ge 1}) = 0$.
  3. Truncation: every $X \in \mathcal{T}$ fits in a distinguished triangle $\tau_{\le 0} X \to X \to \tau_{\ge 1} X \to (\tau_{\le 0} X)[1]$ with $\tau_{\le 0} X \in \mathcal{T}^{\le 0}$ and $\tau_{\ge 1} X \in \mathcal{T}^{\ge 1}$.

The heart $\heartsuit := \mathcal{T}^{\le 0} \cap \mathcal{T}^{\ge 0}$ is automatically an abelian category, and $H^0\colon \mathcal{T} \to \heartsuit$ defined by $H^0(X) = \tau_{\le 0} \tau_{\ge 0} X$ is a cohomological functor sending distinguished triangles to long exact sequences. So $t$-structures equip a triangulated category with a "cohomology theory."

The standard $t$-structure on $D(\mathcal{A})$ has $D^{\le 0}$ = complexes with $H^i = 0$ for $i > 0$, and $D^{\ge 0}$ = complexes with $H^i = 0$ for $i < 0$. Truncation is the classical good truncation: $\tau_{\le 0}(X) = (\dots \to X^{-1} \to \ker d^0 \to 0)$, sending $X$ to its piece concentrated in non-positive degree. The heart $D^{\le 0} \cap D^{\ge 0}$ consists of complexes with cohomology only in degree $0$ — and the embedding $\mathcal{A} \hookrightarrow D(\mathcal{A})$, $M \mapsto M[0]$ is an equivalence onto this heart. So $D(\mathcal{A})$ remembers $\mathcal{A}$.

But $D(\mathcal{A})$ has many $t$-structures, not just the standard one. Tilting and perversity exploit this: the perverse $t$-structure on $D^b_c(X)$ (constructible sheaves) has heart the abelian category of perverse sheaves — totally different from the standard heart $\mathrm{Sh}_c(X)$, but living inside the same triangulated category. This is the engine of intersection cohomology and the Riemann–Hilbert correspondence.

7. $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,X)$ and Fourier–Mukai

The bounded derived category of coherent sheaves $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,X)$ on a smooth projective variety $X$ is the natural home for intersection theory, vector-bundle problems, and modern moduli. Its Hom sets package classical Ext groups: $\mathrm{Hom}_{D^b}(\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F}[i]) = \mathrm{Ext}^i(\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F})$. Serre duality reads $\mathrm{Hom}_{D^b}(\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F}) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{D^b}(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{E} \otimes \omega_X[\dim X])^\vee$ — the canonical bundle becomes a shift by $\dim X$ inside $D^b$.

A landmark result of Bondal–Orlov: if $X$ has ample (or anti-ample) canonical bundle, then $X \cong Y$ as varieties iff $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,X) \simeq D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,Y)$ as triangulated categories. So for varieties of general type, the derived category recovers the variety. But this fails dramatically for Calabi–Yau and abelian varieties, where genuinely distinct varieties can have equivalent derived categories — these "derived equivalences" are the central phenomenon.

The mechanism is the Fourier–Mukai transform. Given a kernel $\mathcal{P} \in D^b(\mathrm{Coh}(X \times Y))$, define $$\Phi_{\mathcal{P}}\colon D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,X) \to D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,Y), \qquad \Phi_{\mathcal{P}}(\mathcal{E}) = R\pi_{Y,*}\bigl(\pi_X^*\mathcal{E} \otimes^L \mathcal{P}\bigr),$$ where $\pi_X, \pi_Y$ are the projections. Mukai's original example: $X$ an abelian variety, $\hat X$ the dual, $\mathcal{P}$ the Poincaré bundle on $X \times \hat X$. Then $\Phi_{\mathcal{P}}\colon D^b(X) \xrightarrow{\sim} D^b(\hat X)$ is an equivalence with $\Phi_{\mathcal{P}}^{-1} = \Phi_{\mathcal{P}^\vee}[\dim X]$. This was the first example of a non-trivial derived equivalence between non-isomorphic varieties.

Orlov's representability theorem strengthens the picture: every fully faithful triangulated functor $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,X) \to D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,Y)$ between smooth projective varieties is of Fourier–Mukai type for a unique kernel $\mathcal{P}$. So the integral-kernel calculus is forced: any derived equivalence is geometric, given by sheaves on a product.

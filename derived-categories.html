Derived categories

Inverting quasi-isomorphisms: triangulated structure, exact triangles, and derived functors $RF, LF$.

1. The homotopy category $K(\mathcal{A})$

The homotopy category $K(\mathcal{A})$ has chain complexes for objects and homotopy classes of chain maps for morphisms. It identifies maps that differ by a chain homotopy and is the first step toward inverting quasi-isomorphisms.

2. Quasi-isomorphisms and localization

A quasi-isomorphism is a chain map inducing iso on cohomology. The derived category $D(\mathcal{A})$ is the localization of $K(\mathcal{A})$ at the class of quasi-isomorphisms — "objects up to cohomology agreement."

3. The derived category $D(\mathcal{A})$

The derived category $D(\mathcal{A})$ is the universal target of a functor out of $\mathcal{A}$ that inverts quasi-isomorphisms. Its bounded variants $D^+, D^-, D^b$ restrict to complexes that are zero in low / high / both extremes.

4. Triangulated structure and exact triangles

$D(\mathcal{A})$ is a triangulated category: a shift functor $[1]$ and a class of distinguished "exact" triangles $X \to Y \to Z \to X[1]$ replacing short exact sequences. The cohomology long exact sequence is a triangle's projection to $\mathcal{A}$.

5. Derived functors $RF, LF$

Right (resp. left) derived functors compute $F$ on injective (resp. projective) resolutions. They package the classical $R^iF, L_iF$ into a single triangulated functor $D(\mathcal{A}) \to D(\mathcal{B})$ — Ext and Tor become $\mathrm{Hom}_{D}$ and $\otimes^L$.

6. $t$-structures and hearts

A $t$-structure $(D^{\le 0}, D^{\ge 0})$ on a triangulated category gives a notion of "objects concentrated in non-positive (resp. non-negative) degree" and a heart $D^{\le 0} \cap D^{\ge 0}$ that is abelian. Recovers $\mathcal{A}$ from $D(\mathcal{A})$ as the heart of the standard $t$-structure.

7. $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,X)$ and Fourier–Mukai

For a smooth projective variety $X$, the bounded derived category of coherent sheaves $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,X)$ is the modern home of intersection theory and a finer invariant than Picard or cohomology. Fourier–Mukai transforms are equivalences between $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,X)$ and $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\,Y)$ given by integral kernels.

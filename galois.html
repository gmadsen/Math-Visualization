Galois, constructibility, and the quintic

How the symmetry of the roots of a polynomial determines whether you can solve it in radicals — and why the ancient Greeks couldn't double the cube.

1. The symmetry of roots

Given a polynomial $f(x)\in\mathbb{Q}[x]$, suppose you throw in all of its roots to form the smallest field $L\supseteq\mathbb{Q}$ containing them — the splitting field. Any field automorphism of $L$ fixing $\mathbb{Q}$ must permute the roots. The group of such automorphisms is the Galois group

$\operatorname{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q}) \;\hookrightarrow\; S_{\deg f}$.

Galois' astonishing theorem: the subfields of $L$ and the subgroups of $\operatorname{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q})$ correspond bijectively; bigger subgroup ↔ smaller subfield. And the arithmetic of the polynomial — in particular whether you can extract its roots using radicals — reduces to a pure group-theoretic property of this finite group: solvability.

This page walks that story in six steps: starting from compass-and-straightedge (the "quadratic" special case), ending at the non-solvability of $S_5$ which dooms the general quintic.

3. What a compass cannot do

The jump from the preceding gallery to a precise algebraic statement is:

Tower theorem. A real number $\alpha$ is constructible starting from $\{0,1\}$ if and only if there is a tower $$\mathbb{Q}=K_0\subset K_1\subset \cdots\subset K_n\ni\alpha \qquad [K_{i+1}:K_i]=2.$$ Every intersection introduces at worst a square root. Consequently $[\mathbb{Q}(\alpha):\mathbb{Q}]$ is a power of $2$ — this is a necessary condition.

This one-line consequence already settles three problems the Greeks failed to solve:

The converse of the tower theorem — that towers of degree $2$ give constructible numbers — is why the regular pentagon succumbed: $\cos(2\pi/5)=(\sqrt{5}-1)/4$ lives in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$, a single quadratic extension.

Which regular $n$-gons are constructible?

A regular $n$-gon is constructible iff $\zeta_n=e^{2\pi i/n}$ lives in a tower of quadratics iff $\varphi(n)$ is a power of $2$. Writing $n$ as $2^a\cdot p_1\cdots p_r$ with distinct odd primes, $\varphi(n)=2^{a-1}\cdot\prod(p_i-1)$, so each $p_i-1$ must be a power of $2$ — i.e. each $p_i$ is a Fermat prime $F_k=2^{2^k}+1$.

Gauss–Wantzel. The regular $n$-gon is constructible with compass and straightedge iff $$n=2^a\cdot p_1 p_2\cdots p_r$$ for some $a\ge 0$ and distinct Fermat primes $p_i\in\{3,5,17,257,65537\}$ (no other Fermat prime is known).

Gauss was sixteen when he discovered the construction of the regular 17-gon. He asked that it be engraved on his tombstone; the stonemason refused, reportedly saying that so many sides would look like a circle. The 65537-gon — the largest $n$-gon known to be constructible from a single odd prime — was carried out explicitly by Hermes over a ten-year span; the manuscript sits in the attic of the mathematics department in Göttingen.

4. Splitting fields and Galois groups

Fix $f(x)\in\mathbb{Q}[x]$ separable of degree $d$ with roots $\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_d\in\mathbb{C}$. The splitting field is $L=\mathbb{Q}(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_d)$. Every field automorphism $\sigma\colon L\to L$ fixing $\mathbb{Q}$ must send each root to another root, hence

$\operatorname{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q}) \;\subseteq\; S_d$.

The containment is usually strict; extra structure on the roots cuts down the group. Rational relations between roots become constraints a Galois automorphism must respect.

Notice the pattern: more rational relations between the roots ⇒ smaller Galois group. For $\Phi_5$ the roots are powers of a single $\zeta$; the group is cyclic $\mathbb{Z}/4$. For $x^4-2$ the roots come in $\pm$ pairs and conjugate pairs but are otherwise free — the group is the dihedral $D_4$ of order $8$. For $x^5-x-1$ the roots satisfy no non-trivial rational relations and the group is all of $S_5$ (order $120$).

5. Solvable groups

Galois' great insight: the arithmetic operation "extract an $n$-th root" corresponds, on the side of groups, to "take a cyclic quotient". A tower of radical extensions

$\mathbb{Q}=F_0\subset F_1\subset\cdots\subset F_m=L, \qquad F_{i+1}=F_i(\sqrt[n_i]{a_i})$

corresponds to a chain of normal subgroups of $\operatorname{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q})$ with abelian quotients. A finite group with such a chain is called solvable. Equivalently:

$G$ is solvable iff its derived series $$G\;\triangleright\;G'\;\triangleright\;G''\;\triangleright\;\cdots$$ reaches the trivial group in finitely many steps, where $G'=[G,G]$ is generated by all commutators $aba^{-1}b^{-1}$.

Commuting kills: if $G$ is abelian, every commutator is the identity and $G'=\{e\}$ in one step. The derived series collapses whenever you descend into something abelian, and keeps shrinking otherwise. If the chain ever stabilizes above the identity, the group is not solvable.

6. The quintic

We have the two ingredients. Galois' main theorem ties them together:

Galois' solvability theorem. Let $f\in\mathbb{Q}[x]$ have splitting field $L$. The equation $f(x)=0$ can be solved in radicals iff $\operatorname{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q})$ is a solvable group.

The quadratic, cubic, and quartic formulas exist because $S_2, S_3, S_4$ are solvable (their derived series bottom out at $\{e\}$ — you watched it happen in §5). The general quintic has Galois group $S_5$, and §5 showed that $S_5$ is not solvable. So there is no universal formula for the roots of a degree-$5$ polynomial in terms of its coefficients using $+,-,\cdot,\div,\sqrt[n]{\ }$.

The real work is concentrated in $A_5$ is simple — it has no non-trivial normal subgroup. Hence $A_5$ is perfect: $[A_5,A_5]=A_5$. So the derived series of $A_5$ (and of $S_5$, after one step into $A_5$) stabilizes and never reaches $\{e\}$.

Why $A_5$ is simple — a counting argument

Any normal subgroup of $A_5$ must be a union of conjugacy classes (including the identity class) whose total order divides $60 = |A_5|$. The five conjugacy classes of $A_5$ have sizes:

Only $\{e\}$ and all of $A_5$ satisfy the two requirements (contain the identity class; total order divides $60$). So $A_5$ has no proper non-trivial normal subgroup — it is simple. That single fact blocks the quintic.

A concrete quintic that can't be solved in radicals: $x^5-x-1$

Look again at §4, preset $x^5-x-1$. Two of its roots are real, three are complex (as you can see in the widget). The polynomial is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ (by reduction mod $5$, it is irreducible mod $5$, or by the rational root theorem plus a degree check). Therefore its Galois group $G\subseteq S_5$ is transitive on the five roots, so $5\mid |G|$; hence $G$ contains a $5$-cycle. Complex conjugation acts as a transposition (swapping the complex-conjugate pair while fixing the real roots + one complex cycle), so $G$ contains a transposition. A transitive subgroup of $S_5$ containing a transposition and a $5$-cycle is all of $S_5$.

Since $S_5$ is not solvable, the specific equation $x^5-x-1=0$ has no solution expressible in radicals. Its roots are perfectly well-defined real and complex numbers — you can compute them to a thousand digits — but they are not expressible by any combination of rational numbers, arithmetic operations, and $n$-th roots.

Big picture. The polynomial's roots live in the complex numbers, a continuum. But the question "can I write these roots using radicals?" is, secretly, a question about a finite object — the Galois group. Galois turned a question about continuous, analytic objects into a question about combinatorial ones. That's the philosophical payoff: a discrete symmetry obstruction controls an analytic soluability phenomenon.

Abel proved the non-existence of a quintic formula in 1824 without the Galois correspondence. Galois' framework appeared in unsent letters written the night before his duel in 1832; he was twenty. The modern form of the theorem took another forty years to stabilize, largely through the work of Jordan.

7. Normal and separable extensions

Why did §4 quietly require splitting fields? Because an extension $L/K$ deserves to be called Galois only when two conditions are met. First, $L/K$ is normal: every irreducible polynomial in $K[x]$ that has one root in $L$ factors completely over $L$ — equivalently, $L$ is the splitting field of some polynomial. Second, $L/K$ is separable: every $\alpha\in L$ has a minimal polynomial over $K$ with distinct roots in an algebraic closure. In characteristic zero (and for finite fields) separability is free, so a normal finite extension is Galois; in characteristic $p$ the inseparable extension $\mathbb{F}_p(t)/\mathbb{F}_p(t^p)$ shows things can go wrong — its only automorphism is the identity, yet $[L:K]=p$.

Concretely: $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})/\mathbb{Q}$ is not normal — the minimal polynomial $x^3-2$ has one real root in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$ but the other two are complex. Adjoining $\omega=e^{2\pi i/3}$ to form $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2},\omega)$ restores normality and gives $|\!\operatorname{Gal}|=6=[L:\mathbb{Q}]$. The general slogan: $|\!\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)|\le [L:K]$ always, with equality iff $L/K$ is Galois.

Criterion. A finite extension $L/K$ is Galois iff $L^{\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)}=K$, iff $L/K$ is both normal and separable, iff $|\!\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)|=[L:K]$. Any of the three conditions implies the other two.

8. Primitive element theorem

Once separability is in hand, a striking simplification appears: every finite separable extension is simple. That is, if $L/K$ is finite and separable, there exists a single $\theta\in L$ — a primitive element — such that $L=K(\theta)$. So the seemingly multi-generator extension $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3})$ collapses to $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}+\sqrt{3})$: one checks that $\theta=\sqrt{2}+\sqrt{3}$ satisfies the degree-$4$ polynomial $\theta^4-10\theta^2+1=0$, whose splitting field is exactly $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3})$.

The proof sketch (for infinite $K$): given generators $\alpha,\beta$, almost every $K$-linear combination $\theta=\alpha+c\beta$ works — only finitely many values of $c$ can fail, because a failure forces two conjugates of $\theta$ to coincide, and there are only finitely many pairs of conjugates. Computationally, this means Galois theory for characteristic-zero fields reduces to the study of a single irreducible polynomial $m_\theta(x)\in K[x]$ of degree $[L:K]$: the $\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)$-action is just the permutation action on the roots of $m_\theta$.

Primitive element theorem (Steinitz). If $L/K$ is a finite separable extension then $L=K(\theta)$ for some $\theta\in L$. In particular every finite extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ (or of any characteristic-zero field) is simple.

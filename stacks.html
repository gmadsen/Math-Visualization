Stacks: groupoids, descent, and moduli

A moduli problem wants a fine space. When objects have automorphisms, no scheme can do the job honestly. Stacks fix the deficit by remembering not just isomorphism classes but the automorphisms themselves. This page builds the Deligne–Mumford level of the theory: groupoid-valued functors, the descent condition, the classifying stack $BG$, and $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ as the first truly stacky example.

1. Recap: the moduli problem

A moduli problem asks for a space whose points parameterise some class of geometric objects — elliptic curves, vector bundles, stable maps, and so on. The naive formulation is a functor

$$ \mathcal{M}: \mathrm{Sch}^{\mathrm{op}} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Set}, \qquad R \longmapsto \{\text{objects over } \mathrm{Spec}\, R\}/\cong. $$

If this functor is representable by a scheme $M$, we call $M$ a fine moduli space. For elliptic curves the functor is not representable: the culprit is the non-trivial automorphism $[-1]$ on every elliptic curve, and the extra automorphisms $\mu_4$ at $j=1728$, $\mu_6$ at $j=0$. See the elliptic-curves, moduli-spaces, and functor-of-points pages for the obstruction in detail.

The diagnostic. Take a family $\mathcal{E}\to B$ and an automorphism $\sigma$ of a fibre. If the representing object ignored $\sigma$, twisting $\mathcal{E}$ along a cover by $\sigma$ would give an inequivalent family with the same classifying map. Representability fails because distinct families produce the same classifying morphism.

The standard move — pass to the coarse moduli space (here, the $j$-line $\mathbb{A}^1_j$) — discards the automorphism data entirely. Coarse moduli is a bijection on points but forgets the $\mathrm{Aut}$-groups that made the problem interesting in the first place.

Forward-pointing question. What if, instead of throwing the automorphisms away, we keep them as part of the moduli object?

2. Groupoids, not sets

A groupoid is a category in which every morphism is an isomorphism. Groupoids are the right replacement for sets whenever your objects come with automorphisms you do not want to forget.

If $\mathcal{C}$ is any category (say, elliptic curves over a fixed ring $R$ with isomorphisms of elliptic curves as the only morphisms), its underlying groupoid $\mathcal{C}^{\cong}$ throws away non-invertible arrows but keeps everything else. The set of isomorphism classes $\pi_0(\mathcal{C}^{\cong})$ is the skeletal shadow of this groupoid.

Collapsing a groupoid to its $\pi_0$ is a lossy operation: you recover the number of components but lose the automorphism group $\mathrm{Aut}(x)$ at each object $x$.

The slogan: Schemes are to sets as stacks are to groupoids. To keep automorphism data, redefine the moduli functor to land in groupoids.

3. Fibered categories and groupoid-valued functors

Repackage the moduli problem as

$$ \mathcal{M}: \mathrm{Sch}^{\mathrm{op}} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Grpd}, \qquad R \longmapsto \mathcal{M}(R), $$

where $\mathcal{M}(R)$ is the groupoid whose objects are families $X\to\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ of the desired type and whose arrows are isomorphisms of families over $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$. For a ring map $R\to S$, pullback sends a family over $R$ to a family over $S$ and gives the restriction functor $\mathcal{M}(R)\to\mathcal{M}(S)$.

Subtlety. Pullback is only defined up to canonical isomorphism, not on the nose — $f^*(g^*X)$ and $(gf)^*X$ are canonically isomorphic but not equal. The honest way to handle this is a fibered category $\mathcal{M}\to\mathrm{Sch}$: one single category sitting over $\mathrm{Sch}$, with a prescribed notion of pullback via cartesian arrows. A groupoid-valued pseudo-functor and a category fibered in groupoids carry the same information; use whichever feels lighter.

Either way, a moduli problem is now a functor (or fibered category) valued in groupoids. That is still not a stack — we need one more condition.

4. The descent (sheaf) condition

A presheaf of sets is a sheaf if local data glue uniquely. The groupoid analog has two layers: objects must glue (up to iso), and isomorphisms must glue on the nose.

Stack. A fibered category $\mathcal{X}\to\mathrm{Sch}$ is a stack (for a chosen Grothendieck topology, typically étale or fppf) when for every cover $\{U_i\to U\}$:
  1. Isomorphisms are a sheaf. For any two objects $x,y\in\mathcal{X}(U)$, the presheaf $V\mapsto \mathrm{Iso}_{\mathcal{X}(V)}(x|_V, y|_V)$ is a sheaf on $U$.
  2. Descent for objects. Given local objects $x_i\in\mathcal{X}(U_i)$ with transition isomorphisms $\varphi_{ij}:x_j|_{U_{ij}}\xrightarrow{\sim} x_i|_{U_{ij}}$ satisfying the cocycle $\varphi_{ik}=\varphi_{ij}\circ\varphi_{jk}$ on triple overlaps, there is an object $x\in\mathcal{X}(U)$ (unique up to unique iso) with $x|_{U_i}\cong x_i$ compatibly.

The first condition says "being isomorphic is a local property". The second says "you can glue families". Both were invisible in the set-valued theory because sets have no non-trivial automorphisms.

Two local families on $U$ and $V$, with a transition iso $\varphi$ on $U\cap V$. Click "glue".

Grothendieck topology choice matters. The Zariski topology is too coarse to make moduli functors into stacks; the étale or fppf topologies are the workhorses. All reasonable moduli problems are stacks in the étale or fppf topology.

5. Example: $BG$, the classifying stack

Fix a finite group $G$. The classifying stack $BG$ is defined by

$$ BG(R) \;=\; \text{groupoid of } G\text{-torsors on } \mathrm{Spec}\,R. $$

A $G$-torsor on $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ is an $R$-scheme $P\to\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ with a free $G$-action, étale-locally isomorphic to $G\times\mathrm{Spec}\,R$. For $R$ a field, the set of iso classes is $H^1(\mathrm{Spec}\,R, G)$, and the automorphism group of any torsor is a form of $G$; for the trivial torsor it is literally $G$ acting by translation.

$BG$ is not a scheme. Its unique geometric point $\mathrm{Spec}\,\overline{k}\to BG$ (the trivial torsor) has automorphism group $G$. A scheme cannot have non-trivial automorphisms at a geometric point, because its functor of points lands in sets. So $BG$ is the simplest genuinely-stacky stack.
W3 · $BG$ torsors

$BG$ has a universal property: maps $T\to BG$ from a scheme $T$ correspond to $G$-torsors on $T$. In particular $\mathrm{Spec}\,k\to BG$ classifies the trivial torsor and is itself a smooth surjection — this will be our model for "atlas" in a moment.

6. Quotient stacks $[X/G]$

Generalise $BG$ by letting a group $G$ act on a scheme $X$ (not just a point). The quotient stack $[X/G]$ is defined, like $BG$, through its groupoid of families:

$$ [X/G](T) \;=\; \Bigl\{ \text{pairs } (P \to T,\ P \xrightarrow{\phi} X) \;:\; P \to T \text{ is a } G\text{-torsor},\ \phi \text{ is } G\text{-equivariant}\Bigr\}. $$

When $X=\mathrm{Spec}\,k$ is a point, the $G$-equivariant map $\phi$ carries no information and we recover $BG(T)=\{G\text{-torsors on }T\}$. When $G$ is the trivial group, a $\{e\}$-torsor is just a map $T\to T$, the equivariant map to $X$ is any morphism $T\to X$, so $[X/\{e\}]=X$ (the stack of maps to $X$). The quotient stack interpolates between these two extremes.

Two concrete facts.
  • If $G$ acts freely on $X$, the quotient stack $[X/G]$ is equivalent to the quotient scheme $X/G$ — no stacky enhancement is needed. Stabilisers only appear when the action has fixed points.
  • The geometric point corresponding to $x\in X(\overline{k})$ sits above the image $\overline{x}\in (X/G)(\overline{k})$ with automorphism group equal to the stabiliser $\mathrm{Stab}_G(x)$. So $[X/G]$ is "$X/G$ with stabiliser groups re-attached at every orbit".

A universal-property reading: $X\to [X/G]$ is itself a $G$-torsor, so $[X/G]$ is the universal target classifying $G$-torsors equipped with an equivariant map to $X$. In particular $[X/G]$ has an atlas given by $X$ itself, and the groupoid presentation is the action groupoid

$$ G\times X \;\rightrightarrows\; X, \qquad (g,x)\mapsto x \text{ and } (g,x)\mapsto g\cdot x. $$

Every DM (or Artin) stack is locally of this form: étale-locally on the atlas, a stack looks like $[U/\Gamma]$ for a finite group $\Gamma$. So quotient stacks are the building blocks — understand these and you can reason by pulling back to charts. Example: the coarse shape of $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ near $j=0$ is $[\mathbb{A}^1/\mu_3]$, and near $j=1728$ it is $[\mathbb{A}^1/\mu_2]$.

7. Deligne–Mumford stacks

Not every stack is geometrically tractable. The Deligne–Mumford condition isolates the ones that behave like schemes with finite automorphism groups.

Operational definition. A stack $\mathcal{X}$ (over $\mathrm{Sch}/S$, say) is a Deligne–Mumford stack if
  1. the diagonal $\Delta:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{X}\times_S\mathcal{X}$ is representable, quasi-compact, and unramified — equivalently, automorphism schemes of objects are finite and unramified;
  2. there exists a scheme $X$ and an étale surjection $X\to\mathcal{X}$. We call such an $X\to\mathcal{X}$ an étale atlas.
If instead you only have a smooth surjection from a scheme, you get an Artin (= algebraic) stack — a larger class that includes $BG$ for positive-dimensional $G$ and is not treated here.

The étale atlas $X\to\mathcal{X}$ and its fiber product $X\times_{\mathcal{X}} X$ together form a groupoid in schemes

$$ X \times_{\mathcal{X}} X \;\rightrightarrows\; X, $$

whose two maps are "first projection" and "second projection" and whose composition records how patches overlap. A DM stack is such an étale groupoid object, up to Morita equivalence.

A scheme $X$ covering a stack $\mathcal{X}$ by an étale surjection.

Because the cover is étale, geometric points of $\mathcal{X}$ have étale-locally trivial automorphism action, i.e. finite automorphism groups. This is exactly the tractability you want.

8. Deligne–Mumford vs. Artin

What changes if we only insist on a smooth atlas rather than an étale one? We get the broader class of Artin (a.k.a. algebraic) stacks. Every DM stack is Artin; the converse fails precisely when automorphism groups are positive-dimensional.

Definitions side by side.
  • Artin stack. Diagonal $\Delta:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{X}\times\mathcal{X}$ is representable and quasi-compact; there exists a scheme $X$ with a smooth surjection $X\to\mathcal{X}$.
  • Deligne–Mumford stack. Additionally, the diagonal is unramified; equivalently, one can find an étale surjection $X\to\mathcal{X}$. This forces automorphism schemes to be finite and unramified, hence $0$-dimensional.
A one-line slogan: DM ⇔ Artin with $\dim \mathrm{Aut}(x) = 0$ at every geometric point $x$.

Examples clarify the gap.

  • $B\mu_n$ (finite cyclic group) is DM: the automorphism group $\mu_n$ is finite.
  • $B\mathbb{G}_m$ is Artin but not DM: the automorphism group is $\mathbb{G}_m$, which is one-dimensional. Its coarse space is a point, but its stacky structure encodes every line bundle on the base.
  • The stack of vector bundles $\mathrm{Bun}_n(C)$ on a smooth projective curve $C$ is Artin (smooth, of dimension $n^2(g-1)$) but not DM: every vector bundle has at least the scalar automorphisms $\mathbb{G}_m$.

Formally, Artin stacks sit one rung above DM stacks in a tower that continues upward — $\infty$-stacks, derived stacks — each step admitting richer automorphism phenomena and more relaxed smoothness data. For this page the takeaway is operational:

When to pick which. If your moduli problem has finite automorphism groups (stable curves, elliptic curves away from char $2,3$, finite group torsors), aim for DM — you inherit étale-local machinery, coarse moduli spaces, and clean intersection theory. If automorphisms are positive-dimensional (vector bundles, $G$-bundles, quotients by non-free actions of reductive groups), you must be Artin, and you must work with smooth (not étale) atlases throughout.

9. $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ as a DM stack

Finally we can describe the moduli of elliptic curves honestly. $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ is the stack whose $R$-points are the groupoid of elliptic curves $E\to\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ with their isomorphisms. It is a DM stack.

An atlas: fix $N\ge 3$ prime to the characteristics in sight. The functor "elliptic curve with full level-$N$ structure" $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}(N)$ is already representable by a scheme $Y(N)$ because level-$N$ structure rigidifies (no non-trivial automorphism fixes a level-3 structure). The forgetful map

$$ Y(N) \longrightarrow \mathcal{M}_{1,1} $$

is étale (the Galois group being $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/N)/\{\pm1\}$) and surjective, i.e. an étale atlas.

Coarse shadow. The coarse moduli space of $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ is the $j$-line $\mathbb{A}^1_j$. Generic points have automorphism group $\mu_2 = \{\pm1\}$ (the $[-1]$ involution on every elliptic curve). Two points are stacky in the sense that their automorphism group is larger:
  • $j=1728$: $E: y^2=x^3+x$ has $\mathrm{Aut}(E)=\mu_4$ (complex multiplication by $\mathbb{Z}[i]$);
  • $j=0$: $E: y^2=x^3+1$ has $\mathrm{Aut}(E)=\mu_6$ (CM by $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_3]$).
After quotienting by the generic $\mu_2$ you see residual $\mu_2$ at $j=1728$ and residual $\mu_3$ at $j=0$: locally $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ looks like $[\mathbb{A}^1/\mu_2]$ near $j=1728$ and $[\mathbb{A}^1/\mu_3]$ near $j=0$.

The stack is not a Deligne–Mumford stack in characteristic $2$ or $3$ over the entire base (automorphism schemes become non-reduced there), but the classical statement holds over $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}[1/6]$ and gives you everything you need for the analytic picture.

10. Coarse moduli map

Even though $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ is not a scheme, it has a best-possible scheme approximation. This is a general fact:

Keel–Mori (for DM stacks of finite type). For a DM stack $\mathcal{X}$ of finite type with finite inertia over a locally Noetherian base $S$, there is a scheme $X$ (the coarse moduli space) and a morphism $\pi:\mathcal{X}\to X$ such that:
  1. $\pi$ induces a bijection on geometric points;
  2. any map $\mathcal{X}\to Y$ to a scheme $Y$ factors uniquely through $\pi$.

For $\mathcal{X} = \mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ the coarse moduli is $X = \mathbb{A}^1_j$. For $\mathcal{X} = BG$ over a field $k$ the coarse moduli is just $\mathrm{Spec}\,k$: a single point with all automorphism data discarded. The coarse map is the mechanism that turns the "naive" moduli functor-to-sets back on, and it is why people got so far with coarse spaces before stacks were invented — you are allowed to use them, you just know they are lossy.

A sample scheme morphism $f:\mathcal{M}_{1,1}\to Y$. Since $Y$ is a scheme, $f$ factors uniquely through the coarse space.

Two cautions. First, the map $\pi$ is not a gerbe quotient in general — the fiber of $\pi$ over a stacky point is a classifying stack $B\mathrm{Aut}$, so the coarse space really does forget structure. Second, Chow groups, cohomology, and intersection theory of $\mathcal{X}$ and of its coarse space $X$ differ in a controlled way: for $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ the integer Chow ring is $\mathbb{Z}[t]/(12t)$, not the Chow ring of $\mathbb{A}^1$.

11. Takeaways

  • Slogan. Schemes : Sets :: Stacks : Groupoids. A stack is a sheaf of groupoids on the étale (or fppf) site.
  • Why they exist. Moduli problems with non-trivial automorphisms cannot have fine moduli schemes; stacks are the smallest enlargement of $\mathrm{Sch}$ that admits them.
  • DM stacks. Automorphism groups are finite; there is an étale atlas $X\to\mathcal{X}$. They behave like schemes for most practical purposes — you can do cohomology, Chow theory, deformation theory, intersection numbers.
  • $BG$ and $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$. The two canonical examples. $BG$ is pure automorphism with no moduli; $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ is generic moduli with residual automorphism at two points.
  • Coarse moduli. Always there (Keel–Mori), always a scheme, always a bijection on points, always lossy about automorphisms.
  • What comes next. Artin (algebraic) stacks drop "étale atlas" to "smooth atlas" and accommodate $BG$ for positive-dimensional $G$, $[X/G]$ for non-free actions, and moduli of sheaves. The theory of $\infty$-stacks pushes the groupoid target to simplicial sets / $\infty$-groupoids and is the setting for derived algebraic geometry.
Mental model. A DM stack is "a scheme together with a finite automorphism group attached to each geometric point, glued étale-locally so the gluing data is just the automorphism groupoid of the cover." Everything you want to do with schemes still works — you just carry a finite group along at each point.

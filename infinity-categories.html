Infinity categories

Quasi-categories à la Joyal: composition up to coherent higher homotopy via inner-horn filling.

1. Quasi-categories

A quasi-category is a simplicial set $\mathcal{C}$ in which every inner horn $\Lambda^n_k \to \mathcal{C}$ (for $0 < k < n$) admits a filler $\Delta^n \to \mathcal{C}$. Equivalently, $\mathcal{C}$ is fibrant in the Joyal model structure on $\mathbf{sSet}$. We read $\mathcal{C}_0$ as the set of objects, $\mathcal{C}_1$ as morphisms, and $\mathcal{C}_n$ for $n \geq 2$ as the higher coherence data. Joyal's foundational observation: an ordinary category $C$ is recovered by passing to its nerve $N(C)$, where every inner horn has a unique filler. Relaxing uniqueness to mere existence is exactly what lets composition acquire higher homotopical data.

The slogan "objects, morphisms, homotopies, homotopies between homotopies, …" is literal: a $2$-simplex $$\sigma\colon \Delta^2 \to \mathcal{C}, \qquad \partial_0 \sigma = g,\; \partial_1\sigma = h,\; \partial_2\sigma = f$$ is the data witnessing $h \simeq g \circ f$. The inner-horn filler for $\Lambda^2_1$ provides the composite $g \circ f$ (non-uniquely), and a $3$-simplex with the right boundary data provides the associativity coherence — which is itself only specified up to a higher homotopy supplied by the $\Lambda^3_1$ and $\Lambda^3_2$ fillers. The whole tower of associativity / coherence data is encoded in "every inner horn fills."

Two extremes bracket the picture. If every horn fills (inner and outer), $\mathcal{C}$ is a Kan complex: every morphism is invertible, so $\mathcal{C}$ is an $\infty$-groupoid (the homotopy type of a topological space, by the homotopy hypothesis). At the other extreme, if every inner horn admits a unique filler, $\mathcal{C} \cong N(C)$ for a unique ordinary category $C$ — Joyal's nerve theorem. A general quasi-category sits between: composition exists but is non-unique, and equivalences (1-simplices that are invertible up to a 2-simplex) form the maximal Kan-complex sub-object $\mathcal{C}^{\simeq} \subset \mathcal{C}$.

Why bother? In ordinary category theory, the "category of chain complexes up to quasi-isomorphism" forces you to localize and work with derived categories; coherent functorial constructions (mapping cones, derived tensor products) are notoriously fiddly because the homotopies witnessing functoriality are not part of the data. A quasi-category bakes those homotopies in from the start: the simplicial set $N(\mathrm{Ch}(\mathcal{A}))[\mathrm{qis}^{-1}]$ becomes a quasi-category whose $1$-simplices are zigzags and whose $n$-simplices are coherent diagrams, and $\infty$-functors out of it automatically respect the homotopy structure. References: Lurie HTT §1.1–1.2, Cisinski §3.1, Riehl–Verity Ch. 1.

2. The homotopy category $h(\mathcal{C})$

Given a quasi-category $\mathcal{C}$, two parallel $1$-simplices $f, g\\colon x \to y$ are homotopic, written $f \simeq g$, if there is a $2$-simplex $\sigma\colon \Delta^2 \to \mathcal{C}$ with $\partial_0 \sigma = g$, $\partial_1 \sigma = g$, $\partial_2 \sigma = f$ (or any of the equivalent forms; see the side illustration). Inner-horn filling for $\Lambda^3_1$ and $\Lambda^3_2$ guarantees this is an equivalence relation. The quotient $$h(\mathcal{C})\colon \mathrm{Ob}(h\mathcal{C}) := \mathcal{C}_0, \qquad \mathrm{Hom}_{h\mathcal{C}}(x, y) := \mathcal{C}_1(x, y)/\!\simeq$$ is an honest $1$-category with composition induced by $\Lambda^2_1$-fillers — well-defined because two different fillers differ by a homotopy.

The construction $h\colon \mathbf{QCat} \to \mathbf{Cat}$ is the left adjoint to the nerve $N\colon \mathbf{Cat} \to \mathbf{QCat}$. Concretely: a functor $h(\mathcal{C}) \to D$ of ordinary categories is the same data as a map of simplicial sets $\mathcal{C} \to N(D)$ (a "strict" $\infty$-functor whose target happens to have unique composition). For an ordinary category $C$, the unit map $C \to h(N(C))$ is an isomorphism — passing through the nerve and back loses nothing if you started with an ordinary category to begin with.

What you do lose, though, is everything above dimension $1$. The mapping space $\mathrm{Map}_{\mathcal{C}}(x, y)$ — a Kan complex with possibly nontrivial $\pi_1, \pi_2, \dots$ — is collapsed to its $\pi_0$, and all higher coherence data is forgotten. So $h$ is a "decategorification" rather than an equivalence: typical examples like $h(\mathrm{Top})$ recover the classical homotopy category of spaces, but every higher Toda bracket / Massey product is invisible.

One useful diagnostic: an $\infty$-functor $F\colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ is an equivalence of $\infty$-categories iff $h(F)\colon h(\mathcal{C}) \to h(\mathcal{D})$ is an equivalence of $1$-categories and $F$ induces homotopy equivalences on all mapping spaces. The first condition alone is too weak (it's the Dwyer–Kan / "essentially surjective + fully faithful on $\pi_0$" data); the second supplies the higher information.

3. Functors and natural transformations $\infty$-categorically

An $\infty$-functor $F\colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ between quasi-categories is a map of simplicial sets — full stop. Concretely it consists of, for every $n \geq 0$, a function $F_n\colon \mathcal{C}_n \to \mathcal{D}_n$ commuting with all face and degeneracy maps. So $F_0$ assigns objects to objects, $F_1$ assigns morphisms to morphisms, $F_2$ supplies the chosen "compositor" $F(g) \circ F(f) \simeq F(g \circ f)$ as a $2$-simplex, and so on. Coherence at every level is part of the data — nothing to verify separately.

This simplicity is a major simplification over $1$-categorical "homotopy-coherent diagrams," which require a tower of compatible isomorphisms. The cost of that compatibility is paid up-front by the simplicial-set machinery: $F$ being a map of simplicial sets is the homotopy-coherent data. The induced functor $h(F)\colon h(\mathcal{C}) \to h(\mathcal{D})$ on homotopy categories is the underlying $1$-functor; "what $F$ does on objects and morphisms up to homotopy" is the $h$-shadow, with the rest of $F$ supplying the higher coherences.

Natural transformations are equally crisp. The functor $\infty$-category $\mathrm{Fun}(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{D}) := \mathcal{D}^{\mathcal{C}}$ is a quasi-category whose $0$-simplices are $\infty$-functors and whose $1$-simplices are natural transformations — equivalently, maps $\mathcal{C} \times \Delta^1 \to \mathcal{D}$ (with the obvious endpoint conditions). The proof that this exponential is a quasi-category uses Joyal's lifting theorem: $\mathcal{D}^{\mathcal{C}}$ inherits the inner-horn-filling property from $\mathcal{D}$.

The $\infty$-Yoneda embedding lifts: for $\mathcal{C}$ a small $\infty$-category, $\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C}) := \mathrm{Fun}(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathcal{S})$ — where $\mathcal{S}$ is the $\infty$-category of spaces (Kan complexes) — is the $\infty$-category of presheaves of spaces. The embedding $y\colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C})$, $y(x) = \mathrm{Map}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, x)$, is fully faithful: $\mathrm{Map}_{\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C})}(y(x), y(y)) \simeq \mathrm{Map}_{\mathcal{C}}(x, y)$ as Kan complexes (Lurie HTT 5.1.3). Crucially, $\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C})$ is the free cocomplete $\infty$-category on $\mathcal{C}$ — the universal property that makes $\infty$-presheaf categories the engine of derived algebraic geometry.

4. $\infty$-categorical (co)limits

Given a diagram $p\colon K \to \mathcal{C}$ from a simplicial set $K$ to a quasi-category $\mathcal{C}$, the slice quasi-category $\mathcal{C}_{/p}$ has $0$-simplices the cones over $p$ — pairs $(c, \alpha)$ where $\alpha\colon \Delta^0 \star K \to \mathcal{C}$ extends $p$ along $K \hookrightarrow \Delta^0 \star K$ — and higher simplices the obvious "extended cones." The limit of $p$ is a terminal object of $\mathcal{C}_{/p}$, when one exists. Dually, the colimit is an initial object of the coslice $\mathcal{C}_{p/}$. Terminal objects in a quasi-category are unique up to a contractible space of choices, so $\lim p$ — if it exists — is determined up to a contractible Kan complex of identifications.

The big payoff: this definition automatically encodes homotopy limits and colimits. In the $\infty$-category $\mathcal{S}$ of spaces (presented by Kan complexes), a pullback of a span $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$ is the homotopy pullback — which differs from the strict pullback unless one map is a fibration. In the $\infty$-category $\mathrm{D}(\mathbf{Ab})^{\geq 0}$ of nonnegatively-graded chain complexes (presented as a quasi-category via the Dold–Kan equivalence), a pullback gives a long exact Mayer–Vietoris sequence — automatically. The "deriving" step that ordinary category theory would force you to perform is built into the definition.

Concrete shapes worth knowing:

  • $\infty$-product in $\mathcal{S}$: the categorical product of Kan complexes, which agrees with the cartesian product of underlying sets (no homotopy correction needed).
  • $\infty$-pullback in $\mathcal{S}$: the homotopy pullback $X \times_Z^h Y \simeq \{ (x, \gamma, y) : \gamma\colon p(x) \rightsquigarrow q(y) \}$, the space of triples (point, path, point).
  • $\infty$-equalizer in $\mathcal{S}$: the path space $\{ x : f(x) \rightsquigarrow g(x) \}$, i.e. the homotopy fibre of the difference.
  • Geometric realization $|X_\bullet| = \mathrm{colim}_{[n] \in \Delta^{\mathrm{op}}} X_n$ in $\mathcal{S}$: the actual topological realization, taking the simplicial nerves of higher-categorical fibration data into account.

An $\infty$-category $\mathcal{C}$ is cocomplete if every diagram $p\colon K \to \mathcal{C}$ from a small simplicial set $K$ has a colimit; presentable $\infty$-categories (a localization of a presheaf $\infty$-category along a small set of morphisms) are the workhorse of $\infty$-category theory because they are automatically cocomplete and admit the adjoint-functor theorem (Lurie HTT 5.5).

5. Adjunctions $\infty$-categorically

An $\infty$-adjunction $f \dashv g$ between quasi-categories $\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{D}$ has several equivalent formulations (see Riehl–Verity Ch. 4 for the comparison). The cleanest is via mapping spaces: a unit $\eta\colon \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{C}} \to gf$ and counit $\varepsilon\colon fg \to \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{D}}$ — both as $1$-simplices in their respective functor $\infty$-categories — together with a chosen $2$-simplex (and higher coherences) witnessing the triangle identities $\varepsilon f \cdot f \eta \simeq \mathrm{id}_f$ and $g\varepsilon \cdot \eta g \simeq \mathrm{id}_g$ up to homotopy. Equivalently: a natural equivalence of Kan complexes $$\mathrm{Map}_{\mathcal{D}}(f(c), d) \;\simeq\; \mathrm{Map}_{\mathcal{C}}(c, g(d)) \qquad \text{for all } c \in \mathcal{C}, d \in \mathcal{D}.$$

The third packaging — Lurie's preferred one — is via cocartesian/cartesian fibrations: an adjunction $f \dashv g$ is a single fibration $p\colon \mathcal{M} \to \Delta^1$ that is both cocartesian and cartesian, with fibres $\mathcal{C}$ over $0$ and $\mathcal{D}$ over $1$. The cocartesian transport gives $f$, the cartesian transport gives $g$, and the joint structure encodes both $\eta$ and $\varepsilon$ at once. This is the formulation in HTT 5.2.

Most ordinary adjunctions lift: if $F\colon C \rightleftarrows D : G$ is an ordinary adjunction with $C, D$ presentable as $\infty$-categories (e.g. arising from a Quillen adjunction between model categories), then $F$ and $G$ derive to an $\infty$-adjunction $\mathbb{L}F \dashv \mathbb{R}G$ between the underlying $\infty$-categories. This is how, e.g., $\mathbb{L} \otimes^{\mathbb{L}} \dashv \mathbb{R}\mathrm{Hom}$ on derived categories arises from the chain-complex tensor/Hom adjunction.

The $\infty$-adjoint functor theorem (Lurie HTT 5.5.2.9): a functor $g\colon \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{C}$ between presentable $\infty$-categories has a left adjoint iff it preserves small limits and is accessible. Dually, a right adjoint exists iff $g$ preserves small colimits. The accessibility hypothesis is the essential extra condition over the $1$-categorical SAFT — and it's automatic in most of practice (presentability is closed under limits and accessible localizations).

6. $\infty$-categorical Kan extensions

Given $\infty$-functors $F\colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{E}$ and $i\colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$, a left Kan extension of $F$ along $i$ is an $\infty$-functor $\mathrm{Lan}_i F\colon \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{E}$ together with a unit $\eta\colon F \to (\mathrm{Lan}_i F) \circ i$ exhibiting it as universal: precomposition with $\eta$ gives an equivalence $$\mathrm{Map}_{\mathrm{Fun}(\mathcal{D}, \mathcal{E})}(\mathrm{Lan}_i F,\, G) \;\xrightarrow{\sim}\; \mathrm{Map}_{\mathrm{Fun}(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{E})}(F,\, G \circ i).$$ Right Kan extensions $\mathrm{Ran}_i F$ are dual, with the universal property reversed.

The headline theorem (Lurie HTT 4.3.2.15): when $\mathcal{E}$ admits enough colimits, $\mathrm{Lan}_i F$ exists and is computed pointwise by the colimit formula $$(\mathrm{Lan}_i F)(d) \;\simeq\; \mathop{\mathrm{colim}}_{(c, \alpha) \in \mathcal{C} \times_{\mathcal{D}} \mathcal{D}_{/d}} F(c)$$ where $\mathcal{C} \times_{\mathcal{D}} \mathcal{D}_{/d}$ is the comma $\infty$-category: pairs $(c, \alpha\colon i(c) \to d)$ with $1$-simplices the obvious commutative triangles. In the $1$-categorical case this recovers the classical end/coend formulas; the $\infty$-categorical version automatically takes the homotopy colimit, so it computes the derived Kan extension.

Special cases dominate the applications:

  • Free cocompletion via Yoneda. If $i\colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C})$ is the Yoneda embedding and $F\colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{E}$ with $\mathcal{E}$ cocomplete, then $\mathrm{Lan}_i F\colon \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C}) \to \mathcal{E}$ is the unique colimit-preserving extension. This is why $\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C})$ is the free cocompletion of $\mathcal{C}$.
  • Derived functors. For an exact functor $F\colon \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}$ between abelian categories, the left derived functor $\mathbb{L}F\colon D^-(\mathcal{A}) \to D^-(\mathcal{B})$ is the left Kan extension of the (lifted) $F$ along the localization $K^-(\mathcal{A}) \to D^-(\mathcal{A})$. This is one of Lurie's principal motivations for $\infty$-category theory: derived functors are Kan extensions, no separate "deriving" needed.
  • Ind-completion. $\mathrm{Ind}(\mathcal{C}) \subset \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C})$ is the closure of $y(\mathcal{C})$ under filtered colimits — itself a left Kan extension along the inclusion of finite-shape diagrams.

Right Kan extensions exist when $\mathcal{E}$ has enough limits; the formula uses $\mathcal{C} \times_{\mathcal{D}} \mathcal{D}_{d/}$ and computes a limit. The duality is exact, but the cocomplete vs. complete asymmetry matters in practice — most categories of interest in homotopy theory (spaces, spectra, sheaves of spectra) are presentable hence cocomplete by default, while $\mathrm{Ran}$-style limit constructions sometimes require additional hypotheses.

Morphisms and fiber products

How one scheme sits over another, and how to pull one scheme back along a map. The geometric life of a polynomial family, from ring maps to ramification.

1. What is a morphism of schemes?

A scheme is the pair $(X,\mathcal{O}_X)$ of a topological space $X$ together with its structure sheaf of rings. (For background on sheaves and how $\mathcal{O}_X$ records the local regular functions, see sheaves.html.) A morphism of schemes $f\colon X\to Y$ is not just a continuous map of underlying spaces. It comes with two pieces of data:

  • a continuous map $f\colon |X|\to |Y|$ of topological spaces;
  • a map of sheaves of rings $f^{\#}\colon \mathcal{O}_Y\longrightarrow f_{*}\mathcal{O}_X$.

Think of $f^{\#}$ as the rule "pull back regular functions." For every open $V\subseteq Y$ we get a ring homomorphism $f^{\#}_V\colon \mathcal{O}_Y(V)\to \mathcal{O}_X(f^{-1}V)$ compatible with restriction — that's what a map of sheaves is.

But there is a crucial extra condition: $f^{\#}$ must be local on stalks. For every point $x\in X$ with image $y=f(x)$, the induced map on stalks $$f^{\#}_x\colon \mathcal{O}_{Y,y}\longrightarrow \mathcal{O}_{X,x}$$ must be a local ring homomorphism: it sends the maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_y$ into $\mathfrak{m}_x$. Equivalently, $f^{\#}_x$ pulls back "functions vanishing at $y$" to "functions vanishing at $x$."

Why insist on this? Without it one gets maps that are algebraically nonsense. Consider $X=Y=\Spec{}k[t]_{(t)}$ (the local ring at the origin of the affine line). As topological spaces both are two-point sets $\{\text{generic},\text{closed}\}$. The identity continuous map is fine; the sheaf map, however, has exactly two choices of ring endomorphism: the identity $k[t]_{(t)}\to k[t]_{(t)}$, and the inclusion $k[t]_{(t)}\hookrightarrow \operatorname{Frac}(k[t]_{(t)})=k(t)$ followed by… wait, that lands in a different ring. The real test case is $\Spec k(t)\to \Spec k[t]_{(t)}$: there is a ring map $k[t]_{(t)}\hookrightarrow k(t)$ (localization), and it is not local — it sends $t\in\mathfrak{m}$ to a unit. Geometrically, the generic point of the local line is being mapped to the generic point of the same line; the associated underlying continuous map sends the one point of $\Spec k(t)$ to the generic point, and the stalk map is $k[t]_{(t)}\to k(t)$ which is not local. And that's fine — the underlying continuous map chose the generic fiber, so the condition "send $\mathfrak{m}_{\text{gen}}=(0)$ into $\mathfrak{m}_{\text{gen}}=(0)$" is vacuous. Locality is automatic when the target point is generic; it bites when we try to map a closed point to a non-closed point.

The moral: locality enforces that the geometric picture of where points go agrees with the algebraic pullback of "functions vanishing at a point." If you pick the continuous map $f$ on underlying spaces, the sheaf data $f^{\#}$ is in most cases then forced (up to some sheafy flexibility), and locality is the condition that makes it consistent with $f$.

Here is the test that's closer to the point. Suppose we have two different local rings at two different points of some scheme: $\mathcal{O}_{Y,y}$ is a DVR with uniformizer $t_y$, and $\mathcal{O}_{X,x}$ is a DVR with uniformizer $t_x$. Any ring map $\mathcal{O}_{Y,y}\to \mathcal{O}_{X,x}$ sends units to units, but could in principle send $t_y$ anywhere. Locality says $t_y\mapsto$ (something in $\mathfrak{m}_x$) — i.e., $t_y\mapsto u t_x^e$ for some unit $u$ and integer $e\geq 1$. That integer $e$ is the ramification index. If $e=0$ (i.e. $t_y\mapsto$ a unit) then the stalk map is non-local and the corresponding map of schemes is not a morphism in our sense.

So from the very beginning, the locality axiom is what allows us to define things like ramification. Nothing else about $f^{\#}$ cares.

Formal definition. The category $\mathsf{Sch}$ has schemes as objects and the above pairs $(f,f^{\#})$ as morphisms, with composition $(g,g^{\#})\circ(f,f^{\#}) = (g\circ f,\; f^{\#}\circ f_{*}g^{\#})$. This is a full subcategory of the category of locally ringed spaces.

Almost everything else on this page is an unpacking of a single slogan:

geometry on schemes ↔ commutative algebra on rings,

implemented by the anti-equivalence between affine schemes and commutative rings that we turn to next.

Why "locally ringed"? A ringed space is just a space equipped with a sheaf of rings. A locally ringed space is a ringed space in which every stalk is a local ring. Schemes are locally ringed spaces whose opens are covered by affines. Morphisms of locally ringed spaces are precisely what we described: continuous maps plus sheaf maps that are local on stalks. The entire category of schemes is a full subcategory of locally ringed spaces — a fact that is very useful for extending structural results from topology or differential geometry.

One more piece of intuition before we dive into affines. The slogan "points of $X$ correspond to maps into $X$" has a useful partial refinement: for any scheme $X$ and any field $k$, the $k$-rational points of $X$ are the morphisms $\Spec k\to X$. These form a set $X(k) = \Hom_{\mathsf{Sch}}(\Spec k, X)$. The functor $k\mapsto X(k)$ (valued in sets) is called the functor of points of $X$, and by Yoneda's lemma it determines $X$ up to unique isomorphism. Whenever we specialize the capstone family to a specific value of $a_0\in k$ and ask for roots $x$ of $x^2 = a_0$ in $k$, we're computing $X(k)$ at that specialization.

2. The affine case: morphisms are ring maps (backwards)

Let $A,B$ be commutative rings. The fundamental theorem of affine schemes is:

$\Hom_{\mathsf{Sch}}(\Spec B,\; \Spec A) \;=\; \Hom_{\mathsf{Ring}}(A,\; B)$.

Given a ring homomorphism $\varphi\colon A\to B$, the associated morphism of schemes $\Spec(\varphi)\colon \Spec B\to \Spec A$ is defined on points by contraction of prime ideals: $$\Spec(\varphi)\colon \mathfrak{q}\longmapsto \varphi^{-1}(\mathfrak{q}).$$ (That $\varphi^{-1}$ of a prime is a prime is a one-line check: if $\varphi(xy)\in\mathfrak{q}$ then $xy\in\varphi^{-1}\mathfrak{q}$, and use that $\mathfrak{q}$ is prime.)

On sheaves, the map $\varphi$ is literally the sheaf map, because $\mathcal{O}_{\Spec A}(D(a))=A[a^{-1}]$ and $\varphi$ extends to localizations. On the stalk at $\mathfrak{q}\in\Spec B$ the stalk map is $\varphi_{\mathfrak{q}}\colon A_{\varphi^{-1}\mathfrak{q}}\to B_{\mathfrak{q}}$, which is automatically local: the preimage of $\mathfrak{q}B_{\mathfrak{q}}$ is $\varphi^{-1}\mathfrak{q}\cdot A_{\varphi^{-1}\mathfrak{q}}$. So the affine side just works.

The inverse direction — given a morphism of schemes, recover the ring map — is "take global sections": $\varphi = f^{\#}(\Spec A)\colon A\to B$. A bit of bookkeeping verifies this is a two-sided inverse.

Two sanity checks that the sign conventions are correct:

  • If $A = \mathbb{Z}$ (the initial object in $\mathsf{Ring}$), then $\Spec\mathbb{Z}$ is the terminal object in $\mathsf{Sch}$: every scheme has a unique map to it. That's why we always work "over $\Spec\mathbb{Z}$" — it's the empty base choice.
  • If $A = k$ is a field, then $\Spec k$ is a one-point scheme. A $k$-rational point of a scheme $X$ is a map $\Spec k \to X$, which by the affine anti-equivalence (locally) is a ring map $\mathcal{O}_X(U)\to k$ — i.e., a maximal ideal with residue field $k$.
Consequence. The functor $\Spec\colon \mathsf{Ring}^{\mathrm{op}}\to \mathsf{Sch}$ is fully faithful. Affine schemes and commutative rings are the same, with arrows reversed.

Geometric intuition for $\Spec(\varphi)\colon \mathfrak{q}\mapsto \varphi^{-1}\mathfrak{q}$:

  • If $\varphi\colon A\hookrightarrow B$ is an inclusion (e.g., $\mathbb{Z}\hookrightarrow \mathbb{Q}$), then $\Spec(\varphi)$ sends every prime of $B$ to its "trace" in $A$.
  • If $\varphi\colon A\twoheadrightarrow A/I$ is a quotient, then $\Spec(\varphi)$ is the closed immersion $V(I)\hookrightarrow \Spec A$ — primes of $A/I$ correspond to primes of $A$ containing $I$, which is the closed subset $V(I)$.
  • If $\varphi$ is the structure map $\mathbb{Z}\to R$ (unique!), then $\Spec(\varphi)\colon \Spec R\to\Spec\mathbb{Z}$ records the "characteristic behavior" of $R$ — which primes of $\mathbb{Z}$ sit under primes of $R$.

3. Immersions

Three kinds of morphism should feel like "subobject inclusions," and in $\mathsf{Sch}$ they're packaged as immersions.

Open immersions

A morphism $j\colon U\hookrightarrow X$ is an open immersion if $j$ factors as a homeomorphism of $U$ onto an open subset of $X$, and $j^{\#}$ identifies $\mathcal{O}_U$ with $\mathcal{O}_X|_U$. The standard affine example: for $f\in A$, the inclusion $$\Spec(A_f) \;\hookrightarrow\; \Spec A$$ is an open immersion onto the basic open $D(f)=\{\mathfrak{p} : f\notin\mathfrak{p}\}$. The ring map is the localization $A\to A_f$, and one checks that primes of $A_f$ correspond exactly to primes of $A$ not containing $f$.

Closed immersions

A morphism $i\colon Z\hookrightarrow X$ is a closed immersion if it's a homeomorphism of $Z$ onto a closed subset, and $i^{\#}\colon \mathcal{O}_X\to i_{*}\mathcal{O}_Z$ is surjective. The kernel $\mathcal{I}=\ker i^{\#}$ is a quasi-coherent ideal sheaf on $X$; conversely, any such ideal sheaf $\mathcal{I}\subseteq \mathcal{O}_X$ cuts out a closed immersion $V(\mathcal{I})\hookrightarrow X$. Affine-locally: given $A$ and an ideal $I\subseteq A$, the ring surjection $A\twoheadrightarrow A/I$ induces $$\Spec(A/I) \;\hookrightarrow\; \Spec A,$$ a homeomorphism onto $V(I)=\{\mathfrak{p}\supseteq I\}$.

Two ideal sheaves can cut out the same topological closed subset yet give different closed subschemes. $\Spec(k[x]/(x))\hookrightarrow \mathbb{A}^1_k$ and $\Spec(k[x]/(x^2))\hookrightarrow \mathbb{A}^1_k$ have the same image $\{0\}$ but different structure sheaves; the second is non-reduced. A closed immersion is really the pair (topological inclusion, surjection of structure sheaves), and the sheaf map is the interesting part.

Locally closed immersions

A locally closed immersion is the composition of a closed immersion followed by an open immersion (equivalently, an open followed by a closed). Equivalently, the image is locally closed in $X$: the intersection of an open and a closed subset.

One useful operational fact: a morphism $f\colon X\to Y$ factors uniquely through its scheme-theoretic image, the smallest closed subscheme of $Y$ through which $f$ factors. Affine-locally this corresponds to the quotient by $\ker(A\to B)$, where $\varphi\colon A\to B$ is the ring map.

Immersions are the "monomorphism-like" maps of scheme theory, though the category-theoretic monomorphisms in $\mathsf{Sch}$ are a bit more delicate. For the gluing and fiber-product arguments below, just keep the pictures in mind: open immersion = cut out a neighborhood; closed immersion = enforce vanishing relations.

A worked example

Consider the node curve $X = \Spec\mathbb{Q}[x,y]/(y^2-x^2(x+1))\subset\mathbb{A}^2_{\mathbb{Q}}$. It has a singular point at the origin. One standard move is to resolve the singularity by a blow-up; a more elementary observation is to look at two immersions:

  • The closed immersion $\{(0,0)\}\hookrightarrow X$, corresponding to the quotient $\mathbb{Q}[x,y]/(y^2-x^2(x+1))\twoheadrightarrow \mathbb{Q}$ killing $x$ and $y$.
  • The open immersion $X\setminus\{(0,0)\}\hookrightarrow X$, corresponding to the localization at the maximal ideal's complement. As a scheme, $X\setminus\{(0,0)\}$ is smooth and is a union of two affine opens given by inverting $x$ and $y$.

These two immersions cover $X$ in the sense that their images are complementary. Many structural theorems about schemes are proved by separately analyzing an open dense subset and its closed complement — a flavor of decomposition that becomes much more flexible in the scheme category than in the classical-variety category.

4. Fiber products

Given two morphisms $f\colon X\to S$ and $g\colon Y\to S$ to a common base $S$, the fiber product (or pullback) $X\times_S Y$ is a scheme with two projections $p\colon X\times_S Y\to X$ and $q\colon X\times_S Y \to Y$ making the square commute, universal for that property:

The fiber product square
pick an example · corners update to a concrete instance

Universal property: for any scheme $T$ with morphisms $\alpha\colon T\to X$ and $\beta\colon T\to Y$ such that $f\alpha = g\beta$, there is a unique morphism $T\to X\times_S Y$ making the two sub-triangles commute.

Existence — affine case

If $X=\Spec A$, $Y=\Spec B$, and $S=\Spec R$, then $$X\times_S Y \;=\; \Spec(A\otimes_R B),$$ where $A$ and $B$ are viewed as $R$-algebras via the maps $R\to A$ and $R\to B$ coming from $f$ and $g$. This just says: pushouts of commutative rings are tensor products, and Spec turns pushouts into pullbacks (because Spec is contravariant and preserves all limits it can).

The two projections are induced by $A\to A\otimes_R B$, $a\mapsto a\otimes 1$ and $B\to A\otimes_R B$, $b\mapsto 1\otimes b$. The universal property of the fiber product translates verbatim into the universal property of the tensor product of $R$-algebras.

Existence — general case

For arbitrary schemes one glues. Pick affine open covers $X=\bigcup U_i$, $Y=\bigcup V_j$, $S=\bigcup W_k$ with $f(U_i), g(V_j)\subseteq W_k$ compatibly. Form each affine piece $U_i\times_{W_k}V_j$ via tensor product, and verify the pieces glue along their overlaps using the universal property. The classical reference is Hartshorne II.3.3 or Stacks Project tag 01JO; the bookkeeping is long but the key move is exactly the affine case above.

Slogan. In affine-land, fiber product = tensor product. Everything else is gluing.

Some consequences that one derives from the universal property alone:

  • Symmetry: $X\times_S Y \cong Y\times_S X$.
  • Associativity: $(X\times_S Y)\times_S Z \cong X\times_S (Y\times_S Z)$ canonically.
  • Unit: $X\times_S S \cong X$.
  • Compatibility with open/closed immersions: if $Y\hookrightarrow S$ is a closed (resp. open) immersion, so is $X\times_S Y \hookrightarrow X$.

A subtle pitfall: the underlying set of $X\times_S Y$ is not in general the set-theoretic fiber product of $|X|$ and $|Y|$ over $|S|$. It's typically larger, because a pair $(x,y)$ with $f(x) = g(y) = s$ can have several preimages — one for each prime of $\kappa(x)\otimes_{\kappa(s)}\kappa(y)$. The set-theoretic fibered-set is a quotient of the scheme-theoretic one.

Explicit example: $\Spec\mathbb{F}_4 \times_{\Spec\mathbb{F}_2} \Spec\mathbb{F}_4 = \Spec(\mathbb{F}_4\otimes_{\mathbb{F}_2}\mathbb{F}_4) = \Spec(\mathbb{F}_4\times\mathbb{F}_4)$, which has two points — even though each factor had only one point and the base also had only one. Galois of $\mathbb{F}_4/\mathbb{F}_2$ is the source of the doubling.

5. Base change

A fiber product comes into its own when one of the two legs is viewed as "the family" and the other as "the base we're changing to." Given $X\to S$ and a map $S'\to S$, the base change of $X$ to $S'$ is

$X_{S'} \;:=\; X\times_S S'$,

with its projection $X_{S'}\to S'$. This construction is functorial in both arguments: a morphism $X\to X'$ of $S$-schemes induces $X_{S'}\to X'_{S'}$, and a composition $S''\to S'\to S$ gives $X_{S''} = (X_{S'})_{S''}$.

Three pictures of what base change does:

  • Change of coefficients. If $S=\Spec k$ and $S'=\Spec k'$ is a larger field, then $X_{k'} = X\times_k \Spec k'$ is "the same $X$ but with coordinates in $k'$." E.g. $\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{Q}}$ base-changed to $\Spec\mathbb{C}$ is $\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}$. A variety's geometry comes out differently over bigger fields — this is how you pass from $\mathbb{Q}$-rational data to geometric data.
  • Restriction to a subscheme. If $S'\hookrightarrow S$ is a closed immersion, $X_{S'}$ is the preimage (scheme-theoretically) of $S'$ under $X\to S$. It is a closed subscheme of $X$.
  • Fiber at a point. If $S' = \Spec\kappa(s)$ where $\kappa(s)$ is the residue field at a point $s\in S$, then $X_{S'}$ is the scheme-theoretic fiber of $X\to S$ over $s$.

Base change commutes with itself in sensible ways: if $Y\to S$ is another $S$-scheme, $$(X\times_S Y)_{S'} \;=\; X_{S'} \times_{S'} Y_{S'}.$$ This is just functoriality of the tensor product.

Many properties of morphisms are preserved by base change — closed immersion, open immersion, surjectivity, flatness, smoothness, and so on. Others (like "being a bijection on points") fail spectacularly after base change. A large chunk of scheme theory is classifying which properties survive, and much of EGA IV is devoted to exactly that bookkeeping.

Flatness is the technical heart of the subject: a morphism $f\colon X\to S$ is flat if $\mathcal{O}_X$ is a flat $\mathcal{O}_S$-module on stalks. Flatness says "the fibers vary continuously": fiber dimension, length, intersection numbers, are constant on connected components of $S$. The capstone example below is flat (free of rank $2$), which is why the fibers are all length $2$.

Three small base-change examples

  • Complexification of a real variety. Let $X = \Spec\mathbb{R}[x,y]/(x^2+y^2-1)$ — the real circle. Base-change to $\Spec\mathbb{C}$: $X_{\mathbb{C}} = \Spec\mathbb{C}[x,y]/(x^2+y^2-1)$, isomorphic (via $x+iy\mapsto u$, $x-iy\mapsto v$) to $\Spec\mathbb{C}[u,v]/(uv-1) = \Spec\mathbb{C}[u,u^{-1}] = \mathbb{G}_{m,\mathbb{C}}$. The real circle is not $\mathbb{G}_m$, but its complexification is. Base change exposes structure.
  • Reduction mod $p$. Let $X = \Spec\mathbb{Z}[x,y]/(y^2-x^3-x)$, an elliptic curve model over $\mathbb{Z}$. Base-change to $\Spec\mathbb{F}_p$: $X_{\mathbb{F}_p} = \Spec\mathbb{F}_p[x,y]/(y^2-x^3-x)$, the reduction mod $p$. This fails to be smooth exactly when the discriminant $-4\cdot(-1)^3 - 27\cdot 0^2 = 4$ vanishes mod $p$ — i.e., at $p = 2$. Bad reduction at primes dividing a discriminant is a structural fact recorded by base change.
  • Generic fiber. For any $X\to \Spec\mathbb{Z}$, the generic fiber $X_{\mathbb{Q}} = X\times_{\mathbb{Z}}\Spec\mathbb{Q}$ is a scheme over $\mathbb{Q}$ that carries the "geometry," while closed fibers $X_{\mathbb{F}_p}$ carry the "arithmetic." The interplay between them is the business of arithmetic geometry.

6. Fibers of a morphism

For a morphism $f\colon X\to Y$ and a point $y\in |Y|$, the scheme-theoretic fiber is $$X_y \;:=\; X\times_Y \Spec\kappa(y),$$ where $\kappa(y) = \mathcal{O}_{Y,y}/\mathfrak{m}_y$ is the residue field at $y$ and $\Spec\kappa(y)\to Y$ is the canonical map (the composition $\Spec\kappa(y)\hookrightarrow \Spec\mathcal{O}_{Y,y}\to Y$). The underlying topological space of $X_y$ is the (set-theoretic) fiber $f^{-1}(y)$, but the scheme structure keeps track of much more.

Concretely, in the affine case $f\colon \Spec B\to \Spec A$ with ring map $\varphi\colon A\to B$, and $y=\mathfrak{p}\subset A$, the residue field is $\kappa(\mathfrak{p}) = \operatorname{Frac}(A/\mathfrak{p})$, and the fiber is $$X_y \;=\; \Spec\bigl( B\otimes_A \kappa(\mathfrak{p}) \bigr) \;=\; \Spec\bigl( B_{\mathfrak{p}} / \mathfrak{p}B_{\mathfrak{p}} \bigr).$$ It's a scheme over the field $\kappa(\mathfrak{p})$ — possibly with non-reduced structure, possibly disconnected, possibly a single point with a big residue field extension.

Scheme-theoretic vs. geometric fiber. If $\bar\kappa(y)$ is an algebraic closure, the geometric fiber $X_{\bar y} = X\times_Y \Spec\bar\kappa(y)$ further base-changes. The scheme-theoretic fiber remembers the arithmetic of $\kappa(y)$; the geometric fiber throws it away. A separable extension $\kappa(y)\subseteq \bar\kappa(y)$ can split a degree-$d$ residue field extension into $d$ distinct geometric points.

Fibers are what turn a morphism into a "family of schemes parameterized by $Y$." The family jumps in character as $y$ moves through $Y$: generically the fibers look one way, at special points they degenerate. Understanding that jumping behavior is a lot of what scheme theory is good for.

Small vocabulary for what can happen in a fiber:

  • Reduction. The fiber may fail to be reduced — some element of its structure ring is nilpotent. Example: fiber of $\Spec k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)\to \Spec k$ is $\Spec k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$ itself, a fat point.
  • Reducibility. The fiber may split into several connected components. Example: $\Spec\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt 2)\otimes_{\mathbb{Q}}\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt 2) \cong \Spec(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt 2)\times \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt 2))$ — two points.
  • Residue-field extension. A fiber may be a single point, but with a residue field strictly larger than $\kappa(y)$. Example: the unique prime of $\mathbb{F}_p[x]/(f(x))$ for $f$ irreducible of degree $d$ has residue field $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$.
  • Empty fiber. Can happen! If no prime of $B$ contracts to $y$. Example: $\Spec\mathbb{Q}\to\Spec\mathbb{Z}$ has empty fiber over every closed point of $\Spec\mathbb{Z}$.

A single morphism can exhibit all four phenomena at different points of its base. The capstone of this page (next section) does exactly that in a one-parameter family — reducibility at $a$ a nonzero square, residue-field extension at $a$ a nonsquare, non-reduced-ness at $a=0$, over one giant 2D base.

7. Capstone: the family $x^2 - a$

Time to put it all together. Consider the morphism $$f\colon \Spec\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2 - a) \;\longrightarrow\; \Spec\mathbb{Z}[a],$$ induced by the ring inclusion $\mathbb{Z}[a] \hookrightarrow \mathbb{Z}[a,x]/(x^2-a) = \mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2-a)$ (where the second equality is the observation that in the quotient, $a$ is redundantly encoded as $x^2$, so we may as well eliminate it — but the map from $\mathbb{Z}[a]$ to this quotient is what it is). The total space is a hypersurface in $\mathbb{A}^2_{\mathbb{Z}}$ (with coordinates $a$ and $x$), and we're projecting to the $a$-axis.

This is a beautiful toy because every phenomenon — reduction, reducibility, ramification — shows up in a one-parameter family.

The base: $\Spec\mathbb{Z}[a]$

$\mathbb{Z}[a]$ is a polynomial ring in one variable over $\mathbb{Z}$. Its spectrum is a 2-dimensional arithmetic surface. Closed points have the form $(p,\,g(a))$ for $p$ a prime and $g\in\mathbb{Z}[a]$ irreducible modulo $p$. The closed points most useful for us are $(p, a-a_0)$ for integers $a_0$: "the point where $a=a_0$ in characteristic $p$."

Height-one primes include:

  • Horizontal primes $(g(a))$ for $g$ irreducible primitive in $\mathbb{Z}[a]$ — e.g. $(a), (a-1), (a^2-2)$. Geometrically, a horizontal curve over $\Spec\mathbb{Z}$.
  • Vertical primes $(p)$ for $p$ a rational prime. Geometrically, a whole $\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{F}_p}$ sitting inside.

The generic point is $(0)$ — its residue field is $\mathbb{Q}(a)$.

Fibers over specialization $a = a_0$

Fix an integer $a_0$ and consider the generic point of the horizontal prime $(a-a_0)$. Its residue field is $\mathbb{Q}$ (generic fiber of $V(a-a_0) = \Spec\mathbb{Z} \hookrightarrow \Spec\mathbb{Z}[a]$ over the generic point of $\Spec\mathbb{Z}$). The fiber of $f$ over this generic point of $V(a-a_0)$ is $$\Spec\Bigl(\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2-a) \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}[a]} \mathbb{Q}\Bigr) \;=\; \Spec\bigl(\mathbb{Q}[x]/(x^2-a_0)\bigr),$$ where $\mathbb{Q}$ is $\mathbb{Z}[a]$-algebra via $a\mapsto a_0$.

Three cases, keyed by the discriminant $\Delta = 4a_0$:

  • $a_0$ a nonzero square in $\mathbb{Q}$ (e.g. $a_0 = 1, 4, 9$): $x^2-a_0 = (x-\sqrt{a_0})(x+\sqrt{a_0})$ splits, and the fiber is $\Spec\mathbb{Q}\sqcup \Spec\mathbb{Q}$ — two distinct $\mathbb{Q}$-rational points.
  • $a_0$ a nonsquare, $a_0\neq 0$ (e.g. $a_0 = 2, 3, 5, -1$): $x^2-a_0$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$, so the fiber is $\Spec\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_0})$ — a single closed point with residue field a degree-$2$ number field.
  • $a_0 = 0$: $x^2-0 = x^2$ is a perfect square, and the fiber is $\Spec\mathbb{Q}[x]/(x^2)$ — a single non-reduced point of length $2$. This is the ramification point of the family over $\mathbb{Q}$: a fat doubled point where the two branches collide.
The discriminant $\Delta = 4a_0$ vanishes precisely at $a_0 = 0$, which is exactly where the fiber becomes non-reduced. More generally, for a finite flat family of degree $n$, the locus where the fiber is non-reduced is cut out by the vanishing of the discriminant (viewed as a function on the base). That's the branch locus.

Fibers over characteristic $p$

Now fix a prime $p$ and a residue $a_0\in\mathbb{F}_p$. The closed point $(p, a-a_0)\in \Spec\mathbb{Z}[a]$ has residue field $\mathbb{F}_p$, and the fiber of $f$ is $$\Spec\bigl(\mathbb{F}_p[x]/(x^2-a_0)\bigr).$$

  • $p$ odd, $a_0$ a nonzero square mod $p$: $x^2-a_0 = (x-r)(x+r)$ in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$, two distinct $\mathbb{F}_p$-points.
  • $p$ odd, $a_0$ a nonsquare mod $p$: $x^2-a_0$ is irreducible, fiber is $\Spec\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$ — one point with residue field $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$.
  • $p$ odd, $a_0\equiv 0\pmod p$ (i.e., $p\mid a_0$): $x^2$ in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$, so the fiber is $\Spec\mathbb{F}_p[x]/(x^2)$ — fat point. Ramification.
  • $p = 2$, any $a_0$: in $\mathbb{F}_2[x]$, $x^2 - a_0 = x^2 + a_0 = (x + \sqrt{a_0})^2$ because squaring is Frobenius, a ring endomorphism of $\mathbb{F}_2$. If $a_0\equiv 0$, it's $x^2$; if $a_0\equiv 1$, it's $(x+1)^2$. Either way — always a fat point. The family is wildly ramified everywhere over $p=2$, because the discriminant $4a_0$ is always $\equiv 0 \pmod 2$.
Ramification locus. $f$ is ramified at $(p, a-a_0)$ precisely when $p\mid \Delta = 4a_0$. Rationally this means $p = 2$ or $p\mid a_0$. The ramification divisor of the family inside $\Spec\mathbb{Z}[a]$ is thus $V(4a) = V(2) \cup V(a)$ — the "characteristic-2 line" and the "$a=0$ line."

Reading the family globally

Putting the above together, the morphism $f\colon \Spec\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2-a)\to \Spec\mathbb{Z}[a]$ is:

  • Finite flat of degree $2$: every fiber has length $2$ (count multiplicities). This is because $\mathbb{Z}[a,x]/(x^2-a)$ is a free $\mathbb{Z}[a]$-module with basis $\{1,x\}$.
  • Étale away from the branch locus $V(4a)\subset \Spec\mathbb{Z}[a]$. Away from $a=0$ and away from characteristic $2$, fibers are étale: either two distinct points (split) or one point with a separable residue-field extension (inert).
  • Ramified on $V(4a) = V(2)\cup V(a)$. Over the "line $p=2$" the family is wildly ramified; over the "line $a=0$" it is tamely ramified (in odd characteristic) or part of the wild locus (at $(2,a)$, the doubly-ramified point).

The analogous classical picture in characteristic $0$ is a double cover $\mathbb{A}^1 \to \mathbb{A}^1$ given by $x\mapsto x^2 = a$ — a parabola projecting to the $a$-axis, branched at the origin. Adding arithmetic (the $\Spec\mathbb{Z}$ direction) makes the picture two-dimensional and adds ramification along $p = 2$: the arithmetic branch locus is the union of the classical one with a characteristic-$2$ stripe.

One way to see the total space is as follows. $\mathbb{Z}[a,x]/(x^2-a) \cong \mathbb{Z}[x]$ because $a$ is determined by $x$ (via $a = x^2$). So the total space $X = \Spec\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2-a) \cong \mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{Z}}$, and the morphism $f\colon X\to \Spec\mathbb{Z}[a]$ is the squaring map $\Spec\mathbb{Z}[x]\to \Spec\mathbb{Z}[a]$ sending $x\mapsto a\cdot (\text{coord. on base})$. Concretely, $f$ is induced by the ring map $\mathbb{Z}[a]\to \mathbb{Z}[x]$ sending $a\mapsto x^2$. The total space is smooth (it's $\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{Z}}$), but the morphism is ramified — a perfectly normal situation for a branched cover.

This is worth pausing on. Ramification is a property of the morphism, not of the total or base space alone. The map $\mathbb{A}^1\to \mathbb{A}^1, x\mapsto x^2$ has everything smooth; the ramification lives entirely in the failure of $f$ to be étale. The differential $df = 2x\,dx$ vanishes exactly at $x = 0$ — the single ramification point on the total space, with image $a = 0$ on the base. The $2$ in $2x\,dx$ is the reason ramification spreads to characteristic $2$: in that characteristic, $df = 0$ everywhere.

Why $p=2$ is special. The discriminant of $x^2-a$ is $-4a$. Two primes can divide it: any prime dividing $a$, and the prime $2$ (always). Over $\mathbb{Z}$ this is a general feature — "the prime $2$ is a ramified prime of $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{a}]$ unless $a\equiv 1\pmod 4$," a classical number-theoretic fact here visible as the ramification of a morphism of schemes.

Geometric versus scheme-theoretic fiber

One last subtlety, visible in the $p = 3, a_0 = 2$ case. The scheme-theoretic fiber is $\Spec\mathbb{F}_9$ — a single point with residue field $\mathbb{F}_9$, degree $2$ over $\mathbb{F}_3$. The geometric fiber — base-change to $\overline{\mathbb{F}_3}$ — is $\Spec(\mathbb{F}_9\otimes_{\mathbb{F}_3}\overline{\mathbb{F}_3}) = \overline{\mathbb{F}_3}\sqcup\overline{\mathbb{F}_3}$, two separate geometric points. The inertness is an arithmetic phenomenon: geometrically the two roots exist, but they are swapped by the Galois action $\Gal(\mathbb{F}_9/\mathbb{F}_3)$ and so cannot be distinguished over the small field $\mathbb{F}_3$.

This is a concrete instance of the general principle: scheme-theoretic fibers see the arithmetic of the residue field; geometric fibers forget it. Galois groups mediate between the two.

Counting fiber lengths

A useful sanity check on every family: the sum of residue field degrees, weighted by multiplicity, over a fiber should equal the degree of the total morphism. For $f\colon \Spec\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2-a)\to \Spec\mathbb{Z}[a]$, that degree is $2$, and indeed in every case above the fiber sums to $2$:

  • Split case: two closed points, each of degree $1$ over the base residue field. $1+1 = 2$.
  • Inert case: one closed point of degree $2$. Multiplicity $1\cdot 2 = 2$.
  • Fat case: one closed point of degree $1$, multiplicity $2$. $1\cdot 2 = 2$.

That this holds identically across the base is the precise meaning of "flat family of degree $2$." The three classical cases — split, inert, ramified — are the three partitions of $2$: $2 = 1+1$ (split), $2 = 2$ with degree $2$ (inert), $2 = 2$ with multiplicity $2$ (ramified). In a degree-$n$ family there would be an entire partition-of-$n$ worth of cases, one for each conjugacy class of the Galois group of the generic fiber (after tame adjustments).

Relation to Dedekind's theorem

For $n\in\mathbb{Z}$ squarefree and $\neq 1\pmod 4$, the ring of integers of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{n})$ is $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{n}] = \mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2-n)$. The fiber of $\Spec\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{n}]\to\Spec\mathbb{Z}$ over a prime $p$ is exactly $\Spec\mathbb{F}_p[x]/(x^2-n)$ — a special case of the capstone with $a_0 = n$ fixed. So Dedekind's "splitting theorem" for quadratic extensions — $p$ splits iff $n$ is a nonzero QR mod $p$, is inert iff a non-residue, ramifies iff $p\mid n$ (or $p = 2$ with $n \equiv 2,3\pmod 4$) — is a statement about fibers of this morphism. The capstone visualizes this splitting law on a slider.

8. Separated and proper morphisms

Once one has fiber products, most of the useful adjectives of scheme theory are defined by pulling some condition back through a diagonal or base-change square. The two adjectives every algebraic geometer meets first — and the two that the coda below pointed to as gaps — are separated and proper.

The diagonal and separatedness

For a morphism $f\colon X\to S$ there is a canonical diagonal $$\Delta_{f}\colon X \;\longrightarrow\; X\times_S X, \qquad x\mapsto (x,x).$$ It is always a locally closed immersion. The morphism $f$ is separated iff $\Delta_f$ is a closed immersion. Equivalently, the image of $\Delta_f$ is Zariski-closed inside $X\times_S X$.

This is the scheme-theoretic analog of the Hausdorff condition: in a Hausdorff space, the diagonal is closed inside the product. The prototype non-separated scheme is the line with doubled origin, obtained by gluing two copies of $\mathbb{A}^1_k$ along the open $\mathbb{A}^1_k\setminus\{0\}$. The two origins converge to each other, just like two limits of the same sequence in a non-Hausdorff topological space. Its diagonal fails to be closed at the pair "(left origin, right origin)."

All affine morphisms are separated: the diagonal map $A\otimes_R A\twoheadrightarrow A,\ a\otimes b\mapsto ab$ is surjective, so $\Spec(A)\to\Spec(A)\times_{\Spec(R)}\Spec(A)$ is a closed immersion. In practice, almost every scheme you meet — affine varieties, projective space, any quasi-projective scheme — is separated. The doubled-origin line is a useful warning, not a real obstacle.

Properness: compactness relative to a base

A morphism $f\colon X\to S$ is proper if it is separated, of finite type, and universally closed — meaning for every base change $S'\to S$, the induced map $X\times_S S'\to S'$ sends closed sets to closed sets. Universal closedness is the scheme-theoretic cousin of properness in topology: preimages of compact sets are compact after arbitrary base change.

The universal closure condition is equivalent (for Noetherian targets) to the valuative criterion of properness: for every discrete valuation ring $R$ with fraction field $K$, every diagram $$\begin{array}{ccc}\Spec K & \longrightarrow & X \\ \downarrow & & \downarrow f \\ \Spec R & \longrightarrow & S\end{array}$$ admits a unique lift $\Spec R\to X$. "Points defined generically extend uniquely across a boundary point" — exactly what compactness should mean for schemes.

The canonical example is projective space: $\mathbb{P}^n_k\to\Spec k$ is proper. Affine space $\mathbb{A}^n_k\to\Spec k$ is not proper, since the valuative criterion fails: a $K$-point like $1/t\in\mathbb{A}^1_K$ does not extend to a $R$-point of $\mathbb{A}^1$. You have to projectivize before properness kicks in.

Stability properties

Both properties are well-behaved under the natural operations of scheme theory:

In particular, "proper over a field" is stable under all the constructions of the preceding sections. Moduli spaces want to be proper (so that limits of families exist), and modern algebraic geometry is largely about finding the right proper compactification of a given open moduli problem — e.g., $\overline{M}_{g,n}$ is the properification of $M_{g,n}$ by adding stable nodal curves, exactly so that the moduli functor extends across the boundary.

Slogan. Separated replaces Hausdorff; proper replaces compact-plus-Hausdorff. The valuative criterion is the scheme-theoretic incarnation of "a sequence has at most one limit, and always has one." Together they pin down what "a family without escape routes to infinity" means for schemes.

9. Coda

Morphisms and fiber products form the verbs-and-grammar of scheme theory. With them, one can talk about:

  • Properties relative to a base. A morphism $f\colon X\to S$ is proper, separated, smooth, étale, finite, affine, … — each one a condition on $f$ that is stable under base change. These are the adjectives you'll want to pin down next.
  • Representability. Many functors "point to schemes of things" — Hilbert schemes, moduli spaces — are constructed by gluing representable fiber-product pieces. The universal property of fiber products is literally the engine.
  • Descent. Given a property that holds after base change $S'\to S$, when does it descend to $S$? For faithfully flat maps (fpqc, fppf) the answer is often "yes" — a story that eats up a graduate seminar.

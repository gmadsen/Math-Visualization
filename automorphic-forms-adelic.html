Automorphic forms, adelically

A classical modular form $f \in M_k(\Gamma_0(N))$ is a function on $\mathbb{H}$ with an $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$-equivariance. Reading the same data on the adelic group $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q})$ unifies every prime $p$ and the archimedean place into a single object, makes the Hecke action local at each prime, and turns $L$-functions into a global Euler product over places. This is the entry door to the Langlands programme.

1. $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q})$ as a restricted product

Recall that the adèle ring $\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{R} \times {\prod_p}' \mathbb{Q}_p$ is the restricted direct product over all places of $\mathbb{Q}$, with the restriction $x_p \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ for almost every prime. Apply the matrix-group functor $\mathrm{GL}_2$ pointwise: $$\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}) \;=\; {\prod_v}' \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_v) \;=\; \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R}) \times {\prod_p}' \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_p).$$ The restriction at each finite place is membership in the compact open subgroup $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p) \subset \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_p)$: $g_p \in \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p)$ for all but finitely many $p$. Equivalently, $g \in \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q})$ if and only if $\det g \in \mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}^\times$ and $g_p$ has entries in $\mathbb{Z}_p$ with $|\det g_p|_p = 1$ for almost every $p$.

Two factor split. A more practical decomposition for downstream use is $$\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}) \;=\; \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R}) \;\times\; \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_f), \qquad \mathbb{A}_f := {\prod_p}' \mathbb{Q}_p,$$ separating the archimedean factor from the finite-adelic factor. The classical theory lives entirely on the $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ side; the level / Hecke / arithmetic data lives on the $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_f)$ side.

The standard maximal compact

The compact open subgroup $$K \;=\; \mathrm{O}_2(\mathbb{R}) \;\times\; \mathrm{GL}_2(\hat{\mathbb{Z}}) \;\subset\; \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}), \qquad \hat{\mathbb{Z}} \;=\; \prod_p \mathbb{Z}_p,$$ is the standard maximal compact. Inside the finite part, the relevant level-$N$ subgroup is $$K_0(N) \;=\; \Bigl\{\, g \in \mathrm{GL}_2(\hat{\mathbb{Z}}) \,:\, c \equiv 0 \pmod{N \hat{\mathbb{Z}}}\, \Bigr\}, \qquad g = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}.$$ $K_0(N)$ depends only on the primes $p \mid N$: away from those primes it agrees with $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p)$.

2. Strong approximation and $\Gamma_0(N) \backslash \mathbb{H}$

The classical modular curve $Y_0(N) = \Gamma_0(N) \backslash \mathbb{H}$ has a clean adelic description. The bridge is strong approximation for $\mathrm{SL}_2$:

Strong approximation (one form). For any open compact $K_f \subset \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_f)$ that contains $\mathrm{SL}_2(\hat{\mathbb{Z}})$, $$\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}) \;=\; \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}) \cdot \bigl( \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})^+ \cdot K_f \bigr),$$ where $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})^+$ is the connected component (positive determinant). The intersection $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}) \cap \bigl( \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})^+ \cdot K_f \bigr)$ is the classical congruence subgroup $\Gamma_f \subset \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$.

Take $K_f = K_0(N)$. Then $\Gamma_f = \Gamma_0(N)$, and the chain of identifications is: $$\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}) \backslash \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}) / \bigl( Z(\mathbb{R}) \cdot \mathrm{O}_2(\mathbb{R}) \cdot K_0(N) \bigr) \;\cong\; \Gamma_0(N) \backslash \bigl( \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})^+ / Z(\mathbb{R})^+ \mathrm{SO}_2(\mathbb{R}) \bigr) \;\cong\; \Gamma_0(N) \backslash \mathbb{H}.$$ The classical curve is a single double-coset space inside the adelic group.

3. Adelic automorphic forms

An automorphic form on $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q})$ is a function $$\phi : \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}) \backslash \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}) \longrightarrow \mathbb{C}$$ satisfying three regularity conditions. Each is the natural adelic generalisation of a classical condition on $f \in M_k(\Gamma_0(N))$:

conditionclassical analogue
Right $K$-finiteness: the right-translates $\{\phi(\cdot k) : k \in K\}$ span a finite-dimensional space.Weight-$k$ modular forms transform by $(c\tau+d)^k$ — a finite-dimensional rep of $\mathrm{O}_2(\mathbb{R})$.
$\mathfrak{z}$-finiteness: $\phi$ is annihilated by an ideal of finite codimension in $Z(\mathfrak{g}_\mathbb{C}) = $ centre of the universal enveloping algebra.Holomorphy of $f$ is a Cauchy–Riemann eigen-condition for the Laplace–Beltrami operator on $\mathbb{H}$.
Moderate growth: $|\phi(g)| \ll \|g\|^N$ for some $N$, $\|g\|$ a height function.$f$ is holomorphic at the cusps (no $q^{-n}$ tail).
Cusp form. $\phi$ is a cusp form iff its constant term along the upper-triangular unipotent vanishes: $$\int_{\mathbb{Q} \backslash \mathbb{A}} \phi\!\left( \begin{pmatrix} 1 & x \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} g \right) dx \;=\; 0 \qquad \text{for every } g \in \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}).$$ This is the adelic incarnation of "$f$ vanishes at every cusp of $\Gamma_0(N) \backslash \mathbb{H}$".

The space $\mathcal{A}(\mathrm{GL}_2)$ of all such $\phi$ is a $(\mathfrak{g}_\infty, K_\infty) \times \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_f)$-module, and decomposes (as a unitary representation of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A})$ on $L^2$) as $$L^2_{\mathrm{disc}}\bigl( \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}) \backslash \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}) \bigr) \;=\; \bigoplus_\pi m(\pi) \cdot \pi$$ into a direct sum of irreducible automorphic representations. Multiplicity one for $\mathrm{GL}_2$ asserts $m(\pi) \in \{0, 1\}$ — and for cuspidal $\pi$ the multiplicity is exactly $1$.

4. The classical-to-adelic dictionary

Section 2's strong-approximation decomposition says each $g \in \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A})$ has a (non-unique) factorisation $g = \gamma \cdot g_\infty \cdot k_f$ with $\gamma \in \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q})$, $g_\infty \in \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})^+$, and $k_f \in K_0(N)$. Given a classical modular form $f \in M_k(\Gamma_0(N))$, define $$\phi_f(g) \;:=\; (f \big|_k g_\infty)(i),$$ where $f|_k g$ is the weight-$k$ slash operator $(f|_k g)(\tau) = (\det g)^{k/2} (c\tau + d)^{-k} f(g \cdot \tau)$ (and $\det g_\infty > 0$).

The function $\phi_f$ is well-defined: the ambiguities in the strong-approximation factorisation are exactly the elements of $\Gamma_0(N) \subset \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q})$, which act on $f$ by the modular automorphy factor — and on $i \in \mathbb{H}$ by an isometry of the maximal compact $\mathrm{SO}_2(\mathbb{R})$ — so the values agree.

5. The spherical Hecke algebra and the Satake isomorphism

Fix a prime $p \nmid N$, an unramified prime. The spherical Hecke algebra at $p$ is $$\mathcal{H}_p \;=\; C_c\bigl( \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p) \backslash \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_p) / \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p) \bigr),$$ the algebra (under convolution) of compactly supported, bi-$\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p)$-invariant functions on $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_p)$. By the Cartan decomposition, every element of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_p)$ lies in a unique double coset $$\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p) \cdot \begin{pmatrix} p^a & 0 \\ 0 & p^b \end{pmatrix} \cdot \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p), \qquad a \ge b \in \mathbb{Z},$$ so $\mathcal{H}_p$ has a basis $\{\mathbb{1}_{a,b}\}_{a \ge b}$.

Local-to-classical Hecke

The element $T_p^{\mathrm{loc}} := \mathbb{1}_{1,0} + \mathbb{1}_{0,1}$ acts on the unramified part of an automorphic form, and unwinds (via the dictionary) to the classical $T_p$. Crucially, $\mathcal{H}_p$ is commutative: $(\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_p), \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p))$ is a Gelfand pair.

Satake isomorphism. There is an algebra isomorphism $$\mathcal{S} : \mathcal{H}_p \;\xrightarrow{\;\;\sim\;\;}\; \mathbb{C}\bigl[ X^{\pm 1}, Y^{\pm 1} \bigr]^{S_2}$$ onto the symmetric Laurent polynomials in two variables (the $S_2$ being swap of $X \leftrightarrow Y$, the Weyl group of $\mathrm{GL}_2$). Concretely, an unramified irreducible representation $\pi_p$ is determined by its Satake parameters $(\alpha_p, \beta_p) \in (\mathbb{C}^\times)^2 / S_2$; characters of $\mathcal{H}_p$ on the $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_p)$-fixed line of $\pi_p$ are $$\mathcal{S}(T_p^{\mathrm{loc}})(\alpha_p, \beta_p) \;=\; p^{1/2}(\alpha_p + \beta_p).$$

The Satake parameters are the local incarnation of the Hecke eigenvalue. The classical eigenvalue $a_p(f)$ on a weight-$k$ eigenform reads $$a_p \;=\; (\alpha_p + \beta_p) \cdot p^{(k-1)/2},$$ and $\alpha_p \beta_p = p^{k-1}$ (or $1$ in the unitary normalisation), recovering the Euler factor $1 - a_p p^{-s} + p^{k-1-2s}$.

6. Whittaker model and the new-form

Fix a non-trivial additive character $\psi : \mathbb{Q} \backslash \mathbb{A} \to \mathbb{C}^\times$. A Whittaker functional on a representation $\pi$ is a linear $\Lambda : \pi \to \mathbb{C}$ satisfying $$\Lambda\!\left( \pi(n_x) v \right) \;=\; \psi(x) \, \Lambda(v), \qquad n_x = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & x \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.$$ For an irreducible admissible $\pi_v$ that is generic (= admits any such $\Lambda \ne 0$), the space of Whittaker functionals is one-dimensional — local multiplicity one.

Equivalently, the Whittaker model is the embedding $\pi \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Wh}_\psi$ that sends $v \mapsto W_v(g) := \Lambda(\pi(g) v)$. These functions $W_v : G \to \mathbb{C}$ are concrete, computable objects.

The new-vector

Casselman proved a remarkable structure theorem. Let $\pi_p$ be irreducible admissible, and let $c_p \ge 0$ be the smallest integer such that $\pi_p^{K_0(p^{c_p})} \ne 0$. Then:

  • $c_p$ is well-defined; called the local conductor exponent.
  • $\dim \pi_p^{K_0(p^{c_p})} = 1$ — there is a unique fixed vector up to scaling, the local new-vector.
  • For any $j \ge 0$, $\dim \pi_p^{K_0(p^{c_p + j})} = j + 1$.

The global conductor of an automorphic representation $\pi$ is $N(\pi) = \prod_p p^{c_p}$. For $\pi$ corresponding to a classical $f \in S_k(\Gamma_0(N))$, this $N(\pi)$ matches $N$ — the level. Atkin–Lehner new-form theory and Casselman's local theorem are two sides of the same statement.

7. Automorphic $L$-functions

To a cuspidal automorphic representation $\pi = \bigotimes_v \pi_v$ of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q})$ one attaches an $L$-function $$L(s, \pi) \;=\; \prod_v L_v(s, \pi_v),$$ a product of local factors over all places. At an unramified $p$ with Satake parameters $(\alpha_p, \beta_p)$: $$L_p(s, \pi_p) \;=\; \det\!\bigl( I - \mathrm{diag}(\alpha_p, \beta_p) \cdot p^{-s} \bigr)^{-1} \;=\; (1 - \alpha_p p^{-s})^{-1} (1 - \beta_p p^{-s})^{-1}.$$ At ramified primes $p \mid N$, the factor is $(1 - a_p p^{-s})^{-1}$ with $a_p$ a single Hecke eigenvalue or, in worse cases, $1$. At the archimedean place, $L_\infty(s, \pi_\infty)$ is a product of gamma factors determined by the weight.

Match with the classical $L(s, f)$. For $\pi$ corresponding to a classical eigenform $f$, the unramified Euler factor unwinds to the classical Hecke factor: $$\bigl(1 - a_p p^{-s} + p^{k-1-2s}\bigr)^{-1} \;=\; (1 - \alpha_p p^{-s})^{-1}(1 - \beta_p p^{-s})^{-1},$$ where $\alpha_p + \beta_p = a_p$ and $\alpha_p \beta_p = p^{k-1}$ (in the classical normalisation). The full identity reads $L(s, \pi) = L(s, f)$ up to gamma-factor convention.

Why the adelic packaging is necessary

The story so far concerned $\mathrm{GL}_2$. Beyond it, classical holomorphic modular forms do not exist in the same sense — the symmetric space for $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$ at $n \ge 3$ is not a hermitian domain. The adelic formulation $$\phi : \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}) \backslash \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{A}) \to \mathbb{C}$$ makes sense for every $n$, with the same three regularity conditions and the same Euler-product $L$-function. This is the gateway to the Langlands programme: a conjectural correspondence between automorphic representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n$ and $n$-dimensional Galois representations, with matching $L$-functions on both sides.

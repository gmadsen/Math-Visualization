Differential forms — $d$, Stokes, and de Rham

Forms are the things you integrate. They eat tangent vectors, return numbers, and transform correctly under pullback so that integration over a manifold doesn't depend on coordinates. This page follows the arc from 1-forms through the exterior derivative, Stokes's theorem, and the first whisper of cohomology — the failure of closed forms to be exact.

1. Why forms?

Calculus on $\mathbb{R}^n$ has three kinds of "fields" that students meet early: scalar functions $f$, vector fields $X$, and — if one is lucky — the gradient $\nabla f$, the curl $\nabla \times X$, and the divergence $\nabla \cdot X$. These are useful but coordinate-bound: the notation hides that $\nabla f$ is a different kind of object from $X$, and the curl only makes sense in three dimensions.

A differential $k$-form on a smooth manifold $M$ is a smooth section of $\Lambda^k T^*M$. Unpacked: at each point $p\in M$ it is an alternating multilinear map

$\omega_p \colon T_pM \times \cdots \times T_pM \to \mathbb{R}$ ($k$ factors).

It eats $k$ tangent vectors and gives a number, and the number flips sign when any two inputs are swapped.

Forms are the things you integrate. A $k$-form on $M^n$ is exactly the algebraic object that can be integrated over a $k$-dimensional oriented submanifold. This is not a coincidence; it is the whole reason for the antisymmetry. Changing coordinates produces a Jacobian determinant, which is precisely the alternating multilinear thing you need.

The grand payoff is Stokes's theorem, $\int_{\partial M}\omega = \int_M d\omega$, which makes the fundamental theorem of calculus, Green, Stokes (classical), and Gauss-divergence into a single statement. And the kernel-versus-image game for the operator $d$ — closed modulo exact — is a topological invariant of $M$: de Rham cohomology.

A quick contrast

  • Scalar fields: functions $M\to\mathbb{R}$. These are $0$-forms.
  • Vector fields: sections of $TM$. Dual to $1$-forms via a metric, but not the same thing.
  • $1$-forms (covectors): sections of $T^*M$. These are what you integrate along curves. In coordinates, $\omega = \sum_i \omega_i(x)\,dx^i$.

On $\mathbb{R}^n$ with its Euclidean metric the distinction between vector fields and $1$-forms is papered over: physicists write $\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$, mathematicians write $\omega$. Once a metric is in the picture, vectors and covectors can be raised and lowered. But the algebra — wedge, $d$, Stokes — lives purely on the covector side, and that is where coordinate-freeness comes from.

2. 1-forms and the differential $df$

Fix $p\in M$. A covector at $p$ is a linear functional $T_pM\to\mathbb{R}$. The dual space $T_p^*M$ has the same dimension as $T_pM$. A $1$-form is a smooth choice of covector at every point. In coordinates $(x^1,\ldots,x^n)$ the basis $1$-forms $dx^1,\ldots,dx^n$ are defined by

$dx^i\!\left(\partial/\partial x^j\right) = \delta^i_j,$

and any $1$-form can be written uniquely as $\omega = \omega_i(x)\,dx^i$ (summation convention). The action on a tangent vector $v = v^j\,\partial/\partial x^j$ is just $\omega(v) = \omega_i v^i$.

The differential of a function

For a smooth function $f\colon M\to\mathbb{R}$, the differential $df$ is the $1$-form whose value on a vector $v$ is the directional derivative:

$df_p(v) = v(f) = \frac{d}{dt}\Big|_{t=0} f(\gamma(t))$

for any curve $\gamma$ with $\gamma(0)=p$, $\dot\gamma(0)=v$. In coordinates, $df = \sum_i \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}\,dx^i$ — exactly the "total differential" from multivariable calculus, but now honestly typed as a covector.

Picture. A $1$-form $\omega$ at a point can be pictured as a family of equally spaced level hyperplanes in $T_pM$. The value $\omega(v)$ counts (with sign) how many of those hyperplanes the vector $v$ pierces. The kernel of $\omega$ — vectors tangent to the level hyperplanes — is where $\omega$ vanishes. For $df$, the level hyperplanes are the tangent planes to level sets of $f$, and $df(v)$ measures how fast $f$ grows in the direction $v$.

3. The wedge product and $k$-forms

Given two $1$-forms $\alpha,\beta$ on $M$, their wedge product $\alpha\wedge\beta$ is the $2$-form defined by

$(\alpha\wedge\beta)(v,w) = \alpha(v)\beta(w) - \alpha(w)\beta(v).$

It is bilinear and antisymmetric: $\alpha\wedge\beta = -\beta\wedge\alpha$, and in particular $\alpha\wedge\alpha = 0$. Antisymmetry is exactly what makes $(\alpha\wedge\beta)(v,w)$ a signed area: the action on the parallelogram spanned by $v,w$ is a determinant in the $(\alpha,\beta)$ components.

More generally, $\Omega^k(M) := \Gamma(\Lambda^k T^*M)$ is the space of smooth $k$-forms, and

$\wedge\colon \Omega^k\otimes\Omega^\ell \to \Omega^{k+\ell}$

is bilinear, associative, and graded-commutative: $\alpha\wedge\beta = (-1)^{k\ell}\,\beta\wedge\alpha$. A basis of $\Omega^k(\mathbb{R}^n)$ over $C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n)$ is

$\{\,dx^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^{i_k}\ :\ 1\le i_1

so $\dim \Omega^k(\mathbb{R}^n) = \binom{n}{k}$. In particular $\Omega^k=0$ for $k>n$, and $\Omega^n$ is one-dimensional (spanned by the volume form $dx^1\wedge\cdots\wedge dx^n$).

Antisymmetry = oriented-area. Plugging the same vector in twice into any alternating form gives $0$. Swapping inputs flips sign. So a $2$-form is the data of a signed area on every $2$-plane of tangent vectors — a "ruler for parallelograms." A $k$-form is a ruler for oriented $k$-parallelepipeds.

4. The exterior derivative

The exterior derivative is a linear operator $d\colon \Omega^k(M)\to \Omega^{k+1}(M)$ satisfying three axioms that characterize it uniquely:

  • $df$ for $f\in\Omega^0$ is the ordinary differential (the $1$-form from §2);
  • $d$ is a graded derivation: $d(\alpha\wedge\beta) = d\alpha\wedge\beta + (-1)^{|\alpha|}\alpha\wedge d\beta$;
  • $d^2 = 0$, i.e. $d(d\omega)=0$ for every form.

In coordinates on $\mathbb{R}^n$, write $\omega = \sum_I \omega_I\,dx^I$ for $I=(i_1<\cdots

$d\omega = \sum_I \sum_j \frac{\partial \omega_I}{\partial x^j}\,dx^j\wedge dx^I.$

Recovering grad, curl, div

On $\mathbb{R}^3$ with the usual metric, identify vector fields with $1$-forms via $X=(P,Q,R)\leftrightarrow \omega_X = P\,dx + Q\,dy + R\,dz$, and $2$-forms via the Hodge star: $\star\omega_X = P\,dy\wedge dz + Q\,dz\wedge dx + R\,dx\wedge dy$. Then the three classical operators are all incarnations of $d$:

classicalvia forms
$\nabla f$$df = f_x\,dx + f_y\,dy + f_z\,dz$
$\nabla \times X$$d\omega_X = (R_y-Q_z)\,dy\wedge dz + (P_z-R_x)\,dz\wedge dx + (Q_x-P_y)\,dx\wedge dy$
$\nabla\cdot X$$d(\star\omega_X) = (P_x + Q_y + R_z)\,dx\wedge dy\wedge dz$

Now $d^2=0$ reads: $\nabla\times(\nabla f)=0$ and $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times X)=0$. Two identities, one proof.

"$d$ is the boundary of the form." Stokes will explain this: $\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M}\omega$, so integrating $d\omega$ over a region is the same as integrating $\omega$ over the boundary. From this perspective, $d$ plays for forms what $\partial$ plays for chains. The relation $d^2=0$ is dual to $\partial\partial=0$ ("the boundary of a boundary is empty").

5. Integration of forms over chains

A smooth $k$-simplex in $M$ is a smooth map $\sigma\colon\Delta^k\to M$ from the standard $k$-simplex, and a smooth $k$-chain is a finite formal $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination $c=\sum n_i\,\sigma_i$ of such. The integral of a $k$-form over a chain is defined by pullback to the standard simplex plus linearity:

$\displaystyle \int_\sigma \omega \;:=\; \int_{\Delta^k} \sigma^*\omega,\qquad \int_{\sum n_i\sigma_i}\omega \;:=\; \sum n_i \int_{\sigma_i}\omega.$

Pullback-invariance is what makes this well-defined: reparametrizing $\sigma$ by an orientation-preserving diffeomorphism of $\Delta^k$ leaves the integral unchanged (Jacobian = $+1$ at the form level), while an orientation-reversing reparametrization flips the sign, matching the $\pm 1$ coefficient on $\sigma$ in the chain.

This is the bridge between §4 and §5 below: integrating a $(k{-}1)$-form over the boundary chain $\partial M$ and integrating $d\omega$ over the bulk $k$-chain $M$ are both defined via the same pullback mechanism — the only question is whether they agree. They do, and that statement is Stokes.

5.1. Pullback of forms (aside)

For a smooth map $f\colon N\to M$, the pullback $f^*\colon\Omega^k(M)\to\Omega^k(N)$ acts on $k$-forms contravariantly. On a decomposable form,

$f^*(\omega_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\omega_k)(v_1,\ldots,v_k) \;=\; \omega_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\omega_k\bigl(df(v_1),\ldots,df(v_k)\bigr).$

Two identities make pullback the "right" operation:

  • $f^*(d\omega) = d(f^*\omega)$ — pullback commutes with the exterior derivative.
  • $f^*(\omega\wedge\eta) = (f^*\omega)\wedge(f^*\eta)$ — pullback is a ring map.

Together they say $f^*\colon \Omega^\bullet(M)\to\Omega^\bullet(N)$ is a map of differential graded algebras. This is why integration of forms is coordinate-invariant: changing coordinates is a pullback, and pulling back a top form along a diffeomorphism picks up the Jacobian determinant exactly once, giving the usual change-of-variables formula for free.

6. Stokes's theorem

Let $M$ be a compact oriented smooth $k$-manifold with boundary $\partial M$ (with induced orientation). For any $(k{-}1)$-form $\omega$ on an open neighborhood of $M$,

$$\int_{\partial M} \omega = \int_M d\omega.$$

This one identity specializes to several classical theorems you have already seen. Each row here is the same theorem, in different coordinates:

$(k, M)$namestatement
$k=1$, intervalFTC$\int_a^b f'(x)\,dx = f(b)-f(a)$
$k=2$, planar regionGreen$\oint_{\partial D}(P\,dx+Q\,dy) = \iint_D (Q_x - P_y)\,dA$
$k=2$, surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$Stokes (classical)$\oint_{\partial S} \vec F\cdot d\vec r = \iint_S (\nabla\times\vec F)\cdot d\vec S$
$k=3$, solid in $\mathbb{R}^3$Gauss$\iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\vec F)\,dV = \oiint_{\partial V}\vec F\cdot d\vec S$

The only thing to check in each case is that the coefficient of $d\omega$ is what the classical statement says — and that is the grad/curl/div dictionary of §4.

What is a proof of Stokes? Cover $M$ by charts, use a partition of unity, reduce to the model case $M = [0,1]^k$ with $\omega$ supported in the interior (trivial) or with $\omega$ having exactly one boundary face contributing (one-dimensional FTC in the corresponding variable). The combinatorics of "which faces of the cube show up" is exactly antisymmetry.

7. Closed vs exact — a first look at de Rham cohomology

The condition $d^2=0$ makes $(\Omega^\bullet(M), d)$ into a cochain complex:

$\Omega^0(M) \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^1(M) \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^2(M) \xrightarrow{d} \cdots.$

Call $\omega$ closed if $d\omega=0$, and exact if $\omega=d\eta$ for some $\eta$. Exact forms are closed (because $d^2=0$); the converse is the interesting question.

The de Rham cohomology of $M$ is

$H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \;=\; \dfrac{\ker\bigl(d\colon\Omega^k\to\Omega^{k+1}\bigr)}{\operatorname{im}\bigl(d\colon\Omega^{k-1}\to\Omega^k\bigr)}.$

A priori each $H^k$ is a real vector space defined with analytic objects. The deep fact (de Rham's theorem) is that $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \cong H^k(M;\mathbb{R})$ — the same number you get from simplicial/singular cohomology. Closed-modulo-exact of smooth forms is a topological invariant.

Poincaré lemma

On any star-shaped open set $U\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n$ (i.e. there exists $x_0\in U$ with the segment from $x_0$ to any $x\in U$ contained in $U$), every closed form is exact: $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(U)=0$ for $k\ge 1$. The proof is a homotopy operator: integrating along the rays from $x_0$ produces a primitive explicitly.

In particular, $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathbb{R}^n)=0$ for $k\ge 1$. All cohomology comes from topology, not from local analysis.

The annulus: the angle form $d\theta$

Let $M = \mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{0\}$ (or, equivalently for cohomology, any annulus). Consider

$\omega \;=\; \dfrac{-y\,dx + x\,dy}{x^2+y^2}.$

A direct computation (you saw it in §4) shows $d\omega = 0$. In polar coordinates $\omega = d\theta$ locally, but $\theta$ is only defined modulo $2\pi$: there is no global function whose differential is $\omega$. Concretely, integrating along a loop winding once around the origin gives

$\oint \omega = 2\pi \neq 0,$

whereas an exact form would integrate to $0$ over any closed loop (by Stokes: $\oint d\eta = \int_{\partial(\text{loop})} d\eta$ — but a closed loop has empty boundary). So $\omega$ is closed but not exact. In fact $[\omega]$ generates $H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{0\}) \cong \mathbb{R}$, and the cohomology class detects the winding number of loops around the origin.

Slogan. $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}$ counts, in dimension $k$, the ways your manifold fails to be contractible. "Closed-not-exact" = "there is a hole."

7. Where this connects

Differential forms and $d$ are the common language of half of modern geometry. A few directions to follow:

Riemann surfaces and Hodge theory

On a compact oriented Riemann surface $X$ of genus $g$, the first de Rham cohomology $H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}(X)$ is $2g$-dimensional, and the Hodge decomposition $H^1 = H^{1,0}\oplus H^{0,1}$ splits it into holomorphic and antiholomorphic $1$-forms. The space of holomorphic $1$-forms is $g$-dimensional — this is the dimension of the genus, the number of "independent" closed-but-not-exact holomorphic forms. Most of classical complex analysis on surfaces is a special case of this picture.

Lie groups and Maurer–Cartan

On a Lie group $G$, the left-invariant $1$-forms form a copy of $\mathfrak{g}^*$. Putting them all together gives the Maurer–Cartan form $\theta\in\Omega^1(G;\mathfrak{g})$, and it satisfies

$d\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}[\theta,\theta] = 0,$

a first-order equation encoding the entire Lie algebra structure. Connections on principal bundles are "twisted" Maurer–Cartan forms; the failure of $d\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}[\theta,\theta]$ to vanish is the curvature.

Sheaf cohomology and Čech-de Rham

The de Rham complex is a resolution of the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ by fine sheaves, so $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \cong H^k(M;\underline{\mathbb{R}})$ as computed by Čech cohomology on a good cover. This is how de Rham cohomology talks to algebraic topology. Shift from $\mathbb{R}$ to the sheaf of holomorphic functions and you get Dolbeault cohomology; shift to an algebraic setting and you get algebraic de Rham cohomology, then crystalline cohomology, and so on.

Physics

Gauge fields are connection $1$-forms; field strengths are curvature $2$-forms; Bianchi identities are $d^2=0$. Charge conservation is $d\star J = 0$. Topological charge (instanton number, Chern number) is the integral of an $n$-form over a closed manifold — a de Rham integral whose value is a topological invariant.

Recurring motif. Every time you meet a differential operator squaring to zero ($d$, $\partial$, $\bar\partial$, $\delta$, a BRST operator), you get a cohomology theory, and its classes see the global shape of whatever you are working on. Closed-modulo-exact is one of the most general and most useful ideas in mathematics.

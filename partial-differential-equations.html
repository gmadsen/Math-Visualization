Partial differential equations

Three model equations — Laplace, heat, wave — set the type signature for everything else: their discriminant says whether information sits still, smooths out, or travels along characteristics. Weak solutions and Lax–Milgram turn each into a Hilbert-space problem; elliptic regularity and Sobolev embedding hand the smoothness back.

1. Classification: elliptic / parabolic / hyperbolic

A second-order linear PDE in two variables, written symmetrically as

$$a(x,y)\,u_{xx} + 2b(x,y)\,u_{xy} + c(x,y)\,u_{yy} + (\text{lower order}) = 0,$$

has a single number — the discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - ac$ — that controls almost every qualitative feature of its solutions. The sign of $\Delta$ partitions linear PDE into three universes:

$\Delta$typemodelcharacteristicswell-posed data
$<0$elliptic$\Delta u = 0$none realboundary values on $\partial\Omega$
$=0$parabolic$u_t = \Delta u$one familyinitial $+$ lateral data
$>0$hyperbolic$u_{tt} = c^2\Delta u$two familiesCauchy data on a non-characteristic curve

The characteristic curves are the integral curves of $a\,dy^2 - 2b\,dx\,dy + c\,dx^2 = 0$. Real roots exist exactly when $\Delta\ge 0$; the wave equation has two of them (the light-cone slopes $\pm 1/c$), the heat equation has the degenerate single family $t=\text{const}$, and Laplace has none — which is why elliptic problems take all-around boundary data instead of evolving in any direction.

2. Heat equation and the heat kernel

The heat equation

$$u_t = \Delta u, \qquad u(x,0) = u_0(x)$$

describes how a temperature distribution $u_0$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ relaxes toward equilibrium. Plugging in the Gaussian ansatz $u(x,t) = (4\pi t)^{-n/2} e^{-|x|^2/4t}$ and computing both sides shows it solves $u_t=\Delta u$ for $t>0$, and as $t\to 0^+$ it concentrates at the origin: this is the heat kernel

$$K_t(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}}\,e^{-|x|^2/4t}, \qquad K_0 = \delta_0.$$

Convolution gives the general solution: $u(x,t) = (K_t * u_0)(x)$. Three properties are immediate.

Smoothing. Even if $u_0$ is merely $L^1$ or a distribution, $u(\cdot,t)\in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n)$ for every $t>0$. Heat kills singularities; rough data becomes analytic instantly.

Infinite propagation speed. $K_t(x)>0$ for every $x\in\mathbb{R}^n$ and every $t>0$. A point of heat at the origin is felt — however faintly — everywhere, immediately. This is the qualitative opposite of the wave equation.

Maximum principle. On a bounded cylinder $\Omega\times[0,T]$, $\max u$ is taken on the parabolic boundary $\{t=0\}\cup(\partial\Omega\times[0,T])$ — never strictly inside, unless $u$ is constant. Heat dampens; it cannot create new hot spots.

3. Wave equation and d'Alembert

The wave equation $u_{tt} = c^2\Delta u$ is the prototype hyperbolic equation. In one space dimension it factors:

$$\partial_t^2 - c^2\partial_x^2 \;=\; (\partial_t - c\partial_x)(\partial_t + c\partial_x),$$

which says the operator annihilates anything constant along $x \pm ct$. The general solution is therefore $u(x,t) = F(x-ct) + G(x+ct)$ — a right-mover plus a left-mover. Matching the initial data $u(x,0)=f$, $u_t(x,0)=g$ produces d'Alembert's formula:

$$u(x,t) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[f(x-ct) + f(x+ct)\bigr] \;+\; \frac{1}{2c}\!\int_{x-ct}^{x+ct}\!g(s)\,ds.$$

Two halves of the position datum travel in opposite directions; the velocity datum spreads as an integrated pulse between the two characteristics.

Finite speed of propagation. $u(x_0,t_0)$ depends only on data inside the segment $[x_0-ct_0, x_0+ct_0]$ — its backward light cone. A signal cannot outrun $c$.

Huygens' principle. In odd $n\ge 3$ the dependence shrinks further — to the boundary of the cone. A pulse on $\mathbb{R}^3$ has a sharp leading and trailing edge: that is why we hear discrete claps rather than infinite reverberation. In even dimensions there is a residual tail and Huygens fails.

4. Laplace equation and the Dirichlet problem

$\Delta u = 0$ is the steady-state limit of $u_t = \Delta u$: solutions are harmonic. They satisfy the mean-value property

$$u(x_0) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\partial B_r(x_0)|}\!\int_{\partial B_r(x_0)}\! u\,dS \;=\; \frac{1}{|B_r(x_0)|}\!\int_{B_r(x_0)}\! u\,dx$$

for every ball $B_r(x_0)\subset\Omega$, which forces the strong maximum principle: a non-constant harmonic function attains its extrema only on $\partial\Omega$. Consequence: the Dirichlet problem

$$\Delta u = 0 \text{ in } \Omega, \qquad u|_{\partial\Omega} = g$$

has a unique solution (subtract any two solutions; the difference is harmonic with zero boundary data, hence zero by maximum principle).

Poisson kernel on the disk

On the unit disk $\mathbb{D}\subset\mathbb{R}^2$, the unique harmonic extension of a continuous boundary value $g(\theta)$ is given by the Poisson integral

$$u(r,\theta) \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi}\!\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\! P_r(\theta-\phi)\, g(\phi)\,d\phi, \qquad P_r(\alpha) \;=\; \frac{1-r^2}{1-2r\cos\alpha + r^2}.$$

The kernel is non-negative, integrates to $2\pi$, and concentrates on $\alpha=0$ as $r\to 1^-$ — so $u(r,\theta)\to g(\theta)$ at every point of continuity. The Neumann problem $\partial_\nu u = g$ on $\partial\Omega$ is solvable only when $\int_{\partial\Omega} g\,dS = 0$ (a divergence-theorem compatibility condition), and is then unique up to a constant.

5. Weak solutions and Galerkin approximation

For irregular data — or simply for the natural Hilbert-space framework — we relax "$-\Delta u = f$" to its weak formulation. Multiply by a test function $v\in C_c^\infty(\Omega)$ and integrate by parts:

$$\int_\Omega \nabla u\cdot\nabla v\,dx \;=\; \int_\Omega f\,v\,dx \qquad\text{for every } v\in C_c^\infty(\Omega).$$

This is well-defined whenever $u,v\in H^1(\Omega)$ and $f\in L^2(\Omega)$. Closing $C_c^\infty$ in the $H^1$ norm gives $H^1_0(\Omega)$ — Sobolev functions with zero boundary trace, the natural home for Dirichlet conditions.

Definition (weak solution). $u\in H^1_0(\Omega)$ is a weak solution of $-\Delta u = f$ with zero Dirichlet data if

$$a(u,v) := \int_\Omega \nabla u\cdot\nabla v\,dx \;=\; \langle f, v\rangle \qquad\forall\, v\in H^1_0(\Omega).$$

The bilinear form $a$ is bounded ($|a(u,v)|\le \|u\|_{H^1}\|v\|_{H^1}$) and coercive ($a(u,u) = \|\nabla u\|_{L^2}^2 \ge C\|u\|_{H^1}^2$ by Poincaré). So existence and uniqueness will follow from Lax–Milgram in §6.

Galerkin's method

Choose a finite-dimensional subspace $V_n = \mathrm{span}(\phi_1,\ldots,\phi_n)\subset H^1_0$. Look for $u_n=\sum_j c_j\phi_j$ with

$$a(u_n,\phi_i) = \langle f,\phi_i\rangle, \qquad i=1,\ldots,n.$$

This is a linear system $A c = b$ with $A_{ij}=a(\phi_j,\phi_i)$ and $b_i=\langle f,\phi_i\rangle$. Coercivity makes $A$ positive definite, so $c$ exists uniquely. As $\bigcup V_n$ becomes dense in $H^1_0$, the discrete solutions converge: $u_n\to u$ in $H^1_0$. Picking $\phi_i$ to be hat functions on a triangulation gives the finite element method.

6. Lax–Milgram, elliptic regularity, embedding

Three theorems wrap up the existence-and-smoothness story for elliptic problems.

Lax–Milgram

Let $H$ be a Hilbert space and $a\colon H\times H\to\mathbb{R}$ a bilinear form satisfying

  • boundedness: $|a(u,v)| \le M\|u\|\,\|v\|$,
  • coercivity: $a(u,u)\ge \alpha \|u\|^2$ for some $\alpha>0$.

Then for every $\ell\in H^*$ there exists a unique $u\in H$ with $a(u,v) = \ell(v)$ for all $v\in H$, and $\|u\|\le \alpha^{-1}\|\ell\|$. When $a$ is symmetric this reduces to the Riesz representation theorem; the asymmetric case is the new content.

Applied to $H=H^1_0(\Omega)$ with $a(u,v)=\int\nabla u\cdot\nabla v$ and $\ell(v)=\int fv$: existence, uniqueness, and an a-priori estimate $\|u\|_{H^1}\le C\|f\|_{L^2}$ — all from coercivity.

Elliptic regularity

The weak solution from Lax–Milgram is in $H^1_0$; we want to know it is actually smooth when $f$ is. Interior regularity: if $-\Delta u = f$ weakly and $f\in H^k_{\mathrm{loc}}(\Omega)$, then $u\in H^{k+2}_{\mathrm{loc}}(\Omega)$. Boundary regularity: on a $C^{k+2}$ domain with appropriate boundary data, the gain extends up to $\partial\Omega$. Bootstrapping: $f\in C^\infty\Rightarrow u\in C^\infty$.

Sobolev embedding

To turn weak derivatives in $L^p$ back into pointwise smoothness, the Sobolev embedding theorem trades $kp$ orders against the dimension $n$:

regimeconditionconclusion
subcritical$kp < n$$W^{k,p}\hookrightarrow L^{p^*}$, $\;p^* = \dfrac{np}{n-kp}$
critical$kp = n$$W^{k,p}\hookrightarrow L^q$ for every $q<\infty$ (BMO at the edge)
supercritical$kp > n$$W^{k,p}\hookrightarrow C^{0,\gamma}$, $\;\gamma = k - n/p \;(\bmod 1)$

So once enough weak derivatives lie in $L^p$, weak solutions become Hölder continuous, then $C^k$. Combine with elliptic regularity: a smooth right-hand side gives a smooth weak solution, classically.

7. Connections

PDEs are the engine of modern analysis — every advance in regularity, weak solutions, or function spaces has fed back into the rest of mathematics.

Functional analysis and Sobolev spaces

Functional analysis + Sobolev spaces supply the weak-solution machinery: Lax–Milgram, Riesz representation, compact embeddings. Galerkin approximation reduces existence to finite-dimensional linear algebra.

Harmonic functions and elliptic regularity

Harmonic-function theory is the prototype; maximum principles and Harnack inequalities transfer (with technical work) to general elliptic operators in non-divergence form.

Stochastic calculus and Feynman–Kac

Stochastic calculus and Brownian motion give probabilistic representations of solutions to parabolic PDEs; conversely, PDE methods analyse SDEs.

Differential geometry

Curvature flows (Ricci, mean-curvature), heat-equation methods (Bochner, Bismut), and the analytic side of Riemannian geometry all run through PDE theory.

Open frontiers: regularity for nonlinear elliptic / parabolic systems (de Giorgi–Nash–Moser refinements); free-boundary problems; rough paths and stochastic PDEs; geometric analysis program (Cheeger–Colding, Perelman); gradient flows in metric spaces.

Group cohomology

Derived $G$-fixed points: a single machine that controls extensions, twisted forms, Hilbert 90, and Brauer groups.

Let $G$ be a group and $M$ a (left) $G$-module ŌĆö i.e. an abelian group on which $G$ acts. The functor $M\mapsto M^G=\{m\in M:g\cdot m=m\;\forall g\}$ of fixed points is left exact but not right exact: a surjection $M\twoheadrightarrow N$ of $G$-modules need not surject on fixed points (an element of $N^G$ may lift to $M$ only after twisting by a cocycle). The right derived functors $H^n(G,-)=R^n(-)^G$ are group cohomology. Equivalently ŌĆö and this is the working definition ŌĆö the cohomology of the complex of cochains on the bar resolution of $\mathbb{Z}$ over $\mathbb{Z}[G]$.

Three things make this notebook entry unusually fertile. First, the low-dimensional groups have crisp interpretations: $H^0=M^G$, $H^1$ classifies twisted forms / crossed homomorphisms, $H^2$ classifies extensions. Second, when $G=\Gal(\bar k/k)$ acts on a Galois module, group cohomology becomes Galois cohomology, and the punch lines are Hilbert 90 and the Brauer group. Third, for cyclic $G$ the cohomology is periodic with period 2 ŌĆö a genuine miracle that powers class field theory.

1. The bar resolution and $H^n(G,M)$

To compute $\mathrm{Ext}^n_{\mathbb{Z}[G]}(\mathbb{Z},M)$ we need a free (or projective) resolution of the trivial $G$-module $\mathbb{Z}$. The bar resolution is universal and explicit:

$$\cdots\;\xrightarrow{\,d_3\,}\;\mathbb{Z}[G]^{\otimes 3}\;\xrightarrow{\,d_2\,}\;\mathbb{Z}[G]^{\otimes 2}\;\xrightarrow{\,d_1\,}\;\mathbb{Z}[G]\;\xrightarrow{\,\varepsilon\,}\;\mathbb{Z}\;\to\;0,$$

where $\varepsilon(\sum n_g g)=\sum n_g$ is the augmentation, and a typical generator $[g_1|g_2|\dots|g_n]$ in degree $n$ has differential

$$d[g_1|\dots|g_n]\;=\;g_1[g_2|\dots|g_n]\;+\;\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}(-1)^i[g_1|\dots|g_ig_{i+1}|\dots|g_n]\;+\;(-1)^n[g_1|\dots|g_{n-1}].$$

Apply $\Hom_{\mathbb{Z}[G]}(-,M)$. An $n$-cochain is then a function $\varphi:G^n\to M$ (with $\varphi=m\in M$ when $n=0$), and the coboundary becomes

$$(d\varphi)(g_1,\dots,g_{n+1})\;=\;g_1\cdot\varphi(g_2,\dots,g_{n+1})\;+\;\sum_{i=1}^{n}(-1)^i\varphi(\dots,g_ig_{i+1},\dots)\;+\;(-1)^{n+1}\varphi(g_1,\dots,g_n).$$

The cohomology of $(\Hom_{\mathbb{Z}[G]}(\mathbb{Z}[G]^{\otimes\bullet+1},M),d^*)$ is $H^n(G,M)$. Different resolutions of $\mathbb{Z}$ (free $\mathbb{Z}[G]$-resolutions are non-unique) give canonically isomorphic cohomology ŌĆö that is the whole point of derived functors.

2. $H^0(G,M)=M^G$

The degree-0 cohomology is exactly the fixed points:

$$H^0(G,M)\;=\;M^G\;=\;\{\,m\in M\;:\;g\cdot m=m\;\forall g\in G\,\}.$$

Indeed a $0$-cochain is just an element $m\in M$, and the coboundary $(dm)(g)=g\cdot m-m$ vanishes precisely when $m$ is fixed.

Although it sits at the bottom of the tower, $H^0$ is doing the only thing the higher groups exist to repair. The functor $(-)^G:\mathsf{Mod}_{\mathbb{Z}[G]}\to\mathsf{Ab}$ is left exact: applying it to a short exact sequence $$0\;\to\;A\;\to\;B\;\to\;C\;\to\;0$$ of $G$-modules gives an exact sequence $$0\;\to\;A^G\;\to\;B^G\;\to\;C^G$$ but the map $B^G\to C^G$ need not be surjective. The obstruction lives in $H^1$, and the long exact sequence of cohomology $$0\to A^G\to B^G\to C^G\xrightarrow{\delta} H^1(G,A)\to H^1(G,B)\to H^1(G,C)\xrightarrow{\delta}H^2(G,A)\to\cdots$$ is the central computational tool of the entire subject.

3. $H^1$: crossed homomorphisms and Hilbert 90

Untangling the bar formulas in degree 1: a $1$-cocycle (also called a crossed homomorphism or derivation) is a function $\varphi:G\to M$ satisfying the twisted homomorphism identity

$$\varphi(gh)\;=\;\varphi(g)\;+\;g\cdot\varphi(h).$$

If $G$ acts trivially this collapses to the ordinary homomorphism law. A $1$-coboundary is one of the form $\varphi(g)=g\cdot m-m$ for some $m\in M$. So

$$H^1(G,M)\;=\;\frac{\{\text{crossed homomorphisms }G\to M\}}{\{\text{principal: }g\mapsto gm-m\}}.$$

When the action is trivial, every $1$-cocycle is a homomorphism and every coboundary is zero, so $H^1(G,M)=\Hom(G,M)$. When the action is nontrivial, things twist.

Hilbert's Theorem 90 (cyclic version). Let $L/K$ be a cyclic extension of degree $n$ with $G=\Gal(L/K)=\langle\sigma\rangle$, and let $L^\times$ be the multiplicative group of $L$, viewed as a $G$-module. Then $$H^1(G,L^\times)\;=\;1.$$ Equivalently: every element $\beta\in L^\times$ with $N_{L/K}(\beta)=1$ has the form $\beta=\sigma(\alpha)/\alpha$ for some $\alpha\in L^\times$.

The proof is short and instructive. A $1$-cocycle $\varphi:G\to L^\times$ is determined by $\beta=\varphi(\sigma)\in L^\times$ (using the cocycle identity to extend), and the cocycle identity in cyclic form is exactly $N_{L/K}(\beta)=\beta\cdot\sigma\beta\cdots\sigma^{n-1}\beta=1$. To exhibit a coboundary $\beta=\sigma(\alpha)/\alpha$, set $$\alpha\;=\;\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\beta\cdot\sigma\beta\cdots\sigma^{i-1}\beta\cdot\sigma^i(c)$$ for a $c\in L^\times$ chosen so the sum is nonzero (possible by linear independence of characters). Then $\sigma(\alpha)=\beta^{-1}\alpha$ ŌĆö done.

4. $H^2$ classifies group extensions

A group extension of $G$ by an abelian group $A$ (with $G$ acting on $A$) is a short exact sequence $$1\;\to\;A\;\to\;E\;\xrightarrow{\pi}\;G\;\to\;1$$ of groups in which $A$ is normal in $E$ and the conjugation action of $E$ on $A$ factors through the prescribed action of $G$. Two extensions are equivalent if there is an isomorphism $E\to E'$ inducing the identity on both $A$ and $G$.

Choose a set-theoretic section $s:G\to E$ with $\pi\circ s=\mathrm{id}_G$ and $s(e)=e$. Multiplication in $E$ then becomes $$s(g)\,s(h)\;=\;c(g,h)\cdot s(gh)$$ for some function $c:G\times G\to A$ ŌĆö the cocycle measuring failure of $s$ to be a homomorphism. Associativity in $E$ forces $c$ to be a $2$-cocycle; changing the section by $s\mapsto s\cdot a$ for $a:G\to A$ changes $c$ by the coboundary $da$.

Theorem. The map "extension $\mapsto$ cocycle class" is a bijection $$\{\text{equiv. classes of extensions } 1\to A\to E\to G\to 1\}\;\xrightarrow{\sim}\;H^2(G,A).$$ The split (semidirect-product) extensions correspond to the trivial class $0\in H^2$.

Reincarnated yet again: $H^2(G,A)$ classifies gerbes, projective representations, and obstruction classes throughout deformation theory.

5. Cyclic groups and Tate periodicity

For $G=C_n=\langle\sigma\rangle$ cyclic of order $n$, there is a remarkably small free resolution of $\mathbb{Z}$:

$$\cdots\;\xrightarrow{N}\;\mathbb{Z}[G]\;\xrightarrow{\sigma-1}\;\mathbb{Z}[G]\;\xrightarrow{N}\;\mathbb{Z}[G]\;\xrightarrow{\sigma-1}\;\mathbb{Z}[G]\;\xrightarrow{\varepsilon}\;\mathbb{Z}\;\to\;0$$

where $N=1+\sigma+\sigma^2+\cdots+\sigma^{n-1}$ is the norm element. Apply $\Hom_{\mathbb{Z}[G]}(-,M)=(-)^G$-style and read off:

6. InflationŌĆōrestriction and LyndonŌĆōHochschildŌĆōSerre

If $H\tr{}ianglelefteq G$ is normal, the canonical maps

  • Restriction $\mathrm{res}^G_H:H^n(G,M)\to H^n(H,M)$ ŌĆö restrict a cochain on $G$ to one on $H$;
  • Inflation $\mathrm{inf}:H^n(G/H,M^H)\to H^n(G,M)$ ŌĆö pull back a cochain on the quotient (well-defined on $M^H$);

fit into the inflationŌĆōrestriction exact sequence in low degree:

$$0\;\to\;H^1(G/H,M^H)\;\xrightarrow{\mathrm{inf}}\;H^1(G,M)\;\xrightarrow{\mathrm{res}}\;H^1(H,M)^{G/H}\;\to\;H^2(G/H,M^H)\;\to\;H^2(G,M).$$

This is the five-term tail of a much bigger machine: the LyndonŌĆōHochschildŌĆōSerre spectral sequence

$$E_2^{p,q}\;=\;H^p\!\left(G/H,\,H^q(H,M)\right)\;\Longrightarrow\;H^{p+q}(G,M).$$

You read it as: cohomology of $G$ is built in two stages ŌĆö first do the cohomology of $H$ (which carries a residual $G/H$-action), then do the cohomology of the quotient with coefficients in those groups. Convergence assembles the $E_2$-page into the full target through a sequence of differentials and filtrations.

7. Galois cohomology and the Brauer group

Let $\bar k/k$ be a separable closure and $G_k=\Gal(\bar k/k)$ the absolute Galois group. For any (continuous) $G_k$-module $M$, set $$H^n(k,M)\;:=\;H^n_{\mathrm{cts}}(G_k,M)$$ where cochains are required to be continuous for the profinite (Krull) topology on $G_k$. This is Galois cohomology. It is the same machine as group cohomology, restricted to continuous cochains.

Hilbert 90 (Galois form). $H^1(k,\bar k^\times)=1$ for every field $k$. Equivalently: $H^1_{\mathrm{\acute et}}(\Spec\,k,\mathbb{G}_m)=0$.

This is the cohomology engine behind much of arithmetic. Combined with the Kummer exact sequence $$1\;\to\;\mu_n\;\to\;\bar k^\times\;\xrightarrow{x\mapsto x^n}\;\bar k^\times\;\to\;1$$ (valid when $n$ is invertible in $k$), Hilbert 90 gives the long exact sequence whose first chunk reads $$1\to\mu_n(k)\to k^\times\xrightarrow{n}k^\times\to H^1(k,\mu_n)\to H^1(k,\bar k^\times)=0.$$ So $H^1(k,\mu_n)=k^\times/(k^\times)^n$ ŌĆö the Kummer isomorphism. Galois descent from $\bar k^\times$ down to a clean cohomological invariant.

Continuing one step:

$$\mathrm{Br}(k)\;:=\;H^2(k,\bar k^\times).$$

The Brauer group classifies central simple $k$-algebras up to Morita equivalence. The Kummer sequence then yields the short exact

$$0\;\to\;\mathrm{Br}(k)[n]\;\to\;H^2(k,\mu_n)\;\to\;\mathrm{Br}(k)\xrightarrow{n}\mathrm{Br}(k),$$

so $H^2(k,\mu_n)=\mathrm{Br}(k)[n]$ recovers the $n$-torsion of the Brauer group ŌĆö the bridge from Galois cohomology to division-algebra theory and to the localŌĆōglobal Hasse principle.

8. Connections

The functor $H^*(G,-)$ packages a great deal of arithmetic and topological information. Each of the threads below is a standard place where it surfaces.

Galois cohomology and Brauer groups

$H^2(\Gal(\bar K/K), \bar K^\times) = \mathrm{Br}(K)$ is the Brauer group; Galois cohomology develops this in detail. The same machinery computes Selmer groups for elliptic curves and is the algebraic engine of KolyvaginŌĆÖs Euler-system argument.

Group extensions and representation theory

The classification of representations of $G$ on a fixed module $M$ is controlled by $H^1(G, M)$; central extensions by $H^2(G, M)$. Schur multipliers are $H_2(G,\mathbb{Z})$. The cohomological language unifies the various extension problems into one Ext-functor.

Topological models

For discrete $G$ the cohomology agrees with $H^*(BG; M)$ where $BG$ is the classifying space, computed in algebraic topology. LyndonŌĆōHochschildŌĆōSerre is the spectral sequence for a normal subgroup ŌĆö the algebraic shadow of the fibre sequence $BN \to BG \to BQ$.

Homological algebra mainline

All of the above are special cases of $\mathrm{Ext}_{\mathbb{Z}[G]}^*(\mathbb{Z}, M)$ in the category of $G$-modules; homological algebra develops Ext, derived functors, and spectral sequences in their natural generality.

Open frontiers: profinite group cohomology and the BlochŌĆōKato conjecture (now VoevodskyŌĆÖs motivic-cohomology theorem), Iwasawa theory of $\mathbb{Z}_p$-extensions, equivariant stable homotopy theory and its replacement of "$G$-module" with "genuine $G$-spectrum".

1. Mordell–Weil: a finitely generated group of rational points

Fix $E/\mathbb{Q}$ in Weierstrass form $y^2 = x^3 + Ax + B$ with nonzero discriminant $\Delta = -16(4A^3 + 27 B^2)$. The rational solutions, with a point at infinity $O$ and the chord-and-tangent group law, form an abelian group $E(\mathbb{Q})$.

Mordell–Weil (1922/1928). For any elliptic curve over a number field $K$, $E(K)$ is finitely generated. Over $\mathbb{Q}$: $$E(\mathbb{Q}) \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}^r \oplus E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}},$$ with $r \ge 0$ the rank and $E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}$ finite.

Torsion is completely understood: Mazur (1977) classified $E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}$ as one of 15 groups ($\mathbb{Z}/n$ for $n \le 10$ or $n=12$, or $\mathbb{Z}/2 \oplus \mathbb{Z}/2n$ for $n \le 4$). The rank is different: no algorithm provably computes $r$, and boundedness of ranks is open.

The proof has two pieces. Weak Mordell–Weil shows $E(\mathbb{Q})/nE(\mathbb{Q})$ is finite, via a Galois cohomology calculation. A height function $\hat h$ with $\hat h(nP) = n^2 \hat h(P)$ then descends: a quasi-basis of $E(\mathbb{Q})/nE(\mathbb{Q})$ plus finitely many small-height points generates the whole group.

The widget shows six curves of known rank and torsion. The doubling formula for $P = (x, y)$ with $y \ne 0$ is $$\lambda = \frac{3x^2 + A}{2y}, \quad x_{2P} = \lambda^2 - 2x, \quad y_{2P} = \lambda(x - x_{2P}) - y.$$

Doubling $P$ on a rank-$\ge 1$ curve produces rational points with rapidly growing heights. For $y^2 = x^3 - x + 1$, doubling $P = (0,1)$ gives $(1/4, -7/8), (72/49, 611/343), \ldots$ — all free multiples of the same generator, with coordinates exploding at rate $\hat h(nP) = n^2 \hat h(P)$.

The next widget animates the full chord-and-tangent construction on a rank-$1$ Mordell–Weil group. Starting from a known generator $P$ on $E : y^2 = x^3 - 2$ (the Fermat-style curve with $P = (3, 5)$), each step builds the secant through two chosen points, reads off the third intersection with $E$, and reflects across the $x$-axis to land at the sum. Iterating $P \mapsto 2P \mapsto 3P \mapsto \cdots$ traces out the free cyclic subgroup $\mathbb{Z}\cdot P \subset E(\mathbb{Q})$ — and the rational coordinates balloon quadratically in height, the hallmark of an infinite-order rational point.

The free rank behaves like a lattice: once a basis is fixed, points with canonical height $\hat h(P)\le H$ grow roughly like the number of lattice points in a ball of radius $\sqrt H$, i.e. $\asymp H^{r/2}$. This gives the next geometric readout.

2. Counting points modulo $p$ and the original BSD heuristic

For a prime $p$ of good reduction ($p \nmid \Delta$), reduce $E \bmod p$ to get $\tilde E / \mathbb{F}_p$, and count: $$N_p = \#\tilde E(\mathbb{F}_p), \qquad a_p = p + 1 - N_p.$$ Hasse's theorem: $|a_p| \le 2\sqrt p$, so each factor $N_p/p \approx 1$ — each prime sees about $p$ points, give or take $2\sqrt p$.

In 1963 Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer ran a pile of curves through EDSAC and plotted $$\Pi(X) \;=\; \prod_{\substack{p \le X \\ p \text{ good}}} \frac{N_p}{p}.$$ Naively each factor fluctuates about $1$, so $\Pi(X)$ should stay bounded. Instead it grew, and the growth rate tracked the Mordell–Weil rank:

BSD heuristic. As $X \to \infty$, $$\prod_{\substack{p \le X \\ p \text{ good}}} \frac{N_p}{p} \;\sim\; c \cdot (\log X)^{r},$$ where $r = \operatorname{rank} E(\mathbb{Q})$ and $c > 0$ is a constant depending on $E$. Higher rank ⇒ systematically more points mod $p$.

Heuristically, many rational points force many reductions — $a_p$ biases negative, nudging $N_p/p$ above $1$. The same data packaged as an Euler product $\prod (1 - a_p p^{-s} + p^{1-2s})^{-1}$ gives $L(E, s)$, and BSD ties its order of vanishing at $s = 1$ to this rank.

The widget computes $a_p$ by brute force — count $(x, y)$ with $y^2 \equiv x^3 + Ax + B \pmod p$ via Euler's criterion — forms $\Pi(X)$, and plots $\log \Pi$ vs $\log p$. A line fit to $\log \Pi(X)$ vs $\log \log X$ recovers an empirical slope hovering near $r$.

3. The conjecture: analytic rank = algebraic rank

Package the local data globally. The Hasse–Weil $L$-function is $$L(E, s) \;=\; \prod_{p \text{ good}} (1 - a_p p^{-s} + p^{1-2s})^{-1} \cdot \prod_{p \text{ bad}} (1 - a_p p^{-s})^{-1},$$ convergent for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 3/2$. Modularity (Wiles, BCDT) identifies $L(E, s) = L(f, s)$ for a weight-2 newform $f$ of level $N$, yielding entire continuation and a functional equation $\Lambda(E, s) = \pm \Lambda(E, 2-s)$ with center $s = 1$.

BSD (weak form). $\quad \operatorname{ord}_{s=1} L(E, s) \;=\; \operatorname{rank} E(\mathbb{Q}).$

That is: $L(E, s)$ vanishes at $s=1$ to the same order as the free rank. The left side is analytic (Taylor expansion); the right side is algebraic (group theory). BSD says they agree.

Refined (strong) BSD. The leading Taylor coefficient is explicitly predicted: $$\lim_{s \to 1} \frac{L(E, s)}{(s-1)^r} \;=\; \frac{\Omega \cdot R \cdot |\Sha(E/\mathbb{Q})| \cdot \prod_p c_p}{|E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}|^2},$$ where $\Omega$ is the real period, $R$ the regulator (determinant of the Néron–Tate height pairing on a basis of the free part), $\Sha$ the Tate–Shafarevich group (conjecturally finite!), $c_p$ the Tamagawa numbers at bad primes, and $|E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}|$ the size of the torsion subgroup.

The widget sketches $L(E, s)$ near $s=1$ using toy Taylor models $L(s) \approx c(s-1)^r(1 + a(s-1))$. Toggling rank $0, 1, 2$ visibly changes the zero: a nonzero value, a transverse crossing, or a tangential touch.

A richer zoo: real elliptic curves from the Cremona tables span analytic ranks $0, 1, 2, 3$. For each one we can truncate the Euler product $L(E, s) \approx \prod_{p \le X}(1 - a_p p^{-s} + p^{1-2s})^{-1}$ at a moderate $X$ and evaluate near the critical point. The order of vanishing at $s = 1$ is read off by sampling $L(E, 1 + h)$ for small $h$; the algebraic rank is stored alongside as a list of explicit Mordell–Weil generators. BSD is the statement that these two columns always match.

What is known (for modular $E$, so all $E/\mathbb{Q}$):

  • Kolyvagin (1989): if analytic rank $\le 1$, then algebraic rank agrees and $\Sha$ is finite.
  • Gross–Zagier (1986): for analytic rank $1$, a Heegner point gives a non-torsion element of $E(\mathbb{Q})$ whose height ties to $L'(E, 1)$.
  • Rank $\ge 2$: wide open in both directions.

BSD is a Clay Millennium Prize Problem — a \$1M bounty on the statement that two different ways of counting rational points agree. It is the arithmetic heart of the Langlands philosophy: the analytic behavior of an automorphic $L$-function encodes the arithmetic of the motive it comes from.

4. The Néron–Tate height and the regulator

The free part of $E(\mathbb{Q})$ is a lattice, but it needs a metric before anyone can measure its covolume. The canonical (Néron–Tate) height $\hat h : E(\mathbb{Q}) \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ fixes this. Starting from the naive Weil height of $x(P)$, Tate's averaging trick $$\hat h(P) \;=\; \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{h(x(2^n P))}{4^n}$$ produces a function that is exactly quadratic: $\hat h(nP) = n^2 \hat h(P)$ and $\hat h(P) = 0$ iff $P \in E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}$.

Polarizing gives the Néron–Tate pairing $\langle P, Q \rangle = \tfrac12(\hat h(P+Q) - \hat h(P) - \hat h(Q))$, a positive-definite inner product on the $\mathbb{R}$-vector space $E(\mathbb{Q})/\text{tors} \otimes \mathbb{R} \cong \mathbb{R}^r$. The regulator is the Gram determinant on any $\mathbb{Z}$-basis $P_1, \ldots, P_r$: $$R(E/\mathbb{Q}) \;=\; \det\bigl(\langle P_i, P_j \rangle\bigr)_{1 \le i,j \le r}.$$ It is the squared covolume of the Mordell–Weil lattice, and it is the factor of refined BSD that "measures how big the infinite part of $E(\mathbb{Q})$ is."

For rank $r = 0$ we declare $R = 1$ (empty determinant). For rank $1$ the regulator reduces to $\hat h(P_1)$ for any generator. Congruent-number curves $y^2 = x^3 - n^2 x$ of rank $1$ famously have tiny heights — of order $0.1$–$0.3$ — which is why their $L'(E, 1)$ values stay small even when the analytic rank is simple.

Two sanity checks the widget surfaces. First, the Cauchy–Schwarz constraint $\langle P_1, P_2 \rangle^2 \le \hat h(P_1)\hat h(P_2)$ holds automatically for the real pairing; violating it on a slider collapses the lattice onto a line. Second, shrinking the off-diagonal entry increases $\det G$: the more independent the generators, the bigger the regulator, and the smaller the $L$-value has to be to satisfy refined BSD.

Why this is hard. No one knows a provable algorithm to compute $\hat h(P)$ to arbitrary precision starting from the Weierstrass coefficients alone, though in practice the Tate series + descent gives any desired digit. Worse: to compute $R$ you need to exhibit a basis of $E(\mathbb{Q})/\text{tors}$, and finding that basis is the same problem as computing the rank — which is itself conjecturally solvable only via BSD.

5. The Tate–Shafarevich group Ш

The last mysterious factor in refined BSD is $|\Sha(E/\mathbb{Q})|$. To see where it lives, consider the $n$-descent exact sequence coming from $0 \to E[n] \to E \xrightarrow{[n]} E \to 0$ and Galois cohomology: $$0 \to E(\mathbb{Q})/nE(\mathbb{Q}) \to H^1(\mathbb{Q}, E[n]) \to H^1(\mathbb{Q}, E)[n] \to 0.$$ Weak Mordell–Weil says the left term is finite, but $H^1(\mathbb{Q}, E[n])$ itself is enormous. The Selmer group $\mathrm{Sel}_n(E)$ is the subgroup of cohomology classes that become trivial everywhere locally — at every $\mathbb{Q}_p$ and at $\mathbb{R}$. It is computable, finite, and contains $E(\mathbb{Q})/nE$.

The quotient is exactly the Tate–Shafarevich group: $$\Sha(E/\mathbb{Q})[n] \;=\; \mathrm{Sel}_n(E) \,/\, \bigl(E(\mathbb{Q})/nE(\mathbb{Q})\bigr).$$ Equivalently, $\Sha$ classifies principal homogeneous spaces (torsors) $C/\mathbb{Q}$ for $E$ that have a $\mathbb{Q}_p$-point for every $p$ and an $\mathbb{R}$-point, yet have no $\mathbb{Q}$-point. Every element of $\Sha$ is a failure of the Hasse principle, genuinely global, invisible to any single local obstruction.

Folklore conjecture. $\Sha(E/\mathbb{Q})$ is always finite. In the refined BSD formula its order appears as a positive integer factor: $$\lim_{s \to 1} \frac{L(E,s)}{(s-1)^r} \;=\; \frac{\Omega \cdot R \cdot |\Sha| \cdot \prod_p c_p}{|E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}|^2}.$$ Cassels–Tate: if $\Sha$ is finite then its order is a perfect square. So the plausible values are $1, 4, 9, 16, 25, \ldots$

Known for modular $E$ with analytic rank $\le 1$: $\Sha$ is finite (Kolyvagin 1989). In small conductor tables most curves satisfy $|\Sha| = 1$, but $|\Sha| = 4, 9, 16, 25$ show up readily, and curves with $|\Sha| = 7^2, 11^2$ are tabulated. Computing $|\Sha|$ even heuristically requires descent by 2, 3, and higher, hence "$n$-descent" machinery; a rigorous unconditional $|\Sha|$ exists only in very special cases.

The Tamagawa numbers $c_p$ appearing alongside $|\Sha|$ are much tamer: $c_p = [E(\mathbb{Q}_p) : E_0(\mathbb{Q}_p)]$ is the component group of the Néron model at a bad prime, and vanishes for $p$ of good reduction. For the modular congruent-number curve $y^2 = x^3 - 25x$, $N = 32$, one has $c_2 = 2$ and all other $c_p = 1$; $|\Sha|$ is believed to be $1$, $\Omega \approx 2.621$, $R = \hat h(P_1) \approx 0.2529$, and the refined BSD formula gives $L'(E, 1) \approx 0.331$, agreeing with numerical computation of the modular form.

Conceptually: $R$ quantifies the rational points you see; $\Sha$ quantifies the rational points you don't see but locally "should." Together with the Tamagawa and torsion fudge factors, they explain the entire discrepancy between the analytic $L^{(r)}(E, 1)/r!$ and the algebraic $\Omega$. BSD is the statement that nothing else is missing.

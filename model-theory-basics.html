Model theory

Where syntax meets the structures it describes. First-order logic gives us a language; model theory studies what that language can — and crucially, cannot — see about groups, orders, fields, and graphs. By the end you'll know why $(\mathbb{Q},<)$ and $(\mathbb{R},<)$ look identical to first-order logic, why connectivity isn't first-order, and how compactness gives Ax–Grothendieck for free.

Logic supplies sentences; structures supply the worlds they describe. Two structures may be very different objects yet satisfy exactly the same first-order sentences — they are elementarily equivalent. Or they may look superficially similar yet be separated by a single sentence of low quantifier rank. Model theory is the systematic study of this gap: what does first-order logic see, what does it miss, and what tools — types, saturation, Ehrenfeucht–Fraïssé games, transfer principles — let us compute the answer.

Plan. §1 defines structures and signatures. §2 introduces elementary equivalence — first-order indistinguishability. §3 contrasts it with isomorphism: when do they coincide, when do they part? §4 introduces types and saturation, the local-to-global engine of the subject. §5 gives EF games as a combinatorial test for elementary equivalence. §6 collects three applications to algebra: completeness of ACF, the Lefschetz principle, and Ax–Grothendieck.

1. Structures and signatures

A signature (or language) $\sigma$ is a list of symbols, each labelled with an arity:

  • Constant symbols $c$ (arity $0$).
  • Function symbols $f$ of arity $n\ge 1$.
  • Relation symbols $R$ of arity $n\ge 1$.

A $\sigma$-structure $\mathfrak{A}$ is a non-empty set $A$ (the domain) together with an interpretation of every symbol: each constant $c$ gets an element $c^{\mathfrak{A}}\in A$, each $n$-ary function $f$ gets a function $f^{\mathfrak{A}}\colon A^n\to A$, each $n$-ary relation $R$ gets a subset $R^{\mathfrak{A}}\subseteq A^n$. The signature is the grammar; the structure is the world the grammar names.

The standard zoo.
  • Group: $\sigma_{\mathrm{gp}} = \{e, \cdot, ^{-1}\}$ — constant, binary, unary.
  • Order: $\sigma_{<} = \{<\}$ — one binary relation.
  • Ring: $\sigma_{\mathrm{rg}} = \{0, 1, +, \cdot, -\}$ — two constants, two binary, one unary.
  • Graph: $\sigma_{\mathrm{gr}} = \{E\}$ — one binary relation, usually symmetric and irreflexive.
The same domain can host different structures depending on which signature you read it through — $(\mathbb{Z}, +, \cdot, 0, 1)$ is a ring, $(\mathbb{Z}, <)$ is a discrete order, $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$ is a group.

2. Elementary equivalence

Once we have structures and a logic to talk about them, the natural question is: when are two structures logically the same?

Elementary equivalence. Two $\sigma$-structures $\mathfrak{A},\mathfrak{B}$ are elementarily equivalent, written $\mathfrak{A}\equiv\mathfrak{B}$, iff for every first-order $\sigma$-sentence $\varphi$, $$\mathfrak{A}\models\varphi \;\iff\; \mathfrak{B}\models\varphi.$$ Equivalently, $\mathrm{Th}(\mathfrak{A}) = \mathrm{Th}(\mathfrak{B})$ — the structures have identical complete first-order theories.

This is much coarser than isomorphism. Cardinality is invisible (Löwenheim–Skolem); the standard model of arithmetic $\mathbb{N}$ and any non-standard model are equivalent by construction; $(\mathbb{Q},<)$ and $(\mathbb{R},<)$ are both dense linear orders without endpoints, and that theory is complete, so they too are elementarily equivalent.

Why a theory being "complete" matters. A theory $T$ is complete when, for every sentence $\varphi$, $T\vdash\varphi$ or $T\vdash\neg\varphi$. Equivalently, all models of $T$ are elementarily equivalent. Many natural theories — DLO (dense linear orders without endpoints), ACF$_p$ (algebraically closed fields of characteristic $p$), the theory of $(\mathbb{Z},+)$ — are complete. Many others — PA, ZFC, the theory of groups — are not.

3. Isomorphism vs elementary equivalence

Isomorphism preserves everything: every relation, every operation, every cardinality fact, every sentence — first-order or higher. Elementary equivalence preserves only first-order sentences. So $\mathfrak{A}\cong\mathfrak{B}$ implies $\mathfrak{A}\equiv\mathfrak{B}$ but not the converse.

Coincidence theorem (finite case). For finite structures over a finite signature, $\mathfrak{A}\equiv\mathfrak{B}$ iff $\mathfrak{A}\cong\mathfrak{B}$. The reason: a finite structure has a complete diagram — a single sentence describing every atomic fact about every element pair — and that sentence is first-order.

For infinite structures the gap opens up. The classical example is Cantor's back-and-forth.

Cantor's theorem (1895). Any two countable dense linear orders without endpoints are isomorphic. So $(\mathbb{Q},<) \cong $ any other countable DLO. The theory DLO is therefore $\aleph_0$-categorical: it has exactly one model up to isomorphism in cardinality $\aleph_0$.

The proof is the prototype back-and-forth construction. List both domains as $\{a_0,a_1,\ldots\}$ and $\{b_0,b_1,\ldots\}$. Build a partial isomorphism in stages. At even stages, take the smallest unmatched $a_i$ and pick any $b_j$ in $B$ that fits the order pattern (density supplies one). At odd stages, do the reverse from $B$. The union is an isomorphism.

4. Types and saturation

Sentences talk about a structure as a whole. To talk about elements we need formulas with free variables — and to talk about possible elements (in this or any elementary extension) we need types.

Type. Fix a $\sigma$-structure $\mathfrak{A}$ and a parameter set $A_0\subseteq A$. A complete $n$-type over $A_0$ is a maximal consistent set $p(\bar x)$ of $\sigma$-formulas with parameters from $A_0$ and free variables $\bar x = (x_1,\ldots,x_n)$. The set of all complete $n$-types over $A_0$ is the type space $S_n(A_0)$.

An $n$-tuple $\bar a$ in some elementary extension realises $p$ when $\mathfrak{A}'\models\varphi(\bar a)$ for every $\varphi\in p$. By compactness, every type is realised in some elementary extension. The question is whether it is already realised in $\mathfrak{A}$ itself.

Example: a non-realised type. In $(\mathbb{Q},<)$ the set $$p(x) = \{x > q : q\in\mathbb{Q}\}$$ is consistent — every finite subset is satisfied by any rational larger than the listed $q$'s — but no rational is greater than every rational. So $p$ is omitted in $(\mathbb{Q},<)$. Compactness produces an elementary extension where it is realised — that extension contains a "point at infinity," exactly as in non-standard analysis.
Saturation. A structure $\mathfrak{A}$ is $\omega$-saturated if every type over a finite parameter set is already realised in $\mathfrak{A}$. More generally, $\mathfrak{A}$ is $\kappa$-saturated if every type over a parameter set of size $<\kappa$ is realised. Saturated models are richer than necessary — they contain witnesses to every consistent local description.

Saturation is the model-theoretic analogue of compactness in topology: it ensures that "type-theoretic limits" exist. It is the workhorse behind back-and-forth proofs (e.g., Cantor's theorem says every countable DLO is $\omega$-saturated), and behind transfer principles in §6.

5. Ehrenfeucht–Fraïssé games

Elementary equivalence is a yes/no question: do $\mathfrak{A},\mathfrak{B}$ agree on every first-order sentence? Ehrenfeucht–Fraïssé games refine it: how many quantifiers does it take to tell them apart?

The $n$-round EF game. Players are Spoiler and Duplicator, on $\sigma$-structures $\mathfrak{A},\mathfrak{B}$. Each round Spoiler picks an element from one structure; Duplicator must respond with an element from the other. After $n$ rounds Duplicator wins iff the resulting partial map (between the $2n$ chosen elements, paired by round) is a partial isomorphism — i.e. preserves all atomic relations.
EF theorem (Fraïssé 1954, Ehrenfeucht 1961). Duplicator has a winning strategy in the $n$-round EF game on $\mathfrak{A},\mathfrak{B}$ iff $\mathfrak{A}$ and $\mathfrak{B}$ agree on every first-order sentence of quantifier rank at most $n$. (Quantifier rank = maximum nesting depth of $\forall, \exists$.)

The proof is by induction on $n$, mirrored on both sides: a winning strategy is the witness that no rank-$n$ separator exists, and conversely.

Application: connectivity is not first-order. Take $\mathfrak{A}=C_{2n+1}$ (one cycle of length $2n+1$, connected) and $\mathfrak{B}=C_n\sqcup C_{n+1}$ (two cycles, disconnected). For $n$ rounds Duplicator can mirror Spoiler's moves locally — distances of order $\le 2^n$ are visible to a rank-$n$ formula, but the global topology isn't. So no rank-$n$ sentence distinguishes connected from disconnected for arbitrary $n$.

6. Applications to algebra

Model theory's most striking consequences come from transfer principles: first-order facts can be moved between models of the same complete theory, even when the models look very different.

ACF$_p$ is complete. The theory of algebraically closed fields of fixed characteristic $p$ (for $p$ prime or $p=0$) is complete. Equivalently, any two algebraically closed fields of the same characteristic are elementarily equivalent. (Indeed, ACF$_p$ admits quantifier elimination — every formula reduces to a Boolean combination of polynomial equations.)
Lefschetz principle (model-theoretic version). A first-order sentence $\varphi$ in the language of rings holds in $\mathbb{C}$ iff it holds in $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ for all but finitely many primes $p$.

The transfer is engineered by compactness: if $\varphi$ holds in $\mathbb{C}$ but fails in $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ for infinitely many $p$, then the theory $\mathrm{ACF} \cup \{\varphi\} \cup \{p \neq 0 : p\text{ prime}\}$ is consistent (each finite subset has a model among $\overline{\mathbb{F}_q}$ for $q$ large), so by compactness it has a model — an algebraically closed field of characteristic $0$ where $\varphi$ fails — contradicting completeness of ACF$_0$.

Ax–Grothendieck

Ax–Grothendieck theorem (1968). Every injective polynomial map $f\colon\mathbb{C}^n\to\mathbb{C}^n$ is surjective.

The model-theoretic proof is two lines once you have the principle:

  1. For every fixed degree $d$, "every injective polynomial map of degree $\le d$ in $n$ variables is surjective" is a first-order sentence in the language of rings (you can encode polynomial identities up to that degree).
  2. Over $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ this holds trivially: $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ is a union of finite fields $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$, and any injective map between finite sets is surjective.

Therefore the sentence holds in $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ for all $p$. By Lefschetz it holds in $\mathbb{C}$. Done.

The upper half-plane and hyperbolic geometry

A single slice of $\mathbb{C}$, equipped with a new metric, becomes the world's cleanest model of negative curvature. Geodesics are semicircles, triangles have angle sum less than $\pi$, and $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ is the isometry group. This is the home of modular forms.

1. $\mathbb{H}$, the upper half-plane

Let

$\mathbb{H} \;=\; \{\, z = x + iy \in \mathbb{C} \,:\, y > 0 \,\}.$

As a subset of $\mathbb{C}$ this is nothing new — just the open upper half-plane, diffeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^2$. The interesting move is to equip it with a different Riemannian metric, one in which distances near the real axis blow up and the axis itself is pushed off to infinity. What looks flat suddenly has constant curvature $-1$.

The model goes back to Beltrami, Klein, and Poincaré: it is one of the standard incarnations of hyperbolic geometry — Lobachevsky's non-Euclidean plane, the geometry in which Euclid's parallel postulate fails and through a point not on a line there are infinitely many lines that don't meet it.

Preview. The modular group $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ acts on $\mathbb{H}$ by Möbius transformations; holomorphic functions on $\mathbb{H}$ that transform nicely under this action are called modular forms. Everything on this page is the geometric substrate for that theory.

2. The hyperbolic metric

We give $\mathbb{H}$ the Riemannian metric

$ds^2 \;=\; \dfrac{dx^2 + dy^2}{y^2}.$

At height $y$, one Euclidean unit of length counts as $1/y$ hyperbolic units. So as $y \to \infty$ the space feels "compressed" (Euclidean steps get cheaper in hyperbolic terms? no — more expensive per Euclidean unit? let's be careful). Concretely: a horizontal Euclidean segment from $(0,y)$ to $(L,y)$ has hyperbolic length $L/y$. Moving up, the same hyperbolic step requires an ever-larger Euclidean step. Near $y=0$, conversely, one hyperbolic unit is a tiny Euclidean sliver.

The real axis $\{y=0\}$ is at infinite hyperbolic distance from every interior point: integrating $\int_{\epsilon}^{1} dy/y = -\log\epsilon$ diverges as $\epsilon \to 0^+$. The axis is the boundary at infinity, written $\partial\mathbb{H} = \mathbb{R}\cup\{\infty\}$.

3. Geodesics

To minimize arc length $\int \sqrt{\dot x^2+\dot y^2}/y \, dt$ is a clean calculus-of-variations problem whose answer is strikingly simple:

Theorem. The geodesics of $(\mathbb{H}, ds^2 = (dx^2+dy^2)/y^2)$ are exactly:
(a) vertical half-lines $\{x=c,\, y>0\}$, and
(b) Euclidean semicircles $\{(x-c)^2 + y^2 = r^2,\, y>0\}$ whose centers lie on the real axis.

Both cases meet the real axis at right angles, which is the hyperbolic-geometry statement that geodesics "run off to infinity orthogonally to $\partial\mathbb{H}$".

Given two points $z,w \in \mathbb{H}$, the unique geodesic joining them is either a vertical line (if $\mathrm{Re}\,z = \mathrm{Re}\,w$) or the unique semicircle through $z$ and $w$ with center on $\mathbb{R}$. The hyperbolic distance is

$d(z,w) \;=\; \operatorname{arcosh}\!\left( 1 + \dfrac{|z-w|^2}{2\,\mathrm{Im}(z)\,\mathrm{Im}(w)} \right).$

4. $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ acts on $\mathbb{H}$

Let $g = \begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ (so $ad-bc=1$). Its Möbius action is

$g \cdot z \;=\; \dfrac{az+b}{cz+d}.$

A two-line calculation verifies this preserves $\mathbb{H}$:

$\mathrm{Im}(g\cdot z) \;=\; \dfrac{\mathrm{Im}(z)}{|cz+d|^2}.$

Since $|cz+d|^2 > 0$ whenever $\mathrm{Im}(z)>0$, the image stays in $\mathbb{H}$. A slightly longer computation shows this is an isometry for the hyperbolic metric: the scale factor $1/|cz+d|^2$ on Euclidean lengths exactly cancels the change in $1/y$.

The action is transitive: any two points of $\mathbb{H}$ can be swapped by some $g$. The stabilizer of $i$ is

$\mathrm{Stab}(i) \;=\; \left\{ \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix} \right\} \;=\; \mathrm{SO}(2),$

so we get the homogeneous-space identification

$\mathbb{H} \;\cong\; \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{R})/\mathrm{SO}(2).$

The kernel of the action is $\pm I$, so the effective isometry group is $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{R}) = \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{R})/\{\pm I\}$; this is the full group of orientation-preserving hyperbolic isometries.

5. Three isometry types

Conjugacy classes in $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ are controlled by one invariant: $|\mathrm{tr}\,g| = |a+d|$. The trichotomy exactly parallels the classification of conic sections and of Möbius transformations of the sphere.

typetracefixed pointsgeometry
elliptic$|\mathrm{tr}\,g| < 2$one fixed point in $\mathbb{H}$, one in lower half-planerotation about the fixed point (pure angular action)
parabolic$|\mathrm{tr}\,g| = 2$, $g\ne\pm I$one fixed point on $\partial\mathbb{H}$ (real axis or $\infty$)horocycle flow — translations, e.g. $z\mapsto z+1$
hyperbolic$|\mathrm{tr}\,g| > 2$two fixed points on $\partial\mathbb{H}$translation along the geodesic through the two fixed points
Why the trace? The fixed points of $z \mapsto (az+b)/(cz+d)$ solve $cz^2 + (d-a)z - b = 0$; its discriminant is $(d-a)^2 + 4bc = (a+d)^2 - 4(ad-bc) = \mathrm{tr}^2 - 4$. Sign of the discriminant = location of fixed points.

6. Hyperbolic triangles

Take three points in $\mathbb{H}$ and connect them with geodesic arcs. Because the geometry has curvature $-1$, the Gauss–Bonnet theorem specializes to the following gem.

Theorem (Gauss–Bonnet in $\mathbb{H}$). Let $\Delta$ be a geodesic triangle with angles $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$. Then
$\operatorname{area}(\Delta) \;=\; \pi - (\alpha + \beta + \gamma).$

Three things to notice:

  • The angle sum is always less than $\pi$ — the hallmark of negative curvature.
  • Area is bounded: no hyperbolic triangle has area more than $\pi$ (the limit is the ideal triangle with all three vertices on $\partial\mathbb{H}$, angles all $0$, area exactly $\pi$).
  • Scaling is impossible: a hyperbolic triangle with prescribed angles has a unique size. You cannot shrink it and keep the angles.

7. The Poincaré disk

The upper half-plane has a cousin: the Poincaré disk

$\mathbb{D} \;=\; \{ z \in \mathbb{C} : |z| < 1 \}, \qquad ds^2 \;=\; \dfrac{4\,|dz|^2}{(1-|z|^2)^2}.$

The Cayley transform

$\Phi(z) \;=\; \dfrac{z - i}{z + i} \;:\; \mathbb{H} \xrightarrow{\ \sim\ } \mathbb{D}$

is a biholomorphic isometry. It sends $i \mapsto 0$, the real axis $\mathbb{R}\cup\{\infty\}$ to the unit circle, and maps vertical half-lines / semicircles perpendicular to $\mathbb{R}$ to arcs of circles meeting the unit circle at right angles.

The disk model is visually democratic: the action of rotations about the origin is obvious, and the whole infinite plane is compressed into a bounded picture. It is the model Escher used for his Circle Limit woodcuts — the fish at the "edge" are the same hyperbolic size as those at the center; the Euclidean shrinking by the factor $(1-|z|^2)/2$ exactly compensates.

Both models sit inside a common family called the Klein/projective and hyperboloid models; all are isometric, and for different questions different models are most convenient.

8. Preview: the modular group $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$

Inside $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ sits the subgroup

$\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) \;=\; \left\{\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} : a,b,c,d\in\mathbb{Z},\ ad-bc=1\right\}.$

This is the modular group. Because it is discrete, its action on $\mathbb{H}$ is properly discontinuous: every point has a neighborhood only finitely many of whose translates meet it. The quotient $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})\backslash\mathbb{H}$ is therefore a reasonable space (it is a non-compact orbifold of finite hyperbolic area $\pi/3$).

$\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ is generated by

$T \;=\; \begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{pmatrix} : z \mapsto z+1, \qquad S \;=\; \begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix} : z \mapsto -1/z.$

A fundamental domain for the action is the "ice-cream cone"

$F \;=\; \left\{ z \in \mathbb{H} : |\mathrm{Re}\,z| \le \tfrac{1}{2},\ |z| \ge 1 \right\}.$

Every $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$-orbit meets $F$, and meets its interior exactly once (identifications on the boundary identify $z \sim z+1$ along the vertical walls, and $z \sim -1/z$ along the lower arc). Tiling $\mathbb{H}$ with translates of $F$ gives the famous modular tessellation.

Holomorphic functions on $\mathbb{H}$ that transform under $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ by
$f\!\left(\tfrac{az+b}{cz+d}\right) = (cz+d)^k f(z)$
are called modular forms of weight $k$. They descend to sections of a line bundle on $F$ (with corners). The full theory picks up in modular-forms.html (forthcoming).

9. Why this page exists

Hyperbolic geometry on $\mathbb{H}$ is not a self-contained curiosity; it is the geometric floor on which several subjects in this notebook rest.

  • Moduli of elliptic curves. Every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{C}$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ for some lattice $\Lambda$; rescaling, $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau$ for $\tau\in\mathbb{H}$. Two $\tau$'s give the same curve iff they differ by $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$. Hence $\mathcal{M}_{1,1} \;=\; \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})\backslash\mathbb{H}.$ The fundamental domain from §8 is the moduli space. See elliptic curves and moduli spaces.
  • Modular forms. Holomorphic sections of the weight-$k$ line bundle on $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$. This leads to the Eisenstein series $E_k$, the discriminant $\Delta$, the $j$-invariant, and the whole of the theory of automorphic forms. Forward pointer: modular-forms.html (Wave 2).
  • Uniformization. Every compact Riemann surface of genus $\ge 2$ is a quotient $\Gamma\backslash\mathbb{H}$ for a torsion-free discrete $\Gamma \subset \mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ — a Fuchsian group. Hyperbolic geometry is the intrinsic geometry of higher-genus surfaces. See Riemann surfaces.
  • Complex analysis. The Cayley transform links this geometry to the unit disk's automorphism group $\mathrm{PSU}(1,1)$. See complex analysis §Möbius.
  • Riemannian geometry. $\mathbb{H}$ is the canonical 2-dimensional model of constant negative curvature, dual to the sphere; it is a symmetric space of the form $G/K$ with $G = \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{R}), K = \mathrm{SO}(2)$. See Riemannian geometry.
  • Lie groups. $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ is the smallest non-compact simple Lie group; its representation theory (Bargmann's discrete, principal, and complementary series) was the template for the whole unitary-representation program. See Lie groups.

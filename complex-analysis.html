Complex analysis — amplitwist, contours, residues

A Needham-flavored tour: holomorphic maps as local rotations, Cauchy's theorem as a curl-free vector field, residues as bookkeeping for poles, and conformal maps as the geometry of $\hat{\mathbb{C}}$.

1. The plane and the sphere

The complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$ are $\mathbb{R}^2$ with multiplication added. A complex number $z=x+iy = re^{i\theta}$ is a point with a length and an angle. The metric is the Euclidean one: $|z-w| = \sqrt{(x_1-x_2)^2+(y_1-y_2)^2}$.

For complex analysis the right ambient space is not $\mathbb{C}$ but its one-point compactification, the Riemann sphere $\hat{\mathbb{C}} = \mathbb{C}\cup\{\infty\}$. Place the sphere of radius $\tfrac12$ tangent to the plane at the origin and project from the north pole; this is stereographic projection. The plane corresponds to the sphere minus the north pole, and the formal symbol $\infty$ is the north pole itself.

Two facts make stereographic projection the natural picture:

conformal it preserves angles, and circles ↔ circles it sends circles on the sphere to circles or lines in the plane (a line being a "circle through $\infty$").

A Möbius transformation is any map $T(z) = \dfrac{az+b}{cz+d}$ with $ad-bc\ne 0$. Three remarkable facts:

(i) Möbius transformations form a group, isomorphic to $\mathrm{PGL}_2(\mathbb{C})$.
(ii) On $\hat{\mathbb{C}}$ they are precisely the conformal automorphisms — bijective angle-preserving maps.
(iii) On the sphere they are exactly the rigid rotations and reflections combined with dilations — but if you restrict to the subgroup that preserves the spherical metric, you recover $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ acting as the rotation group of the sphere, realized in coordinates as $T(z) = \dfrac{az - \bar c}{cz + \bar a}$ with $|a|^2+|c|^2=1$ (i.e.\ $\mathrm{SU}(2)/\{\pm 1\}$).

A Möbius transformation is determined by what it does to three points: given any two triples of distinct points $(z_1,z_2,z_3)$ and $(w_1,w_2,w_3)$ in $\hat{\mathbb{C}}$, there is a unique $T$ with $T(z_i)=w_i$. The "three points" are the analogue of "two points determine a line" in projective geometry.

The invariant of four points under all Möbius transformations is the cross-ratio

$$[z_1,z_2;z_3,z_4] \;=\; \frac{(z_1-z_3)(z_2-z_4)}{(z_1-z_4)(z_2-z_3)}.$$

Cross-ratio is real iff the four points are concyclic — a tidy way to detect "four points on a circle" using only complex arithmetic.

2. Holomorphic functions — the amplitwist picture

A function $f:U\to\mathbb{C}$ on an open set $U\subseteq\mathbb{C}$ is holomorphic at $z_0$ if

$$f'(z_0) \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)}{h}$$

exists as a complex limit — meaning the limit is the same no matter how $h\in\mathbb{C}$ approaches $0$. This is much more restrictive than $\mathbb{R}^2$-differentiability.

Write $f = u+iv$, viewed as a map $\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2$. Real-differentiability gives a Jacobian

$$Df = \begin{pmatrix} u_x & u_y \\ v_x & v_y \end{pmatrix}.$$

For $f$ to be complex-differentiable, this Jacobian must commute with multiplication by $i$ — i.e.\ it must be a similarity matrix (a rotation composed with a scaling). A short calculation shows this is equivalent to the Cauchy–Riemann equations

$$u_x = v_y, \qquad u_y = -v_x,$$

and then $f'(z_0) = u_x + i v_x = v_y - i u_y$.

Amplitwist. Needham's word: near $z_0$, a holomorphic map acts on infinitesimal vectors as rotation by $\arg f'(z_0)$ and scaling by $|f'(z_0)|$. That single complex number $f'(z_0)$ encodes both. Wherever $f'(z_0)\ne 0$, $f$ is conformal: it preserves angles between curves through $z_0$.

Compare: the map $z\mapsto \bar z$ is real-differentiable but not holomorphic. Its Jacobian is a reflection, not a rotation+scaling. Reflections reverse orientation, so $\bar z$ is "anti-conformal" — it preserves angle magnitudes but flips signs.

The amplitwist picture is what makes complex analysis qualitatively different from $\mathbb{R}^2$ analysis. Every theorem we are about to see is, in some sense, a topological consequence of "all infinitesimal motion is rotation + scaling."

3. Contour integration

A contour is a piecewise smooth curve $\gamma:[a,b]\to\mathbb{C}$. Given a continuous $f$ on its image, define

$$\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz \;=\; \int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\,\gamma'(t)\,dt.$$

Read $dz = dx + i\,dy$ as a complex-valued 1-form. Then $f\,dz = (u+iv)(dx+i\,dy) = (u\,dx - v\,dy) + i(v\,dx + u\,dy)$, two real line integrals.

The key observation: those two real 1-forms are closed exactly when $u,v$ satisfy Cauchy–Riemann. The "real part" 1-form $u\,dx - v\,dy$ has $d = (-v_x - u_y)\,dx\wedge dy$, which vanishes iff $u_y = -v_x$. The "imaginary part" 1-form $v\,dx + u\,dy$ has $d = (u_x - v_y)\,dx\wedge dy$, vanishing iff $u_x = v_y$. Both pieces are closed precisely when $f$ is holomorphic.

Curl-free, source-free. Think of $\overline{f(z)}$ as a vector field on the plane. Cauchy–Riemann equations say its divergence and curl both vanish: it is incompressible and irrotational. That is the physical content of "$f$ is holomorphic," and it is why integrals around closed loops vanish.

Cauchy's theorem (star-shaped form). If $f$ is holomorphic on a star-shaped open set $U$ and $\gamma\subset U$ is a closed contour, then $\oint_\gamma f\,dz = 0$.

Sketch: holomorphic $f$ on a star-shaped (in particular simply connected) domain has an antiderivative $F$ — define $F(z) = \int_{z_*}^z f$ along straight rays from the star centre. Then $\oint f\,dz = F(\text{end}) - F(\text{start}) = 0$ for a closed loop. Equivalently: by Stokes, $\oint_\gamma f\,dz = \iint_D df\wedge dz$, but $df = f'\,dz$ so $df\wedge dz = 0$.

The general (Goursat) version drops the $C^1$ hypothesis on $f'$ and works for any simply connected $U$. The proof bisects triangles and is elegant; we won't reproduce it here.

Cauchy's integral formula

If $f$ is holomorphic on a domain containing the closed disk $\overline{D(z_0,r)}$, then for every interior point $a\in D(z_0,r)$,

$$f(a) \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z-z_0|=r}\frac{f(z)}{z-a}\,dz.$$

The values of $f$ on the boundary determine $f$ in the interior — a phenomenon with no analogue in real analysis. Differentiating under the integral $n$ times,

$$f^{(n)}(a) \;=\; \frac{n!}{2\pi i}\oint \frac{f(z)}{(z-a)^{n+1}}\,dz.$$

Holomorphic functions are infinitely differentiable. That is the main miracle.

4. Consequences

From Cauchy's integral formula nearly everything follows.

Power series everywhere

If $f$ is holomorphic on $D(z_0,R)$, then for any $a\in D(z_0,R)$, the geometric series in $1/(z-a) = 1/((z-z_0) - (a-z_0))$ converges uniformly on $|z-z_0|=r$ for $|a-z_0|

$$f(a) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n (a-z_0)^n, \qquad c_n = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint \frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}dz = \frac{f^{(n)}(z_0)}{n!}.$$

Holomorphic ⟺ analytic, with radius of convergence equal to the distance from $z_0$ to the nearest singularity.

Liouville and FTA

Liouville's theorem. A bounded entire function is constant. Proof: by CIF for $f'$, $|f'(z_0)| \le M/r$ for any $r$, so $f'\equiv 0$.

Fundamental theorem of algebra. Every nonconstant polynomial $p(z)$ has a complex root. Proof 1: if $p$ never vanishes, $1/p$ is bounded entire (it tends to $0$ at infinity), hence constant by Liouville — absurd.

The geometric proof of FTA. Consider a circle of huge radius $R$. On it, $p(z)\sim a_n z^n$, so $p$ wraps $|z|=R$ around $0$ exactly $n$ times. Now shrink the circle to a point. At radius $0$, $p$ is constant $p(0)$, wrapping $0$ times. Winding number is integer-valued and varies continuously, so somewhere along the way it must cross zero — and that means $p$ hits $0$ at some intermediate $z$.

Morera, max modulus, open mapping, Schwarz

Morera. If $f$ is continuous on $U$ and $\oint_\Delta f = 0$ for every triangle $\Delta\subset U$, then $f$ is holomorphic. (Converse to Cauchy.)

Maximum modulus. If $f$ is holomorphic on $U$ and $|f|$ attains a local max at an interior point, $f$ is constant. (Hence on a compact domain, $|f|$ achieves its sup on the boundary.)

Open mapping. A nonconstant holomorphic map sends open sets to open sets. (One way to see it: $f-w_0$ has finitely many zeros, none on a small circle, and Rouché shows $f-w$ has the same number for all $w$ near $w_0$.)

Schwarz lemma. If $f:\mathbb{D}\to\mathbb{D}$ is holomorphic with $f(0)=0$, then $|f(z)|\le |z|$ and $|f'(0)|\le 1$. Equality at any interior $z$ forces $f(z) = e^{i\theta}z$.

5. Laurent series and residues

Drop the assumption that $f$ is holomorphic on the whole disc; allow an isolated singularity at $z_0$. On any annulus $0 < |z - z_0| < R$ on which $f$ is holomorphic, we have a unique Laurent expansion

$$f(z) \;=\; \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty c_n (z-z_0)^n.$$

The coefficients are still given by Cauchy's formula:

$$c_n = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z-z_0|=r}\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz.$$

Singularities are classified by which negative coefficients appear:

removable all $c_{n}=0$ for $n<0$ — $f$ extends holomorphically across $z_0$ (Riemann's removable-singularity theorem: this happens iff $f$ is bounded near $z_0$).

pole of order $k$ $c_{-k}\ne 0$ but $c_n=0$ for $n<-k$ — $f$ blows up like $1/(z-z_0)^k$.

essential infinitely many $c_n\ne 0$ for $n<0$. Casorati–Weierstrass: $f$ comes arbitrarily close to every complex value in any neighbourhood of $z_0$. Picard sharpens this dramatically: $f$ hits every value (with at most one exception), infinitely often.

The residue of $f$ at $z_0$ is

$$\operatorname{Res}(f,z_0) \;=\; c_{-1} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint f\,dz.$$

For a simple pole $\operatorname{Res}(f,z_0) = \lim_{z\to z_0}(z-z_0)f(z)$. For a pole of order $k$, $\operatorname{Res}(f,z_0) = \dfrac{1}{(k-1)!}\lim_{z\to z_0}\dfrac{d^{k-1}}{dz^{k-1}}\bigl((z-z_0)^k f(z)\bigr)$.

Residue theorem. Let $\gamma$ be a positively oriented simple closed contour bounding a region $D$, with $f$ holomorphic on a neighbourhood of $\overline D$ except for finitely many isolated singularities $z_k\in D$. Then $$\oint_\gamma f\,dz \;=\; 2\pi i\sum_k \operatorname{Res}(f, z_k).$$

Real definite integrals fall to this, almost mechanically. Famous example: closing $\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{dx}{x^2+1}$ in the upper half-plane with a giant semicircle. The semicircular piece tends to $0$ (the integrand decays), the only pole inside is $z=i$ with residue $1/(2i)$, and so the integral is $2\pi i\cdot 1/(2i) = \pi$. Replace the integrand by $e^{ix}/(x^2+1)$ and the answer is $\pi/e$.

Try it: the contour widget in §3 above already computes $\oint f\,dz$ as $2\pi i \sum\mathrm{Res}$ for the enclosed poles. Drag a vertex over a pole to see the sum jump.

6. Argument principle and Rouché

Apply the residue theorem to the logarithmic derivative $f'/f$. If $f$ is meromorphic with zeros $a_j$ of order $m_j$ and poles $b_k$ of order $n_k$, then $f'/f$ has simple poles at each $a_j$ with residue $m_j$, and at each $b_k$ with residue $-n_k$. Hence

$$\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f'(z)}{f(z)}\,dz \;=\; Z - P,$$

where $Z = \sum m_j$ counts zeros enclosed by $\gamma$ (with multiplicity) and $P = \sum n_k$ counts poles. This is the argument principle.

The geometric meaning: the integrand is $d(\log f) = d(\log|f|) + i\,d(\arg f)$. The real part integrates to $0$ around a closed loop (it is exact). The imaginary part integrates to $2\pi$ times the winding number of the image curve $f\circ\gamma$ around $0$. So $Z - P$ is exactly how many times the image of $\gamma$ winds around the origin.

Rouché's theorem. If $|f(z) - g(z)| < |f(z)|$ on a contour $\gamma$, then $f$ and $g$ have the same number of zeros inside. Picture: the image $g(\gamma)$ is "less than $|f(\gamma)|$ away" from $f(\gamma)$, so it cannot loop around $0$ a different number of times.

7. Conformal maps

A holomorphic map $f$ is conformal wherever $f'\ne 0$. Globally, a conformal isomorphism is a holomorphic bijection with holomorphic inverse. The principle theorem is overwhelming:

Riemann mapping theorem. Every simply connected proper open subset of $\mathbb{C}$ is conformally equivalent to the open unit disk $\mathbb{D}$. (And the equivalence is unique once you fix a base point and a tangent direction.)

Topology says the disk and any other simply connected domain are homeomorphic; the Riemann mapping theorem says they are holomorphically equivalent. The proof is non-constructive — the equivalence is the unique normalized solution to an extremal problem (maximize $|f'(z_0)|$ among holomorphic injections into $\mathbb{D}$).

Automorphisms

The conformal automorphisms of the disk are exactly

$$\mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{D}) = \left\{ z\mapsto e^{i\theta}\frac{z-a}{1-\bar a z} \;:\; \theta\in\mathbb{R},\, a\in\mathbb{D}\right\} \;\cong\; \mathrm{PSU}(1,1).$$

This is the isometry group of the hyperbolic plane, with the Poincaré metric $ds^2 = \dfrac{4|dz|^2}{(1-|z|^2)^2}$.

The Cayley transform $z\mapsto i\dfrac{1-z}{1+z}$ identifies the disk with the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H} = \{z : \mathrm{Im}\,z > 0\}$, whose automorphism group is $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ acting by Möbius transformations with real coefficients.

Joukowski and Schwarz–Christoffel

The Joukowski map $J(z) = \tfrac12(z + 1/z)$ has the celebrated property that it sends a circle through $z=1$ (or $z=-1$) to an "airfoil" shape — historically used in early 20th-century aerodynamics to compute flow around wings.

Schwarz–Christoffel maps express the Riemann map from the upper half-plane to a polygon explicitly:

$$f(z) = \int_0^z \prod_{k=1}^n (\zeta - x_k)^{\alpha_k - 1}\,d\zeta,$$

where $x_k\in\mathbb{R}$ are the pre-images of the polygon vertices and $\pi\alpha_k$ are the interior angles at those vertices.

8. Coda — harmonic functions, continuation, monodromy

If $f = u + iv$ is holomorphic, both $u$ and $v$ are harmonic: they satisfy Laplace's equation $\Delta u = u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0$. Conversely, any harmonic function on a simply connected domain is the real part of some holomorphic $f$, unique up to a real additive constant. The imaginary part $v$ is the harmonic conjugate of $u$; you reconstruct it by integrating the Cauchy–Riemann equations.

This identification is the bridge to potential theory: the steady-state heat distribution, the gravitational potential in vacuum, and the electrostatic potential in a charge-free region are all harmonic, hence locally real parts of holomorphic functions. Conformal maps then move boundary value problems between domains.

Analytic continuation. A holomorphic function on a small disk has a unique extension to any simply connected domain containing the disk on which the extension exists. (Identity theorem: two holomorphic functions agreeing on a set with an accumulation point agree everywhere on the connected component.) The natural domain of $\sqrt{z}$ or $\log z$ is not $\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}$ but a bigger object — a Riemann surface. Walking $\log z$ around a loop encircling $0$ adds $2\pi i$; that increment is the monodromy.

Riemann surfaces are the natural homes of multi-valued analytic functions. The compact ones are classified by genus; the simply connected ones by uniformization (every compact Riemann surface of genus $\ge 2$ is a quotient of the upper half-plane by a Fuchsian group). Modular forms — automorphic functions on the upper half-plane — pick up here. Forward pointer: a future modular-forms.html will continue this thread.

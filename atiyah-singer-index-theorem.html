The Atiyah–Singer index theorem

An elliptic operator $D$ on a closed manifold has two integers attached to it: the analytic index $\ind(D)=\dim\ker D-\dim\mathrm{coker}\,D$, computed by hard PDE; and the topological index, computed from the K-theory class of its symbol. Atiyah–Singer (1963) says the two agree, and the resulting cohomological pairing $\int_M\mathrm{ch}\cdot\mathrm{Td}$ packages Gauss–Bonnet, Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch, the signature theorem, and the $\hat A$-genus into a single equation.

1. The index theorem

Let $M$ be a closed (compact, no boundary) smooth manifold and $E,F\to M$ smooth complex vector bundles. A linear differential operator $D\colon\Gamma(E)\to\Gamma(F)$ is elliptic if its principal symbol is invertible off the zero section of $T^*M$ (made precise in §2). On a closed manifold, ellipticity forces $D$ to be Fredholm as a map between Sobolev completions, and so

$$\ind(D) \;=\; \dim\ker D \;-\; \dim\mathrm{coker}\,D \;\in\;\mathbb{Z}$$

is a finite integer. The miracle is that this analytic invariant is rigid: it only depends on the homotopy class of the principal symbol $\sigma(D)$, viewed as a K-theory class on $T^*M$.

The theorem (Atiyah–Singer 1963). For any elliptic $D$, $$\boxed{\;\ind(D) \;=\; \bigl\langle\,\mathrm{ch}\bigl([\sigma(D)]\bigr)\smile\mathrm{Td}\bigl(TM\otimes\mathbb{C}\bigr),\,[T^*M]\,\bigr\rangle.\;}$$ The left side is computed by counting solutions and obstructions of a PDE; the right side, by integrating universal characteristic-class polynomials over $T^*M$. Both sides are integers, and they agree.

What makes the equation interesting is the asymmetry between the two sides. The left side is genuinely about the operator: write down a different elliptic $D'$ with the same symbol class, and the kernel and cokernel will generally differ — but $\dim\ker-\dim\mathrm{coker}$ stays put. The right side is purely topological, mechanical to compute given the symbol and the topology of $M$. Atiyah–Singer is the bridge.

Index of a $1$-parameter family of $1\times 1$ operators
slide $\lambda$ — eigenvalues cross zero, $\dim\ker-\dim\mathrm{coker}$ stays fixed
$D_\lambda = -i\partial_\theta+\lambda$ on $S^1$. Sliding $\lambda$ shifts the eigenvalue lattice; eigenvalues cross zero, but the analytic index of the toy chiral operator $D=\partial_x+f(x,\lambda)$ stays fixed.

The integer $\ind(D)$ is therefore a homotopy invariant of the symbol — exactly the kind of object K-theory was built to measure.

2. Elliptic operators and the Fredholm property

A linear differential operator of order $m$, locally $$D \;=\; \sum_{|\alpha|\le m} a_\alpha(x)\,\partial^\alpha,$$ has principal symbol $\sigma_m(D)(x,\xi)=\sum_{|\alpha|=m}a_\alpha(x)(i\xi)^\alpha$, a polynomial in $\xi\in T_x^*M$ of degree exactly $m$. Globally $\sigma_m(D)$ is a bundle map $\pi^*E\to\pi^*F$ over $T^*M$, where $\pi\colon T^*M\to M$.

$D$ is elliptic if $\sigma_m(D)(x,\xi)$ is invertible for every $\xi\ne 0$. Three running examples:

operatorsymbolelliptic?
Laplacian $-\Delta$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$$|\xi|^2$yes — vanishes only at $\xi=0$
heat operator $\partial_t-\Delta$ on $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$$i\tau+|\xi|^2$no — vanishes when $\tau=0,\,\xi=0$ (parabolic)
Cauchy–Riemann $\bar\partial=\tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_x+i\partial_y)$$\tfrac{i}{2}(\xi_1+i\xi_2)$yes — non-zero off origin
Why ellipticity is Fredholm. Pseudodifferential calculus produces a parametrix $Q$ for any elliptic $D$: an operator with $QD=I-S$ and $DQ=I-T$ where $S,T$ are smoothing (and hence compact between any Sobolev spaces). Compactness of the error pieces is the technical core: $D\colon H^s(E)\to H^{s-m}(F)$ has finite-dimensional kernel, closed range with finite-codimensional cokernel, and so a well-defined index. Elliptic regularity then says $u\in\ker D\Rightarrow u\in C^\infty$ — kernels are smooth, not merely $L^2$.
Symbol of the Laplacian on $\mathbb{R}^2$
$\sigma_2(-\Delta)(\xi)=|\xi|^2$ — vanishes only at the origin
$-\Delta$: $\sigma_2(\xi)=\xi_1^2+\xi_2^2$ — vanishes only at the origin, invertible elsewhere. The zero set in the cotangent fibre is just $\{0\}$, the canonical signature of ellipticity.

3. The topological index via $\mathrm{ch}\cdot\mathrm{Td}$

The symbol of an elliptic operator is more than a function on $T^*M$: it is a bundle map $\sigma\colon\pi^*E\to\pi^*F$ that is an isomorphism over $T^*M\setminus 0$. Such data assemble into a compactly supported K-theory class $$[\sigma(D)] \;\in\; K^0_{\mathrm{c}}(T^*M).$$ Outside a compact subset of $T^*M$ the bundles $\pi^*E$ and $\pi^*F$ are identified by $\sigma$, so the difference $[\pi^*E]-[\pi^*F]$ is supported on a compact piece — exactly the data $K^0_{\mathrm{c}}$ records.

Once the symbol lives in K-theory, the topological index is constructed by a sequence of natural pushforwards. Choose a closed embedding $i\colon M\hookrightarrow\mathbb{R}^N$ (Whitney). Push forward $[\sigma(D)]$ first to $K^0_{\mathrm{c}}(T^*\mathbb{R}^N)\cong\mathbb{Z}$ via the K-theoretic Thom isomorphism, then read off the integer:

$$\ind_{\mathrm{top}}(D) \;=\; \int_{T^*M}\mathrm{ch}\bigl([\sigma(D)]\bigr)\cdot\mathrm{Td}\bigl(TM\otimes\mathbb{C}\bigr).$$

Why the Todd class. The K-theory pushforward and the ordinary-cohomology pushforward (integration of the Chern character) disagree. The discrepancy is the Todd genus of the normal bundle of the embedding, which after some manipulation reduces to $\mathrm{Td}(TM\otimes\mathbb{C})$. Demanding that the index be a homotopy invariant of $[\sigma(D)]$ alone forces this correction term — it is the K-theoretic Riemann–Roch defect.

The Todd polynomial in formal Chern roots $x_i$ of a complex bundle is $$\mathrm{Td}(E) \;=\; \prod_i \frac{x_i}{1-e^{-x_i}} \;=\; 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}c_1 + \tfrac{1}{12}(c_1^2 + c_2) + \tfrac{1}{24}c_1 c_2 + \cdots,$$ a power series in the Chern classes with rational coefficients. The first few are revealing: for a curve $X$, $\mathrm{Td}(TX)=1+\tfrac{1}{2}c_1(TX)$, and Riemann–Roch says $\chi(X,L)=\deg L+1-g$.

4. Classical specialisations

The point of bundling Gauss–Bonnet, Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch, and the signature theorem under one umbrella is that all three pick a specific elliptic operator $D$ and read off the right-hand side of $\ind(D)=\int\mathrm{ch}\cdot\mathrm{Td}$.

$d+d^*\colon\Omega^{\mathrm{ev}}\to\Omega^{\mathrm{odd}}$$\chi(M)$$\int_M e(TM)$ — Gauss–Bonnet–Chern

Gauss–Bonnet–Chern in dim 2. Take $M^2$ a closed oriented surface and $D=d+d^*$ between even and odd forms. Hodge theory gives $\dim\ker D=\dim H^0+\dim H^2$ and $\dim\mathrm{coker}\,D=\dim H^1$. So $\ind(D)=\chi(M)$, and the topological side is $\int_M e(TM)=\tfrac{1}{2\pi}\int_M K\,dA$. This is the classical Gauss–Bonnet theorem for surfaces.
Hirzebruch signature in dim 4. Take $M^4$ closed oriented; the signature operator on $\Omega^2_+\oplus\Omega^0\oplus\Omega^4$ has index $\sigma(M)$, the signature of the intersection form on $H^2$. The right side is $\int_M L_1(TM)=\tfrac{1}{3}\langle p_1(TM),[M]\rangle$, and so $\sigma(M)=\tfrac{1}{3}p_1[M]$. This forces $p_1$ to be divisible by $3$ on closed oriented $4$-manifolds.

Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch deserves separate treatment: on a closed complex manifold $X$ with a holomorphic vector bundle $E\to X$, the Dolbeault operator $\bar\partial_E$ from $(0,\mathrm{even})$- to $(0,\mathrm{odd})$-forms has index equal to the holomorphic Euler characteristic $\chi(X,E)=\sum(-1)^k\dim H^k(X,E)$. Atiyah–Singer says $$\chi(X,E) \;=\; \int_X \mathrm{ch}(E)\cdot\mathrm{Td}(TX).$$ For $X=\mathbb{P}^1$ and $E=\mathcal{O}(d)$ this collapses to $\chi(\mathbb{P}^1,\mathcal{O}(d))=d+1$. For a curve of genus $g$ and a line bundle $L$ of degree $d$, $\chi(L)=d+1-g$ — Riemann–Roch.

5. Dirac operators and the $\hat A$-genus

The Dirac operator is the cleanest setting for Atiyah–Singer. On a Riemannian spin manifold $M^{2n}$ the spinor bundle $S\to M$ splits as $S=S^+\oplus S^-$, and the Dirac operator $D\colon\Gamma(S^+)\to\Gamma(S^-)$ is built from the spin connection by $$D \;=\; \sum_i e_i\cdot\nabla_{e_i},$$ with $e_i\cdot$ acting by Clifford multiplication. Its principal symbol $\sigma(D)(x,\xi)=i\xi\cdot$ is invertible on $\xi\ne 0$ since Clifford multiplication squares to $-|\xi|^2$ — so $D$ is elliptic.

The Dirac index. Atiyah–Singer says $$\ind(D) \;=\; \int_M \hat A(TM), \qquad \hat A \;=\; \prod_i \frac{x_i/2}{\sinh(x_i/2)} \;=\; 1 - \tfrac{1}{24}p_1 + \tfrac{1}{5760}(7p_1^2 - 4p_2) + \cdots,$$ where $x_i$ are formal Pontryagin roots and $p_i$ are Pontryagin classes. On a closed spin $4$-manifold, $\ind(D)=-\langle p_1(TM),[M]\rangle/24$. By the signature theorem, this equals $-\sigma(M)/8$, recovering Rokhlin's $\sigma(M)\equiv 0\pmod{16}$ for spin $4$-manifolds.

The Dirac operator's spectrum encodes geometric information directly. On the round $n$-sphere with metric of curvature $1$, $D^2=\nabla^*\nabla+\tfrac{R}{4}$ (Lichnerowicz), and the eigenvalues are explicitly $$\lambda_k \;=\; \pm\bigl(k+\tfrac{n}{2}\bigr), \qquad k=0,1,2,\ldots,$$ with multiplicities given by representation-theoretic Weyl-dimension formulas. None of the eigenvalues are zero, so $\ker D=0$ and $\ind(D)=0$ — consistent with $\hat A(S^n)=0$ for $n\ge 1$.

Lichnerowicz vanishing. If $M$ is a closed spin manifold admitting a metric with strictly positive scalar curvature, then $\ker D=0$ and so $\hat A(M)=\ind(D)=0$. This is a sharp obstruction: the K3 surface has $\hat A=2$, hence carries no positive-scalar-curvature metric — even though $K3$ is simply connected and topologically tame.

slide the radius — eigenvalues $\pm(k+n/2)/r$ rescale, but the index stays $0$

6. Heat kernel, families, equivariant, anomalies

The heat-kernel proof

McKean–Singer (1967): for a Dirac-type operator $D$ on a closed manifold, $$\ind(D) \;=\; \tr\bigl(e^{-tD^*D}\bigr) - \tr\bigl(e^{-tDD^*}\bigr), \qquad \forall\,t>0.$$ The right-hand side is independent of $t$ because non-zero eigenvalues of $D^*D$ pair with equal eigenvalues of $DD^*$ (via $u\mapsto Du/\|Du\|$) and contribute opposite signs that cancel. Sending $t\to 0$, the heat-kernel asymptotic $$K_{D^2}(t,x,x) \;\sim\; \sum_{k\ge 0} t^{(k-n)/2}\,a_k(x)$$ produces a local index density $a_n(x)$ whose integral over $M$ gives $\ind(D)$. After cancellation of the lower-order coefficients (a miraculous algebraic identity — Getzler's symbol calculus is the cleanest derivation), what survives is exactly $\hat A(TM)\,\mathrm{ch}(E/S)$.

The families index theorem

Now let $D=\{D_b\}_{b\in B}$ be a family of elliptic operators, smoothly parametrised by $b$ ranging over a compact base $B$. Naively the kernels and cokernels at different $b$ are vector spaces of varying dimension. After a small perturbation they assemble into a virtual bundle on $B$: $$\ind_{\text{fam}}(D) \;=\; [\ker D] - [\mathrm{coker}\,D] \;\in\; K^0(B).$$ The families index theorem of Atiyah–Singer (1971): this class is computed by a fibre integration of $\mathrm{ch}\cdot\mathrm{Td}$ in cohomology of the total space. When $B=\mathrm{pt}$ this collapses to the single-operator theorem.

Equivariant index

If a compact Lie group $G$ acts on $M$ and the operator $D$ is $G$-equivariant, $\ker D$ and $\mathrm{coker}\,D$ are finite-dimensional $G$-representations. The $G$-equivariant index $$\ind_G(D) \;\in\; R(G)$$ lives in the representation ring of $G$. Atiyah–Bott's fixed-point formula computes its character at $g\in G$ as a sum of contributions localised at the fixed-point set $M^g$.

Anomalies and instantons

In gauge theory on a closed $4$-manifold $M$, the Dirac operator coupled to a gauge connection $A$ on a vector bundle $V\to M$ has index $$\ind(D_A) \;=\; \int_M \hat A(TM)\,\mathrm{ch}(V) \;=\; \int_M c_2(V) - \tfrac{1}{24}\langle p_1(TM),[M]\rangle\,\mathrm{rk}(V) + \cdots.$$ The leading $\int c_2(V)$ is the instanton number — the topological charge of the gauge field, classifying $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ bundles up to isomorphism. The chiral anomaly in physics literature is exactly the statement that $\ind(D_A)$ does not vanish: the symmetry between left- and right-handed fermions is broken by the topology of the gauge bundle, by an integer the index theorem computes.

Connections

Atiyah–Singer is a hub theorem: it pulls together K-theory, characteristic classes, elliptic PDE, spin geometry, and gauge-theoretic physics into one accountancy. The frontier is broad — equivariant K-theory of loop spaces (Witten genus), index theory on non-compact and singular spaces (Atiyah–Patodi–Singer eta invariants and APS boundary conditions), $L^2$-index theorems for covering spaces (Atiyah–Connes), and the recent NCG / coarse-index programme (Connes–Higson–Roe) where Atiyah–Singer becomes a special case of an index in $K$-homology paired against $K$-theory.

  • K-theory — applications — supplies the topological-index home $K^0(T^*M)$ and the Bott periodicity that makes the construction natural.
  • Chern–Weil theory — provides the de Rham representatives $\mathrm{ch}$ and $\mathrm{Td}$ that get integrated.
  • PDE — elliptic regularity — the analytic underpinning that makes $\dim\ker$ and $\dim\mathrm{coker}$ finite in the first place.
  • Differential geometry — connections, curvature, and spin structures are the inputs to the Dirac operator.
  • Spectral theory — Fredholm-operator framework on Hilbert spaces, the abstract setting in which the index is defined.

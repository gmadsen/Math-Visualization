Algebraic cycles modulo rational equivalence: Chow rings, Chern classes, and Bezout generalized.

1. Algebraic cycles and rational equivalence

Fix a variety (or scheme) $X$ over a field $k$. The group of algebraic $k$-cycles $Z_k(X)$ is the free abelian group on closed irreducible subvarieties of dimension $k$. A cycle $\alpha \in Z_k(X)$ is therefore a finite formal sum $\sum n_i [V_i]$ with $n_i \in \mathbb{Z}$ and each $V_i \subseteq X$ a closed subvariety of dimension exactly $k$. Note the dimension grading is by algebraic dimension; the codimension grading $Z^c(X) = Z_{\dim X - c}(X)$ is the one used when $X$ is equidimensional.

Naked cycles are too rigid: $[\{0\}]$ and $[\{1\}]$ are different elements of $Z_0(\mathbb{A}^1)$, even though every reasonable invariant should regard them as the "same" $0$-cycle. The remedy is rational equivalence. Two $k$-cycles $\alpha, \alpha' \in Z_k(X)$ are rationally equivalent, $\alpha \sim_{\mathrm{rat}} \alpha'$, if there exist $(k+1)$-dimensional subvarieties $W_j \subseteq X$ and rational functions $f_j \in k(W_j)^\times$ such that

$$ \alpha - \alpha' \;=\; \sum_j \mathrm{div}_{W_j}(f_j) \;\in\; Z_k(X). $$

Each $\mathrm{div}_{W_j}(f_j)$ is the principal Weil divisor on $W_j$ pushed forward to $X$ — a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of the codimension-one subvarieties of $W_j$, weighted by the order of vanishing or pole of $f_j$ along each. The slogan is: rational equivalence is the connecting homotopy in the world of algebraic cycles, with $\mathbb{P}^1$ playing the role of the unit interval. Indeed, $\alpha \sim_{\mathrm{rat}} \alpha'$ iff there is a cycle $\Gamma$ on $X \times \mathbb{P}^1$ (flat over $\mathbb{P}^1$ on each component) whose fibre over $0$ is $\alpha$ and whose fibre over $\infty$ is $\alpha'$.

Concretely on $\mathbb{P}^1$ itself: any two distinct points are rationally equivalent, because the rational function $f(t) = t - a$ on $\mathbb{P}^1$ has $\mathrm{div}(f) = [a] - [\infty]$ — proving $[a] \sim [\infty]$ for every $a \in \mathbb{A}^1$, hence all $0$-cycles of degree $1$ on $\mathbb{P}^1$ collapse to a single class. The widget below walks through the core example: a one-parameter family $t \mapsto [1 : t]$ that degenerates two distinct points on $\mathbb{P}^1$ into a single doubled point, exhibiting the rational equivalence as a divisor on a $1$-cycle (the family graph) sitting inside $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$. Compare this with $\bezout$'s degree count and with the cycle classes that show up as fundamental classes in $\schemes$ — rational equivalence is what turns subvarieties into "homology-like" classes.

2. Chow groups $A_*(X)$

Quotienting out by rational equivalence packages every cycle on $X$ into a graded abelian group:

$$ A_k(X) \;=\; Z_k(X) \big/ \mathrm{Rat}_k(X), \qquad A_*(X) \;=\; \bigoplus_{k \ge 0} A_k(X). $$

For smooth $X$ of pure dimension $n$ we re-grade by codimension $A^c(X) = A_{n-c}(X)$ and write $A^*(X) = \bigoplus_c A^c(X)$. The intersection product (next section) makes $A^*(X)$ a graded ring; without smoothness one only has $A_*$ as a covariantly-functorial graded group with a cap-product action.

Three computations to internalise. First: $A_*(\mathbb{A}^n) = A_n(\mathbb{A}^n) \cdot [\mathbb{A}^n] \cong \mathbb{Z}$ — every cycle of dimension $< n$ is rationally equivalent to $0$ via translation in the family $\mathbb{A}^n \times \mathbb{A}^1 \to \mathbb{A}^n$ (push everything to infinity). Second: $A_*(\mathbb{P}^n) \cong \mathbb{Z}^{n+1}$, generated by the class $[L^k]$ of a linear subspace of dimension $k$ for each $0 \le k \le n$. Third (functoriality): a proper morphism $f\colon X \to Y$ induces a covariant push-forward $f_*\colon A_k(X) \to A_k(Y)$ via $f_*[V] = \deg(V/f(V)) \cdot [f(V)]$ (with $\deg = 0$ when $V \to f(V)$ is not generically finite); a flat morphism induces a contravariant pull-back $f^*\colon A_k(Y) \to A_{k+r}(X)$ where $r$ is the relative dimension.

Conceptually, $A_*$ is the algebraic analogue of singular homology: there is a cycle map $\mathrm{cl}\colon A_*(X) \to H_{2*}^{\mathrm{BM}}(X(\mathbb{C}); \mathbb{Z})$ to Borel–Moore homology when $X$ is over $\mathbb{C}$, but it is generally far from injective or surjective — the kernel detects "algebraic but homologically trivial" cycles (Griffiths' results on the Fano threefold), and the cokernel reflects transcendental classes invisible to algebraic geometry. 3. Intersection product on smooth $X$

For a smooth variety $X$ of dimension $n$, the Chow group $A^*(X)$ carries a graded-commutative intersection product

$$ \cdot \;:\; A^p(X) \otimes A^q(X) \longrightarrow A^{p+q}(X) $$

characterised on transverse pairs by counting set-theoretic intersection points with multiplicity. Concretely, if $Y \subset X$ is a subvariety of codimension $p$ and $Z \subset X$ is of codimension $q$, and if $Y, Z$ meet transversally (every component of $Y \cap Z$ has the expected codimension $p + q$ and the tangent spaces span $T_x X$ at every smooth point of intersection), then $$ [Y] \cdot [Z] \;=\; [Y \cap Z] \;\in\; A^{p+q}(X). $$ When $p + q = n$ the right-hand side is a $0$-cycle, and one gets a numerical intersection number $\#(Y \cdot Z) \in \mathbb{Z}$ by passing to degree (i.e. push-forward to $\mathrm{Spec}\, k$).

The non-transverse case is the whole game. Two cycles need not meet transversally — even worse, $[Y] \cdot [Y]$ asks for the self-intersection, which is meaningless set-theoretically. The moving lemma (Chow, Roberts) rescues this on smooth quasi-projective $X$: every cycle class is rationally equivalent to one in general position with any fixed cycle, so we can replace one of $Y, Z$ by an equivalent cycle that does meet the other transversally and then count points. Well-definedness is the content of the moving-lemma proof. On singular $X$ the moving lemma fails in general — one has to descend to operational Chow groups or work with the bivariant theory of Fulton–MacPherson.

The local intersection multiplicity at an isolated component $W$ of $Y \cap Z$ is computed by Serre's formula $$ i(Y, Z; W) \;=\; \sum_{i \ge 0} (-1)^i \, \mathrm{length}_{\mathcal{O}_{X,W}} \mathrm{Tor}^{\mathcal{O}_{X,W}}_i(\mathcal{O}_Y, \mathcal{O}_Z). $$ When $Y, Z$ are Cohen–Macaulay and meet in the expected codimension only the $i=0$ term survives, giving the geometric multiplicity $\mathrm{length}\, \mathcal{O}_{X,W}/(I_Y + I_Z)$. 4. $A^*(\mathbb{P}^n) = \mathbb{Z}[h]/(h^{n+1})$

Among all smooth varieties, projective space has the simplest Chow ring imaginable: a truncated polynomial ring on a single generator. Let $h \in A^1(\mathbb{P}^n)$ denote the class of a hyperplane $H \subset \mathbb{P}^n$. Then

$$ A^*(\mathbb{P}^n) \;=\; \mathbb{Z}[h] \,\big/\, (h^{n+1}), \qquad A^k(\mathbb{P}^n) = \mathbb{Z} \cdot h^k \quad (0 \le k \le n). $$

The geometric content of $h^k$ is the class of any codimension-$k$ linear subspace $L^{n-k} \subset \mathbb{P}^n$ — every two such are rationally equivalent (move them via a linear family). The relation $h^{n+1} = 0$ is forced by dimension: a codimension-$(n+1)$ subvariety of $\mathbb{P}^n$ doesn't exist (it would have negative dimension), so its class is $0$.

The intersection product is multiplication of polynomials in $h$ followed by truncation. Two effective hypersurfaces of degrees $d_1, d_2$ have classes $d_1 h$ and $d_2 h$, so they intersect in the class $d_1 d_2 \, h^2$. For $n = 2$ this is exactly Bézout's theorem: $C \cdot D = (\deg C)(\deg D)$ points (with multiplicity) in $\mathbb{P}^2$. For general $n$, intersecting $n$ hypersurfaces of degrees $d_1, \ldots, d_n$ in general position gives $$ d_1 h \cdot d_2 h \cdots d_n h \;=\; (d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n) \, h^n \;\in\; A^n(\mathbb{P}^n) = \mathbb{Z}, $$ recovering the projective Bézout count. Higher products of the same generator pile up "schubert-like" classes: $h^k$ has degree $1$ as the class of a codimension-$k$ linear space, and the intersection $h^a \cdot h^b = h^{a+b}$ if $a + b \le n$, else $0$.

The proof uses the affine cell decomposition $\mathbb{P}^n = \mathbb{A}^n \sqcup \mathbb{A}^{n-1} \sqcup \cdots \sqcup \mathbb{A}^0$ (Schubert cells), Quillen's affine bundle lemma $A_*(\mathbb{A}^n) = \mathbb{Z}$, and the localization sequence to assemble $A^*(\mathbb{P}^n)$ from the cells. 5. Chern classes

A rank-$r$ vector bundle $E$ on a smooth variety $X$ carries an axiomatically-defined sequence of Chern classes $c_i(E) \in A^i(X)$ for $i = 0, 1, \ldots, r$, packaged into the total Chern class $$ c(E) \;=\; 1 + c_1(E) + c_2(E) + \cdots + c_r(E) \;\in\; A^*(X). $$ Concretely, $c_i(E)$ is the class of the locus where $r - i + 1$ generic sections of $E$ become linearly dependent — the obstruction to extending $i$ chosen sections to a frame on a Zariski-open set.

The Grothendieck axioms pin them down uniquely:

  • Naturality. $c_i(f^*E) = f^* c_i(E)$ for any morphism $f\colon Y \to X$.
  • Whitney sum / multiplicativity. For every short exact sequence $0 \to E' \to E \to E'' \to 0$ of vector bundles on $X$, the total Chern classes multiply: $$ c(E) \;=\; c(E') \cdot c(E'') \;\in\; A^*(X). $$ Equivalently, in degrees: $c_k(E) = \sum_{i+j=k} c_i(E') \, c_j(E'')$.
  • Normalisation. For a line bundle $L$, $c(L) = 1 + c_1(L)$, where $c_1(L)$ is the class of the divisor of zeros of any rational section.

Multiplicativity is the lever that makes Chern classes computable. Given a filtration $0 = E_0 \subset E_1 \subset \cdots \subset E_r = E$ by sub-bundles with line-bundle quotients $L_i = E_i / E_{i-1}$, the total Chern class factors as $$ c(E) \;=\; \prod_{i=1}^r (1 + \alpha_i), \qquad \alpha_i = c_1(L_i). $$ The classes $\alpha_i$ are the Chern roots of $E$. They live not in $A^*(X)$ but in the cohomology of an auxiliary "splitting principle" cover; the elementary symmetric functions of the $\alpha_i$ recover $c_i(E)$. Anything you want to compute about $E$ — Chern character, Todd class, top Chern class as Euler class — comes from these Chern roots.

6. Grothendieck–Riemann–Roch (preview)

The classical Riemann–Roch theorem on a smooth projective curve $C$ of genus $g$ asserts that for any divisor $D$, $$ \chi(C, \mathcal{O}(D)) \;=\; \deg D + 1 - g. $$ For a smooth surface $S$ and an invertible sheaf $\mathcal{O}(D)$, the analogue is $$ \chi(S, \mathcal{O}(D)) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} D \cdot (D - K_S) + \chi(S, \mathcal{O}_S). $$ Each formula is an Euler-characteristic computation expressed in terms of intersection numbers and a discrete invariant of $X$ (genus, canonical class, Euler characteristic). Grothendieck–Riemann–Roch is the statement that uniformly produces every such formula from a single functorial equation.

The data: define the Chern character by $\mathrm{ch}(E) = \sum_i e^{\alpha_i} \in A^*(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ where the $\alpha_i$ are the Chern roots; concretely $\mathrm{ch}(E) = \mathrm{rk}(E) + c_1 + \tfrac12(c_1^2 - 2c_2) + \cdots$. Define the Todd class by $\mathrm{td}(E) = \prod_i \tfrac{\alpha_i}{1 - e^{-\alpha_i}}$; its first few terms are $\mathrm{td}(E) = 1 + \tfrac12 c_1 + \tfrac1{12}(c_1^2 + c_2) + \cdots$. Both are multiplicative in short exact sequences (after suitable interpretation), and both extend to the Grothendieck group $K_0(X)$ — the abelian group generated by coherent sheaves modulo the relation $[\mathcal{F}] = [\mathcal{F}'] + [\mathcal{F}'']$ for short exact sequences.

The theorem: for a proper morphism $f\colon X \to Y$ of smooth varieties and a coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, $$ \boxed{ \;\mathrm{ch}(f_* \mathcal{F}) \cdot \mathrm{td}(T_Y) \;=\; f_* \bigl(\mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{F}) \cdot \mathrm{td}(T_X)\bigr) \;\in\; A^*(Y) \otimes \mathbb{Q}. \;} $$ When $Y = \mathrm{Spec}\, k$, the left-hand side is just $\chi(X, \mathcal{F}) \cdot 1$ and the right-hand side is the Hirzebruch–Riemann–Roch formula $\int_X \mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{F}) \cdot \mathrm{td}(T_X)$. Specialising further to curves recovers the classical Riemann–Roch; specialising to surfaces gives Noether's formula and the surface Riemann–Roch above.

Sums of squares

Which integers are sums of two, three, or four squares? The answers run through Fermat's descent, the Gaussian integers, Legendre–Gauss, Lagrange, and finally the generating function $\theta(\tau)^k$ that connects it all to modular forms.

1. The question

For $k\ge 1$ and $n\ge 0$, let $$r_k(n)=\#\bigl\{(x_1,\ldots,x_k)\in\mathbb{Z}^k : x_1^2+\cdots+x_k^2=n\bigr\},$$ the number of ways to write $n$ as an ordered sum of $k$ integer squares (with signs and zero allowed). The natural questions split in two:

  • Qualitative. For which $n$ is $r_k(n)>0$?
  • Quantitative. Can we compute $r_k(n)$ in closed form?

The goal is to understand why these patterns appear, first by elementary number theory (Fermat, Gauss, Lagrange) and then by recognising the $r_k(n)$ as Fourier coefficients of a modular form.

2. Fermat's two-squares theorem

The key prime-level result, conjectured by Girard (1625), claimed by Fermat (1640), and proved by Euler by infinite descent (1749):

Theorem (Fermat). An odd prime $p$ is a sum of two squares, $$p=a^2+b^2,\qquad a,b\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0},$$ if and only if $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$. The representation is then unique up to order and signs. (The prime $p=2=1^2+1^2$ is the exceptional case.)

The easy direction

If $p=a^2+b^2$ with $p$ odd, reduce mod $4$: squares are $0$ or $1$, so $a^2+b^2\in\{0,1,2\}\pmod 4$. Since $p$ is odd, we need $a^2+b^2\equiv 1\pmod 4$, hence $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$.

The hard direction: Euler's descent

Assume $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$. Then $-1$ is a quadratic residue mod $p$ (by Euler's criterion, $(-1)^{(p-1)/2}=1$), so there is $m$ with $m^2\equiv -1\pmod p$, giving $m^2+1^2\equiv 0\pmod p$. Thus $p\mid a^2+b^2$ for some $a,b$ not both divisible by $p$. Now the descent: starting from a multiple $kp=a^2+b^2$ with $k

3. Why it works: the Gaussian integers

Fermat's theorem is the surface of a deeper structural fact. Pass from $\mathbb{Z}$ to the Gaussian integers $$\mathbb{Z}[i]=\{a+bi : a,b\in\mathbb{Z}\}\subset\mathbb{C}.$$ The norm $N(a+bi)=a^2+b^2$ is multiplicative, and $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ is a Euclidean domain (division with remainder in $\mathbb{C}$, then round to nearest Gaussian integer), hence a unique factorization domain. Writing $p=a^2+b^2$ means exactly $p=(a+bi)(a-bi)$, i.e. the rational prime $p$ factors in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$.

Whether a rational prime $p$ factors in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ is controlled by the splitting behaviour of the ideal $(p)$:

$p$behaviour in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$factorisationcondition
$2$ramified$(2)=(1+i)^2\cdot(\text{unit})$$p=2$
$p\equiv 1\pmod 4$split$(p)=\mathfrak{p}\bar{\mathfrak{p}}$$(-1/p)=+1$
$p\equiv 3\pmod 4$inert$(p)$ stays prime$(-1/p)=-1$

The splitting is controlled by the Legendre symbol $(-1/p)$, which is $+1$ iff $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$ by Euler's criterion — a special case of the broader machinery developed on the quadratic reciprocity page. The theorem of Fermat is then a one-line corollary:

$p$ splits in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ iff $p=\pi\bar\pi=N(\pi)=a^2+b^2$. And $p$ splits iff $(-1/p)=+1$ iff $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$.

Visually: the Gaussian integers form the square lattice in $\mathbb{C}$. The primes $\pi\in\mathbb{Z}[i]$ above a rational prime $p$ are exactly the lattice points on the circle $|\alpha|^2=p$. For $p=5$ there are eight such points ($(\pm 1\pm 2i)$ and $(\pm 2\pm i)$, giving four associate pairs — one prime up to units); for $p=3$ the circle $|\alpha|^2=3$ contains no lattice points at all.

4. Two squares for general $n$

Multiplicativity in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ lets us read off the answer for composite $n$. Factor $n=2^{e_0}\prod_{p\equiv 1} p^{e_p}\prod_{q\equiv 3} q^{f_q}$.

Theorem. $n$ is a sum of two squares iff every prime $q\equiv 3\pmod 4$ appears in $n$ to an even power — i.e. every $f_q$ is even.

Reason: each $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$ splits as $\pi\bar\pi$, so $p^{e_p}=(\pi\bar\pi)^{e_p}$ is freely a norm. Each $q\equiv 3\pmod 4$ is inert; $q^{f_q}$ has norm $q^{2f_q}$ inside $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, and to be a norm of a single Gaussian integer it must come in pairs, forcing $f_q$ even. The prime $2$ is ramified and never an obstruction.

Jacobi's counting formula

More precisely, counting ordered signed representations:

Theorem (Jacobi). For $n\ge 1$, $$r_2(n)=4\bigl(d_1(n)-d_3(n)\bigr),$$ where $d_j(n)$ is the number of positive divisors of $n$ congruent to $j\pmod 4$.

E.g. $n=25$: divisors $1,5,25$, all $\equiv 1\pmod 4$, so $r_2(25)=4(3-0)=12$. And indeed $25=0^2+(\pm 5)^2=(\pm 3)^2+(\pm 4)^2$, which with ordering gives $4+8=12$. The factor of $4$ is the unit group $\{\pm 1,\pm i\}$ of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$.

5. Three squares: Legendre–Gauss

Sums of three squares are more delicate — they do not form a semigroup under multiplication. Legendre (1798) and Gauss (1801) settled the question:

Theorem (Legendre–Gauss). A non-negative integer $n$ is a sum of three squares iff $n$ is not of the form $4^a(8b+7)$ for some $a,b\ge 0$.

The easy direction: if $n=x^2+y^2+z^2$, reduce mod $8$. Squares mod $8$ are $\{0,1,4\}$, so sums of three squares mod $8$ miss the value $7$; a quick 2-adic argument then rules out $4^a(8b+7)$. The hard direction requires the theory of ternary quadratic forms and genus theory; Gauss showed that $r_3(n)$ is essentially a class number of imaginary quadratic orders — a remarkable bridge to algebraic number theory.

Gauss's exact formula: for $n$ squarefree, $n>3$, $$r_3(n)=\begin{cases} 12\,h(-4n), & n\equiv 1,2\pmod 4,\\ 24\,h(-n), & n\equiv 3\pmod 8,\\ 0, & n\equiv 7\pmod 8,\end{cases}$$ where $h(D)$ is the class number of discriminant $D$. This is the first appearance of a class number in the count of something concrete.

6. Four squares: Lagrange

Finally, everything becomes a sum of four squares:

Theorem (Lagrange, 1770). Every non-negative integer $n$ is a sum of four integer squares: $$n=a^2+b^2+c^2+d^2.$$

The standard modern proof mirrors Fermat's descent:

  1. Multiplicativity. Sums of four squares are closed under multiplication, via Euler's four-square identity $$(a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2+a_4^2)(b_1^2+b_2^2+b_3^2+b_4^2) = c_1^2+c_2^2+c_3^2+c_4^2$$ for explicit $c_i$ that are bilinear in $a,b$. (This is the norm form of the Hurwitz quaternions $\mathbb{H}_\mathbb{Z}$, exactly as Brahmagupta–Fibonacci is the norm form of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$.)
  2. Reduce to primes. By (1), it suffices to show every prime $p$ is a sum of four squares.
  3. Prime step. For odd prime $p$, the sets $\{a^2\bmod p\}$ and $\{-1-b^2\bmod p\}$ each have $(p+1)/2$ elements, so they intersect: $\exists a,b$ with $a^2+b^2\equiv -1\pmod p$, i.e. $mp=a^2+b^2+1$ for some $m

7. Waring's problem

If four squares suffice for every $n$, what about higher powers? Waring asked (1770): is there, for each $k\ge 1$, a finite number $g(k)$ such that every positive integer is a sum of at most $g(k)$ $k$-th powers?

Theorem (Hilbert, 1909). $g(k)$ is finite for every $k\ge 1$.

Sharper variants:

  • $g(k)$ — the smallest $s$ such that every $n\ge 1$ is a sum of $s$ $k$-th powers.
  • $G(k)$ — the smallest $s$ such that every sufficiently large $n$ is a sum of $s$ $k$-th powers. $G(k)\le g(k)$, with strict inequality the interesting case.

8. Forward: $\theta$-series and modular forms

All of these counts $r_k(n)$ are Fourier coefficients of a single object. Define the Jacobi theta series $$\theta(\tau)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} q^{n^2},\qquad q=e^{2\pi i\tau},\ \tau\in\mathbb{H}.$$ Squaring the series and counting coefficients by total exponent, $$\theta(\tau)^k = \sum_{n\ge 0} r_k(n)\,q^n.$$ So every question about $r_k(n)$ is a question about the $q$-expansion of $\theta^k$.

The key miracle, due to Jacobi, is that $\theta(\tau)$ is a modular form of weight $1/2$ on a congruence subgroup — more precisely, $\theta(\tau)$ satisfies functional equations under $\tau\mapsto \tau+1$ (trivially) and $\tau\mapsto -1/4\tau$ (via Poisson summation). Hence $\theta^k$ is a modular form of weight $k/2$, and the ring of modular forms on $\Gamma_0(4)$ is finite-dimensional in each weight. This forces identities like $\theta^4=1+8\sum_{d}\sigma^{(2)}(d) q^d$ (an Eisenstein series), recovering Jacobi's $r_4(n)=8\sum_{d\mid n,\,4\nmid d}d$ for free.

The same machinery powers:

  • $r_6(n)$, $r_8(n)$, $\ldots$ — closed forms from Eisenstein series of higher weight;
  • the deep formula for $r_3(n)$ in terms of class numbers (a weight-$3/2$ modular form);
  • and, transposing to elliptic curves, the modularity theorem for $E/\mathbb{Q}$.

Two pages in this notebook will develop the tools:

  • theta-functions.html — $\theta$ as an entire object: transformation laws, Jacobi's triple product, applications to partitions.
  • modular-forms.html — modular forms for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ and congruence subgroups, Eisenstein and cusp forms, Hecke operators, and the identification of $\theta^k$.

So: the puzzle of which integers are sums of $k$ squares, which took two hundred years to untangle by elementary means, turns out to be one equation among generating functions on the upper half-plane — a story continued at upper-half-plane-hyperbolic.html and onward.

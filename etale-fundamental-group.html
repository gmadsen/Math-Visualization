Etale fundamental group

$\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(X, \bar{x})$: Grothendieck's Galois theory unifying covers and fields.

1. Finite étale covers

A finite étale cover $f\colon Y \to X$ is a morphism of schemes that is finite, flat, and unramified — equivalently, finite and smooth of relative dimension zero. Locally on $X$, the structure sheaf $f_*\mathcal{O}_Y$ is a locally free $\mathcal{O}_X$-algebra of constant rank $n$ (the degree of the cover), and at every geometric point $\bar{x}$ the fiber $Y_{\bar{x}}$ is a finite étale $\bar{k}(\bar{x})$-algebra — a product of $n$ copies of the residue field $\bar{k}(\bar{x})$.

Why these three conditions? Finite ensures fibers are 0-dimensional, i.e. honest finite sets; flat guarantees that the rank of $f_*\mathcal{O}_Y$ is locally constant, so the cover doesn't gain or lose sheets as we move; and unramified means the relative cotangent sheaf $\Omega^1_{Y/X}$ vanishes — there is no ramification, no infinitesimal collapse of sheets at any point. Together, étaleness is the algebro-geometric translation of "local homeomorphism with finite fibers." Over $\mathbb{C}$, an étale morphism between smooth varieties induces a topological covering map on $\mathbb{C}$-points (Riemann existence).

The category $\mathrm{F\acute{E}t}/X$ — finite étale covers of $X$ with morphisms over $X$ — is closed under fiber products, disjoint unions, quotients by finite group actions, and contains the trivial covers $\bigsqcup_{i=1}^n X \to X$. It is a Galois category in Grothendieck's sense once we choose a geometric point: that structure is what powers the fundamental group.

Worked example. Take $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[t, t^{-1}] = \mathbb{G}_{m,k}$, the multiplicative group over a field $k$ with $\mathrm{char}(k) \nmid n$. The map $Y \to X$, $Y = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[s, s^{-1}]$ with $s^n = t$, is finite étale of degree $n$ — the algebraic version of the $n$-th-power covering of the circle by itself. The fiber over a geometric point $t = a$ is the set of $n$-th roots of $a$ in $\bar{k}$, a torsor under $\mu_n(\bar{k})$. Over $\mathbb{F}_p$ this fails for $n = p$ (the cover $s^p = t$ is purely inseparable, hence not étale) — a foreshadowing of the wild-ramification subtleties that don't arise in characteristic 0.

2. The fiber functor at a geometric point

A geometric point of $X$ is a morphism $\bar{x}\colon \mathrm{Spec}\,\Omega \to X$ where $\Omega$ is a separably closed field — concretely, the choice of an algebraic-closure point lying over a scheme-theoretic point of $X$. It plays the role of a topological basepoint, but with the extra data of an embedding of residue fields into a fixed algebraic closure (different geometric points over the same scheme-theoretic point give canonically isomorphic, non-equal fiber functors).

Each geometric point $\bar{x}$ defines the fiber functor $$F_{\bar{x}}\colon \mathrm{F\acute{E}t}/X \longrightarrow \mathrm{FinSet}, \qquad (Y \to X)\;\mapsto\;\mathrm{Hom}_X(\bar{x}, Y) = Y_{\bar{x}}(\Omega).$$ On objects, $F_{\bar{x}}(Y)$ is the (finite) set of $\Omega$-points of $Y$ lying over $\bar{x}$. On morphisms, $F_{\bar{x}}(g\colon Y\to Y')$ is the induced map of fibers. Faithfulness, exactness, and the fact that the values are finite sets are exactly what make $\mathrm{F\acute{É}t}/X$ a Galois category (SGA 1, V).

The functor packs all the cover data into a single combinatorial gadget: knowing $F_{\bar{x}}$ on every cover plus how $F_{\bar{x}}$ acts on morphisms determines the cover up to isomorphism. The étale fundamental group is then the symmetry of this packaging — the automorphisms of $F_{\bar{x}}$ as a functor.

Worked example. Take $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Q}$, $\bar{x} = \mathrm{Spec}\,\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$. Then $F_{\bar{x}}(\mathrm{Spec}\,L) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{Q}\text{-alg}}(L, \overline{\mathbb{Q}})$ — the set of embeddings of the finite separable extension $L/\mathbb{Q}$ into $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$. By the primitive-element theorem, $|F_{\bar{x}}(\mathrm{Spec}\,L)| = [L:\mathbb{Q}]$. An automorphism of $F_{\bar{x}}$ must permute these embedding sets compatibly across all $L$ — exactly an element of $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$.

3. $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(X, \bar{x}) = \mathrm{Aut}(F_{\bar{x}})$

The étale fundamental group is defined as $$ \pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(X, \bar{x}) \;:=\; \mathrm{Aut}(F_{\bar{x}}), $$ the group of natural automorphisms of the fiber functor. An element $\sigma$ is the data of a permutation $\sigma_Y\in\mathrm{Sym}(F_{\bar{x}}(Y))$ for every cover $Y\to X$, compatibly with morphisms of covers — i.e. for $g\colon Y\to Y'$, the square $F_{\bar{x}}(g)\circ\sigma_Y = \sigma_{Y'}\circ F_{\bar{x}}(g)$ commutes.

Topology. Each $\mathrm{Sym}(F_{\bar{x}}(Y))$ is a finite group, and $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}$ embeds into the inverse limit $\varprojlim_Y \mathrm{Sym}(F_{\bar{x}}(Y))$ as $Y$ ranges over $\mathrm{F\acute{É}t}/X$. With the inverse-limit topology this makes $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(X,\bar{x})$ a profinite group — compact, totally disconnected, Hausdorff. Continuous group homomorphisms out of it correspond to compatible families of finite permutations; this is why every cover automatically receives a continuous action.

Grothendieck's main theorem (SGA 1, Exp. V). The fiber functor lifts to an equivalence of categories $$ F_{\bar{x}}\colon \mathrm{F\acute{E}t}/X \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; \pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(X,\bar{x})\text{-}\mathrm{FinSet}, $$ where the right-hand side is finite sets with continuous left $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}$-action. Connected covers correspond to transitive actions; under this dictionary, a connected cover $Y\to X$ of degree $n$ corresponds to a continuous transitive action on an $n$-element set, equivalently an open subgroup $H\le\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}$ of index $n$ (the stabilizer of a chosen point of $F_{\bar{x}}(Y)$). Universal covers are not schemes in general; they are pro-objects in $\mathrm{F\acute{É}t}/X$ — but the profinite group always exists.

The proof-scrubber below walks through the construction of this equivalence: assemble all covers, take a compatible system of fiber points, read off the Galois group, and recover each cover from its $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}$-set.

4. $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(\mathrm{Spec}\,k) = \mathrm{Gal}(\bar{k}/k)$

The single most important worked example. Take $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,k$ for $k$ a field, and choose $\bar{x} = \mathrm{Spec}\,k^{\mathrm{sep}}$ as a geometric point (we use the separable closure rather than the full algebraic closure — they agree in characteristic 0 and for perfect fields). Then: $$ \pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(\mathrm{Spec}\,k, \bar{x}) \;\cong\; \mathrm{Gal}(k^{\mathrm{sep}}/k), $$ the absolute Galois group of $k$, viewed with its profinite topology as $\varprojlim_{L/k\text{ finite Galois}}\mathrm{Gal}(L/k)$.

Why this works. A finite étale cover of $\mathrm{Spec}\,k$ is, by definition unwound, a finite étale $k$-algebra $A$. By the structure theorem, $A\cong \prod_i L_i$ where each $L_i/k$ is a finite separable extension. So a connected cover is exactly $\mathrm{Spec}\,L$ for a finite separable extension $L/k$. The fiber functor at $\bar{x}$ sends $\mathrm{Spec}\,L$ to $\mathrm{Hom}_{k\text{-alg}}(L, k^{\mathrm{sep}})$ — a finite set of size $[L:k]$ on which $\mathrm{Gal}(k^{\mathrm{sep}}/k)$ acts continuously by post-composition. This action is transitive (any two embeddings differ by a Galois element), so the connected covers correspond to open subgroups; under the dictionary, a connected cover of degree $n$ ↔ an open subgroup of index $n$ ↔ a finite separable extension $L\subset k^{\mathrm{sep}}$ with $[L:k]=n$. That is exactly the classical Galois correspondence.

Conversely, every continuous transitive action of $\mathrm{Gal}(k^{\mathrm{sep}}/k)$ on a finite set arises from a unique (up to iso) finite separable $k$-algebra. So Grothendieck's equivalence collapses to the textbook fundamental theorem of Galois theory: $$ \{\text{finite separable }L/k\} \;\xleftrightarrow{\;1{:}1\;}\; \{\text{open subgroups of }\mathrm{Gal}(k^{\mathrm{sep}}/k)\}. $$

Worked example. $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{R}) = \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{C}/\mathbb{R}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$, generated by complex conjugation. Connected covers: only $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{R}$ (trivial, $H = \pi_1$, index 1) and $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}$ ($H = 1$, index 2). Disconnected covers: products of these. There are no other finite étale extensions of $\mathbb{R}$ — checked directly against the corresponding subgroup lattice $\{1\}\subset\mathbb{Z}/2$.

5. Frobenius generates $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_q)$

The cleanest non-trivial étale fundamental group. Take $k = \mathbb{F}_q$, $q = p^r$. The separable closure $\overline{\mathbb{F}_q} = \bigcup_{n\ge 1}\mathbb{F}_{q^n}$ is the union of all finite extensions, each $\mathbb{F}_{q^n}/\mathbb{F}_q$ is Galois with cyclic Galois group of order $n$, and the Galois group is generated by the Frobenius automorphism $$\mathrm{Frob}_q \colon \overline{\mathbb{F}_q} \to \overline{\mathbb{F}_q}, \qquad x\mapsto x^q.$$ Passing to the inverse limit: $$ \pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_q) \;=\; \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{F}_q}/\mathbb{F}_q) \;=\; \varprojlim_n \mathbb{Z}/n \;=\; \hat{\mathbb{Z}}, $$ topologically generated by $\mathrm{Frob}_q$.

What this group looks like. $\hat{\mathbb{Z}} = \prod_{\ell\text{ prime}}\mathbb{Z}_\ell$ is the profinite completion of $\mathbb{Z}$. Continuous homomorphisms $\hat{\mathbb{Z}}\to G$ to a finite group $G$ correspond to elements of $G$ (the image of the topological generator $\mathrm{Frob}_q$). So a finite étale cover of $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_q$ of degree $n$ is the data of a permutation of an $n$-element set — namely, the action of $\mathrm{Frob}_q$ on the fiber. Connected covers correspond to single-cycle permutations: $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_{q^n}\to\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_q$ is the unique connected cover of degree $n$, and $\mathrm{Frob}_q$ acts on its $n$-point fiber as an $n$-cycle.

This is the structural reason that varieties over $\mathbb{F}_q$ are tractable: all étale-cover information is summarized by the action of a single element. The same picture, scaled up, drives the Lefschetz trace formula in étale cohomology — point counts of $X(\mathbb{F}_{q^n})$ are traces of $\mathrm{Frob}_q^n$.

The clock below shows the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/n$ — the Galois group of the unique connected degree-$n$ cover $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_{q^n}\to\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_q$. The "multiplication by $a$" map plays the role of $\mathrm{Frob}_q^a$ on the $n$-point fiber: when $\gcd(a,n)=1$, $\mathrm{Frob}_q^a$ still generates the cyclic group (the cover is still connected); when $\gcd(a,n)>1$, the $a$-th iterate fixes a proper subgroup — the orbits collapse, mirroring how the cover decomposes when restricted to an intermediate $\mathbb{F}_{q^d}$.

The slider $n$ picks the cover $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_{q^n}\to\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{F}_q$; $a$ picks an element $\mathrm{Frob}_q^a\in\hat{\mathbb{Z}}/n\mathbb{Z}=\mathbb{Z}/n$. The cover is connected iff $a$ generates $\mathbb{Z}/n$, i.e. $\gcd(a,n)=1$ — exactly when $\mathrm{Frob}_q^a$ permutes the $n$-point fiber as a single cycle.

6. Comparison with topological $\pi_1$

Over $\mathbb{C}$ we have two fundamental groups available — the topological $\pi_1^{\mathrm{top}}(X(\mathbb{C}), x)$ from algebraic topology, and the étale $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(X, \bar{x})$ from §3. Riemann's existence theorem links them: $$ \pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(X, \bar{x}) \;\cong\; \widehat{\pi_1^{\mathrm{top}}(X(\mathbb{C}), x)} \;=\; \varprojlim_{N\triangleleft\pi_1^{\mathrm{top}},\,[\pi_1^{\mathrm{top}}:N]<\infty} \pi_1^{\mathrm{top}}/N, $$ the profinite completion of the topological $\pi_1$.

Why. A finite topological cover of $X(\mathbb{C})$ corresponds to a subgroup of finite index in $\pi_1^{\mathrm{top}}$. By Riemann's existence theorem (analytic continuation of regular maps along finite topological covers gives algebraic structure), every such finite topological cover is the analytification of a finite étale cover of $X$, and conversely. So the two categories of finite covers — étale-algebraic and finite-topological — agree, and finite quotients of $\pi_1^{\mathrm{top}}$ match finite quotients of $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}$.

Examples. $X = \mathbb{G}_m = \mathbb{A}^1\setminus\{0\}$ over $\mathbb{C}$: topologically $\mathbb{C}^\times \simeq S^1$, so $\pi_1^{\mathrm{top}} = \mathbb{Z}$, and $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(\mathbb{G}_{m,\mathbb{C}}) = \hat{\mathbb{Z}}$. The connected degree-$n$ étale cover is $\mathbb{G}_m \to \mathbb{G}_m$, $z\mapsto z^n$ — exactly the algebraic incarnation of the $n$-fold cover $S^1\to S^1$. For an elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{C}$, $\pi_1^{\mathrm{top}}(E(\mathbb{C})) = \mathbb{Z}^2$, so $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(E_{\mathbb{C}}) = \hat{\mathbb{Z}}^2$, and the finite étale covers are the $n^2$-fold ones $[n]\colon E\to E$, recovering Tate-module data.

What goes wrong in characteristic $p$. In positive characteristic, $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}$ is sensitive to wild ramification — Artin–Schreier covers $y^p - y = f$ are étale but have no characteristic-zero analogue, so $\pi_1^{\acute{e}t}(\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{F}_p}) \neq \{1\}$ even though $\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}$ is simply connected (in both senses). Comparison only makes the étale side equal a profinite completion in characteristic 0, or after passing to tame quotients in $\mathrm{char}\,p$.

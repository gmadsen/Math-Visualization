Zeta values — $\zeta$ at integers

The Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty n^{-s}$ conceals one of mathematics' great treasures: at integer arguments, its values reveal $\pi$, rational numbers, and deep irrationality theorems. From Euler's 1734 proof that $\zeta(2)=\pi^2/6$ to Apéry's 1978 bombshell that $\zeta(3)$ is irrational, and from the trivial zeros at negative even integers to the functional equation connecting left and right half-planes, this page uncovers the arithmetic of $\zeta$ at integers.

1. The Basel problem: $\zeta(2)=\pi^2/6$

The Basel problem, posed by Pietro Mengoli in 1650, asks for the exact value of the series $1 + \tfrac{1}{4} + \tfrac{1}{9} + \tfrac{1}{16} + \cdots$. Jakob Bernoulli confirmed it converges but could not find the sum. In 1734, the twenty-seven-year-old Euler stunned the mathematical world by announcing the answer: $\pi^2/6$.

Euler's product proof

Euler's key insight was to treat $\sin(\pi x)$ as an "infinite polynomial" with roots at $x=\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3,\ldots$ Factoring it analogously to a finite polynomial with leading term $\pi x$ (since $\sin(\pi x)/(\pi x)\to 1$ as $x\to 0$) gives the Weierstrass product:

$$\sin(\pi x) = \pi x \prod_{n=1}^{\infty}\!\left(1 - \frac{x^2}{n^2}\right).$$

Dividing both sides by $\pi x$:

$$\frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} = \prod_{n=1}^{\infty}\!\left(1 - \frac{x^2}{n^2}\right).$$

Now expand both sides in powers of $x^2$. The Taylor series gives $\sin(\pi x)/(\pi x)=1-\tfrac{(\pi x)^2}{6}+\tfrac{(\pi x)^4}{120}-\cdots$, so the coefficient of $x^2$ is $-\pi^2/6$. The product expansion gives

$$\prod_{n\ge 1}\!\left(1-\frac{x^2}{n^2}\right) = 1 - \left(\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{1}{n^2}\right)x^2 + \cdots$$

Matching coefficients of $x^2$:

$$\boxed{\zeta(2) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}.}$$
Rigour note. Euler's argument assumed without proof that $\sin(\pi x)$ could be treated like a finite polynomial for coefficient-matching purposes. Rigorous justification via uniform convergence of infinite products came later. The result is nonetheless correct.

2. Even values: $\zeta(2n)$ via Bernoulli numbers

Euler did not stop at $\zeta(2)$. By extending his product argument — or equivalently, by using the Bernoulli generating function — he derived a complete formula for all even positive-integer values. The statement requires the Bernoulli numbers $B_{2n}$:

$$\boxed{\zeta(2n) = (-1)^{n+1}\frac{B_{2n}\,(2\pi)^{2n}}{2\,(2n)!}, \qquad n=1,2,3,\ldots}$$

The Bernoulli numbers $B_{2n}$ are the rational coefficients in the generating function $\tfrac{t}{e^t-1}=\sum_{k\ge 0}B_k \tfrac{t^k}{k!}$, with $B_0=1$, $B_2=\tfrac{1}{6}$, $B_4=-\tfrac{1}{30}$, $B_6=\tfrac{1}{42}$, $\ldots$ The formula confirms that every $\zeta(2n)$ is a rational multiple of $\pi^{2n}$.

3. Odd values and Apéry's constant $\zeta(3)$

While every even positive-integer value of $\zeta$ admits the elegant formula above, odd values are a mystery. We know $\zeta(3)\approx 1.20206$ from numerical computation, but no closed form involving $\pi$ is known — and there are strong heuristic reasons to believe none exists.

Apéry's theorem (1978)

In 1978, Roger Apéry announced at a conference that $\zeta(3)$ is irrational. The audience was initially skeptical — the proof came with no prior publications and used an obscure sequence. Within months, mathematicians including Alfred van der Poorten verified the proof. It rests on the Apéry numbers $a_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k}^2\binom{n+k}{k}^2$ and a rapid rational approximation to $\zeta(3)$:

$$\zeta(3) = \frac{5}{2}\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^3\binom{2n}{n}}.$$

The denominators of the partial sums grow slower than the denominators of any rational approximation to $\zeta(3)$ — a contradiction if $\zeta(3)$ were rational. The proof establishes irrationality but says nothing about transcendence.

What about $\zeta(5), \zeta(7), \ldots$? In 2001, Tanguy Rivoal proved that infinitely many odd zeta values are irrational, and in 2022 Wadim Zudilin showed at least one of $\zeta(5),\zeta(7),\zeta(9),\zeta(11)$ is irrational. Whether each individual odd value is irrational — let alone whether any has a $\pi^{2k+1}$-type closed form — remains completely open.

4. Negative-integer values and trivial zeros

The Dirichlet series $\sum n^{-s}$ converges only for $\operatorname{Re}(s)>1$. But Riemann proved in 1859 that $\zeta(s)$ extends to a meromorphic function on all of $\mathbb{C}$, with a single simple pole at $s=1$ with residue $1$. This analytic continuation assigns finite values to all non-pole points, including the negative integers.

The values at negative integers are determined by the Bernoulli numbers:

$$\zeta(-n) = -\frac{B_{n+1}}{n+1}, \qquad n=0,1,2,3,\ldots$$

For instance:

$\zeta(0)=-\tfrac{1}{2},\quad \zeta(-1)=-\tfrac{1}{12},\quad \zeta(-2)=0,\quad \zeta(-3)=\tfrac{1}{120},\quad \zeta(-4)=0,\ldots$

The zeros $\zeta(-2)=\zeta(-4)=\zeta(-6)=\cdots=0$ are the trivial zeros of $\zeta$. They arise because $B_3=B_5=B_7=\cdots=0$ (odd Bernoulli numbers past $B_1$ vanish), so $\zeta(-2k)=-B_{2k+1}/(2k+1)=0$ for $k\ge 1$.

Not actual sums. Writing $1+2+3+\cdots=-\tfrac{1}{12}$ is a shorthand for $\zeta(-1)=-\tfrac{1}{12}$, where $\zeta$ is the analytically continued function. The ordinary divergent series has no value; the Riemann zeta provides a consistent extension of the summation concept to regions where the series diverges.

5. The functional equation $\xi(s)=\xi(1-s)$

Riemann's 1859 memoir introduced a completed zeta function that absorbs the gamma factor encoding the Archimedean place:

$$\xi(s) = \pi^{-s/2}\,\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{s}{2}\right)\zeta(s).$$

The profound symmetry is the functional equation:

$$\xi(s) = \xi(1-s).$$

This reflects the critical strip $0\le\operatorname{Re}(s)\le 1$ about the line $\operatorname{Re}(s)=\tfrac{1}{2}$. Written out as a relation between $\zeta(s)$ and $\zeta(1-s)$:

$$\zeta(1-s) = 2\,(2\pi)^{-s}\cos\!\left(\tfrac{\pi s}{2}\right)\Gamma(s)\,\zeta(s).$$

This is the bridge between the two worlds: values for $\operatorname{Re}(s)>1$ (where the Dirichlet series converges) and values for $\operatorname{Re}(s)<0$ (where Bernoulli numbers live).

Recovering the Bernoulli formulas

At $s=2n$ (positive even integer), $\cos(\pi n)=(-1)^n$, $\Gamma(2n)=(2n-1)!$, and $\zeta(2n)=(-1)^{n+1}B_{2n}(2\pi)^{2n}/(2(2n)!)$. Substituting into the right side and collecting powers of $2\pi$ gives $\zeta(1-2n)=-B_{2n}/(2n)$, the Bernoulli-number formula for the negative values.

Non-trivial zeros. A zero of $\zeta$ in $0<\operatorname{Re}(s)<1$ is a non-trivial zero. The functional equation forces zeros to come in pairs $\{\rho, 1-\rho\}$. The Riemann Hypothesis asserts all such $\rho$ satisfy $\operatorname{Re}(\rho)=\tfrac{1}{2}$; it remains unproven.

Modular curves

Quotients of $\mathcal{H}$ by congruence subgroups, compactified to Riemann surfaces, parametrize elliptic curves with level structure — the geometric stage where Hecke operators, Jacobians, and modular forms meet.

1. $Y_0(N)$, $X_0(N)$, and the cusps

Inside the modular group $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ sit the congruence subgroups. The most important for our story is $$\Gamma_0(N) \;=\; \Bigl\{ \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) \,:\, c \equiv 0 \pmod N \Bigr\}.$$ It is a finite-index subgroup; the index can be computed with Möbius inversion to give $$[\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) : \Gamma_0(N)] \;=\; N \prod_{p \mid N} \!\Bigl(1 + \tfrac{1}{p}\Bigr).$$

The quotient $Y_0(N) = \mathcal{H}/\Gamma_0(N)$ is a smooth, non-compact Riemann surface. It fails to be compact in exactly the way a punctured plane fails: certain sequences in $\mathcal{H}$ — those tending to $i\infty$ or to a rational point on $\mathbb{R} \cup \{\infty\}$ — have no limit in $Y_0(N)$. The orbits of $\Gamma_0(N)$ on $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q} \cup \{\infty\}$ form a finite set; we call its elements cusps. Adjoining the cusps and giving each one a chart $q = e^{2\pi i \tau / h}$ (where $h$ is the cusp's width) produces the smooth compact modular curve $$X_0(N) \;=\; Y_0(N) \,\cup\, \{\text{cusps}\}.$$

For prime $N$, the index is $N+1$ and there are exactly two cusps, traditionally called $0$ and $\infty$. The coset representatives for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})/\Gamma_0(N)$ can be taken to be $I, ST^0, ST^1, \ldots, ST^{N-1}$, and the fundamental domain of $\Gamma_0(N)$ is the union of these $N+1$ images of the canonical $\mathcal{F}$.

2. $Y_0(N)$ as a moduli space of elliptic curves

The reason modular curves are central to number theory is that $Y_0(N)$ is not just a quotient — it is a moduli space. A point of $Y_0(N)$ encodes a piece of geometric data:

$$Y_0(N)(\mathbb{C}) \;\longleftrightarrow\; \{\text{pairs } (E, C) : E \text{ an elliptic curve}/\mathbb{C},\ C \subset E \text{ cyclic of order } N\} / \cong.$$

The bijection is explicit. Given $\tau \in \mathcal{H}$ with class in $\mathcal{H}/\Gamma_0(N)$, attach the lattice $\Lambda_\tau = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau$, the elliptic curve $E_\tau = \mathbb{C}/\Lambda_\tau$, and the cyclic subgroup $$C_\tau \;=\; \Bigl\langle \tfrac{1}{N} \Bigr\rangle \;=\; \tfrac{1}{N}\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z} \;\subset\; \mathbb{C}/\Lambda_\tau.$$ This subgroup has order exactly $N$ — it is generated by the image of $1/N$ — and it is canonical: any element of $\Gamma_0(N)$ preserves the pair $(\Lambda_\tau, C_\tau)$ up to scaling, and conversely two pairs are isomorphic iff their $\tau$ values are $\Gamma_0(N)$-equivalent.

Two facts make this a true moduli interpretation:

  • Surjectivity. Every pair $(E, C)$ over $\mathbb{C}$ comes from some $\tau$: pick any uniformization $E \cong \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ with $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$, scale to get $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau$, and adjust the basis so that $C = \langle 1/N \rangle$.
  • Quotient by exactly $\Gamma_0(N)$. Two values $\tau, \tau'$ give isomorphic pairs iff they differ by $\Gamma_0(N)$ — the upper-triangular-mod-$N$ condition is precisely what preserves the distinguished cyclic subgroup.

This is the level-$N$ analogue of the classical $j$-invariant moduli interpretation $Y(1) = \mathcal{H}/\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{C}$ (parametrizing elliptic curves alone). Replacing $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ with $\Gamma_0(N)$ adds the level structure $C$.

3. Hecke correspondences on $X_0(N)$

Once $X_0(N)$ is realized as a moduli space, the Hecke operators acquire a beautifully geometric incarnation. For a prime $p \nmid N$, define

$$T_p \colon (E, C) \;\longmapsto\; \sum_{\substack{D \subset E \\ |D| = p,\ D \cap C = 0}} \bigl(E/D,\ (C+D)/D\bigr).$$

The right-hand side is a formal sum over the $p+1$ cyclic subgroups of $E$ of order $p$ (since $E[p] \cong (\mathbb{Z}/p)^2$ and $p \nmid N$ guarantees $D \cap C = 0$ automatically). Each summand is again a pair (elliptic curve, cyclic order-$N$ subgroup), so it lives in $X_0(N)$. The map $T_p$ is therefore not a single-valued morphism but a correspondence: a divisor inside the product $X_0(N) \times X_0(N)$.

Translated to $q$-expansions of weight-$k$ modular forms, $T_p$ becomes $$T_p\!\left( \sum_{n \ge 0} a_n q^n \right) \;=\; \sum_{n \ge 0} \!\Bigl( a_{pn} + p^{k-1} a_{n/p} \Bigr) q^n,$$ with the convention $a_{n/p} = 0$ unless $p \mid n$. The two terms come from the geometric setup: $a_{pn}$ from the "spread out" subgroups disjoint from a chosen reference, and $p^{k-1}a_{n/p}$ from the diagonal-twisted contribution. This is the bridge that makes Hecke eigenforms — forms with $T_p f = a_p f$ for all $p$ — generate Euler products for L-functions.

4. The Jacobian $J_0(N)$ and Eichler–Shimura

For any smooth compact Riemann surface $X$ of genus $g$, the Jacobian $$J(X) \;=\; \mathrm{Pic}^0(X) \;\cong\; H^0(X, \Omega^1)^* \,/\, H_1(X, \mathbb{Z})$$ is a complex torus of dimension $g$ — and in fact an abelian variety, because $X$ being projective gives the lattice a polarization. Applied to $X_0(N)$ this gives the modular Jacobian $$J_0(N) \;=\; \mathrm{Pic}^0(X_0(N)),$$ a $g(X_0(N))$-dimensional abelian variety defined over $\mathbb{Q}$ (descended from the moduli interpretation).

The Eichler–Shimura isomorphism is the bridge:

$$H^0(X_0(N), \Omega^1) \;\xrightarrow{\sim}\; S_2(\Gamma_0(N)), \qquad \omega \;\longmapsto\; f(\tau)\, d\tau \text{ where } \omega = f(\tau)\,d\tau.$$

The cusp condition on the right matches the holomorphicity of $\omega$ at the cusps on the left — an Eisenstein series of weight $2$ would give a meromorphic differential with poles at the cusps, not a holomorphic one. Consequently $$\dim_{\mathbb{C}} S_2(\Gamma_0(N)) \;=\; g(X_0(N)) \;=\; \dim J_0(N).$$

Widget 4 · Eichler–Shimura: forms ↔ differentials ↔ Jacobianclick levels to see $g$, $\dim S_2$, and $\dim J_0(N)$ matched

Crucially, the Hecke correspondences from §3 act on $J_0(N)$ as endomorphisms (push-forward of divisor classes). Each newform $f \in S_2(\Gamma_0(N))$ is a simultaneous Hecke eigenform, and Eichler–Shimura associates to $f$ a quotient abelian variety $$A_f \;\subseteq\; J_0(N),$$ whose $\ell$-adic Tate module recovers exactly the Galois representation attached to $f$. For weight-$2$ newforms with rational eigenvalues, $A_f$ is one-dimensional — an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$, the modular incarnation that is the heart of the modularity theorem.

5. Genus formulas for $X_0(N)$, $X_1(N)$, $X(N)$

Riemann–Hurwitz applied to the cover $X_0(N) \to X(1) = \mathbb{P}^1$ gives the explicit genus formula:

$$g(X_0(N)) \;=\; 1 \;+\; \frac{[\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}):\Gamma_0(N)]}{12} \;-\; \frac{\nu_2(N)}{4} \;-\; \frac{\nu_3(N)}{3} \;-\; \frac{\nu_\infty(N)}{2}.$$

Here:

  • $\nu_2(N)$ counts elliptic points of order $2$ on $X_0(N)$. By a classical computation, $\nu_2(N) = \prod_{p \mid N}\bigl(1 + \bigl(\tfrac{-1}{p}\bigr)\bigr)$ if $4 \nmid N$, and $0$ otherwise.
  • $\nu_3(N)$ counts elliptic points of order $3$, given by $\nu_3(N) = \prod_{p \mid N}\bigl(1 + \bigl(\tfrac{-3}{p}\bigr)\bigr)$ if $9 \nmid N$, and $0$ otherwise.
  • $\nu_\infty(N) = \sum_{d \mid N} \varphi\bigl(\gcd(d, N/d)\bigr)$ counts cusps.

The fractional denominators $4, 3, 2$ are not arbitrary: they record the orders of the stabilizers in $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ at the elliptic fixed points $i$ (order $2$) and $\rho = e^{2\pi i/3}$ (order $3$), and the parabolic stabilizer at each cusp (which has infinite order in $\mathrm{PSL}_2$ but contributes a $\frac{1}{2}$ from the boundary geometry).

The smallest prime $N$ for which $X_0(N)$ has positive genus is $N = 11$, where $g = 1$ — making $X_0(11)$ itself an elliptic curve. After that, the genus tends to grow linearly: for prime $N \ge 17$ one has $g(X_0(N)) \approx (N+1)/12$ (the elliptic-point and cusp corrections become negligible).

6. Cusps and Galois action over $\mathbb{Q}$

The moduli interpretation of $Y_0(N)$ — pairs $(E, C)$ with $E$ an elliptic curve and $C$ cyclic of order $N$ — descends to give a model of $X_0(N)$ over $\mathbb{Q}$. So $\Gal(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$ acts on $X_0(N)(\overline{\mathbb{Q}})$, and one can ask which cusps are individually $\mathbb{Q}$-rational.

The cusps of $X_0(N)$ are parametrized by

$$\bigl\{(d, a) \,:\, d \mid N,\ a \in (\mathbb{Z}/\gcd(d, N/d))^\times / \{\pm 1\}\bigr\},$$ giving the count $$\nu_\infty(N) \;=\; \sum_{d \mid N} \varphi\bigl(\gcd(d, N/d)\bigr).$$ The cusps $0$ (corresponding to $d = N$) and $\infty$ (corresponding to $d = 1$) are always individually $\mathbb{Q}$-rational, but for general $d$ the Galois group acts non-trivially on the parameter $a$.

Widget 6 · Cusps of $X_0(N)$ and the Atkin–Lehner involutionchoose $N$, see all cusps and the action of $w_N$

The Atkin–Lehner involution $w_N \colon \tau \mapsto -1/(N\tau)$ acts on $X_0(N)$ as an involutive automorphism. In moduli terms it sends the pair $(E, C)$ to $(E/C, E[N]/C)$, the "dual" pair under the cyclic $N$-isogeny $E \to E/C$. It exchanges the two cusps $0$ and $\infty$, and its quotient $X_0(N) / w_N$ is itself a curve of arithmetic interest — for instance $X_0(11)/w_{11}$ has genus $0$, even though $X_0(11)$ has genus $1$.

The Galois action on cusps and the action of the Atkin–Lehner involutions are the first arithmetic structures one encounters on $X_0(N)$. Together with the Hecke action they generate the rich endomorphism algebra of the Jacobian $J_0(N)$, which is where modular forms, Galois representations, and elliptic curves all converge.

7. Connections

Modular curves are the moduli spaces of elliptic curves with level structure, and almost every theorem about elliptic curves passes through them.

Modular forms and Hecke operators

Modular forms live as global sections of line bundles on modular curves; Hecke operators act both on forms and as algebraic correspondences on the curves. Eichler–Shimura ties the two together.

Elliptic curves

A point of $Y_0(N)$ is an elliptic curve with a cyclic $N$-isogeny; elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ with conductor $N$ correspond to rational points of $X_0(N)$. The modularity theorem (Wiles–Taylor) is a statement about which Galois representations come from $X_0(N)$.

Riemann surfaces and complex geometry

Each $X_0(N)$ is a compact Riemann surface; its genus, cusps, and elliptic points are determined explicitly by $N$. The hyperbolic structure on $\mathbb{H}/\Gamma_0(N)$ supplies the metric side.

Galois representations and arithmetic

Galois representations attached to weight-2 newforms come from the Tate module of $J_0(N)$; this is the bridge to modularity / FLT.

Open frontiers: explicit Mazur-style level-lowering; modular curves at composite level (X(N), X_1(N), Atkin–Lehner involutions); the geometric Langlands program for function fields, where modular curves become Hitchin moduli; rational points on modular curves and Mazur's torsion theorem.

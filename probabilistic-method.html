Probabilistic method

Erdős's principle: to prove an object with property $P$ exists, define a random object and show $\mathbb{P}(P) > 0$. From the Ramsey lower bound $R(k,k)\ge 2^{k/2}$ to high-girth high-chromatic-number graphs, the Lovász Local Lemma, and the threshold phenomena of $G(n,p)$ — counting by averaging.

1. Existence by expectation

Suppose you want to prove some combinatorial object with property $P$ exists. The probabilistic method puts a probability measure on the space of candidate objects and exploits the trivial fact:

Existence principle. If a random object $\omega$ satisfies $\mathbb{P}(\omega \text{ has } P) > 0$, then there exists at least one $\omega^*$ with property $P$.

The proof is one line: if no $\omega$ had $P$, the event would have measure zero. There is no algorithm here — the argument is non-constructive. But that is precisely its power: random objects are easy to analyse via expectations and tail bounds, even when no explicit construction is known.

The same logic phrased through expectations gives an even more useful tool. If $X$ is an integer-valued random variable on the candidate set, and $\mathbb{E}[X] \ge \alpha$, then there must be a point in the sample space where $X$ achieves at least $\alpha$ (and one where it achieves at most $\alpha$). You don't have to find that point — its existence is guaranteed by the average.

$\displaystyle \exists\,\omega: X(\omega) \;\ge\; \mathbb{E}[X],\qquad \exists\,\omega: X(\omega) \;\le\; \mathbb{E}[X].$

Warm-up: a tournament with many Hamilton paths

A tournament on $n$ players is an orientation of $K_n$: between any two vertices, exactly one directed edge. A Hamilton path is a directed path visiting every vertex once.

Toss a fair coin to orient each edge. For any fixed permutation $\pi$ of the $n$ vertices, the probability that the edges $(\pi_1,\pi_2),(\pi_2,\pi_3),\ldots,(\pi_{n-1},\pi_n)$ are all oriented forward is $2^{-(n-1)}$, since the coin tosses are independent. Let $X$ be the number of Hamilton paths. By linearity,

$\displaystyle \mathbb{E}[X] \;=\; n!\cdot 2^{-(n-1)}.$

For $n=8$ this is $8!/128 = 315$. So some tournament on $8$ vertices has at least $315$ Hamilton paths. Szele (1943) used exactly this argument — the very first appearance of the probabilistic method.

2. Ramsey lower bound $R(k,k)\ge 2^{k/2}$

The Ramsey number $R(k,k)$ is the smallest $n$ such that every $2$-edge-colouring of $K_n$ contains a monochromatic $K_k$. Ramsey's theorem (1930) proves $R(k,k)$ exists; the upper bound $R(k,k) \le \binom{2k-2}{k-1}\sim 4^{k}$ is by induction.

The lower bound was wide open until 1947, when Erdős opened his three-page paper with what is now the canonical "first" probabilistic argument:

Theorem (Erdős, 1947). If $\binom{n}{k}\,2^{1-\binom{k}{2}} < 1$ then $R(k,k) > n$. In particular $R(k,k) > \lfloor 2^{k/2}\rfloor$ for $k\ge 3$.

Proof. Colour each edge of $K_n$ red or blue independently with probability $1/2$. For any fixed $k$-subset $S\subseteq[n]$, the probability that $S$ is monochromatic is $2 \cdot 2^{-\binom{k}{2}} = 2^{1-\binom{k}{2}}$ — pick the colour, then force all $\binom{k}{2}$ edges to match it. Union-bounding over all $\binom{n}{k}$ subsets,

$\displaystyle \mathbb{P}(\exists\text{ monochromatic }K_k) \;\le\; \binom{n}{k}\,2^{1-\binom{k}{2}}.$

If this is strictly less than $1$, the complementary event has positive probability — there exists a $2$-colouring with no monochromatic $K_k$, so $R(k,k) > n$. Setting $n = \lfloor 2^{k/2}\rfloor$ and using $\binom{n}{k} \le n^k/k!$ verifies the inequality for $k\ge 3$. $\blacksquare$

The bound $R(k,k) > 2^{k/2}$ has not been improved by more than a factor of $2$ in the leading exponent since 1947. The recent breakthrough of Campos–Griffiths–Morris–Sahasrabudhe (2023) lowered the upper bound from $4^k$ to $(4-\varepsilon)^k$ — the gap to the lower bound $\sqrt{2}^k$ remains the central open problem.

3. Linearity of expectation

The single most-used tool in the probabilistic method is the most elementary identity in probability:

$\displaystyle \mathbb{E}[X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_m] \;=\; \mathbb{E}[X_1] + \cdots + \mathbb{E}[X_m].$

No independence required. The variables can be wildly correlated, indicator-valued, or both. This makes counting expectations almost as cheap as counting elements.

An independence number for $G(n,1/2)$

Let $G\sim G(n,1/2)$ — every edge is included independently with probability $1/2$. The independence number $\alpha(G)$ is the size of the largest vertex set spanning no edge. For any fixed $k$-subset $S$, the probability that $S$ is independent is $2^{-\binom{k}{2}}$, and

$\displaystyle \mathbb{E}[\#\{k\text{-independent sets}\}] \;=\; \binom{n}{k}\,2^{-\binom{k}{2}}.$

If this expectation is $< 1$ the variable is, with positive probability, $0$ — i.e. with positive probability $G$ has no independent set of size $k$, so $\alpha(G) < k$ for that draw. Plugging $k = \lceil 2\log_2 n\rceil$ shows $\mathbb{E}\alpha(G) \le 2\log_2 n + O(1)$ — random graphs of edge density $1/2$ have surprisingly small independent sets.

Triangle-free graphs of high chromatic number

Erdős proved (1959) that for every $g, \chi$ there exist graphs of girth $\ge g$ and chromatic number $\ge \chi$ — and the proof uses linearity twice:

  1. Sample $G(n,p)$ with $p = n^{\theta-1}$ for small $\theta$.
  2. By linearity, the expected number of cycles of length $\le g-1$ is $\sum_{i=3}^{g-1} (n)_i p^i / (2i)$ — a polynomial in $n$ of degree at most $g-1$.
  3. By the same trick, $\alpha(G) < n^{1-\theta/2}$ with high probability via a Chebyshev-type bound on $\#\{\text{independent sets of size }k\}$.
  4. Delete one vertex from every short cycle: at most a $o(n)$ deletion. The remaining graph $G'$ has girth $\ge g$ and $\alpha(G') \le \alpha(G) < n^{1-\theta/2}$, so its chromatic number $\chi(G') \ge n/\alpha(G') > n^{\theta/2}$ — arbitrarily large.

The deletion in step 4 is the alteration method (§4) — but linearity is what makes the global accounting tractable.

4. Alterations: the deletion method

The basic probabilistic method asks the random object to satisfy property $P$ outright. The alteration method is a softer version: build a random object that almost works, then locally repair the few defects.

Alteration template. Sample a random object with $\mathbb{E}[\#\text{bad sub-structures}] \le \beta$. Delete one element from each bad sub-structure. The result has no bad sub-structures, and is smaller by at most $\beta$.

This is exactly what step 4 of the Erdős high-girth argument does: the random graph $G\sim G(n,p)$ has, in expectation, at most $\beta = O(n^{(g-1)\theta})$ short cycles. Delete one vertex from each — losing at most $\beta = o(n)$ vertices when $\theta < 1/(g-1)$ — and the remaining graph has girth $\ge g$. Independence number is monotone under deletion, so the chromatic-number lower bound from §3 carries through.

Independence number, sharpened

Plain linearity gave $\alpha(G(n,1/2)) \lesssim 2\log_2 n$. A finer alteration argument: consider $G\sim G(n,p)$ for general $p$. Pick a random subset $S$ where each vertex is included with some probability, then delete one endpoint from each edge inside $S$. The expected size of the "altered" independent set is

$\displaystyle \mathbb{E}[|S| - e(S)] \;=\; n\,q - \binom{n}{2}\,q^2 p$

(where $q$ is the inclusion probability of each vertex). Optimising $q = 1/(np)$ gives independence number $\ge n/(2\sqrt{p\,\binom{n}{2}})$ — the Turán-type bound $\alpha(G) \ge \sum_v 1/(d_v + 1)$ in disguise, recovered by random-then-delete. Direct counting wouldn't have given this without struggle.

Constructive vs. probabilistic. The alteration method is non-constructive in the same sense as the basic method: you know an object exists, but you have not built it. For Ramsey lower bounds and high-girth high-chromatic graphs no construction matching the probabilistic existence is known — half a century of progress in derandomisation has not closed the gap.

5. The Lovász Local Lemma

The union bound is the workhorse of §2. But it is wasteful: $\mathbb{P}(\bigcup A_i) \le \sum\mathbb{P}(A_i)$ assumes nothing about how the events interact. When the bad events are locally independent — each $A_i$ is independent of all but a few others — Lovász discovered (1975) that you can do exponentially better.

Lovász Local Lemma (symmetric). Let $A_1,\dots,A_m$ be events with $\mathbb{P}(A_i)\le p$. Suppose each $A_i$ is mutually independent of all but at most $d$ other events. If $$e\,p\,(d+1) \;\le\; 1,$$ then $\mathbb{P}\!\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^m \bar A_i\right) > 0$.

The asymmetric form replaces $p$ and $d$ with per-event quantities $x_i\in[0,1)$ satisfying $\mathbb{P}(A_i)\le x_i\prod_{j\sim i}(1-x_j)$. Both forms guarantee the existence of a sample point avoiding all bad events — a goal the union bound cannot reach when $\sum\mathbb{P}(A_i) \ge 1$.

$k$-SAT lower bound

A $k$-CNF formula on $n$ Boolean variables has $m$ clauses, each a disjunction of $k$ literals. Set the variables uniformly at random. For each clause $C_i$, the bad event $A_i = \{C_i \text{ violated}\}$ has probability $2^{-k}$. Two clauses' events are independent unless they share a variable.

If no variable appears in more than $T$ clauses, then each $A_i$ is independent of all but at most $d = k(T-1)$ events. The LLL gives a satisfying assignment provided $e \cdot 2^{-k} \cdot k\,T \le 1$, i.e.

$\displaystyle T \;\le\; \frac{2^k}{e\,k}.$

So any $k$-CNF in which each variable appears at most $2^k/(ek)$ times is satisfiable. The naïve union bound only covers $T \le 2^k/m$ — useless when $m$ grows.

Moser–Tardos: the proof became an algorithm

For decades the LLL was a paradigm of non-constructive existence. In 2010 Moser and Tardos turned it into a polynomial-time randomised algorithm: pick a violated clause, resample its variables, repeat. The expected number of resamplings is $\sum x_i / (1-x_i)$ — finite under the LLL hypothesis. Suddenly, every existence proof via the LLL became an efficient algorithm. The entropy compression reformulation by Tao gave a self-contained proof of the same bound.

6. Thresholds in $G(n,p)$

The Erdős–Rényi random graph $G(n,p)$ exhibits sharp threshold phenomena: as $p$ crosses some critical scaling $p_c(n)$, a monotone graph property flips from "almost surely false" to "almost surely true" inside an interval shrinking to $0$. The standard tool is the second moment method.

First and second moment

Let $X = X_n$ count copies of some sub-structure (cliques, cycles, perfect matchings…). The first moment method uses Markov:

$\displaystyle \mathbb{P}(X \ge 1) \;\le\; \mathbb{E}[X].$

If $\mathbb{E}[X]\to 0$, then $X = 0$ asymptotically almost surely (a.a.s.) — no copies exist. This is the easy direction.

The hard direction needs the second moment method (Paley–Zygmund / Chebyshev):

$\displaystyle \mathbb{P}(X = 0) \;\le\; \frac{\operatorname{Var}(X)}{\mathbb{E}[X]^2} \;=\; \frac{\mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mathbb{E}[X]^2}{\mathbb{E}[X]^2}.$

If $\mathbb{E}[X]\to\infty$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X) = o(\mathbb{E}[X]^2)$, then $X > 0$ a.a.s.

Triangle threshold

Let $X = X_T$ count triangles in $G(n,p)$. Each fixed triple has probability $p^3$ of forming a triangle, and there are $\binom{n}{3}$ triples, so $\mathbb{E}[X] \sim n^3 p^3 / 6$. The threshold is at $p_c = 1/n$:

regime$\mathbb{E}[X_T]$conclusion
$p \ll 1/n$$\to 0$no triangles a.a.s. (first moment)
$p \gg 1/n$$\to\infty$triangles exist a.a.s. (second moment)
$p = c/n$$\to c^3/6$$X_T \to \mathrm{Pois}(c^3/6)$ in distribution

The variance computation is the heart: pairs of triangles either share $0$, $1$, $2$, or all $3$ edges, each with a different multiplicity. Above $p_c$ the dominant pair-type is "share $0$", giving $\operatorname{Var} \sim \mathbb{E}[X]$ — much smaller than $\mathbb{E}[X]^2$. Chebyshev wins.

Connectivity ($p_c = \log n / n$), Hamiltonicity ($p_c = \log n / n$), and the giant component ($p_c = 1/n$) all follow the same template.

7. Concentration: Markov, Chebyshev, Chernoff, Azuma

The probabilistic method asserts existence by showing $\mathbb{P}(\text{good}) > 0$. Concentration inequalities tighten this to "good with overwhelming probability" by quantifying how a random variable clusters around its mean.

inequalitystatementrequires
Markov$\mathbb{P}(X\ge a) \le \mathbb{E}[X]/a$$X\ge 0$
Chebyshev$\mathbb{P}(|X-\mu|\ge t) \le \sigma^2/t^2$finite variance
Chernoff$\mathbb{P}(S_n \ge (1+\delta)\mu) \le e^{-\mu\delta^2/3}$$S_n=\sum X_i$, $X_i\in[0,1]$ independent
Hoeffding$\mathbb{P}(|S_n-\mu|\ge t) \le 2\exp(-2t^2/\sum c_i^2)$$X_i\in[a_i,b_i]$ independent, $c_i=b_i-a_i$
Azuma$\mathbb{P}(|M_n-M_0|\ge t) \le 2\exp(-t^2/(2\sum c_i^2))$martingale with bounded differences $|M_i - M_{i-1}|\le c_i$

The progression is: Markov uses one moment, Chebyshev uses two, Chernoff/Hoeffding use the moment generating function (so all moments). Azuma is Chernoff for martingales — exactly the right tool when the random variable is built up step by step with bounded local effects.

Application: chromatic number is concentrated

Shamir–Spencer (1987): the chromatic number $\chi(G(n,p))$ is concentrated in a window of width $O(\sqrt{n\log n})$ around its median, regardless of $p$. The proof exposes vertices one at a time and applies Azuma to the vertex-exposure martingale: revealing vertex $i$'s edges to $\{1,\dots,i-1\}$ changes $\chi$ by at most $1$. Then $|c_i|\le 1$, and Azuma gives

$\displaystyle \mathbb{P}(|\chi - \mathbb{E}\chi| \ge t) \;\le\; 2\exp(-t^2/(2n)).$

So $\chi$ deviates from its mean by more than $\sqrt{2n\log n}$ with probability $\le 2/n$. Sharp for $p$ in much of the range, despite the variable being a discrete graph invariant with no obvious smoothness.

Talagrand's inequality (1995) is the deeper successor: for "configuration functions" — random variables that depend on independent inputs in a Lipschitz, certifiable way — concentration is on a scale of $\sqrt{m}$ where $m$ is the certificate size, rather than $\sqrt{n}$ where $n$ is the input dimension. This breaks the $\sqrt{n\log n}$ Shamir–Spencer barrier for many problems and is the standard tool of modern combinatorial probability.

8. Connections

The probabilistic method is a *lemma* about random objects, but the lemma is so widely-applicable that it has reshaped combinatorics. Below: the standard places it surfaces.

Extremal combinatorics

Extremal combinatorics is the natural consumer: lower bounds on Ramsey numbers, Turán-type density results, and chromatic-threshold theorems all use the probabilistic method as the core argument.

Probability and concentration

The Lovász local lemma, Janson, Talagrand, and the entropy method are all probability-theory statements; concentration of measure (Azuma, Talagrand) is the technical core that makes existence proofs into explicit-construction strategies.

Random graphs and structure

The Erdős–Rényi $G(n,p)$ model, threshold phenomena, and the structure of the giant component are the canonical playground; spectral graph theory often picks up where the probabilistic argument leaves off.

Algorithmic derandomisation

Pairwise independence and conditional expectation transform probabilistic existence proofs into deterministic polynomial-time algorithms; this is the algorithmic side of the probabilistic method, and a major bridge to complexity theory.

Open frontiers: the polynomial method (Croot–Lev–Pach, Ellenberg–Gijswijt) which uses character-sum and finite-field-arithmetic averaging; Razborov’s flag algebras (a "convex relaxation" of extremal questions); the structure-vs-randomness dichotomy that drives the additive-combinatorics revolution.

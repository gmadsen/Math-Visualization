Power sums, Bernoulli numbers, and the shadow of $\zeta$

From $1+2+\cdots+n=\tfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$ to $\zeta(2)=\pi^2/6$ to $1+2+3+\cdots=-\tfrac{1}{12}$ ŌĆö one family of rational numbers, the Bernoulli numbers $B_n$, ties the three together and drives Euler-Maclaurin summation, the functional equation of $\zeta$, and the analytic continuation of every L-function in the notebook.

1. Faulhaber: sums of $k^m$ as polynomials in $n$

Gauss's schoolboy trick tells you $1+2+\cdots+n=\tfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$ ŌĆö a quadratic in $n$. Less well known, but equally elementary:

$\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6},\qquad \sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3 = \left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2.$

Each sum is a polynomial in $n$. The pattern, discovered by Jacob Bernoulli (and earlier by Faulhaber in special cases), is that

$\displaystyle S_m(n):=\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^m = \frac{1}{m+1}\sum_{j=0}^{m}\binom{m+1}{j} B_j\, n^{m+1-j},$

where $B_j$ are the Bernoulli numbers (┬¦2). So $S_m$ is a polynomial of degree $m+1$ in $n$ with leading coefficient $\tfrac{1}{m+1}$ ŌĆö unsurprising, since $S_m(n)$ is the Riemann sum for $\int_0^n x^m\,dx=\tfrac{n^{m+1}}{m+1}$, and the Bernoulli numbers are the discrete correction to the integral.

Why leading $\tfrac{1}{m+1}$? The largest term $k=n$ contributes $n^m$; summing $n$ such terms near the top gives roughly $\int_0^n x^m\,dx=n^{m+1}/(m+1)$. The remaining Bernoulli corrections all have lower degree in $n$.

2. Bernoulli numbers and their generating function

The Bernoulli numbers $B_n$ are defined by the single generating-function identity

$\displaystyle \frac{t}{e^{t}-1} \;=\; \sum_{n\ge 0} B_n\, \frac{t^n}{n!}.$

Expanding the left side as a power series in $t$ and comparing coefficients gives $B_0=1$, $B_1=-\tfrac12$, $B_2=\tfrac16$, $B_3=0$, $B_4=-\tfrac{1}{30}$, and so on. A key symmetry is visible by rewriting

$\displaystyle \frac{t}{e^{t}-1}+\frac{t}{2} \;=\; \frac{t}{2}\cdot\frac{e^{t}+1}{e^{t}-1} \;=\; \frac{t}{2}\coth(t/2),$

which is an even function of $t$. Subtracting off the $B_1 t/1!=-t/2$ term, all odd powers must vanish: $B_{2k+1}=0$ for $k\ge 1$. The even values alternate in sign and grow faster than any geometric rate.

3. Euler's formula for $\zeta(2n)$

In 1734 Euler solved the Basel problem by proving $\zeta(2)=\pi^2/6$. The full even-integer result, in his later notation:

$\displaystyle \zeta(2n) \;=\; (-1)^{n+1}\frac{B_{2n}(2\pi)^{2n}}{2\,(2n)!}.$

The modern derivation: start from the Weierstrass product $\sin(\pi z)=\pi z\prod_{k\ge 1}(1-z^2/k^2)$, take logarithmic derivative, compare with $\pi\cot(\pi z)=\tfrac1z - 2\sum_{n\ge 1}\zeta(2n) z^{2n-1}$, then use the cotangent-to-Bernoulli expansion

$\displaystyle \pi z\cot(\pi z) \;=\; \sum_{n\ge 0}\frac{(-1)^n B_{2n}(2\pi z)^{2n}}{(2n)!}.$

The negative-integer values, by contrast, fall out of the functional equation (┬¦5) as

$\displaystyle \zeta(-n) \;=\; -\frac{B_{n+1}}{n+1}.$

Since $B_3=B_5=B_7=\cdots=0$, this gives $\zeta(-2)=\zeta(-4)=\cdots=0$ ŌĆö the trivial zeros. And $\zeta(-1)=-B_2/2=-1/12$, the regularised value that makes the string theorist's $1+2+3+\cdots$ finite.

4. Euler-Maclaurin summation

The same Bernoulli numbers that solve $\sum k^m$ also bridge finite sums to integrals of arbitrary smooth functions. Euler-Maclaurin:

$\displaystyle \sum_{k=a}^{b} f(k) \;=\; \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx \;+\; \frac{f(a)+f(b)}{2} \;+\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{B_{2k}}{(2k)!}\,\bigl[f^{(2k-1)}(b)-f^{(2k-1)}(a)\bigr] \;+\; R_K,$

where $R_K$ is a remainder bounded in terms of $\|f^{(2K+1)}\|_\infty$. Only the even Bernoulli numbers enter the main series (the odd ones, past $B_1$, vanish; $B_1$ is hidden inside the $(f(a)+f(b))/2$ trapezoidal term). Two ways to think about it:

  • Operator calculus. Writing $\Delta=E-1$ where $Ef(x)=f(x+1)$, and $D=d/dx$, Taylor gives $E=e^D$. Then $\Delta=e^D-1$, so $\Delta^{-1}=D^{-1}\cdot\tfrac{D}{e^D-1}=\tfrac{1}{D}\sum B_n D^n/n!$. Applying this operator identity formally to $f$ produces Euler-Maclaurin.
  • Repeated integration by parts. On each unit interval $[k,k+1]$, integrate $f$ against the periodic Bernoulli polynomials $\widetilde B_n(x)=B_n(\{x\})$; the resulting telescoping recovers the series.
It is an asymptotic series, not a convergent one. For most $f$, the error as $K\to\infty$ eventually grows. The trick is to stop near the smallest term; the accuracy is typically $e^{-c\,K}$ for well-chosen $K$.

5. Functional equation preview

The special values of ┬¦3 are not two unrelated facts ŌĆö they are one fact seen from two sides of a reflection. Define the completed zeta function:

$\displaystyle \xi(s) \;=\; \pi^{-s/2}\,\Gamma(s/2)\,\zeta(s).$

Riemann's 1859 memoir proves

$\displaystyle \xi(s) \;=\; \xi(1-s),$

so $\xi$ is symmetric across the critical line $\operatorname{Re}(s)=\tfrac12$. The proof goes via the theta function $\theta(y)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z} e^{-\pi n^2 y}$ and its Poisson-summation symmetry $\theta(1/y)=\sqrt{y}\,\theta(y)$ ŌĆö that is why the Mellin transform (see dirichlet-series) is the right bridge.

Pulling $\xi(s)=\xi(1-s)$ apart gives the asymmetric form

$\displaystyle \zeta(1-s) \;=\; 2\,(2\pi)^{-s}\cos(\pi s/2)\,\Gamma(s)\,\zeta(s).$

Plug in $s=2n$: the $\cos(\pi n)=(-1)^n$ factor, together with Euler's $\zeta(2n)=(-1)^{n+1}B_{2n}(2\pi)^{2n}/(2(2n)!)$ and $\Gamma(2n)=(2n-1)!$, collapses to

$\displaystyle \zeta(1-2n) \;=\; -\frac{B_{2n}}{2n}.$

Combined with $\zeta(-2k)=0$ (trivial zeros, from odd $B=0$), this is exactly the reflection of ┬¦3 ŌĆö the Bernoulli numbers are the same numbers on both sides of the mirror.

6. Application: accurate $\zeta(s)$ near $s=1$

The na├»ve series $\zeta(s)=\sum_{k\ge 1} k^{-s}$ converges absolutely for $\operatorname{Re}(s)>1$ but agonizingly slowly as $s\to 1^{+}$: the tail $\sum_{k>N} k^{-s}\approx N^{1-s}/(s-1)$. Applying Euler-Maclaurin to the tail gives

$\displaystyle \zeta(s) \;\approx\; \sum_{k=1}^{N-1}\frac{1}{k^{s}} \;+\; \frac{N^{1-s}}{s-1} \;+\; \frac{1}{2 N^{s}} \;+\; \sum_{k=1}^{K}\frac{B_{2k}}{(2k)!}\,\frac{s(s+1)\cdots(s+2k-2)}{N^{s+2k-1}}.$

This is the Riemann-Siegel predecessor: a modest $N$ and $K$ already give 10-digit accuracy uniformly across a reasonable strip. The same Euler-Maclaurin machinery powers Riemann-Siegel, the sharpest elementary algorithm for $\zeta(s)$ on the critical line; the even-Bernoulli corrections become a trigonometric series in $t=\operatorname{Im}(s)$, and the asymptotic cutoff is chosen around $K\sim \sqrt{t/(2\pi)}$.

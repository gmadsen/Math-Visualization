Algebraic topology

Turning continuous deformations into algebra — loops, coverings, chain complexes, and the Euler characteristic. A tour of how the global shape of a space is recorded in finite algebraic data.

1. From shapes to invariants

Algebraic topology is the business of replacing a space $X$ — a gelatinous continuous thing — by algebraic invariants: groups, rings, vector spaces. The translation is functorial: a continuous map $f\colon X\to Y$ gets sent to a homomorphism $f_*\colon \pi_1(X)\to\pi_1(Y)$ or $f_*\colon H_n(X)\to H_n(Y)$. The decisive property is that these invariants are homotopy invariants: continuously deforming the map does not change the induced algebraic map.

Paths and homotopy

A path in $X$ is a continuous map $\gamma:[0,1]\to X$, and a homotopy between paths is a continuous deformation through paths. Passing to homotopy classes turns geometric motion into algebraic data, first for loops (yielding $\pi_1$), then for chains (yielding $H_n$).

Simply connected spaces

A space is simply connected if it is path-connected and every loop contracts to a point, equivalently $\pi_1(X)=0$. This hypothesis appears repeatedly in analysis and geometry (for example, contour integration on simply connected domains).

Why would you want to do this? Because continuous problems become combinatorial the moment you apply a functor. Three classic examples:

Brouwer fixed point. Every continuous $f\colon D^n\to D^n$ has a fixed point. Proof: if $f$ had no fixed point, the map $x\mapsto$ (intersection of the ray from $f(x)$ through $x$ with $\partial D^n$) would retract $D^n$ onto $S^{n-1}$. But a retraction would produce a section of $H_{n-1}(S^{n-1})\hookrightarrow H_{n-1}(D^n)=0$, absurd because $H_{n-1}(S^{n-1})=\mathbb{Z}$.
Hairy ball. $S^2$ admits no nowhere-vanishing continuous tangent vector field. The obstruction is the Euler characteristic $\chi(S^2)=2\ne 0$ — a tangent field with a single isolated zero would have total index $\chi=2$, so you can't get rid of the zeros.
Ham sandwich. Given $n$ bounded measurable sets in $\mathbb{R}^n$, there is a single hyperplane that bisects each one. This is a corollary of the Borsuk–Ulam theorem, which in turn follows from the fact that there is no $\mathbb{Z}/2$-equivariant map $S^n\to S^{n-1}$ — a homological statement.

Each time, the argument is: assume the continuous statement fails; derive an algebraic impossibility; contradiction. The main characters in what follows are the fundamental group $\pi_1$ (a refined version of "how loops wind"), the homology groups $H_n$ (counting $n$-dimensional holes), and finally cohomology with its cup-product ring structure. Everything is insensitive to continuous deformation: if $X\simeq Y$ are homotopy-equivalent, all these invariants agree.

2. The fundamental group

Fix a basepoint $x_0\in X$. A loop is a continuous $\gamma\colon[0,1]\to X$ with $\gamma(0)=\gamma(1)=x_0$. Two loops are homotopic (rel basepoint) if one can be continuously deformed into the other through loops at $x_0$. The set of homotopy classes forms a group under concatenation:

$\pi_1(X,x_0) = \{\text{loops at }x_0\}/\text{homotopy}$, multiplication = "first go around $\alpha$, then $\beta$".

The identity is the constant loop; the inverse is the reversed loop. Changing basepoint in a path-connected space gives an isomorphic group (via conjugation by a path), so we usually drop $x_0$.

Theorem. $\pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, and the isomorphism is the winding number.

The proof: the exponential $p\colon\mathbb{R}\to S^1$, $t\mapsto e^{2\pi i t}$, is a covering map (see §3). Any loop $\gamma\colon[0,1]\to S^1$ lifts uniquely to a path $\tilde\gamma\colon[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}$ with $\tilde\gamma(0)=0$; its endpoint $\tilde\gamma(1)\in\mathbb{Z}$ is the winding number. Two loops are homotopic iff their lifts have the same endpoint. So the winding number gives a bijection $\pi_1(S^1)\xrightarrow{\sim}\mathbb{Z}$, and concatenation of loops corresponds to addition of integers.

A clean slogan: $\pi_1$ is abelian whenever loops have "room to slide past each other" — true for any H-space, any product, and any space of dimension $\ge 2$ in the sense of CW dimension if $\pi_1$ happens to commute. In general though — figure-eight, surface groups, braid groups — $\pi_1$ is gloriously non-abelian.

3. The universal cover of S¹

The exponential map $p\colon \mathbb{R}\to S^1$, $t\mapsto e^{2\pi i t}$, is the canonical example of a covering space: every point of $S^1$ has a small arc whose preimage in $\mathbb{R}$ splits into a disjoint family of intervals, one per integer translate. The fibre over the basepoint $*=1$ is exactly $\mathbb{Z}\subset\mathbb{R}$, which is what makes the integers appear inside the topology of the circle.

This cover is called universal because its total space $\mathbb{R}$ is simply connected — and any covering by a simply connected space is initial among covers, dominating all others. Lifting a based loop $\gamma\colon[0,1]\to S^1$ to a path $\tilde\gamma\colon[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}$ with $\tilde\gamma(0)=0$ converts continuous loop data into a single integer endpoint $\tilde\gamma(1)\in\mathbb{Z}$, the winding number. The deck-transformation group $\mathbb{Z}$ acts on $\mathbb{R}$ by integer translation, with quotient $S^1$, and the proof-scrubber below walks through how this picture upgrades into the iso $\pi_1(S^1)\cong\mathbb{Z}$.

4. Covering spaces

A covering map $p\colon \tilde X\to X$ is a continuous surjection such that every $x\in X$ has a neighborhood $U$ whose preimage $p^{-1}(U)$ is a disjoint union of copies of $U$, each mapped homeomorphically by $p$. The fibers $p^{-1}(x)$ are all homeomorphic to a discrete set $F$ — the "sheets" of the cover. Paths lift uniquely (given a starting lift), and homotopies lift too.

Classification (Galois correspondence for covers). Let $X$ be a reasonable space (path-connected, locally path-connected, semilocally simply-connected) with universal cover $\tilde X$. Then connected covers of $X$ (up to isomorphism rel basepoint) correspond bijectively to subgroups $H\le\pi_1(X,x_0)$; the cover is $\tilde X/H$ and its fundamental group is $H$. Normal subgroups correspond to regular covers, whose deck group is $\pi_1(X)/H$.

In particular, the universal cover is the one with $H=\{e\}$. It is simply connected, and $\pi_1(X)$ acts freely on it by deck transformations with quotient $X$:

$\tilde X\longrightarrow X = \tilde X/\pi_1(X)$.

Example: $\mathbb{R}\to S^1$

The universal cover of the circle is the real line, via $p(t)=e^{2\pi i t}$. Each fiber is a coset of $\mathbb{Z}\subset\mathbb{R}$; the deck group $\mathbb{Z}$ acts by translation. This is the cleanest picture of "$\pi_1(S^1)=\mathbb{Z}$": the integers are the deck group of the universal cover.

Universal covers
ℝ → S¹ · tree → figure-eight

For $X=S^1\vee S^1$ the universal cover is the infinite 4-valent tree (the Cayley graph of the free group $F_2$). Every reduced word in $a,b,a^{-1},b^{-1}$ is a distinct vertex, and no nontrivial loop can exist in a tree — exactly what "simply connected" demands.

Connection to Galois theory. The correspondence "subgroups ↔ covers" is a topological analogue of "subgroups of $\operatorname{Gal}(L/K)$ ↔ intermediate fields $K\subseteq M\subseteq L$": fixed subspace = subgroup-cover; normal = Galois/regular; quotient = deck group = $\operatorname{Gal}$. The fundamental group is literally a kind of étale fundamental group, and this isn't a coincidence — Grothendieck unified both under the notion of a Galois category.

5. Simplicial and singular homology

The fundamental group sees only one-dimensional information and is painfully hard to compute (it's non-abelian in general). Homology is its abelianized cousin in all dimensions, and the price of abelianization is that we can actually compute it.

Fix a simplicial complex $K$. Let $C_n(K)$ be the free abelian group on the $n$-simplices, and define the boundary

$\partial_n\colon C_n \to C_{n-1}, \quad [v_0,\dots,v_n]\mapsto\sum_{i=0}^n (-1)^i [v_0,\dots,\hat v_i,\dots,v_n]$.

A direct signed-sum check gives the central identity $\partial_{n-1}\circ\partial_n = 0$, so $\operatorname{im}\partial_{n+1}\subseteq\ker\partial_n$. The $n$-th homology is the quotient:

$H_n(K) = \ker\partial_n / \operatorname{im}\partial_{n+1}$.

Elements of $\ker\partial$ are cycles; elements of $\operatorname{im}\partial$ are boundaries. Homology asks: which cycles bound? The Betti number $b_n=\dim_\mathbb{Q} H_n(K;\mathbb{Q})$ counts independent $n$-dimensional holes.

The fact that changing the triangulation doesn't change $H_*$ — and that $H_*$ agrees with singular homology (defined with continuous simplices) — is a nontrivial theorem. In practice we just compute $H_*$ from any convenient simplicial decomposition.

Some sanity-checking values:

  • a point: $H_0=\mathbb{Z}$, all higher $H_n=0$. Betti: $b_0=1$.
  • $S^1$: $H_0=\mathbb{Z}$, $H_1=\mathbb{Z}$. One connected component, one loop.
  • $S^2$: $H_0=\mathbb{Z}$, $H_1=0$, $H_2=\mathbb{Z}$. One connected component, no 1-holes, one 2-dimensional "cavity".
  • $T^2$: $H_0=\mathbb{Z}$, $H_1=\mathbb{Z}^2$, $H_2=\mathbb{Z}$. The $\mathbb{Z}^2$ comes from the two independent loops; note it matches $\pi_1$ abelianized.
  • $\mathbb{RP}^2$ (over $\mathbb{Q}$): $b_0=1$, $b_1=0$, $b_2=0$. The single nontrivial loop has order 2, so rationally it dies. Over $\mathbb{Z}$ there is a torsion summand $H_1=\mathbb{Z}/2$.

6. Mayer–Vietoris

For $X = U\cup V$ with $U, V$ open (or more generally, a suitable cover), there is a long exact sequence of homology groups

$\cdots\to H_n(U\cap V)\xrightarrow{(\iota_U,\iota_V)} H_n(U)\oplus H_n(V)\xrightarrow{j_U-j_V} H_n(X)\xrightarrow{\partial} H_{n-1}(U\cap V)\to\cdots$

This is the topological analogue of inclusion-exclusion: you patch $X$ from $U$ and $V$, and the sequence tracks how the homology of the pieces combines, with a correction term — the boundary map — that encodes what happens at the intersection.

Derivation of $H_*(S^n)$ by induction. Cover $S^n$ with two open hemispheres $D_\pm$, each contractible (so $H_k(D_\pm)=H_k(\mathrm{pt})$), with intersection homotopy-equivalent to the equator $S^{n-1}$. The Mayer–Vietoris sequence collapses in all but two spots, and you read off $$H_k(S^n) = \begin{cases}\mathbb{Z} & k=0, n \\ 0 & \text{else}\end{cases}$$ for $n\ge 1$. The base case $n=1$ is just $H_*(S^1)=\{\mathbb{Z},\mathbb{Z},0,\dots\}$, which you can compute directly.

7. Euler characteristic and a taste of cohomology

The Euler characteristic of a finite CW-complex $X$ is the alternating sum

$\chi(X) = \sum_{n\ge 0} (-1)^n c_n = \sum_{n\ge 0}(-1)^n b_n$,

where $c_n$ is the number of $n$-cells and $b_n=\dim H_n(X;\mathbb{Q})$. The second equality comes from the fact that alternating sums of dimensions in a bounded chain complex are preserved under passage to homology — the same trick that makes $V-E+F$ independent of the polyhedral subdivision.

Classical corollary. For any convex polyhedron homeomorphic to $S^2$, $V - E + F = 2$. This is Euler's formula. For a closed orientable surface of genus $g$, $\chi = 2 - 2g$.

A taste of cohomology

Dualizing the chain complex $C_\bullet$ gives the cochain complex $C^\bullet=\operatorname{Hom}(C_\bullet,\mathbb{Z})$ with coboundary $\delta\colon C^n\to C^{n+1}$ transpose to $\partial$. Its cohomology $H^n(X)$ is a refinement of homology with extra structure: a graded-commutative ring under the cup product $\smile\colon H^p\otimes H^q\to H^{p+q}$. For instance, $H^*(T^2;\mathbb{Z})\cong\Lambda[\alpha,\beta]$ — the exterior algebra on two degree-1 generators — while $H^*(\mathbb{CP}^n;\mathbb{Z})\cong\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^{n+1})$ with $|x|=2$.

Poincaré duality. If $M$ is a closed oriented $n$-manifold, there is a natural isomorphism $$H^k(M;\mathbb{Z})\;\cong\;H_{n-k}(M;\mathbb{Z}).$$ Equivalently, cap product with the fundamental class $[M]\in H_n(M)$ is an isomorphism. The dual picture: a codimension-$k$ submanifold gives a class in $H^k$. For $M=S^n$: $H^0=H^n=\mathbb{Z}$, everything else zero — the duality is tautological. For surfaces: $b_k=b_{2-k}$; for $T^2$ you get $1,2,1$; for $\Sigma_g$ you get $1, 2g, 1$.
Gauss–Bonnet, one-liner. For a closed oriented Riemannian 2-manifold, $$\int_M K\,dA = 2\pi\,\chi(M).$$ The left side is a local analytic invariant (curvature integrated); the right side is a global discrete invariant (Euler characteristic). A round sphere has $K=1/R^2$, $\int K dA = 4\pi = 2\pi\cdot 2$. A flat torus has $K=0$ and $\chi(T^2)=0$, a perfect match.

The story continues from here — characteristic classes, spectral sequences, stable homotopy theory — but the germ is always the same. Attach a functor $X\mapsto A(X)$ that turns continuous deformations into algebra, and the functor will see shadows of the shape you could not otherwise make precise.

Big picture. The leap from $\pi_1$ to $H_*$ was the leap from non-abelian to abelian — from words to equivalence classes of chains. That move made everything computable. The price: information is lost (a knot complement has $\pi_1$ distinguishing knots that $H_*$ cannot tell apart). The trade-off between computable and sensitive is the central tension of the whole field, and it is why $\pi_n$, $H_n$, $H^n$, $K$-theory, bordism, etc. all coexist: each one picks a different balance on that axis.

Algebraic topology as a named discipline goes back to Poincaré's Analysis Situs (1895). Homology was in something like modern form by the 1920s (Noether insisted on groups instead of Betti numbers and torsion coefficients separately). Eilenberg and Steenrod axiomatized it all in 1952. Category-theoretic refinements from Grothendieck, Quillen, Lurie extend the story into derived and higher algebra — the algebraic topology of today.

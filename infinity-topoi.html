$\infty$-topoi

Lurie's higher topos theory — the capstone tying three threads: classical Grothendieck toposes (algebraic geometry), intuitionistic / Mitchell–Bénabou internal logic (intuitionist logic), and $\infty$-categories (higher homotopy). An $\infty$-topos is, simultaneously, a homotopy-coherent sheaf theory, a model for homotopy type theory, and the natural home for derived étale cohomology.

1. $\mathcal{P}(C)$: presheaf $\infty$-topoi

For $C$ a small $\infty$-category — equivalently, a small quasi-category — the $\infty$-category of $\infty$-presheaves is $\mathcal{P}(C) := \mathrm{Fun}(C^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathcal{S})$, the functor $\infty$-category from $C^{\mathrm{op}}$ to the $\infty$-category of spaces $\mathcal{S} = \mathrm{Kan}_\infty$ (Kan complexes / animae / homotopy types). When $C$ is an ordinary 1-category regarded as an $\infty$-category, this differs essentially from the 1-categorical presheaf topos $\widehat{C} = \mathrm{Fun}(C^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathsf{Set})$: instead of a set of sections one gets a space of sections, with all higher homotopies recorded. A morphism $F \to G$ of presheaves is a homotopy-coherent natural transformation; equivalences are pointwise weak homotopy equivalences.

The fundamental fact (HTT 5.1.5) is that $\mathcal{P}(C)$ is the free cocompletion of $C$ in $\infty$-categorical presentable land. Concretely, the Yoneda embedding $j\colon C \hookrightarrow \mathcal{P}(C)$ sending $c \mapsto \mathrm{Map}_C(-, c)$ has the universal property: for every cocomplete $\infty$-category $\mathcal{D}$, restriction along $j$ induces an equivalence $$\mathrm{Fun}^{L}(\mathcal{P}(C), \mathcal{D}) \xrightarrow{\,\simeq\,} \mathrm{Fun}(C, \mathcal{D}),$$ where $\mathrm{Fun}^L$ denotes colimit-preserving functors. Every functor $F\colon C \to \mathcal{D}$ extends uniquely (up to contractible choice) to a colimit-preserving $\widetilde F\colon \mathcal{P}(C) \to \mathcal{D}$, computable via the colimit $\widetilde F(X) = \mathrm{colim}_{(c, x) \in C/X} F(c)$. This is the higher analogue of the 1-categorical "every presheaf is a colimit of representables".

Two examples worth keeping in mind. (i) When $C = *$ is the terminal $\infty$-category, $\mathcal{P}(*) = \mathrm{Fun}(*^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathcal{S}) \simeq \mathcal{S}$ — the $\infty$-topos of spaces itself. So spaces are the "$\mathsf{Set}$ of higher topos theory". (ii) For $C$ the simplex category $\Delta$, $\mathcal{P}(\Delta) = \mathrm{sSet}_\infty$ is the $\infty$-category of simplicial spaces; the geometric realization $|-|\colon \mathcal{P}(\Delta) \to \mathcal{S}$ is the unique colimit-preserving extension of the constant functor $\Delta \to \mathcal{S}$ at $*$.

$\mathcal{P}(C)$ is presentable, locally cartesian closed, has all $\infty$-(co)limits, and satisfies all the $\infty$-Giraud axioms (§3). It is the prototype $\infty$-topos — every other $\infty$-topos arises as a left-exact localization of some $\mathcal{P}(C)$, exactly as every Grothendieck 1-topos $\mathrm{Sh}(C, J)$ embeds in $\widehat{C}$.

2. $\infty$-topos via accessible left-exact localization

Lurie's definition (HTT 6.1.0.4): an $\infty$-topos is an $\infty$-category $\mathcal{X}$ that admits a presentation $$L\colon \mathcal{P}(C) \rightleftarrows \mathcal{X} : i$$ where $C$ is a small $\infty$-category, $i$ is the fully faithful inclusion of a reflective subcategory, $L$ is its left adjoint, and (this is the crucial extra clause) $L$ is left exact — preserves finite $\infty$-limits. Equivalently $\mathcal{X}$ is an accessible left-exact localization of some presheaf $\infty$-topos $\mathcal{P}(C)$.

This definition mimics the 1-categorical Giraud theorem: a Grothendieck 1-topos $\mathrm{Sh}(C, J)$ is a left-exact reflective localization of $\widehat C$, the localization functor being sheafification $a_J$. The covering condition $J$ is encoded as a class of morphisms in $\widehat C$ that the localization forces to be invertible. In the $\infty$-world, the same picture holds — but now "invertible up to homotopy" means contracting an entire space of inverses, and the left-exactness condition has bite at every level (it preserves not only pullbacks of objects but homotopy fibers).

What gets inverted? A class $S$ of morphisms in $\mathcal{P}(C)$ corresponding to a topology on $C$ — for a 1-site $(C, J)$ this is the saturation of "covering monomorphisms become equivalences". The localized category $S^{-1}\mathcal{P}(C) \simeq \mathrm{Sh}(C, J; \mathcal{S})$ is the $\infty$-topos of $\mathcal{S}$-valued sheaves: presheaves of spaces satisfying descent for $J$-covers, where descent is now a homotopy limit condition on the Čech nerve of a cover — automatically picking up higher gluing data.

Note the contrast with 1-topos sheafification: a 1-categorical sheaf $F$ satisfies $F(U) = \mathrm{eq}(\prod F(U_i) \rightrightarrows \prod F(U_{ij}))$. The $\infty$-version replaces the equalizer with a totalization $\mathrm{Tot}(F(U_\bullet))$ — an infinite homotopy limit over the full Čech simplicial diagram, recording all higher coherences. This is why "$\infty$-sheaf" is genuinely more than "presheaf of spaces": the descent condition is delicate, and forces the higher-cocycle data to be coherent.

The accessibility condition ($L$ accessible, equivalently $\mathcal{X}$ presentable) ensures the localization is governed by a small class of generators and an adjoint-functor-theorem produces $L$ from a small set of inverted morphisms — without it, "left-exact reflective subcategory" is a class-sized notion. With it, $\infty$-topoi form a full subcategory of presentable $\infty$-categories, and $\infty$-Giraud (§3) provides an axiomatic recharacterization that doesn't mention $\mathcal{P}(C)$ at all.

3. $\infty$-Giraud axioms

The Lurie $\infty$-Giraud theorem (HTT 6.1.0.6) characterizes $\infty$-topoi intrinsically — without reference to a presheaf $\infty$-topos $\mathcal{P}(C)$. A presentable $\infty$-category $\mathcal{X}$ is an $\infty$-topos iff it satisfies four axioms:

  1. (G1) Presentability. $\mathcal{X}$ is presentable: cocomplete, accessible, generated under colimits by a small set of $\kappa$-compact objects for some regular cardinal $\kappa$. (This subsumes "has all small colimits" + "Adjoint Functor Theorem applies".)
  2. (G2) Universal coproducts. Coproducts are disjoint (the pullback $X_i \times_{X_i \sqcup X_j} X_j$ is initial for $i \neq j$) and stable under pullback (pulling back along any $f\colon Y \to \sqcup X_i$ produces $\sqcup (X_i \times_{\sqcup X_j} Y)$).
  3. (G3) Effective groupoid objects. Every groupoid object $X_\bullet \in \mathrm{Fun}(\Delta^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathcal{X})$ — i.e. a simplicial object satisfying Segal-style horn-filling conditions — is the Čech nerve of its colimit $|X_\bullet| := \mathrm{colim}_{[n] \in \Delta^{\mathrm{op}}} X_n$. Equivalently, the natural map $X_1 \xrightarrow{(d_0, d_1)} X_0 \times_{|X_\bullet|} X_0$ is an equivalence.
  4. (G4) Universal colimits. Pullback preserves all $\infty$-colimits — equivalently, $\mathcal{X}$ is locally cartesian closed: each slice $\mathcal{X}_{/X}$ has internal homs.

Compare to the 1-categorical Giraud theorem (Grothendieck topos $\Leftrightarrow$ presentable + universal coproducts + effective equivalence relations + small generating set). Two refinements happen on the way up. (i) "Equivalence relation" becomes "groupoid object" — a 1-categorical equivalence relation is a sub-monomorphism $R \hookrightarrow X \times X$ satisfying reflexivity/symmetry/transitivity, which up to homotopy is exactly the data of a groupoid object on $X$ in $\mathcal{X}$. The higher coherences (associativity of composition, etc.) come for free in the $\infty$-categorical Segal definition. (ii) "Effective" sharpens: in 1-toposes you ask the quotient to be the colimit; in $\infty$-toposes the quotient is automatically computed by the higher gluing data of the groupoid, so effectivity is the symmetric condition that recovering the groupoid from its quotient gives back the original groupoid.

The four axioms are tight: dropping any one breaks the equivalence with "lex localization of presheaves". Universal colimits encode the cartesian closure of slices, and that is what powers dependent products in the internal logic (§6) — without it, you cannot have $\Pi$-types. Effective groupoids power dependent sums and quotients-by-equivalence-relations. Disjoint coproducts power the dependent sum on a finite type. Presentability is what makes everything constructible.

4. Geometric morphisms of $\infty$-topoi

A geometric morphism $f\colon \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{E}$ between $\infty$-topoi is an adjunction $$ f^* \colon \mathcal{E} \rightleftarrows \mathcal{F} \,{:}\, f_* \qquad (f^* \dashv f_*) $$ with $f^*$ left exact (preserves finite $\infty$-limits). The convention is the usual one: the geometric morphism $f$ "points the way of $f_*$", which is the direct image — so an inclusion of an open $U \hookrightarrow X$ produces a geometric morphism $\mathrm{Sh}_\infty(U) \to \mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X)$ whose $f_*$ extends a sheaf on $U$ by $j_* \mathcal{F}$. Both adjoint functors are part of the data of $f$, but the geometric direction is fixed by where the topoi "live" geometrically.

The collection of geometric morphisms $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{E}$ assembles into an $\infty$-category $\mathrm{Fun}^*(\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F}) \subset \mathrm{Fun}^L(\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F})$ — colimit-preserving, left-exact functors. The 2-cells (natural transformations of geometric morphisms) are not arbitrary: they correspond to natural transformations $f^* \to g^*$ (the "left adjoint direction"), so $\infty\mathsf{Topoi}$ is genuinely an $(\infty, 2)$-category. Composition of geometric morphisms is given by composing $f^*$'s contravariantly with the data flow.

Two key classes:

  • Étale geometric morphisms. For $X \in \mathcal{E}$, the slice $\mathcal{E}_{/X}$ is again an $\infty$-topos and the projection $\mathcal{E}_{/X} \to \mathcal{E}$ is geometric: $f^* = X \times -$ (pullback) and $f_* = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{E}}(X, -)$ in the right sense. These model "open inclusion" — $f^*$ has a further left adjoint $f_!$ (the dependent sum / extension by zero), making it part of an "étale geometric morphism" in Lurie's terminology. Étale geometric morphisms $\mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{E}$ are equivalent to objects $X \in \mathcal{E}$ — like open inclusions of a space are equivalent to opens.
  • Surjective geometric morphisms. $f$ is surjective if $f^*$ is conservative — reflects equivalences. Models a "cover": $f^*\colon \mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X) \to \mathrm{Sh}_\infty(\widetilde X)$ for an étale cover $\widetilde X \to X$ is conservative because checking equivalences locally suffices.

A subtlety from $\infty$-land: the unit $\eta\colon \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{E}} \to f_* f^*$ and counit $\varepsilon\colon f^* f_* \to \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{F}}$ are not just arrows — they are full simplicial natural transformations. The triangle identities $f^* \xrightarrow{f^*\eta} f^* f_* f^* \xrightarrow{\varepsilon f^*} f^*$ and $f_* \to f_* f^* f_* \to f_*$ are equivalences in $\mathrm{Fun}(\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F})$, not equalities; the full "(co)unit" data is a higher-coherence package (HTT 5.2.2).

5. Hypercompletion and Whitehead's theorem

The classical Whitehead theorem in algebraic topology says: a map of CW complexes that induces an iso on all $\pi_n$ is a homotopy equivalence. The natural $\infty$-topos-internal version asks: in $\mathcal{X}$, is a morphism $f\colon X \to Y$ inducing an equivalence on all homotopy sheaves $\pi_n(f)\colon \pi_n(X) \xrightarrow{\sim} \pi_n(Y)$ necessarily an equivalence? An $\infty$-topos $\mathcal{X}$ is called hypercomplete (HTT 6.5.2) iff the answer is yes — Whitehead's theorem holds internally.

The homotopy sheaf $\pi_n(X)$ is, by definition, the sheafification of the presheaf $U \mapsto \pi_n(\Gamma(U; X))$ of homotopy groups. A map inducing isomorphisms on all $\pi_n$ is called $\infty$-connective (or "$\infty$-equivalence"). The hypercompleteness question is: is every $\infty$-connective map an equivalence?

For a Grothendieck $\infty$-topos $\mathrm{Sh}_\infty(C, J)$ the failure mode is precisely about infinite-dimensional behavior. The standard pathology: for $X$ a "very high cohomological-dimension" space (informally — infinite Krull dimension, or a profinite space with $\mathrm{cd}_\ell = \infty$), the natural inclusion of the hypercomplete localization $\mathrm{Sh}_\infty^{\wedge}(X) \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X)$ is strict — there exist $\infty$-connective maps that are not equivalences. Concretely: a Postnikov tower in $\mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X)$ can fail to converge — its limit need not equal the original sheaf.

The fix is hypercompletion: localize $\mathcal{X}$ further at the class of $\infty$-connective morphisms. The result $\mathcal{X}^{\wedge}$ is the maximal hypercomplete left-exact localization of $\mathcal{X}$, and is itself an $\infty$-topos (HTT 6.5.2.13). For $\mathcal{X}$ hypercomplete, $\mathcal{X}^{\wedge} = \mathcal{X}$. Equivalently: $\mathcal{X}^{\wedge}$ is the $\infty$-category of hypersheaves — presheaves satisfying descent for hypercovers, not just covers.

For most working contexts hypercompletion is automatic or harmless. Topological space $X$ of finite covering dimension: $\mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X)$ is already hypercomplete. Locally Noetherian scheme of finite Krull dimension, étale topology: the étale $\infty$-topos $X_{\mathrm{ét}}^\wedge$ is hypercomplete and is the natural home for $\ell$-adic sheaves. The pathology bites when you try to look at very large bases (infinite Krull dimension; pro-finite covers of profinite groups with infinite virtual cohomological dimension; certain operadic completions) — there hypercompletion is the right move.

6. Internal logic: homotopy type theory and $\infty$-toposes

This is the most striking feature of higher topos theory: every $\infty$-topos $\mathcal{X}$ has an internal language that is precisely homotopy type theory — Martin-Löf dependent type theory augmented with Voevodsky's univalence axiom and higher inductive types. The dictionary is exact (modulo unresolved coherence subtleties; the Awodey–Shulman–Cisinski program closed the residual gaps for Grothendieck $\infty$-topoi):

The most interesting feature is the universe / object classifier. In a 1-topos $\mathcal{E}$, the subobject classifier $\Omega \in \mathcal{E}$ classifies monomorphisms: $\mathrm{Sub}(X) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{E}}(X, \Omega)$. In an $\infty$-topos, monomorphisms are replaced by maps (or, more precisely, by a chosen size of map), and "the" subobject classifier becomes an entire universe of types: a family of objects $\mathcal{U}_\kappa \in \mathcal{X}$ such that, for each $X$, the space $\mathrm{Map}(X, \mathcal{U}_\kappa)$ is the space of "$\kappa$-small fibrations over $X$" (HTT 6.1.6). The universe is univalent — equality of types in the universe is equivalent to equivalence of types — automatically, because the object classifier is built as a moduli object in $\mathcal{X}$.

One useful comparison. In a 1-topos, $\Omega = \{0, 1\}$ (or its Heyting-algebra refinement) is the subobject classifier — but only of $(-1)$-truncated objects (propositions). In an $\infty$-topos, you have a tower $$\Omega = \mathcal{U}_{\leq -1} \subset \mathcal{U}_{\leq 0} \subset \mathcal{U}_{\leq 1} \subset \cdots \subset \mathcal{U}$$ where $\mathcal{U}_{\leq n}$ classifies $n$-truncated maps; the bottom is the classical $\Omega$, which in an $\infty$-topos classifies subobjects in the 0-categorical sense, and the full $\mathcal{U}$ classifies arbitrary small types. This is one place where "1-categorical $\Omega$ extends to ∞-categorical $\mathcal{U}$" is concretely visible.

Why does this matter? Homotopy type theory becomes the synthetic language for $\infty$-topos theory. A statement proven in HoTT — say, the Blakers–Massey theorem, or the synthetic computation of $\pi_4(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ — automatically holds in every $\infty$-topos (Shulman 2019, "All $(\infty,1)$-toposes have strict univalent universes"). Synthetic homotopy theory in HoTT is theorem-proving for $\infty$-topoi simultaneously. Conversely, $\infty$-topos theory provides the semantics that makes HoTT consistent: each $\mathcal{X}$ is a model.

7. The étale $\infty$-topos of a scheme

For a scheme $X$, the small étale site $X_{\mathrm{ét}}$ has objects = étale morphisms $U \to X$, morphisms = $X$-morphisms, and the Grothendieck topology of jointly surjective étale families. The classical 1-topos of étale sheaves is $\mathrm{Sh}_{\mathrm{ét}}(X) := \mathrm{Sh}(X_{\mathrm{ét}}, \mathsf{Set})$, the workhorse of étale cohomology. The $\infty$-categorical refinement is $$ \mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X_{\mathrm{ét}}) := \mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X_{\mathrm{ét}}, \mathcal{S})$$ — sheaves of spaces on the étale site. This $\infty$-topos retains higher gluing data; its $0$-truncation $\tau_{\leq 0} \mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X_{\mathrm{ét}}) = \mathrm{Sh}_{\mathrm{ét}}(X)$ recovers the classical étale topos. Its hypercompletion $\mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X_{\mathrm{ét}})^\wedge$ is the natural home for $\ell$-adic sheaves: classical $\ell$-adic cohomology is the cohomology of objects in this hypercomplete $\infty$-topos in disguise.

The basic test case is $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$. The étale site has, as "geometric points", maps $\mathrm{Spec}\,\bar k \to \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$ for $\bar k$ separably closed — which factor through some maximal-ideal residue field $\mathbb{F}_p$ or through the generic point $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Q}$. Each prime $p$ corresponds to a closed point with local étale-topos data $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}_p^{\mathrm{sh}}$ — the strict henselization of $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$ at $p$, with residue field $\bar{\mathbb{F}}_p$ and absolute Galois group $G_{\mathbb{F}_p} = \widehat{\mathbb{Z}}$ (topologically generated by Frobenius). The generic point has local data $\mathrm{Spec}\,\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$ with absolute Galois group $G_{\mathbb{Q}} = \mathrm{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$. The étale $\infty$-topos packages all this data — local rings, Galois groups, specialization morphisms — into a single homotopy-coherent sheaf theory.

For the $\infty$-version, hypercompletion is the operative move: derived $\ell$-adic cohomology requires either explicit Postnikov-tower convergence or hypersheaf descent. The Lurie–Galois reconstruction theorem (HTT 7.3 + Galois) says that for $X$ a Noetherian scheme of finite Krull dimension, $\mathrm{Sh}_\infty(X_{\mathrm{ét}})^\wedge$ encodes $X$ up to a profinite Galois twist — étale homotopy type, in modern language. This is the categorical setting of derived étale cohomology, $p$-adic Hodge theory in the Bhatt–Scholze prismatic formalism, and pro-étale ∞-topoi.

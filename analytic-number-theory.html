Analytic number theory

From counting primes to controlling them. Riemann's $\zeta(s)$, the explicit formula, zero-free regions, Dirichlet's theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions, sieve methods, and the Bombieri–Vinogradov theorem — analysis as a microscope on the integers.

1. $\pi(x)$ and the prime number theorem

Let $\pi(x) = \#\{p \le x : p \text{ prime}\}$. The earliest serious prediction about $\pi(x)$ is Gauss's, made in 1792 (he was fifteen): the density of primes near $x$ should be $1/\log x$, so $$\pi(x) \;\approx\; \mathrm{Li}(x) \;=\; \int_2^x \frac{dt}{\log t}.$$ Integration by parts gives $\mathrm{Li}(x) = x/\log x + x/(\log x)^2 + 2x/(\log x)^3 + \cdots$, so the leading-order content is $\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$ — the prime number theorem (PNT) of Hadamard and de la Vallée Poussin (1896). The logarithmic integral $\mathrm{Li}(x)$ keeps the higher-order corrections and matches $\pi(x)$ to many leading digits, while $x/\log x$ undershoots by several percent in the range we can compute.

The story splits into two questions. How fast does $\pi(x)/(x/\log x) \to 1$? is a question about the error term in PNT, and the answer is intimately tied to the zeros of the Riemann zeta function. How are primes distributed within a fixed residue class $a \bmod q$? is Dirichlet's question, and its answer involves a family of $L$-functions. We treat both in this page; sieve methods (the third pillar) handle the residual cases — twin primes, almost-primes, primes in short intervals — where $\zeta$ alone is too blunt.

2. $\zeta(s)$ and the explicit formula

The Euler product $$\zeta(s) \;=\; \sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{1}{n^s} \;=\; \prod_p \frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}}, \qquad \mathrm{Re}\,s > 1$$ makes $\zeta$ an analytic encoding of the primes. Taking $-\log$ and differentiating, $$-\frac{\zeta'(s)}{\zeta(s)} \;=\; \sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{\Lambda(n)}{n^s}, \qquad \Lambda(n) = \begin{cases} \log p & n = p^k, \\ 0 & \text{else.} \end{cases}$$ The von Mangoldt $\psi(x) = \sum_{n \le x} \Lambda(n)$ is the natural prime-counting cousin: PNT is equivalent to $\psi(x) \sim x$. Apply Perron's formula to $-\zeta'/\zeta$ and shift the contour past the poles. The pole of $\zeta(s)$ at $s = 1$ becomes the residue $-(-1) \cdot x^1/1 = x$ in $\psi(x)$. Each non-trivial zero $\rho = \beta + i\gamma$ of $\zeta$ contributes a residue $-x^\rho/\rho$ via $-\zeta'(\rho)/\zeta(\rho)$'s pole. The trivial zeros at $-2, -4, \ldots$ contribute $-\tfrac12 \log(1 - x^{-2})$.

The result is Riemann's explicit formula (proved rigorously by von Mangoldt): $$\boxed{\;\psi(x) \;=\; x \;-\; \sum_{\rho} \frac{x^\rho}{\rho} \;-\; \log 2\pi \;-\; \tfrac12 \log\!\bigl(1 - x^{-2}\bigr).\;}$$ The error in $\psi(x) - x$ is exactly the sum over zeros. Riemann's hypothesis — that every non-trivial $\rho$ has $\mathrm{Re}\,\rho = 1/2$ — is precisely the assertion that $\psi(x) - x = O(x^{1/2}(\log x)^2)$, the smallest possible error.

3. Zero-free regions and PNT proofs

To prove $\psi(x) \sim x$, the only thing the explicit formula demands is that no zero $\rho$ has $\mathrm{Re}\,\rho = 1$. Hadamard and de la Vallée Poussin's 1896 argument is short and beautiful. From the Euler product, $$\log \zeta(\sigma + it) \;=\; \sum_p \sum_{k \ge 1} \frac{1}{k\, p^{k(\sigma + it)}} \;=\; \sum_{p,k} \frac{\cos(t k \log p) - i \sin(t k \log p)}{k\, p^{k\sigma}},$$ so $\mathrm{Re}\,\log\zeta(\sigma + it) = \sum_{p,k} \cos(tk\log p)/(k p^{k\sigma})$. The key trigonometric inequality $$3 + 4\cos\theta + \cos 2\theta \;=\; 2(1 + \cos\theta)^2 \;\ge\; 0$$ applied term-by-term to $\theta = tk\log p$ gives $$3\,\mathrm{Re}\log\zeta(\sigma) \;+\; 4\,\mathrm{Re}\log\zeta(\sigma + it) \;+\; \mathrm{Re}\log\zeta(\sigma + 2it) \;\ge\; 0,$$ i.e. $|\zeta(\sigma)^3 \zeta(\sigma+it)^4 \zeta(\sigma+2it)| \ge 1$. As $\sigma \searrow 1$, $\zeta(\sigma)$ blows up like $1/(\sigma-1)$. A double zero $\zeta(1+it) = 0$ would force the product to vanish; the inequality forbids this, so $\zeta(1+it) \ne 0$.

Sharpening the argument with growth bounds for $\zeta$ and $\zeta'/\zeta$ (Borel–Carathéodory) extends the conclusion to a thin region: $$\zeta(\sigma + it) \ne 0 \qquad \text{for} \qquad \sigma \;>\; 1 - \frac{c}{\log|t|}, \quad |t| \ge 2.$$ Combined with the explicit formula and a Hadamard product expansion, this yields PNT with effective remainder $\psi(x) = x + O(x \exp(-c\sqrt{\log x}))$. Vinogradov–Korobov sharpens this to $\sigma > 1 - c / (\log|t|)^{2/3}(\log\log|t|)^{1/3}$, the best known unconditional region.

4. Dirichlet's theorem on primes in AP

Theorem (Dirichlet, 1837). If $\gcd(a, q) = 1$, the arithmetic progression $a, a+q, a+2q, \ldots$ contains infinitely many primes.

Worked example: orthogonality of characters. The proof of Dirichlet's theorem uses character orthogonality to extract the contribution of one residue class from $\sum_{p \le x} 1$.

The proof hinges on Dirichlet $L$-functions $L(s, \chi) = \sum_n \chi(n)/n^s$, where $\chi : (\mathbb{Z}/q)^\times \to \mathbb{C}^\times$ is a multiplicative character. Orthogonality of characters gives $$\frac{1}{\varphi(q)} \sum_{\chi \bmod q} \bar\chi(a)\,\chi(n) \;=\; \mathbf{1}[n \equiv a \pmod q],$$ so summing $\log L(s, \chi)$ weighted by $\bar\chi(a)/\varphi(q)$ over all $\chi$ collapses to the partial sum $\sum_{p \equiv a} p^{-s} + O(1)$. The trivial character contributes $\log\zeta(s)$, which blows up as $s \to 1^+$. Each non-trivial $\chi$ contributes $\log L(s, \chi)$. Provided $L(1, \chi) \ne 0$ for every non-trivial $\chi$, those terms are bounded. So the prime sum diverges, and infinitely many primes hit the class $a$.

The non-vanishing $L(1, \chi) \ne 0$ is the heart of the proof. For complex $\chi$ it follows easily from the meromorphic continuation of the product $\prod_\chi L(s, \chi) = \zeta_K(s)$, the Dedekind zeta of the cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_q)$, which has a simple pole. The hard case is real (quadratic) $\chi$, where Dirichlet's class-number formula $$L(1, \chi) \;=\; \frac{2\pi h_d}{w_d \sqrt{|d|}} \quad \text{(or analogous formula for } \mathrm{Re} > 0\text{)}$$ expresses $L(1, \chi)$ as a positive multiple of the class number $h_d$ — and class numbers are positive integers.

5. Sieve methods

Erathosthenes's sieve runs as inclusion–exclusion. To count integers $n \le x$ with no prime factor $\le z$, $$S(x, z) \;=\; \sum_{n \le x}\,\sum_{d \mid \gcd(n, P(z))} \mu(d) \;=\; \sum_{d \mid P(z)} \mu(d)\, \Bigl\lfloor \frac{x}{d} \Bigr\rfloor, \qquad P(z) = \prod_{p \le z} p.$$ The naive identity is exact, but useless for primes: if $z = \sqrt{x}$, the sifted set is $\{1\} \cup \{\text{primes in } (\sqrt{x}, x]\}$, so $S(x, \sqrt{x}) = \pi(x) - \pi(\sqrt{x}) + 1$. The trouble: $P(z)$ has $2^{\pi(z)}$ divisors, each contributing an $O(1)$ rounding error, and $2^{\pi(\sqrt{x})}$ swamps the main term $x \prod_{p \le \sqrt{x}}(1 - 1/p) \sim 2 e^{-\gamma} \sqrt{x}/\log\sqrt{x}$.

Brun's idea (1915): truncate the inclusion–exclusion at $r$ levels. Brun's pure sieve uses $$\sum_{\substack{d \mid P(z) \\ \omega(d) \le 2r}} \mu(d) \mathbf{1}[d \mid n] \;\ge\; \mathbf{1}[\gcd(n, P(z)) = 1] \;\ge\; \sum_{\substack{d \mid P(z) \\ \omega(d) \le 2r+1}} \mu(d) \mathbf{1}[d \mid n].$$ Truncated upper bounds yield bounds like $\pi_2(x) = \#\{p \le x : p, p+2 \text{ both prime}\} \ll x/(\log x)^2$ — so $\sum_{p, p+2 \text{ prime}}(1/p + 1/(p+2))$ converges to Brun's constant $B_2 \approx 1.902\ldots$. The convergence is the original surprise: even if there are infinitely many twin primes, they are sparse.

Selberg (1947): replace truncation by squares. For any sequence $(\lambda_d)$ with $\lambda_1 = 1$, $$\bigl(\textstyle\sum_{d \mid n} \lambda_d\bigr)^2 \;\ge\; \mathbf{1}[\gcd(n, P(z)) = 1].$$ Summing over $n \le x$ and optimizing in $\lambda$ gives an upper bound that is best possible up to a constant — the Selberg sieve. This is the engine behind Bombieri–Vinogradov (next section), Chen's theorem (every large even number is $p + p_2$ with $p_2$ a prime or product of two primes), and Maynard–Tao bounded gaps between primes.

6. Bombieri–Vinogradov: GRH on average

PNT for arithmetic progressions says $\pi(x; q, a) \sim \mathrm{Li}(x)/\varphi(q)$ for $\gcd(a, q) = 1$. The error term $$E(x; q, a) \;=\; \pi(x; q, a) - \frac{\mathrm{Li}(x)}{\varphi(q)}$$ is the analogue of $\psi(x) - x$ for the progression. The generalized Riemann hypothesis (GRH) for $L(s, \chi)$ would give $E(x; q, a) \ll x^{1/2} (\log x)^2$ uniformly in $q \le x$ — strong enough for almost any application. Unconditionally we have nothing like that. But on average over $q$, we essentially do.

Theorem (Bombieri 1965, A. I. Vinogradov 1965). For every $A > 0$ there exists $B > 0$ such that $$\sum_{q \le Q} \;\max_{\gcd(a, q) = 1} |E(x; q, a)| \;\ll_A\; \frac{x}{(\log x)^A}, \qquad Q = \frac{x^{1/2}}{(\log x)^B}.$$

The bound matches what GRH would give summed over $q \le \sqrt{x}$. Most arithmetic applications need exactly this $\ell^1$-on-moduli norm, not pointwise control, so the slogan "GRH on average" is fair. The proof goes through the large sieve inequality of Montgomery and Bombieri, controlling sums $\sum_{q \le Q} \sum_\chi^* |\sum_{n \le x} a_n \chi(n)|^2$ for primitive characters $\chi$; the prime-counting function feeds into this via a Vaughan-type identity that splits $\Lambda(n)$ into bilinear pieces. Pushing $Q$ past $\sqrt{x}$ is the open Elliott–Halberstam conjecture ($Q$ up to $x^{1-\varepsilon}$), which would lower the Maynard–Tao prime gap from $246$ to as small as $12$.

7. Connections

Analytic number theory translates additive / multiplicative questions about integers into questions about complex-analytic functions — and then exploits the dictionary in both directions.

Dirichlet series and L-functions

Dirichlet series are the engines: $\zeta(s)$ for primes, $L(s, \chi)$ for primes in AP, more elaborate $L$-functions for arithmetic objects. Euler products are the bridge from "sum over $n$" to "product over primes".

Modular forms and Galois representations

Hecke L-functions live both as Mellin transforms of modular forms and as compositions through Galois representations; modular forms, Hecke operators, and Galois representations form a single arc of which analytic NT is the analytic vertex.

Complex analysis

Complex analysis — residues, contour integrals, Phragmén–Lindelöf — is the proof technique. The explicit formula and zero-free regions read off the analytic structure of $\zeta$.

Sieve theory and additive combinatorics

Sieve methods (Brun, Selberg) connect to extremal combinatorics; Bombieri–Vinogradov is the strongest unconditional input for the Goldston–Pintz–Yıldırım circle, leading to bounded gaps between primes.

Open frontiers: the Riemann hypothesis (and the corresponding GRH for Dirichlet $L$-functions); the parity problem in sieve theory; Vinogradov's mean-value theorem and Bourgain–Demeter decoupling; recent work on bounded gaps between primes (Maynard, Tao).

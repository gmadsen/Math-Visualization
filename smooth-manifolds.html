Smooth manifolds

A space is a smooth manifold when it locally looks like $\mathbb{R}^n$, and the patch-to-patch relabellings are smooth. That one idea — charts that agree — is enough to transport all of calculus to curved objects: tangent vectors, vector fields, Lie brackets, immersions, embeddings.

1. Topological vs smooth manifold

A topological $n$-manifold is a topological space $M$ that is

  • Hausdorff,
  • second countable (a countable basis of open sets), and
  • locally Euclidean of dimension $n$: every $p \in M$ has an open neighbourhood $U$ homeomorphic to an open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$.

A homeomorphism $\varphi\colon U \to \varphi(U)\subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is a chart. The Hausdorff and countability conditions are mild but essential technical hygiene: Hausdorff excludes the line-with-two-origins pathology; second countability gives partitions of unity, which is how global constructions get done.

For calculus we need more. A smooth atlas is a collection $\{(U_\alpha,\varphi_\alpha)\}$ of charts whose domains cover $M$ and whose transition maps

$$\varphi_\beta\circ\varphi_\alpha^{-1}\colon \varphi_\alpha(U_\alpha\cap U_\beta)\longrightarrow \varphi_\beta(U_\alpha\cap U_\beta)$$

are $C^\infty$ diffeomorphisms between open subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$. A smooth manifold is $M$ with a maximal such atlas (the smooth structure).

Transition maps live in Euclidean space, so "smooth" has its familiar meaning. The manifold $M$ itself may be curved, disconnected, or non-orientable — the atlas lets us do calculus anyway, as long as what we compute does not depend on which chart we chose.

A general fact: manifolds often admit many atlases, but they all determine the same smooth structure if their union is still smooth. The maximal atlas is the one containing every chart compatible with the given ones — that is what we tacitly fix.

A few conventions

  • All manifolds here are finite-dimensional, without boundary, unless stated.
  • $C^\infty$ is our default smoothness. Everything generalises to $C^k$ or real-analytic, with minor care.
  • "Smooth manifold" is shorthand for "$M$ equipped with a fixed maximal smooth atlas".

Exotic smooth structures exist — $\mathbb{R}^4$ alone admits uncountably many inequivalent smooth structures, whereas $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n\neq 4$ admits only one. We will not touch such wonders.

2. Examples

A short tour. For each, the smooth structure is determined by a natural atlas; verifying smoothness of transitions is an exercise in calculus.

Spheres $S^n$

$S^n = \{x\in\mathbb{R}^{n+1} : \|x\|=1\}$ with the two-chart stereographic atlas from the widget above. The transition map is inversion $x\mapsto x/\|x\|^2$ of $\mathbb{R}^n\setminus\{0\}$, smooth on its domain.

Real and complex projective spaces

$\mathbb{RP}^n$ is the set of lines through $0$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$, equivalently $(\mathbb{R}^{n+1}\setminus\{0\})/{\sim}$ where $x\sim\lambda x$ for $\lambda\neq 0$. Use homogeneous coordinates $[x^0:\cdots:x^n]$; the $n+1$ charts are $U_i = \{x^i\neq 0\}$ with $\varphi_i([x]) = (x^0/x^i,\ldots,\widehat{x^i/x^i},\ldots,x^n/x^i)\in\mathbb{R}^n$. Transitions are rational in the local coordinates — smooth. Same construction gives $\mathbb{CP}^n$ as a $2n$-dimensional real manifold (or complex $n$-manifold).

Tori

$T^n = \mathbb{R}^n/\mathbb{Z}^n$. Charts are small open sets of $\mathbb{R}^n$ whose image under the quotient is injective; transitions are translations by integer vectors — smooth. Equivalently $T^n \cong S^1\times\cdots\times S^1$.

Matrix groups

$\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$ is the open subset $\{\det A\neq 0\}$ of $\mathbb{R}^{n^2}$, hence an $n^2$-dimensional manifold. Inside it sit Lie groups cut out by smooth regular equations:

Grassmannians

$\mathrm{Gr}(k,n)$ is the set of $k$-dimensional linear subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^n$, a compact manifold of dimension $k(n-k)$ generalising $\mathbb{RP}^{n-1} = \mathrm{Gr}(1,n)$. Charts come from choosing a complement: near a subspace $W_0$, nearby $W$ are graphs of linear maps $W_0\to W_0^\perp$, which gives local coordinates.

Every smooth manifold appearing in physics or geometry is built, usually, from these examples plus products, quotients, and preimages. There is no need to manufacture exotic cases to practise calculus — $S^2$, $T^2$, $\mathrm{SO}(3)$, and $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ already generate enough tangent planes for a career.

3. Smooth maps and diffeomorphisms

A map $f\colon M\to N$ between smooth manifolds is smooth if for every chart $(U,\varphi)$ on $M$ and every chart $(V,\psi)$ on $N$ with $f(U)\subseteq V$, the coordinate representative

$$\hat f = \psi\circ f\circ\varphi^{-1}\colon \varphi(U)\to\psi(V)$$

is $C^\infty$. The compatibility of transition maps ensures smoothness is a property of $f$, not of the charts chosen. If $f$ is smooth, bijective, and $f^{-1}$ is smooth, $f$ is a diffeomorphism and $M$, $N$ are diffeomorphic.

Special cases:

  • Smooth functions $M\to\mathbb{R}$ form the ring $C^\infty(M)$. This ring is the algebraic shadow of $M$: the (maximal) ideals of $C^\infty(M)$ are in bijection with points of $M$.
  • Smooth curves are smooth maps $\gamma\colon I\to M$ for $I\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ open.
  • Embeddings, immersions, submersions: see §7.

The differential (also called the tangent map or pushforward) of $f\colon M\to N$ at $p\in M$ is a linear map

$$df_p\colon T_pM\longrightarrow T_{f(p)}N,\qquad df_p(v) = \text{"$v$ pushed through $f$"}.$$

In coordinates, $df_p$ is the Jacobian matrix of $\hat f$ at $\varphi(p)$. Section 4 makes the tangent space precise; here just note that smooth manifolds are wired so that the chain rule holds, so the differential is a functor from manifolds to vector spaces.

Widget · rank of the differential points coloured by rank of $df$

Left panel: domain grid, coloured by $\operatorname{rank}(df_{(x,y)})$. Right panel: the image under $f$. The rank is the number of linearly independent rows of the Jacobian $Df = \begin{pmatrix}\partial f^1/\partial x & \partial f^1/\partial y \\ \partial f^2/\partial x & \partial f^2/\partial y\end{pmatrix}$.

Rank is pointwise: it can drop on a subset where the Jacobian's determinant vanishes. Where rank is maximal (= $\min(\dim M,\dim N)$), $f$ is locally modelled by a linear surjection or injection — this is the content of the constant-rank theorem, which powers §7.

4. The tangent space: three equivalent definitions

The tangent space $T_pM$ at a point $p\in M$ is a real vector space of dimension $\dim M$ that plays the role of "velocity vectors at $p$". The remarkable — and constantly useful — fact is that there are three standard definitions of $T_pM$, all equivalent. Each is the natural formulation for a different purpose.

Definition A · Equivalence classes of curves

A smooth curve at $p$ is a smooth map $\gamma\colon(-\varepsilon,\varepsilon)\to M$ with $\gamma(0)=p$. Two such curves $\gamma_1,\gamma_2$ are equivalent if in some (hence every) chart $\varphi$ around $p$,

$$(\varphi\circ\gamma_1)'(0) = (\varphi\circ\gamma_2)'(0) \in \mathbb{R}^n.$$

An equivalence class $[\gamma]$ is a tangent vector; $T_pM$ is the set of all such classes.

Definition B · Velocity vectors in coordinates

Fix a chart $(U,\varphi)$ near $p$. A tangent vector at $p$ is a pair $(p, v)$ with $v\in\mathbb{R}^n$. Changing to a second chart $(V,\psi)$, the same tangent vector is represented by $(p,w)$ with

$$w = D(\psi\circ\varphi^{-1})_{\varphi(p)}\cdot v.$$

The transformation rule is the Jacobian of the transition map — the only consistent definition, by the chain rule. A tangent vector is thus an equivalence class of pairs $(\text{chart}, v\in\mathbb{R}^n)$ modulo this cocycle.

Definition C · Derivations

A derivation at $p$ is a linear map $X\colon C^\infty(M)\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfying the Leibniz rule

$$X(fg) = X(f)\,g(p) + f(p)\,X(g).$$

The set of derivations at $p$ is a real vector space, and it is canonically isomorphic to the tangent spaces from definitions A and B.

Why they agree

A curve $\gamma$ defines a derivation $X_\gamma(f) = (f\circ\gamma)'(0)$ — this is the directional derivative of $f$ along $\gamma$. Equivalent curves give the same derivation, so there is a well-defined map curves mod equivalence $\to$ derivations. In a chart this sends $[\gamma]$ to $v^i\,\partial_i|_p$, where $v = (\varphi\circ\gamma)'(0)$. Conversely, any derivation $X$ has coordinates $X(x^i)\in\mathbb{R}$, and these determine it. So:

$$T_pM \;\cong\; \{[\gamma]\} \;\cong\; \{(v^1,\ldots,v^n)\} \;\cong\; \left\{\,v^i\,\tfrac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\Big|_p\,\right\}.$$
Widget · three curves, one tangent vector toggle curves through $p = (0,0,0)$ on $z = x^2+y^2$

The paraboloid $z = x^2 + y^2$ has tangent plane $z = 0$ at $p = (0,0,0)$. All three curves pass through $p$ at $t=0$ and have the same velocity vector $(1,0,0)$ there — that $1\cdot\partial_x|_p$ is their common tangent vector. Their second-order behaviour differs, but tangent vectors are a first-order notion.

Expanding $\sin t = t - t^3/6 + \cdots$ and $\tan t = t + t^3/3 + \cdots$ confirms $\gamma_i'(0) = (1,0,0)$ for $i=1,2,3$. The second derivatives differ, but the tangent space only remembers the first derivative.

The basis $\partial/\partial x^i$

In a chart $(U,\varphi)$, the coordinate vector fields $\partial_i = \partial/\partial x^i$ at $p$ form a basis of $T_pM$. A general $X\in T_pM$ is written

$$X = X^i\,\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\bigg|_p,\qquad X^i\in\mathbb{R}.$$

Under a chart change $y = y(x)$, the coefficients transform by the Jacobian:

$$\tilde X^j = \frac{\partial y^j}{\partial x^i}\,X^i.$$

This is the "contravariant" transformation law — so tangent vectors are contravariant tensors of rank 1.

The three definitions trade convenience for manoeuvrability. Curves are closest to intuition and to mechanics. Coordinates are good for computation. Derivations are good for algebra and for defining things intrinsically (without choosing a chart). In proofs one switches between them freely.

5. Tangent bundle and vector fields

Gather all tangent spaces into a single object:

$$TM = \coprod_{p\in M} T_pM = \{(p,v) : p\in M,\ v\in T_pM\}.$$

This is the tangent bundle. It has a natural projection $\pi\colon TM\to M$, $(p,v)\mapsto p$. The fibre $\pi^{-1}(p)$ is $T_pM$, an $n$-dimensional vector space.

$TM$ is itself a smooth manifold of dimension $2n$. A chart $(U,\varphi)$ on $M$ induces a chart on $\pi^{-1}(U)\cong U\times\mathbb{R}^n$ sending $(p, X^i\,\partial_i|_p)\mapsto(\varphi(p), X^1,\ldots, X^n)\in\mathbb{R}^{2n}$. Transition maps between such charts have the form

$$(x, v) \mapsto (y(x), D(y\circ x^{-1})\cdot v),$$

smooth because the original transitions and their Jacobians are smooth. The bundle structure $\pi\colon TM\to M$ is locally trivial: over each chart, $TM$ looks like $U\times\mathbb{R}^n$. This local-product structure is exactly what makes $TM$ a vector bundle.

Vector fields

A vector field is a smooth section of $\pi$: a smooth map $X\colon M\to TM$ with $\pi\circ X = \operatorname{id}_M$. Concretely $X$ assigns to each $p$ a tangent vector $X_p\in T_pM$, depending smoothly on $p$. In a chart,

$$X = X^i(x)\,\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i},$$

where each $X^i\colon U\to\mathbb{R}$ is a smooth function. The set $\mathfrak{X}(M)$ of vector fields is a real vector space and a module over $C^\infty(M)$.

A vector field acts on smooth functions by $(Xf)(p) = X_p(f)$, giving a derivation $X\colon C^\infty(M)\to C^\infty(M)$ — the directional derivative along $X$. Integral curves of $X$ are solutions of the ODE $\dot\gamma(t) = X_{\gamma(t)}$; on a compact manifold they exist for all time and assemble into the flow $\Phi^X_t\colon M\to M$.

Widget · vector fields on $S^2$ every field has at least one zero (hairy ball)

Each field is drawn as arrows on a hemispheric projection. Zeros (where the arrow vanishes) are marked. The hairy-ball theorem says no continuous tangent vector field on $S^2$ is everywhere nonzero — every option above has at least one zero.

The hairy-ball theorem is a consequence of the Poincaré–Hopf theorem: the sum of indices of zeros of a vector field equals the Euler characteristic of $M$. For $S^2$, $\chi = 2\neq 0$, so zeros must exist. For $T^2$, $\chi = 0$, and nowhere-vanishing vector fields do exist — e.g. any constant-coefficient field on $\mathbb{R}^2$ descends to the torus.

6. Integral curves and flows

A smooth vector field $X\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$ defines a first-order ODE on $M$. An integral curve of $X$ through $p\in M$ is a smooth curve $\gamma\colon I\to M$, $0\in I$, with

$$\gamma(0) = p,\qquad \gamma'(t) = X_{\gamma(t)}\ \ \text{for all}\ t\in I.$$

In any chart where $X = X^i(x)\,\partial_i$, this reads $\dot x^i(t) = X^i(x(t))$ — a system of ODEs to which the Picard–Lindelöf theorem applies locally.

Fundamental theorem of flows. For each $p\in M$ there is a unique maximal integral curve $\gamma_p\colon I_p\to M$ with $\gamma_p(0)=p$. The flow of $X$ is the map

$$\Phi^X\colon \mathcal{D}\subseteq \mathbb{R}\times M\longrightarrow M,\qquad \Phi^X(t,p) = \gamma_p(t),$$

defined on an open set $\mathcal{D}$ containing $\{0\}\times M$. Where defined, $\Phi^X_t$ is a diffeomorphism of one open set of $M$ onto another, and the one-parameter group law

$$\Phi^X_{s+t} = \Phi^X_s \circ \Phi^X_t$$

holds whenever both sides make sense.

A vector field is complete if every integral curve extends to all of $\mathbb{R}$, i.e. $\mathcal{D} = \mathbb{R}\times M$. Then $\{\Phi^X_t\}_{t\in\mathbb{R}}$ is a one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms of $M$.

For the rotation field, trajectories are circles closed for all $t$ — the flow is complete. Infinitesimal generator

Conversely, any smooth one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms $\{\Phi_t\}$ of $M$ arises this way: its infinitesimal generator is the vector field

$$X_p = \frac{d}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0}\Phi_t(p) \in T_pM.$$

So "vector fields" and "one-parameter groups of diffeomorphisms" are two faces of the same object on a compact manifold.

Two vector fields $X,Y$ commute as derivations iff their flows commute: $\Phi^X_s\circ\Phi^Y_t = \Phi^Y_t\circ\Phi^X_s$. The precise local measure of non-commutativity is the Lie bracket $[X,Y]$ — the subject of §7.

7. The Lie bracket

Given vector fields $X, Y\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$, each acts on $C^\infty(M)$ as a derivation. Consider the commutator:

$$[X,Y]f \;=\; X(Yf) - Y(Xf).$$

A priori this is a second-order operator, but the second-order pieces cancel — what remains is another derivation, i.e.\ another vector field, the Lie bracket $[X,Y]\in\mathfrak{X}(M)$. In local coordinates $X = X^i\partial_i$, $Y = Y^j\partial_j$,

$$[X,Y]^k = X^i\,\partial_i Y^k - Y^i\,\partial_i X^k.$$

The bracket is the Lie algebra structure on $\mathfrak{X}(M)$:

  • $\mathbb{R}$-bilinear;
  • antisymmetric: $[X,Y] = -[Y,X]$;
  • Jacobi: $[X,[Y,Z]] + [Y,[Z,X]] + [Z,[X,Y]] = 0$.

Geometric meaning: commuting flows

Let $\Phi^X_t$ and $\Phi^Y_t$ be the flows of $X$ and $Y$. Starting at $p$, follow $X$ for time $t$, then $Y$ for $t$, then $-X$ for $t$, then $-Y$ for $t$. For commuting fields you end up where you started. In general you don't — and the displacement measures the failure to commute:

$$\Phi^{-Y}_t\circ\Phi^{-X}_t\circ\Phi^Y_t\circ\Phi^X_t(p) \;=\; p + t^2\,[X,Y]_p + O(t^3).$$
Widget · Lie bracket via flows $X = \partial_x$, $Y = x\,\partial_y$ — so $[X,Y] = \partial_y$

Start at $p = (0, 0.3)$. Flow $X$ for time $t$, then $Y$ for $t$, then $-X$, then $-Y$. Because $[X,Y] = \partial_y \neq 0$, the loop does not close: the endpoint misses $p$ by approximately $t^2\,\partial_y = (0, t^2)$. The dashed red arrow shows the actual displacement; compare with the predicted $t^2[X,Y]_p$.

A cleaner way to say it: $[X,Y]_p = \lim_{t\to 0} t^{-2}\bigl(\Phi^{-Y}_t\Phi^{-X}_t\Phi^Y_t\Phi^X_t(p) - p\bigr)$. This gives the bracket as a second-order feature of flow composition — the first nontrivial correction to "flows commute".

Commuting vector fields ($[X,Y] = 0$) are the exception. When they commute on an open set, one can find coordinates in which $X = \partial_1$ and $Y = \partial_2$ simultaneously (Frobenius). Non-commuting fields generate higher-dimensional foliations — the bracket detects obstructions to integrability.

8. Immersions, submersions, embeddings

A smooth map $f\colon M\to N$ has pointwise-defined differentials $df_p\colon T_pM\to T_{f(p)}N$. The rank of $f$ at $p$ is $\operatorname{rank}(df_p)$. Three distinguished classes:

namecondition on $df_p$picture
immersioninjective for every $p\in M$ (rank = $\dim M$)$M$ injects tangentially; image may self-cross
submersionsurjective for every $p\in M$ (rank = $\dim N$)$M$ projects onto a neighbourhood in $N$
embeddinginjective immersion that is a homeomorphism onto image$M$ sits as a submanifold of $N$

Every injective immersion is locally an embedding (image near $p$ looks like a coordinate subspace). The extra condition in "embedding" is global: no self-limits, no infinitesimal returns. The figure-eight widget below exhibits the distinction sharply.

Submanifolds

An embedded submanifold of $N$ is the image of an embedding $M\hookrightarrow N$. Equivalently, $S\subseteq N$ is an embedded $k$-submanifold if for every $p\in S$ there is a chart of $N$ around $p$ sending $S$ to a $k$-plane. Immersed submanifolds relax the topological matching — useful for integral manifolds of distributions.

Regular values and preimages

A point $q\in N$ is a regular value of $f$ if $df_p$ is surjective at every $p\in f^{-1}(q)$. Then $f^{-1}(q)\subseteq M$ is a smooth submanifold of codimension $\dim N$. This is how most interesting manifolds are built:

  • $S^n = g^{-1}(1)$ for $g(x) = \|x\|^2$ on $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ — $1$ is a regular value.
  • $\mathrm{O}(n) = F^{-1}(I)$ for $F\colon \mathrm{Mat}_n\to\mathrm{Sym}_n$, $A\mapsto A^\top A$ — $I$ is a regular value.
  • $\mathrm{SL}_n(\mathbb{R}) = \det^{-1}(1)$ — $1$ is a regular value of $\det$ on $\mathrm{GL}_n$.

By Sard's theorem, almost every value is regular.

Whitney embedding theorem

Theorem (Whitney, 1936/44). Every smooth $n$-manifold $M$ admits a smooth embedding into $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, and an immersion into $\mathbb{R}^{2n-1}$.

The easy version — $M\hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^{2n+1}$ — is straightforward by partitions of unity. The sharp version needs more. The theorem says that abstract manifolds are not strictly more general than submanifolds of Euclidean space, which is comforting, though intrinsic definitions remain conceptually cleaner.

9. Partitions of unity

Smooth manifolds support a remarkable luxury that complex manifolds and algebraic varieties do not: smooth bump functions. From bumps one builds partitions of unity, the single most important device for passing from local constructions to global ones.

A smooth partition of unity on $M$ subordinate to an open cover $\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in A}$ is a family $\{\rho_\alpha\colon M\to[0,1]\}_{\alpha\in A}$ of smooth functions such that:

  • $\operatorname{supp}(\rho_\alpha)\subseteq U_\alpha$ for every $\alpha$;
  • the family $\{\operatorname{supp}(\rho_\alpha)\}$ is locally finite (each $p\in M$ has a neighbourhood meeting only finitely many supports);
  • $\sum_\alpha \rho_\alpha(p) = 1$ for every $p\in M$.

Theorem. Every smooth manifold (being paracompact) admits a smooth partition of unity subordinate to any given open cover.

Widget · three bumps summing to 1 drag the peak sliders; the green line is Σρᵢ

Three smooth bumps $\tilde\rho_i$ centred at $c_1, c_2, c_3$, then renormalised $\rho_i = \tilde\rho_i/(\tilde\rho_1+\tilde\rho_2+\tilde\rho_3)$ so that $\sum\rho_i\equiv 1$. Each $\tilde\rho_i$ is a $C^\infty$ bump (built from $e^{-1/(1-x^2)}$) supported in a small interval around $c_i$; renormalisation preserves smoothness because the denominator never vanishes when the supports cover the interval.

Existence sketch

On $\mathbb{R}^n$ the canonical bump is

$$b(x) = \begin{cases} e^{-1/(1-\|x\|^2)} & \|x\|<1,\\ 0 & \|x\|\ge 1,\end{cases}$$

which is $C^\infty$ everywhere (every derivative vanishes on $\|x\|=1$). Composing with chart maps produces bumps on any coordinate neighbourhood. Paracompactness gives a locally finite refinement of the cover; summing translated bumps and renormalising gives the partition.

Canonical uses

  • Existence of Riemannian metrics: pick Euclidean metrics $g_\alpha$ on each chart, then $g = \sum_\alpha \rho_\alpha\,g_\alpha$ is a global Riemannian metric (a convex combination of positive-definite forms is positive-definite).
  • Whitney embedding (easy version): assemble finitely many charts weighted by bumps into a smooth map $M\to\mathbb{R}^N$, and check that it is an embedding.
  • Extension of smooth functions: extend functions defined on a closed subset (or a submanifold) to all of $M$.
  • Integration: define $\int_M\omega$ by $\sum_\alpha \int_M \rho_\alpha\,\omega$, reducing to compactly supported integrals in charts.

Holomorphic bump functions do not exist — a non-zero holomorphic function has discrete zero set by the identity theorem, so it cannot be compactly supported. This is why sheaf cohomology and Čech theory replace partitions of unity on complex manifolds.

10. Orientation

At a single point $p\in M$, the $n$-dimensional vector space $T_pM$ has exactly two ordered-basis equivalence classes — two orientations — distinguished by the sign of the determinant of the change-of-basis matrix. An orientation of $M$ is a coherent choice, one at every point, varying continuously.

Three equivalent formulations

  • Oriented atlas: an atlas $\{(U_\alpha,\varphi_\alpha)\}$ with every transition $\varphi_\beta\circ\varphi_\alpha^{-1}$ having positive Jacobian determinant on its domain.
  • Nowhere-vanishing top form: a smooth section $\omega\in\Omega^n(M)$ with $\omega_p\neq 0$ for every $p$. Two such forms differ by a positive smooth function, giving the same orientation.
  • Continuous frame-of-reference: a smooth section of the $\mathrm{GL}^+(n,\mathbb{R})$-bundle of oriented frames, i.e. a reduction of the frame bundle to $\mathrm{GL}^+$.

A connected manifold admits either $0$ or $2$ orientations. When it admits any, we say $M$ is orientable; the two orientations are related by an overall sign flip.

Double cover

Every smooth manifold $M$ has a connected oriented double cover $\tilde M\to M$: $\tilde M$ is the set of pairs $(p, o_p)$ where $o_p$ is an orientation of $T_pM$. If $M$ is orientable, $\tilde M$ consists of two disjoint copies of $M$. If not, $\tilde M$ is connected. For example, the oriented double cover of $\mathbb{RP}^2$ is $S^2$; of the Möbius band is the cylinder; of the Klein bottle is the torus.

Orientation is the prerequisite for integrating top-degree forms: $\int_M\omega$ is defined up to a sign that is fixed only after an orientation is chosen. On a non-orientable manifold, integration of forms still makes sense on densities — see the differential-forms page.

11. Connections to the rest of the notebook

Smooth manifolds are the soil in which every other page's differential geometry grows.

  • Differential forms are the dual story: a $k$-form eats $k$ tangent vectors and spits out a number. On a manifold $M$, $k$-forms are smooth sections of $\Lambda^k T^*M$. The wedge and $d$ operations live in $\Omega^\bullet(M)$ and integrate over oriented submanifolds.
  • Differential geometry of surfaces is the theory of $2$-manifolds, usually embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$, with the induced metric. Gauss curvature, geodesics, Gauss–Bonnet — all live on the 2D case of what we built here.
  • Riemannian geometry adds a metric: a smoothly varying inner product on each $T_pM$. Distances, angles, geodesics, curvature follow. The Levi-Civita connection is the unique torsion-free connection compatible with the metric.
  • Lie groups are smooth manifolds that are also groups, with smooth multiplication and inverse. The tangent space at the identity, $T_eG$, is a Lie algebra under the bracket from §6. The exponential map $\exp\colon T_eG\to G$ integrates the algebra to the group.
  • Riemann surfaces are smooth manifolds with complex structure: holomorphic transitions instead of just smooth ones. Complex 1-manifolds are real 2-manifolds with extra data.

The tangent space is where every differentiable structure — vector fields, forms, metric, connection, group, complex — is first assembled. Get comfortable switching between curves, coordinates, and derivations. Every other page assumes it.

Heyting algebras and toposes

Intuitionist logic inside any topos: $\Omega$ as a Heyting algebra and the Mitchell–Bénabou language.

1. Heyting algebras

A Heyting algebra is a bounded lattice $H$ equipped with a binary operation $\Rightarrow$, the relative pseudo-complement or implication, satisfying the adjunction $$a \wedge b \;\le\; c \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad a \;\le\; b \Rightarrow c.$$ Equivalently, $b \Rightarrow c$ is the largest element of $H$ whose meet with $b$ lands below $c$. From this the negation $\neg a := a \Rightarrow \bot$ falls out automatically, and one proves the intuitionist tautologies $a \le \neg\neg a$, $\neg\neg\neg a = \neg a$, and the Heyting–De Morgan law $\neg(a \vee b) = \neg a \wedge \neg b$. The "missing" classical equation $\neg\neg a = a$ is exactly what fails in general — it is equivalent to the law of excluded middle $a \vee \neg a = \top$.

Two streams feed the definition. Algebraic side: a Heyting algebra is to intuitionist propositional logic what a Boolean algebra is to classical propositional logic — its filters are the consistent theories, its primes encode propositional models, and its Lindenbaum algebra is the free Heyting algebra on the propositional letters. Geometric side: the open sets of any topological space form a Heyting algebra under $\cap, \cup$, with $U \Rightarrow V := \mathrm{int}(U^c \cup V)$ and $\neg U = \mathrm{int}(U^c)$. This is the prototype: open-set lattices are essentially the only Heyting algebras one ever needs (Stone-style duality theorems make this precise via locales).

Boolean algebras are the special case where $\neg\neg a = a$ for all $a$. Equivalently, a Heyting algebra is Boolean iff it is complemented, iff every element is regular ($\neg\neg a = a$), iff LEM holds. The 4-element diamond $\{\bot, a, b, \top\}$ below is the smallest Boolean algebra besides $\{\bot,\top\}$; the open-set lattice on a connected non-Hausdorff space (e.g. the Sierpiński space) is the smallest non-Boolean Heyting algebra. 2. $\Omega$ as an internal Heyting algebra

The subobject classifier $\Omega$ in a topos $\mathcal{E}$ does more than name truth values — it carries internal lattice operations. The trick is universality: a map $\Omega \times \Omega \to \Omega$ is, by classification, a subobject of $\Omega \times \Omega$. So defining $\wedge, \vee, \Rightarrow$ as morphisms is the same as picking out the right subobjects of $\Omega \times \Omega$, then taking their characteristic maps.

Concretely: $\wedge\colon \Omega\times\Omega \to \Omega$ classifies the diagonal subobject $\langle\mathrm{true},\mathrm{true}\rangle\colon 1 \hookrightarrow \Omega \times \Omega$ — the unique pair both equal to $\top$. $\vee$ classifies the image of the map $1 + 1 \to \Omega \times \Omega$ that sends $(*, *)$ to $(\top, x)$ on one summand and $(x, \top)$ on the other (factoring through the union of the two coordinate-true lines). $\Rightarrow$ classifies $\{(p,q) \in \Omega\times\Omega : p \le q\}$ — the partial-order relation on $\Omega$ itself, which is in turn the equaliser of $\wedge\colon \Omega\times\Omega \to \Omega$ and the first projection $\pi_1$. Negation is the special case $\neg = \big(\Omega \xrightarrow{\langle 1, !\circ\bot\rangle} \Omega\times\Omega \xrightarrow{\Rightarrow} \Omega\big)$ — apply implication against $\bot$.

That this internal structure is a Heyting algebra (and not, generically, a Boolean one) is forced by the way subobjects glue: in $\mathbf{Set}$ the lattice is $\{\bot,\top\}$ and is Boolean, but in $G$-$\mathbf{Set}$ the elements of $\Omega$ are the $G$-stable subgroups of $G$ (the subgroup lattice ordered by inclusion), in $\widehat{C}$ the elements over $c$ are sieves on $c$, and in $\mathrm{Sh}(X)$ the elements over $U$ are open subsets of $U$. None of these are Boolean in general. Externally, $\mathrm{Sub}(X)$ inherits the operations componentwise: $S \wedge T = S \cap T$, $S \vee T = S \cup T$, and $S \Rightarrow T$ is the largest subobject $W \subseteq X$ with $W \cap S \subseteq T$.

3. The internal Mitchell–Bénabou language

The Mitchell–Bénabou language of a topos $\mathcal{E}$ is a typed higher-order intuitionist language whose syntax is interpreted directly by morphisms of $\mathcal{E}$. The dictionary is mechanical: every type $A$ names an object $\llbracket A \rrbracket$ of $\mathcal{E}$ (with $1$ for the unit type and $\Omega$ for the propositional type), every term $t \colon A$-in-context-$\Gamma$ names a morphism $\llbracket \Gamma \rrbracket \to \llbracket A \rrbracket$, and every formula $\varphi$ — a term of type $\Omega$ — names a characteristic map $\llbracket \Gamma \rrbracket \to \Omega$, equivalently a subobject of $\llbracket \Gamma \rrbracket$.

Logical operators are interpreted via the internal Heyting structure built in §2. Conjunction $\varphi \wedge \psi$ becomes $\Omega\times\Omega \xrightarrow{\wedge} \Omega$ pre-composed with $\langle\llbracket\varphi\rrbracket, \llbracket\psi\rrbracket\rangle$; quantifiers come from adjoints to pullback along projection: $\exists$ is the left adjoint $\Sigma_\pi$ (image), $\forall$ is the right adjoint $\Pi_\pi$ (the largest subobject whose preimage lies in the given one). Equality $t =_A s$ classifies the diagonal $\Delta_A\colon A \hookrightarrow A \times A$ pulled back along $\langle t, s \rangle$. The constant $\mathrm{true}$ is the top of $\mathrm{Sub}$; provability in the natural-deduction calculus matches subobject inclusion, $\Gamma \vdash \varphi$ iff $\llbracket\top_\Gamma\rrbracket \le \llbracket\varphi\rrbracket$ in $\mathrm{Sub}(\llbracket\Gamma\rrbracket)$.

Two consequences. First, every theorem of intuitionist higher-order logic is true in every topos — the Mitchell–Bénabou language is a conservative reading of $\mathcal{E}$. Second, classical principles (LEM, choice, decidable equality) translate to specific topos-theoretic conditions on $\mathcal{E}$: LEM corresponds to "every subobject has a complement," AC to "every epi splits," etc. So a topos sees its own classical-vs-intuitionist character in this language. Mac Lane–Moerdijk VI.5–7 develops the full apparatus; Goldblatt's Topoi §11 is the textbook entry point.

4. Kripke–Joyal forcing semantics

The internal-language interpretation of §3 is global: a formula either holds (its subobject is everything) or it doesn't. Kripke–Joyal semantics refines this by tracking which generalised elements $\alpha\colon X \to \llbracket\Gamma\rrbracket$ make $\varphi$ true. Write $X \Vdash_\alpha \varphi$ — read "$X$ forces $\varphi$ at $\alpha$" — for the relation $\alpha$ factors through $\{\Gamma \mid \varphi\}$. The whole intuitionist calculus reduces to bookkeeping about generalised elements: $$\begin{aligned} X &\Vdash_\alpha \varphi\wedge\psi &&\iff X \Vdash_\alpha \varphi \;\text{and}\; X\Vdash_\alpha\psi,\\ X &\Vdash_\alpha \varphi\vee\psi &&\iff \exists\;\text{cover}\; X = \bigsqcup X_i,\;\text{each}\; X_i \Vdash \varphi \;\text{or}\; X_i\Vdash\psi,\\ X &\Vdash_\alpha \varphi\to\psi &&\iff \forall f\colon Y\to X,\; Y\Vdash_{f^*\alpha}\varphi \implies Y\Vdash_{f^*\alpha}\psi,\\ X &\Vdash_\alpha \exists y.\varphi &&\iff \exists\;\text{cover}\; \{f_i\colon X_i \to X\}\;\text{and}\; b_i,\; X_i \Vdash \varphi(b_i),\\ X &\Vdash_\alpha \forall y.\varphi &&\iff \forall f\colon Y\to X, b\colon Y\to A,\; Y\Vdash \varphi(b). \end{aligned}$$ Notice the asymmetry: $\to$ and $\forall$ quantify over all $f\colon Y\to X$ (a "stability" or persistence condition), while $\vee$ and $\exists$ require some cover. That asymmetry is exactly what makes the logic intuitionist.

In a presheaf topos $\widehat{C}$ this rule set specialises to the classical Kripke possible-worlds semantics. Worlds are objects of $C$, the accessibility relation is "there is an arrow $c \to c'$", and the persistence clauses for $\to,\forall$ become "if $c$ forces $\varphi\to\psi$ and $c \to c'$, then $c'$ forces $\varphi\to\psi$" — the classic monotonicity built into Kripke models. In a sheaf topos $\mathrm{Sh}(X)$, $X \Vdash \varphi$ means the formula is true on a covering family of opens; this is the topos-theoretic source of constructive analysis ("$\varphi$ holds locally").

5. Why LEM fails: double negation in toposes

The fundamental fact is that $\Omega$ is generally not a Boolean algebra, so the law of excluded middle $\varphi \vee \neg\varphi = \top$ is not a topos-theoretic theorem. The cleanest single example is the open-set lattice of $\mathbb{R}$: take $U = (-\infty,0)$ as a "proposition." Then $\neg U = \mathrm{int}(U^c) = (0,\infty)$, and $U \vee \neg U = (-\infty,0) \cup (0,\infty)$ misses the point $0$. So $U \vee \neg U \ne \mathbb{R}$ — LEM fails. Worse, $\neg\neg U = \mathrm{int}(\overline{U}) = (-\infty,0]^\circ = (-\infty,0) = U$ in this case, but for $V = \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$ we get $\neg\neg V = \mathbb{R} \ne V$. Double-negation is a non-trivial closure operator.

The systematic fix is the double-negation topology $\neg\neg$ on any topos $\mathcal{E}$. It is a Lawvere–Tierney topology — an internal closure operator $j\colon \Omega \to \Omega$ — and the category $\mathrm{Sh}_{\neg\neg}(\mathcal{E})$ of $j$-sheaves is the largest Boolean subtopos of $\mathcal{E}$ (Mac Lane–Moerdijk V.2). Inside $\mathrm{Sh}_{\neg\neg}$, LEM is restored: every $\neg\neg$-stable proposition is decidable. This is the topos-theoretic shadow of Cohen forcing — for the right base topos (sets of names) the $\neg\neg$-sheaves model ZFC where the Continuum Hypothesis fails.

A topos $\mathcal{E}$ is Boolean when $\Omega$ is internally a Boolean algebra, equivalently when every subobject has a complement. The two canonical Boolean toposes are $\mathbf{Set}$ itself and $G$-$\mathbf{Set}$ (sets with a $G$-action) for $G$ any group. Presheaf toposes $\widehat{C}$ for $C$ not a groupoid are non-Boolean; sheaves on a non-discrete topological space are non-Boolean. 6. Geometric morphisms preserve geometric logic

A geometric morphism $f\colon \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{E}$ is an adjoint pair $f^* \dashv f_*$ in which the inverse image $f^*\colon \mathcal{E} \to \mathcal{F}$ preserves finite limits (and arbitrary colimits, since it is a left adjoint). This is precisely the structure that pulls back $\Omega_\mathcal{E}$ to a Heyting algebra in $\mathcal{F}$ and lifts subobjects across the morphism. The constraint that $f^*$ preserves finite limits — not just arbitrary ones — is the categorical content of "$f$ is logical for the geometric fragment."

Geometric logic is the fragment of intuitionist first-order logic generated by $\top, \bot, \wedge$ (binary), $\bigvee$ (arbitrary, including infinitary disjunction), $\exists$, and equality. What is omitted: $\Rightarrow, \neg, \forall$, and infinitary conjunction. The exact match is no accident: $f^*$ preserves $\wedge$ because it preserves finite products of subobjects (i.e. pullbacks). It preserves $\bigvee$ because arbitrary colimits — in particular the union of a family of subobjects — are images of coproducts, and $f^*$ commutes with both. It preserves $\exists$ because images go to images (left adjoints commute with images). It preserves equality because it preserves diagonals. Conversely $f^*$ does not in general preserve $\Rightarrow$ or $\forall$: those are constructed via right adjoints to pullback, and a left adjoint need not commute with right adjoints.

The big consequence is geometric soundness: a geometric sequent $\varphi \vdash_x \psi$ valid in $\mathcal{E}$ is automatically valid in $\mathcal{F}$ for any geometric morphism $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{E}$. This is the engine behind classifying-topos arguments: if $\mathcal{T}$ is a geometric theory, the topos $\mathrm{Set}[\mathcal{T}]$ classifies $\mathcal{T}$-models in any topos via "geometric morphisms $\mathcal{F} \to \mathrm{Set}[\mathcal{T}]$ correspond to $\mathcal{T}$-models in $\mathcal{F}$." Composition $g\circ f$ has $(g\circ f)^* = f^* \circ g^*$, so geometric formulas survive arbitrary chains of geometric morphisms — pull a geometric truth all the way back along Spec$(R) \to$ Spec$(\mathbb{Z}) \to *$ and it stays geometric. 7. Connections

Heyting algebras and the internal logic they organise are not a destination — they are a hinge between several theories that the rest of this notebook develops.

The internal logic and category theory

Every reasoning step you took inside $\mathcal{E}$ — the typing-tree judgments, the universally-quantified Kripke–Joyal clauses — is interpretable in any cartesian-closed category with a subobject classifier. The full account starts from cartesian-closed categories and power-object axioms; what we have here is the slice of that story specific to propositional reasoning.

Sheaf semantics and Grothendieck topoi

The Kripke–Joyal forcing relation $U \Vdash \varphi$ is the same as local-truth in a sheaf: a Grothendieck topology determines which covering families count, and forcing on the resulting topos becomes site-aware. Sites and sheaves is the next step if you want to build topoi rather than reason inside a fixed one.

Higher topoi and homotopy type theory

Replace sets with $\infty$-groupoids and the same internal-language game produces the language of $\infty$-topoi. The propositions live a layer below the higher truth-values, and the $\Omega$ of this page becomes a $(-1)$-truncated object. Univalence in homotopy type theory turns isomorphism into identity at every height.

Constructive mathematics

The double-negation translation in $\S 5$ embeds classical logic into intuitionistic logic — a precise way to say "classical mathematics is intuitionistic mathematics about double-negation-stable propositions". This is the logician's translation; first-order logic develops the syntactic side, and reverse mathematics studies which classical theorems require which non-constructive principles.

Open frontiers: realizability topoi (effectivity carriers a different internal logic), modal type theory (geometric morphisms become modal operators), and synthetic mathematics done inside a topos (synthetic differential geometry, synthetic stability theory) all exploit the same internal-language machinery.

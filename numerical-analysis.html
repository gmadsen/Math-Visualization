Numerical analysis

From a real-number problem to a finite computation: floating-point arithmetic, rootfinding orders, quadrature error scaling, conditioning of linear systems, finite-difference stability for the heat and wave equations, and the Galerkin finite-element method on a mesh. The bridge from real analysis and PDEs to a working algorithm.

1. Floating-point arithmetic

A computer cannot store $\pi$. It stores an approximation of finite length. The IEEE 754 double-precision format encodes a real $x$ as $$ x \;\approx\; (-1)^s \cdot (1.m_1 m_2 \cdots m_{52})_2 \cdot 2^{e-1023}, $$ with $1$ sign bit, $11$ exponent bits, and $52$ mantissa bits — $64$ bits in total. The relative gap between consecutive representable numbers near $1$ is the machine epsilon $\varepsilon_M = 2^{-52} \approx 2.22\times 10^{-16}$.

Every basic operation $\circ\in\{+,-,\times,/\}$ produces a rounded result satisfying $$ \mathrm{fl}(a\circ b) \;=\; (a\circ b)(1+\delta), \qquad |\delta|\le \varepsilon_M. $$ A long computation chains these tiny errors. The key observation: the same numerical answer can come from a stable algorithm on a sensitive problem, or an unstable algorithm on a benign one.

Conditioning vs. stability

  • Conditioning is a property of the problem. A condition number $\kappa$ measures how much $f(x)$ moves when $x$ is perturbed: $\kappa = \sup_{\delta x} \frac{\|f(x+\delta x)-f(x)\|/\|f(x)\|}{\|\delta x\|/\|x\|}$.
  • Stability is a property of the algorithm. Backward stable means the computed $\hat y$ is the exact answer for some perturbed input $x+\delta x$ with $\|\delta x\|\lesssim \varepsilon_M\|x\|$.

Forward error $\le \kappa \cdot \text{(backward error)}$. A backward-stable algorithm on an ill-conditioned problem still loses $\log_{10}\kappa$ digits, and that's not the algorithm's fault.

Catastrophic cancellation
subtract two nearby reals to see digits evaporate
10⁻⁶

Pushing $x$ smaller than $\varepsilon_M$ collapses $1+x$ back to $1$ in finite precision; the subtraction returns $0$ instead of $x$. This is catastrophic cancellation: the relevant information was destroyed in the addition before the subtraction had a chance to recover it. The cure is algorithmic: rewrite $\sqrt{x+1}-\sqrt{x}$ as $1/(\sqrt{x+1}+\sqrt{x})$, or use Kahan summation when adding many terms.

2. Rootfinding: bisection and Newton

Given $f\colon [a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ continuous with $f(a)f(b)<0$, the intermediate value theorem promises a root. Bisection exploits this: at each step, evaluate $f$ at the midpoint $m=(a+b)/2$ and replace whichever endpoint shares its sign. The interval halves on each step, so after $n$ iterations the root is bracketed in a window of width $(b-a)/2^n$.

If $f$ is differentiable, Newton's method linearizes near the current guess: at $x_n$, replace $f$ by its tangent, find where it crosses zero, $$ x_{n+1} \;=\; x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}. $$ Near a simple root $r$ (where $f(r)=0$, $f'(r)\ne 0$), Taylor's theorem gives $|x_{n+1}-r| \le C|x_n-r|^2$ — quadratic convergence. The number of correct digits roughly doubles each step.

Order of convergence

A sequence $x_n\to r$ has order $p$ with rate $C$ if $|x_{n+1}-r|\le C|x_n-r|^p$ for $n$ large. Examples:

The error after each Newton step squares the previous error. Starting from $x_0=2.5$ with $|e_0|\approx 1.09$, three steps reach machine precision; bisection on the same interval would need $\sim 50$ steps. The price is $f'$: when the derivative is unavailable or expensive, the secant method substitutes a finite difference and pays a slightly worse golden-ratio order $p\approx 1.618$.

Failure modes. Newton diverges if $x_0$ is too far from the root (the tangent overshoots), if $f'(r)=0$ (multiple root — order drops to linear), or if iterates fall into a cycle. Robust solvers combine bisection's safety with Newton's speed: Brent's method.

3. Quadrature: trapezoid, Simpson, Gauss

Quadrature means numerical integration: approximate $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ by a finite weighted sum $\sum_i w_i f(x_i)$. Three families dominate, distinguished by how cleverly they place the nodes $x_i$ and choose the weights $w_i$:

  • Newton–Cotes: equally spaced nodes, weights chosen to integrate polynomials. Trapezoid rule (degree $1$), Simpson's rule (degree $3$), and onward.
  • Gauss–Legendre: nodes are zeros of orthogonal polynomials, weights from the Christoffel function. With $n$ nodes, exact for polynomials of degree $\le 2n-1$.
  • Adaptive: refine where $f$ changes rapidly, leave smooth regions alone.

Composite trapezoid and Simpson

Subdivide $[a,b]$ into $n$ panels of width $h=(b-a)/n$. The composite trapezoid rule sums trapezoids: $$ T_n(f) \;=\; h\!\left[\tfrac12 f(x_0) + f(x_1)+\cdots+f(x_{n-1}) + \tfrac12 f(x_n)\right], \qquad \int_a^b f - T_n(f) = -\frac{(b-a)h^2}{12}f''(\xi). $$ The error is $O(h^2)$: doubling $n$ shrinks it by $4$. Simpson's rule fits a parabola through three consecutive nodes and integrates it; the resulting error is $O(h^4)$ — doubling $n$ shrinks it by $16$.

Quadrature error vs. number of panels
log–log plot — slope = order of accuracy

Trapezoid lines have slope $-2$ on the log–log plot — confirming the $O(h^2) = O(N^{-2})$ rate. Simpson's slope is $-4$. Gauss–Legendre with $n$ nodes integrates polynomials of degree $\le 2n-1$ exactly, so on $x^4$ (degree $4$), $n=4$ Gauss nodes nail it to machine precision while trapezoid still has $\sim 10^{-5}$ error at $N=1024$. The Gaussian advantage is worth a thousand panels.

When trapezoid wins. For periodic integrands integrated over a full period, the composite trapezoid rule converges spectrally — faster than any polynomial rate. The Euler–Maclaurin formula explains why: the boundary terms vanish.

4. Linear systems: direct and iterative

Solving $Ax=b$ for $A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ splits into two regimes: direct methods (factor $A$, then back-substitute) and iterative methods (build a sequence $x^{(k)}\to x$). Density and structure decide which is faster.

Direct factorizations

FactorizationFormCostWhen to use

The condition number

The condition number of an invertible $A$ is $\kappa(A) = \|A\|\,\|A^{-1}\|$. For $\|\cdot\|_2$ this equals $\sigma_{\max}(A)/\sigma_{\min}(A)$, the ratio of largest to smallest singular values. Perturbation theory: $$ \frac{\|\delta x\|}{\|x\|} \;\le\; \kappa(A)\!\left(\frac{\|\delta A\|}{\|A\|} + \frac{\|\delta b\|}{\|b\|}\right). $$ Rule of thumb: solving $Ax=b$ in IEEE double loses $\log_{10}\kappa(A)$ digits.

Iterative methods

For large sparse $A$, factorizations destroy sparsity (fill-in) and become impractical. Iterative methods sidestep the issue. Jacobi: split $A=D+L+U$ (diagonal, strict-lower, strict-upper) and iterate $x^{(k+1)} = D^{-1}(b - (L+U)x^{(k)})$. Gauss–Seidel uses the freshly-computed entries: $x^{(k+1)} = (D+L)^{-1}(b - U x^{(k)})$. Both converge iff the spectral radius of the iteration matrix is $<1$.

Conjugate gradient on SPD systems is essentially optimal. After $k$ iterations, the error in the $A$-norm satisfies $$ \|x - x^{(k)}\|_A \;\le\; 2\!\left(\frac{\sqrt{\kappa}-1}{\sqrt{\kappa}+1}\right)^{\!k}\!\|x - x^{(0)}\|_A. $$ So $O(\sqrt\kappa)$ iterations buy a fixed-factor reduction — exponentially better than Jacobi's $O(\kappa)$. Preconditioning (multiply both sides by $M^{-1}\approx A^{-1}$) shrinks the effective $\kappa$ further.

5. Finite differences and CFL stability

Replace derivatives by finite differences on a grid. Three workhorses for $f'(x)$:

$$ \underbrace{\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}}_{\text{forward, }O(h)} \quad \underbrace{\frac{f(x)-f(x-h)}{h}}_{\text{backward, }O(h)} \quad \underbrace{\frac{f(x+h)-f(x-h)}{2h}}_{\text{central, }O(h^2)}. $$

For $f''$, the standard 3-point stencil is $\bigl(f(x-h)-2f(x)+f(x+h)\bigr)/h^2 = f''(x) + O(h^2)$.

Discretizing the heat equation

Discretize $u_t = u_{xx}$ on a grid $x_j = j\Delta x$, $t^n = n\Delta t$. The simplest explicit scheme is forward time, central space (FTCS): $$ u_j^{n+1} \;=\; u_j^n + \lambda\bigl(u_{j+1}^n - 2u_j^n + u_{j-1}^n\bigr), \qquad \lambda = \frac{\Delta t}{(\Delta x)^2}. $$ Insert a Fourier mode $u_j^n = g^n e^{i\xi j\Delta x}$. The amplification factor is $g(\xi) = 1 - 2\lambda(1-\cos(\xi\Delta x)) = 1 - 4\lambda\sin^2(\xi\Delta x/2)$. Stability — $|g|\le 1$ for all $\xi$ — demands $\lambda\le \tfrac12$, i.e. $$ \boxed{\;\Delta t \;\le\; \tfrac12 (\Delta x)^2.\;} $$ Cross this bound and the highest-frequency Fourier mode amplifies geometrically; round-off blows up.

The CFL condition

For the wave equation $u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}$, the explicit centered scheme has stability requirement $$ \frac{c\,\Delta t}{\Delta x} \;\le\; 1 \qquad\text{(Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy).} $$ Geometrically: the numerical domain of dependence (the stencil's reach) must contain the analytic one (the cone with slope $1/c$). If a node depends on data the stencil can't see, the scheme is doomed.

Implicit schemes (backward Euler, Crank–Nicolson) are unconditionally stable: any $\Delta t$ is safe. The price is a tridiagonal solve at every step. For stiff problems where the explicit step is forced absurdly small, the trade is decisive.

6. Finite elements and the Galerkin method

Finite differences live on a regular grid; finite elements live on an unstructured mesh and integrate the weak form of the PDE. For the model problem $-\Delta u = f$ on $\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^2$ with $u=0$ on $\partial\Omega$, multiply by a test function $v\in H^1_0$ and integrate by parts: $$ \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v \;=\; \int_\Omega f v \qquad \text{for every } v\in H^1_0(\Omega). $$ This is the weak formulation. A weak solution $u\in H^1_0$ exists and is unique by Lax–Milgram.

Discretization

Triangulate $\Omega$ into a mesh $\mathcal{T}_h$ of size $h$. For each interior node $i$, let $\varphi_i$ be the piecewise-linear "hat" function with $\varphi_i(x_j)=\delta_{ij}$. The space $V_h = \mathrm{span}\{\varphi_i\}\subset H^1_0$ is finite-dimensional, and we look for $u_h = \sum_i c_i\varphi_i\in V_h$ satisfying the weak form against every $\varphi_j\in V_h$: $$ \sum_i c_i \int_\Omega \nabla\varphi_i\cdot\nabla\varphi_j \;=\; \int_\Omega f\varphi_j \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad K\mathbf{c}=\mathbf{b}. $$ The stiffness matrix $K_{ij} = \int\nabla\varphi_i\cdot\nabla\varphi_j$ is sparse (each hat sees only its neighbours) and SPD.

A priori error estimates

Let $u\in H^2$ be the true weak solution and $u_h\in V_h$ the Galerkin approximation. Céa's lemma says $$ \|u - u_h\|_{H^1} \;\le\; \frac{M}{\alpha}\inf_{v_h\in V_h}\|u - v_h\|_{H^1}, $$ where $M,\alpha$ are the continuity and coercivity constants of the bilinear form. Combining with linear interpolation error gives $$ \|u - u_h\|_{H^1} \le C h\, \|u\|_{H^2}, \qquad \|u - u_h\|_{L^2} \le C h^2\,\|u\|_{H^2}. $$ The $L^2$ rate is one order better, by an Aubin–Nitsche duality argument. Higher-order ($P^k$) elements lift these to $h^k$ and $h^{k+1}$ — at the cost of a denser stiffness matrix.

Connections

Numerical analysis is where infinite-precision mathematics meets finite hardware. Open frontiers cluster around three questions: which problems admit provably stable algorithms (the field of randomized numerical linear algebra and probabilistic backward error analysis); how to design schemes for structure-preserving integration of geometric PDEs (symplectic integrators, mimetic discretization); and how to exploit modern hardware (sparse on accelerators, mixed-precision iterative refinement, ML-accelerated solvers).

  • Partial differential equations — Weak solutions — FEM is the computational realization of the Galerkin idea introduced there.
  • Functional analysis — Hilbert spaces — the inner-product geometry that makes CG converge in $O(\sqrt\kappa)$ steps and that justifies Galerkin orthogonality.
  • Harmonic analysis — von Neumann stability analysis is Fourier analysis on the discrete grid; spectral methods extend trapezoid's spectral accuracy to non-periodic settings.
  • Real analysis — Differentiation — every Newton-style method, every $O(h^p)$ truncation estimate, is a Taylor remainder argument.
  • Probability theory — Monte Carlo and quasi-Monte Carlo methods turn high-dimensional integrals from $O(h^{-d})$ deterministic cost into $O(N^{-1/2})$ stochastic error, escaping the curse of dimensionality.

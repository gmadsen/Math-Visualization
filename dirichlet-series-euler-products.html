Dirichlet series and Euler products

From $\sum a_n/n^s$ to the analytic form of unique factorization. The Riemann zeta function, Dirichlet $L$-functions, the functional equation, and the theorem that every arithmetic progression $a \bmod N$ with $\gcd(a,N)=1$ contains infinitely many primes.

1. Dirichlet series and the half-plane of convergence

A Dirichlet series is a formal expression $$D(s) \;=\; \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{a_n}{n^s}, \qquad s \in \mathbb{C},$$ where the coefficients $a_n$ are complex numbers. Power series live on discs; Dirichlet series live on right half-planes. Write $s = \sigma + it$. If the series converges absolutely at some $s_0 = \sigma_0 + it_0$, then $$\sum_n \frac{|a_n|}{n^{\sigma_0}} < \infty \;\Longrightarrow\; \sum_n \frac{|a_n|}{n^{\sigma}} \le \sum_n \frac{|a_n|}{n^{\sigma_0}} < \infty$$ for every $\sigma \ge \sigma_0$, because $n^{-\sigma} \le n^{-\sigma_0}$ when $n \ge 1$. So the set of $s$ at which $D(s)$ converges absolutely is a right half-plane $$\{\,s : \mathrm{Re}\,s > \sigma_a\,\}$$ for some abscissa of absolute convergence $\sigma_a \in [-\infty, +\infty]$. Conditional convergence has its own abscissa $\sigma_c \le \sigma_a$, and $\sigma_a - \sigma_c \le 1$ — we stay in the absolute regime here for simplicity.

2. The prototype: Riemann's $\zeta(s)$

Take every $a_n = 1$. You get the Riemann zeta function: $$\zeta(s) \;=\; \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s}.$$ Compare with $\int_1^\infty x^{-\sigma}\,dx = 1/(\sigma-1)$ for $\sigma > 1$. The integral test gives absolute convergence for $\mathrm{Re}\,s > 1$ and divergence at $s = 1$ (where the series is the harmonic series). The abscissa of absolute convergence is $\sigma_a = 1$.

Near $s=1$ the function has a simple pole with residue $1$: $$\zeta(s) \;=\; \frac{1}{s-1} \;+\; \gamma \;+\; O(s-1),$$ where $\gamma = \lim_{N\to\infty}\!\bigl(\sum_{n\le N} 1/n - \log N\bigr) \approx 0.5772$ is Euler's constant. One way to see the pole: $\sum_{n=1}^N n^{-s} \approx \int_1^N x^{-s}\,dx = (N^{1-s}-1)/(1-s)$, whose limit as $N\to\infty$ has a $1/(s-1)$ singularity. Read more on the complex-analysis page for what "simple pole with residue" means.

3. Euler products: the analytic form of unique factorization

Here is the miracle. A Dirichlet series whose coefficients $a_n$ are multiplicative — meaning $a_{mn} = a_m a_n$ whenever $\gcd(m,n)=1$, and $a_1 = 1$ — splits as a product indexed by primes: $$\sum_{n\ge 1} \frac{a_n}{n^s} \;=\; \prod_{p\text{ prime}} \Bigl(\sum_{k\ge 0} \frac{a_{p^k}}{p^{ks}}\Bigr).$$ Why? Expand the right side. Unique factorization says every $n \ge 1$ is a finite product $n = p_1^{k_1}\cdots p_r^{k_r}$ in exactly one way, so every term $a_n/n^s$ appears exactly once in the product. That is the entire proof. The Euler product is, in Riemann's words, "the analytic form of unique factorization."

If $a_n$ is completely multiplicative — $a_{mn} = a_m a_n$ for all $m,n$ — the local factor at $p$ collapses to a geometric series: $$\sum_{k\ge 0} \frac{a_p^k}{p^{ks}} \;=\; \frac{1}{1 - a_p/p^s}.$$ So $\zeta(s)$, with $a_n \equiv 1$ (the identity is completely multiplicative), becomes $$\zeta(s) \;=\; \prod_{p}\frac{1}{1-p^{-s}} \qquad (\mathrm{Re}\,s>1).$$ A statement about factoring integers has become a statement about an analytic function. See algebraic-number-theory for why "unique factorization" is the right primitive, and what goes wrong (ideals) when it fails in number rings.

Sieving the primes out

Multiply $\zeta(s)$ by $(1-2^{-s})$: every term whose index is divisible by $2$ cancels, leaving the sum of $1/n^s$ over odd $n$. Multiply by $(1-3^{-s})$: the remaining multiples of $3$ cancel too. Continue over all primes and you are left with $1/1^s = 1$. That is the Euler product rearranged as a sieve: $$\zeta(s) \cdot \prod_{p}(1-p^{-s}) \;=\; 1.$$ Watch the partial sieve converge to $1$.

4. Dirichlet characters and $L$-functions

A Dirichlet character modulo $N$ is a homomorphism $$\chi\colon (\mathbb{Z}/N)^\times \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbb{C}^\times,$$ extended to all of $\mathbb{Z}$ by declaring $\chi(n) = \chi(n \bmod N)$ if $\gcd(n,N)=1$ and $\chi(n)=0$ otherwise. Because the image lands in roots of unity (the group $(\mathbb{Z}/N)^\times$ is finite, so every element has finite order), $|\chi(n)| \le 1$. The trivial character $\chi_0$ is $1$ on units and $0$ on non-units; it is not quite the constant $1$ (it picks up the non-units).

Characters are completely multiplicative: $\chi(mn)=\chi(m)\chi(n)$ for all $m,n$. So the associated Dirichlet $L$-function $$L(s,\chi) \;=\; \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\chi(n)}{n^s}$$ has an Euler product: $$L(s,\chi) \;=\; \prod_p \frac{1}{1-\chi(p)\,p^{-s}} \qquad (\mathrm{Re}\,s > 1).$$ For $\chi = \chi_0$ mod $N$: the local factor at $p\mid N$ is $1$ (since $\chi_0(p)=0$), everywhere else it is $(1-p^{-s})^{-1}$. So $$L(s,\chi_0) \;=\; \zeta(s) \prod_{p\mid N}(1-p^{-s}).$$ For $\chi \ne \chi_0$, the sum is conditionally convergent for $\mathrm{Re}\,s > 0$ too (the character values are bounded and roughly cancel), so $L(s,\chi)$ extends to the half-plane $\mathrm{Re}\,s > 0$ directly — a smaller miracle than $\zeta$'s full continuation.

Example: the non-trivial character mod 4

Here $(\mathbb{Z}/4)^\times = \{1,3\}$ is cyclic of order $2$. The non-trivial character $\chi_3$ sends $1\mapsto 1$, $3\mapsto -1$: $$\chi_3(n) = \begin{cases} +1 & n\equiv 1\pmod 4\\ -1 & n\equiv 3\pmod 4\\ 0 & n\text{ even}\end{cases}$$ giving the famous Leibniz series: $$L(s,\chi_3) \;=\; 1 - \frac{1}{3^s} + \frac{1}{5^s} - \frac{1}{7^s} + \cdots, \qquad L(1,\chi_3) = \frac{\pi}{4}.$$ That specific identity — evaluating the series at $s=1$ — is a prototype of what class-number formulas compute. See quadratic-reciprocity for how quadratic characters $\left(\tfrac{\cdot}{p}\right)$ fit this framework.

5. Analytic continuation and the functional equation

The series $\sum 1/n^s$ only makes sense for $\mathrm{Re}\,s > 1$. But $\zeta$ extends to a meromorphic function on all of $\mathbb{C}$, with a single simple pole at $s=1$. The extension is not merely a technicality — it connects the primes at $\mathrm{Re}\,s > 1$ to the "mirror world" at $\mathrm{Re}\,s < 0$ via a functional equation.

Define the completed zeta $$\Lambda(s) \;=\; \pi^{-s/2}\,\Gamma(s/2)\,\zeta(s).$$ The factor $\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)$ is the "archimedean Euler factor" at the prime at infinity — each prime, including the archimedean one, contributes. The functional equation reads: $$\boxed{\;\Lambda(s) \;=\; \Lambda(1-s).\;}$$

Sketch of Riemann's contour argument

Riemann starts from $\Gamma(s/2)\pi^{-s/2} n^{-s} = \int_0^\infty x^{s/2-1} e^{-\pi n^2 x}\,dx$ (a scaled gamma integral). Summing over $n$ and interchanging: $$\Lambda(s) \;=\; \int_0^\infty x^{s/2-1} \Bigl(\sum_{n\ge 1} e^{-\pi n^2 x}\Bigr) dx \;=\; \int_0^\infty x^{s/2-1}\, \tfrac{\theta(x) - 1}{2}\, dx,$$ where $\theta(x) = \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} e^{-\pi n^2 x}$ is the Jacobi theta function. The key fact is the modular transformation $$\theta(1/x) \;=\; \sqrt{x}\,\theta(x)$$ (a consequence of Poisson summation). Split the integral at $x=1$, apply $\theta(1/x) = \sqrt x\, \theta(x)$ to the $[0,1]$ half, and the symmetry $s \leftrightarrow 1-s$ drops out after an elementary substitution. The formula also exhibits $\Lambda$ as meromorphic on $\mathbb{C}$ with simple poles only at $s=0$ and $s=1$ (so $\zeta$ has only the pole at $s=1$; the one at $0$ is eaten by $\Gamma(s/2)$).

Why this matters. The functional equation trades knowledge of $\zeta$ on $\mathrm{Re}\,s > 1$ (where we have convergence) for knowledge on $\mathrm{Re}\,s < 0$. The "critical strip" $0 \le \mathrm{Re}\,s \le 1$ is where the interesting zeros live. The Riemann Hypothesis conjectures all non-trivial zeros lie on $\mathrm{Re}\,s = 1/2$.

The same game works for Dirichlet $L$-functions: each non-trivial $L(s,\chi)$ extends to an entire function and satisfies a functional equation relating $s$ and $1-s$, with a "root number" $W(\chi)$ (a complex number of absolute value $1$) appearing: $$\Lambda(s,\chi) \;=\; W(\chi)\,\Lambda(1-s,\overline{\chi}).$$

6. The Mellin transform: turning series into integrals

Every identity of the form "a Dirichlet series equals an integral against a theta-like kernel" is an instance of the Mellin transform. For a function $f\colon (0,\infty)\to\mathbb{C}$ decaying fast enough at both ends, define $$\mathcal{M}[f](s) \;=\; \int_0^\infty f(x)\,x^{s-1}\,dx.$$ This is the multiplicative analogue of the Fourier transform: substituting $x=e^u$ converts it into $\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(e^u) e^{us}\,du$, a Laplace/Fourier transform on the line. Its inverse is a vertical contour integral, $$f(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty} \mathcal{M}[f](s)\,x^{-s}\,ds,$$ taken along any line $\mathrm{Re}\,s = c$ inside the strip of absolute convergence.

The key computation that links Mellin to Dirichlet series is the gamma integral $\int_0^\infty e^{-nx} x^{s-1}\,dx = \Gamma(s)\,n^{-s}$. Summing over $n$ and interchanging, $$\Gamma(s)\,\sum_{n\ge 1}\frac{a_n}{n^s} \;=\; \int_0^\infty \Bigl(\sum_{n\ge 1} a_n e^{-nx}\Bigr) x^{s-1}\,dx \;=\; \mathcal{M}\!\bigl[\textstyle\sum_n a_n e^{-nx}\bigr](s).$$ A Dirichlet series has become the Mellin transform of a generating function on the positive real axis. For $a_n=1$ the generating function is $1/(e^x-1)$, giving $\Gamma(s)\zeta(s) = \int_0^\infty x^{s-1}/(e^x-1)\,dx$. For $a_n=1$ on squares (via $\theta(x)$), the same recipe produces Riemann's integral representation of $\Lambda(s)$ from section 5. Complex analysis takes over from here: the integral is an analytic object, so continuation is automatic once you control the kernel.

7. Perron's formula: recovering coefficients from the series

A Dirichlet series $D(s) = \sum_{n\ge 1} a_n/n^s$ encodes the sequence $(a_n)$ as an analytic function. How do we run the encoding backwards? The answer is Perron's formula: for any $c > \sigma_a$ and $x > 0$ not an integer, $$A(x) \;=\; \sum_{n < x} a_n \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty} D(s)\,\frac{x^s}{s}\,ds.$$ It is a Mellin-inversion identity dressed up with the kernel $x^s/s$, whose discontinuous step function $\mathbf{1}[nx$, picking up the residue at $s=0$ in the first case).

The payoff is asymptotic analysis by residue bookkeeping. Shift the contour $\mathrm{Re}\,s=c$ to the left past the poles of $D(s)x^s/s$; each pole contributes a residue that becomes a main term in $A(x)$. For $D = \zeta$, the pole of $\zeta(s)$ at $s=1$ contributes residue $x$, and the simple pole of $1/s$ at $s=0$ contributes $\zeta(0) = -1/2$: $$\sum_{nresidue theorem does the accounting.

8. Dirichlet's theorem: primes in arithmetic progressions

Theorem (Dirichlet, 1837). If $\gcd(a,N) = 1$, then there are infinitely many primes $p$ with $p \equiv a \pmod N$.

Euclid showed infinitely many primes by evaluating $\zeta(s)$ at $s=1$ and watching it blow up. Dirichlet's theorem refines this: not just "infinitely many primes" but "infinitely many primes in each residue class coprime to $N$." The proof runs on $L$-functions.

Recall the Euler product $$L(s,\chi) \;=\; \prod_p \bigl(1-\chi(p)p^{-s}\bigr)^{-1} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \log L(s,\chi) \;=\; \sum_p \frac{\chi(p)}{p^s} + O(1)$$ as $s \to 1^+$ (the $O(1)$ absorbs the convergent higher-power terms). Using orthogonality of characters — $\frac{1}{\varphi(N)}\sum_\chi \overline{\chi(a)}\chi(p) = \mathbf{1}[p \equiv a]$ — we pick out residues: $$\sum_{p \equiv a\,(N)} \frac{1}{p^s} \;=\; \frac{1}{\varphi(N)} \sum_\chi \overline{\chi(a)}\,\log L(s,\chi) + O(1).$$ The trivial character contributes $\log L(s,\chi_0) = \log \zeta(s) + O(1) \to +\infty$ as $s \to 1^+$ (that is Euclid's divergence). If we can show each non-trivial $\log L(s,\chi)$ stays bounded as $s\to 1^+$, then the main term diverges at the rate $\frac{1}{\varphi(N)}\log\zeta(s)$, forcing the residue-class sum $\sum_{p\equiv a} 1/p^s$ to diverge — hence infinitely many such primes.

The crucial non-vanishing

"$\log L(s,\chi)$ stays bounded as $s\to 1^+$" is equivalent to $$\boxed{\;L(1,\chi) \ne 0 \text{ and } L(1,\chi) \text{ is finite for every } \chi \ne \chi_0.\;}$$ Finiteness is easy (the conditionally convergent series $\sum \chi(n)/n$ converges). Non-vanishing is the hard part. For complex characters it follows from comparing to $\zeta$; for real non-trivial characters it requires genuinely new ideas — one slick route uses the fact that the class number of a related quadratic field is positive.

The theorem has a quantitative refinement: primes are equidistributed among the $\varphi(N)$ allowed residue classes. The counting function $\pi(x; N, a) = \#\{p \le x : p \equiv a \pmod N\}$ satisfies $$\pi(x; N, a) \;\sim\; \frac{1}{\varphi(N)}\,\frac{x}{\log x} \qquad (\gcd(a,N)=1).$$

7. What comes next

Once you believe a Dirichlet series encodes arithmetic into analysis, and an Euler product encodes unique factorization into geometry, you can ask the same questions of other arithmetic objects.

  • $L$-functions of modular forms. A modular form $f = \sum a_n q^n$ produces a Dirichlet series $L(f,s) = \sum a_n / n^s$. When $f$ is a Hecke eigenform, $L(f,s)$ has an Euler product — the Hecke eigenvalues at primes $p$ become the local factor.
  • $L$-functions of elliptic curves. An elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ gives $L(E,s) = \prod_p L_p(E,s)^{-1}$, where $L_p(E,s) = 1 - a_p p^{-s} + p^{1-2s}$ and $a_p = p+1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is the error in counting points mod $p$. See the elliptic-curves page.
  • Modularity. The deep theorem at the heart of this circle (Wiles, Breuil–Conrad–Diamond–Taylor): every $L(E,s)$ is also the $L$-function of a weight-$2$ modular form. Analytic continuation for $L(E,s)$ is then automatic.
  • BSD. The Birch–Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture predicts that the rank of $E(\mathbb{Q})$ equals the order of vanishing of $L(E,s)$ at $s=1$. It is the modern analogue of Dirichlet's $L(1,\chi) \ne 0$.

Each of these moves promotes a finite-dimensional arithmetic quantity (a class number, a rank, a point count) into a question about zeros and residues of a generating Dirichlet series.

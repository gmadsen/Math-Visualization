Galois representations

The absolute Galois group $G_\mathbb{Q}$ is unimaginably huge — but you can study it by letting it act on vector spaces. A Galois representation $\rho: G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_n$ packages an entire Diophantine object (an elliptic curve, a modular form, a number field) as a tower of matrices. The Frobenius trace at each unramified prime is the fingerprint.

Prerequisites: Frobenius and reciprocity, representation theory, Galois theory, elliptic curves, $p$-adic numbers.

1. The absolute Galois group $G_\mathbb{Q}$

Let $\bar{\mathbb{Q}} \subset \mathbb{C}$ be the field of all algebraic numbers. The absolute Galois group of $\mathbb{Q}$ is

$$ G_\mathbb{Q} \;=\; \mathrm{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \;=\; \{\sigma:\bar{\mathbb{Q}}\to\bar{\mathbb{Q}} \text{ field auto, fixing } \mathbb{Q}\}. $$

It is the symmetry group of all of algebraic number theory at once. Every finite Galois extension $K/\mathbb{Q}$ appears as a quotient:

$$ G_\mathbb{Q} \;\twoheadrightarrow\; \mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q}). $$

And $G_\mathbb{Q}$ is the inverse limit over all such finite quotients — a profinite group:

$$ G_\mathbb{Q} \;\cong\; \varprojlim_{K/\mathbb{Q}\text{ finite Galois}}\; \mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q}). $$

Its topology is generated by these finite quotients. Two automorphisms are close when they agree on a large finite extension. This topology is compact, Hausdorff, totally disconnected — like the Cantor set.

Frobenius at a prime $p$

For each prime $p$, pick an embedding $\bar{\mathbb{Q}} \hookrightarrow \bar{\mathbb{Q}}_p$. That choice identifies a decomposition subgroup $D_p \subset G_\mathbb{Q}$ — the stabiliser of the embedding. Inside $D_p$ sits the inertia subgroup $I_p$, which encodes wild/tame ramification. When $p$ is unramified in a finite Galois extension $K/\mathbb{Q}$, the quotient $D_p/I_p$ is cyclic, generated canonically by the Frobenius element

$$ \mathrm{Frob}_p \in \mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q}) \big/ I_p, \qquad \mathrm{Frob}_p: x \mapsto x^p \pmod{\mathfrak p}. $$

Different choices of embedding give conjugate $\mathrm{Frob}_p$'s, so $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ is well-defined as a conjugacy class in $G_\mathbb{Q}/I_p$.

Think of $G_\mathbb{Q}$ as "the group of all coherent permutations of algebraic numbers that respect every field relation." Its structure is almost entirely unknown — understanding its representations is a substitute for understanding the group itself.

2. What is a Galois representation?

A Galois representation is a continuous homomorphism

$$ \rho \colon G_\mathbb{Q} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{GL}_n(K) $$

where the coefficient field $K$ is one of three standard choices:

coefficient fieldnamesource
$K = \mathbb{C}$Artin representationfinite image; factors through $\mathrm{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q})$ for some finite $L$
$K = \bar{\mathbb{Q}}_\ell$$\ell$-adic representationfrom étale cohomology, elliptic curves, modular forms
$K = \bar{\mathbb{F}}_\ell$mod-$\ell$ representationreductions of $\ell$-adic representations; images are finite

Continuity is the real content. Since $G_\mathbb{Q}$ is profinite, a continuous $\rho$ must have open kernel on finite quotients, but an $\ell$-adic representation need not factor through any finite quotient — the target $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ itself is profinite, and a generic element of its image can have infinite order.

Why "linear symmetries of $G_\mathbb{Q}$"?

A Galois representation is the $\ell$-adic (or complex) analog of a representation of a finite group. For finite groups, characters classify representations, and characters are determined by conjugacy classes. For $G_\mathbb{Q}$, the trace of Frobenius at each unramified prime plays the role of the character — and the astonishing fact is that this finite amount of data really does determine $\rho$ (up to semisimplification).

3. Three families of examples

(a) The cyclotomic character

The group $G_\mathbb{Q}$ acts on the $\ell^n$-th roots of unity $\mu_{\ell^n} = \{\zeta \in \bar{\mathbb{Q}}: \zeta^{\ell^n}=1\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/\ell^n$. On a generator $\zeta$, any $\sigma \in G_\mathbb{Q}$ acts by

$$ \sigma(\zeta) \;=\; \zeta^{\,\chi_n(\sigma)}, \qquad \chi_n(\sigma) \in (\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n)^\times. $$

These fit together into the $\ell$-adic cyclotomic character

$$ \chi \colon G_\mathbb{Q} \;\longrightarrow\; \varprojlim_n\, (\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n)^\times \;=\; \mathbb{Z}_\ell^\times. $$

This is a 1-dimensional $\ell$-adic Galois representation. At an unramified prime $p \neq \ell$, $\chi(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = p$.

(b) Dirichlet characters

A Dirichlet character modulo $N$ is a homomorphism $\psi:(\mathbb{Z}/N)^\times \to \mathbb{C}^\times$. Composing with the mod-$N$ cyclotomic quotient $G_\mathbb{Q} \twoheadrightarrow (\mathbb{Z}/N)^\times$ gives a 1-dimensional Artin representation

$$ \rho_\psi \colon G_\mathbb{Q} \to (\mathbb{Z}/N)^\times \xrightarrow{\psi} \mathbb{C}^\times. $$

Every 1-dimensional Artin representation is of this form (class field theory for $\mathbb{Q}$). The image is finite, and $\rho_\psi(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = \psi(p)$ for $p \nmid N$.

(c) Artin representations

More generally, any representation $\mathrm{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q}) \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ of a finite Galois group inflates to a continuous $\rho: G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ with finite image. These are Artin representations, and their conjectural $L$-functions (the Artin conjecture) are at the heart of the Langlands program.

(d) The 2-dimensional zoo

The most interesting representations are 2-dimensional and $\ell$-adic, coming from:

  • Tate modules of elliptic curves (§4),
  • weight-2 cusp forms (§7),
  • odd irreducible Artin representations (Khare–Wintenberger theorem).

4. The Tate module of an elliptic curve

Let $E/\mathbb{Q}$ be an elliptic curve and $\ell$ a prime. The $\ell^n$-torsion subgroup $E[\ell^n] \subset E(\bar{\mathbb{Q}})$ is isomorphic to $(\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n)^2$ as an abelian group, and $G_\mathbb{Q}$ acts on it because the torsion points have algebraic coordinates.

The multiplication-by-$\ell$ map $[\ell]: E[\ell^{n+1}] \to E[\ell^n]$ lets us take the inverse limit:

$$ T_\ell E \;=\; \varprojlim_n\, E[\ell^n] \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}_\ell^2. $$

This is the Tate module. It is a free rank-2 $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-module, and $G_\mathbb{Q}$ acts $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-linearly. Choosing a basis gives the $\ell$-adic Galois representation attached to $E$:

$$ \rho_{E,\ell} \colon G_\mathbb{Q} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell). $$

Tensoring with $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$ gives a representation on a 2-dimensional $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-vector space $V_\ell E = T_\ell E \otimes \mathbb{Q}_\ell$.

Widget 4 · The Tate-module tower $\cdots \to E[\ell^3] \to E[\ell^2] \to E[\ell]$ click a level to see the $(\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n)^2$ lattice it represents
The determinant of $\rho_{E,\ell}$ is the cyclotomic character $\chi$: that is, the Weil pairing identifies $\bigwedge^2 T_\ell E \cong \mathbb{Z}_\ell(1)$, the 1-twist of the trivial representation. So $\det \rho_{E,\ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = p$.

5. Frobenius traces and the Hasse bound

Suppose $E/\mathbb{Q}$ has good reduction at $p$ — equivalently, $p$ does not divide the discriminant. Then $p$ is unramified in $\rho_{E,\ell}$ (for $\ell \neq p$), and $\rho_{E,\ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ is a well-defined conjugacy class in $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$. The matrix itself depends on choices, but its characteristic polynomial does not:

$$ \det(t\cdot I - \rho_{E,\ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)) \;=\; t^2 - a_p\, t + p, $$

where

$$ a_p \;=\; p + 1 - |\tilde E(\mathbb{F}_p)| $$

is the trace of Frobenius: count the points of the reduction $\tilde E$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, and subtract from $p+1$. This is where Diophantine geometry meets linear algebra.

Hasse's theorem

The eigenvalues $\alpha_p, \beta_p$ of $\rho_{E,\ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ are algebraic integers satisfying $\alpha_p\beta_p = p$ and $|\alpha_p| = |\beta_p| = \sqrt{p}$. Equivalently,

$$ |a_p| \;=\; |\alpha_p + \beta_p| \;\le\; 2\sqrt{p}. $$

This is the Riemann hypothesis for elliptic curves over $\mathbb{F}_p$. The eigenvalues land on a circle of radius $\sqrt{p}$.

6. Local $L$-factors from Galois representations

Once you have the representation $\rho_{E,\ell}$, the $L$-function of $E$ emerges by a very clean formula. For each unramified prime $p$ (not $\ell$), define the local $L$-factor

$$ L_p(E,s) \;=\; \det\!\Big(1 - \rho_{E,\ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)\, p^{-s}\Big)^{-1} \;=\; \frac{1}{1 - a_p p^{-s} + p^{1-2s}}. $$

This is literally the determinant of a $2\times 2$ matrix. The global $L$-function is the Euler product

$$ L(E,s) \;=\; \prod_p L_p(E,s). $$

For ramified primes (bad reduction) the Euler factor is $1/(1 - a_p p^{-s})$ with $a_p \in \{-1, 0, 1\}$ depending on the reduction type. The conductor $N$ of $E$ records the ramification.

What the determinant encodes

The local factor packages all the local information at $p$: how many points the reduction has, and whether reduction is good/multiplicative/additive. Different Galois representations $\rho$ give different $L$-functions — and conversely, $L(\rho,s)$ nearly determines $\rho$ (strong multiplicity one).

7. Modular forms and Deligne's construction

Let $f = \sum_{n\ge 1} a_n(f) q^n$ be a normalised cuspidal eigenform of weight $k\ge 2$ on $\Gamma_0(N)$ with character $\varepsilon$. Its Fourier coefficients $a_n(f)$ are algebraic numbers, generating a finite extension $K_f \subset \bar{\mathbb{Q}}$.

A theorem of Eichler–Shimura (weight 2) and Deligne (all weights) attaches to $f$, for each prime $\ell$ and each place $\lambda$ of $K_f$ above $\ell$, a continuous 2-dimensional Galois representation

$$ \rho_{f,\lambda} \colon G_\mathbb{Q} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{GL}_2(K_{f,\lambda}) $$

that is unramified outside $N\ell$ and satisfies, for every prime $p \nmid N\ell$,

$$ \operatorname{tr}\rho_{f,\lambda}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) \;=\; a_p(f), \qquad \det\rho_{f,\lambda}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) \;=\; \varepsilon(p)\, p^{k-1}. $$

The Fourier coefficients of an eigenform are traces of Frobenius. This is one of the deepest bridges in number theory: automorphic $\leftrightarrow$ Galois.

Weight 2 and elliptic curves

When $f$ has weight 2 and rational coefficients, $\rho_{f,\ell}$ lives in $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$. Comparing $\operatorname{tr}\rho_{f,\ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = a_p(f)$ to the $a_p$ of an elliptic curve, one is led to ask whether every elliptic curve arises this way. That question has an answer — but you'll find it in its own discussion, not here.

8. Irreducibility and semisimplification

A Galois representation $\rho: G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_n(K)$ is irreducible if $K^n$ has no proper non-zero $G_\mathbb{Q}$-stable subspace, and semisimple if it decomposes as a direct sum of irreducibles. As with finite-group representations, not every representation is semisimple — an upper-triangular $\rho = \begin{pmatrix}\chi_1 & *\\ 0 & \chi_2\end{pmatrix}$ with non-zero extension class is reducible but not a direct sum. The semisimplification $\rho^{\mathrm{ss}} = \chi_1 \oplus \chi_2$ is obtained by throwing away the off-diagonal cocycle.

Because traces are additive on short exact sequences, $\operatorname{tr}\rho(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = \operatorname{tr}\rho^{\mathrm{ss}}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ at every unramified prime. So Frobenius traces only ever see the semisimplification. By the Brauer–Nesbitt / Chebotarev combination, two semisimple $\ell$-adic representations of $G_\mathbb{Q}$ are isomorphic iff their traces of Frobenius agree at a density-one set of primes — a dramatic rigidity statement. For $\rho_{E,\ell}$ from an elliptic curve, a theorem of Serre guarantees irreducibility for all but finitely many $\ell$, so the semisimplification is just $\rho_{E,\ell}$ itself.

9. Inertia, ramification, and the conductor

Fix a prime $p$ and an embedding $\bar{\mathbb{Q}} \hookrightarrow \bar{\mathbb{Q}}_p$. Inside the decomposition group $D_p \subset G_\mathbb{Q}$ sits the inertia subgroup $I_p$, the kernel of $D_p \twoheadrightarrow \mathrm{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{F}}_p/\mathbb{F}_p)$. A representation $\rho: G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_n(K)$ is unramified at $p$ when $\rho(I_p) = 1$; only in that case does $\rho(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ make sense as a well-defined conjugacy class, so the Frobenius machinery of §5 only applies at unramified primes.

For the bad primes, the Artin conductor exponent $f_p(\rho) = \dim(V) - \dim(V^{I_p}) + \text{(wild term)}$ quantifies how badly $\rho$ is ramified at $p$: the first piece counts missing inertia invariants (the tame drop), the wild term measures higher ramification. The global conductor is the product $N(\rho) = \prod_p p^{f_p(\rho)}$, supported on the finitely many ramified primes. For an elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ the conductor $N_E$ satisfies $f_p = 1$ at primes of multiplicative reduction and $f_p \ge 2$ at primes of additive reduction, matching the conductor that appears in the $L$-function of $E$.

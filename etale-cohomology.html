Étale cohomology, ℓ-adic Betti, and the Weil conjectures

Grothendieck's replacement for singular cohomology in characteristic $p$. Covers of schemes that behave like local homeomorphisms, a cohomology theory that counts the same Betti numbers as topology, and a Frobenius whose trace counts $\mathbb{F}_q$-points.

1. Étale morphisms

Over $\mathbb{C}$ a map of complex manifolds is a local homeomorphism iff its differential is invertible everywhere. Over a scheme the condition splits into three algebraic pieces: flat (fibres vary continuously), unramified (each fibre is a product of separable field extensions of the base point), and locally of finite presentation. The conjunction is étale.

Concretely, $f\colon X\to Y$ is étale iff every point has an affine patch $\operatorname{Spec} B \to \operatorname{Spec} A$ with $B = A[t_1,\dots,t_n]/(g_1,\dots,g_n)$ and Jacobian $\det(\partial g_i/\partial t_j)$ a unit in $B$ — a "complete intersection with non-vanishing Jacobian."

The warning bell in char $p$ is Frobenius $F\colon t\mapsto t^p$: its differential $pt^{p-1}$ vanishes, so $F$ is finite, flat, bijective on points, yet ramified everywhere. Étale cohomology refuses to count it as a cover.

On $\mathbb{G}_m = \operatorname{Spec} k[t,t^{-1}]$ the Kummer map $t\mapsto t^n$ is étale exactly when $n$ is invertible in $k$ — equivalently $\gcd(n,p)=1$ in char $p$, always in char $0$. When étale, the fibre over $t$ is the finite étale scheme of $n$-th roots of $t$ with Galois group $\mu_n$.

Étale morphisms are the "right" notion of cover: Zariski is too coarse, topological covers of $X(\mathbb{C})$ don't exist in char $p$, and étale splits the difference — algebraic, and recovering ordinary covers of $X(\mathbb{C})$ over $\mathbb{C}$.

2. ℓ-adic cohomology

A first naive try — $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Z})$ defined by mimicking singular cohomology on the étale site — fails: constant $\mathbb{Z}$-coefficients give vanishing higher cohomology on most $X$. Torsion is where the action is.

Fix a prime $\ell\neq p$. The constant sheaf $\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n$ has a good étale cohomology $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n)$; these finite groups are compatible as $n$ grows. Take the inverse limit and tensor with $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$:

$$H^i_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Q}_\ell) \;:=\; \Big(\varprojlim_n H^i_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n)\Big)\otimes_{\mathbb{Z}_\ell}\mathbb{Q}_\ell.$$

For $X$ smooth proper this is a finite-dimensional $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-vector space with the expected formalism: Künneth, Poincaré duality, a Lefschetz trace formula, a cycle class map. The restriction $\ell\neq p$ is essential — $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Z}/p^n)$ sees Frobenius-kernel pathology and is handled by crystalline cohomology instead.

Comparison theorem. If $X$ is a smooth projective variety over $\mathbb{C}$, then $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Q}_\ell)\cong H^i_{\text{sing}}(X(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Q})\otimes\mathbb{Q}_\ell$ for every $\ell$. The $\ell$-adic Betti numbers $b_i = \dim_{\mathbb{Q}_\ell}H^i_{\text{ét}}$ agree with the classical ones.

For $X$ smooth projective of dimension $d$, some Betti numbers are automatic: $b_0 = b_{2d} = 1$, $b_1 = 2g$ for a curve of genus $g$, $b_{2i}\ge 1$ from the $i$-fold hyperplane class. The Euler characteristic $\chi(X) = \sum(-1)^i b_i$ is $2-2g$ for curves, $c_2$ for surfaces, $n+1$ for $\mathbb{P}^n$.

3. Grothendieck–Lefschetz fixed-point formula

The classical Lefschetz fixed-point theorem says: for a continuous self-map $f\colon X\to X$ of a nice compact space whose graph meets the diagonal transversally, the signed count of fixed points equals the alternating trace of $f$ on cohomology,

$$\#\mathrm{Fix}(f) \;=\; \sum_i (-1)^i \,\mathrm{Tr}\big(f^*\,\big|\,H^i(X,\mathbb{Q})\big).$$

Grothendieck translates this to the étale setting. For $X$ smooth proper over $\overline{\mathbb{F}_q}$ and an endomorphism $f\colon X\to X$ with graph $\Gamma_f$ transverse to the diagonal $\Delta\subset X\times X$, the intersection number $\Gamma_f\cdot\Delta$ can be computed by pulling back the class of $\Delta$ through $f$ on $H^*_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$. The algebraic version reads

$$(\Gamma_f\cdot\Delta)_{X\times X} \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{2\dim X}(-1)^i\,\mathrm{Tr}\!\big(f^*\,\big|\,H^i_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Q}_\ell)\big).$$

Apply this to the geometric $q$-power Frobenius $F_q$. A fixed point of $F_q^{\,m}$ on $X(\overline{\mathbb{F}_q})$ is exactly an $\mathbb{F}_{q^m}$-rational point, and transversality holds automatically (the derivative of Frobenius is zero, so graph and diagonal meet cleanly). Out falls the identity driving the Weil conjectures:

$$\#X(\mathbb{F}_{q^m}) \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{2\dim X}(-1)^i\,\mathrm{Tr}\!\big(F_q^{\,m}\,\big|\,H^i_{\text{ét}}\big).$$

Why transversality is free for Frobenius. On a smooth variety over $\overline{\mathbb{F}_q}$, the differential $dF_q$ is identically zero on tangent spaces. So $T_x(\Gamma_{F_q})\oplus T_x(\Delta) = T_x(X\times X)$ at every fixed point — every intersection is transverse, every fixed point contributes $+1$, and the formula gives the honest count $\#X(\mathbb{F}_{q^m})$ with no correction terms.

The same machinery reproduces many classical theorems. On $\mathbb{P}^n$ every odd cohomology vanishes and $F_q$ acts as $q^i$ on $H^{2i}$, so $\#\mathbb{P}^n(\mathbb{F}_{q^m}) = 1+q^m+q^{2m}+\dots+q^{nm}$. On a curve of genus $g$ the formula collapses to $\#X(\mathbb{F}_{q^m}) = 1+q^m - \sum_j(\alpha_j^m+\bar\alpha_j^m)$, and the Weil bound $|\,\#X(\mathbb{F}_q)-(q+1)\,|\le 2g\sqrt q$ is immediate from $|\alpha_j|=\sqrt q$.

4. Weil conjectures and the Frobenius trace

With the trace formula from §3 in hand, the payoff is the zeta function. Packaging point counts over all $m$ gives $Z(X,t) = \exp\big(\sum_m \#X(\mathbb{F}_{q^m})\,t^m/m\big)$; the Lefschetz trace plus the identity $\exp(-\operatorname{Tr}\log(1-tA)) = \det(1-tA)^{-1}$ yields

$$Z(X,t) \;=\; \prod_{i=0}^{2\dim X}\det\!\big(1 - t\,\operatorname{Frob}_q\,\big|\,H^i_{\text{ét}}\big)^{(-1)^{i+1}}.$$

Three striking consequences are the Weil conjectures, proved by a team spanning Dwork, Grothendieck, and Deligne:

W1 — Rationality. $Z(X,t)\in\mathbb{Q}(t)$. Each factor $\det(1-t\,\operatorname{Frob}|H^i)$ is a polynomial with $\mathbb{Z}$-coefficients of degree $b_i$.
W2 — Functional equation. Poincaré duality forces $Z(X, 1/(q^d t)) = \pm q^{d\chi/2} t^{\chi} Z(X,t)$, with $d = \dim X$ and $\chi = \sum(-1)^i b_i$.
W3 — Riemann hypothesis (Deligne). Every eigenvalue $\alpha$ of $\operatorname{Frob}_q$ on $H^i_{\text{ét}}$ is an algebraic integer with $|\iota(\alpha)| = q^{i/2}$ for every complex embedding $\iota$.

For a smooth curve of genus $g$ with $b_0=b_2=1$, $b_1=2g$: $Z(X,t) = L(t)/((1-t)(1-qt))$, $L(t) = \prod_j (1-\alpha_j t)(1-\bar\alpha_j t)$, $|\alpha_j|=\sqrt q$. For $g=1$, $L(t) = 1 - a_q t + q t^2$, $a_q = \alpha+\beta$, $\alpha\beta = q$, Hasse $|a_q|\le 2\sqrt q$; the eigenvalues sit at $\pm\theta$ on the circle of radius $\sqrt q$.

The upshot. Point-counts over $\mathbb{F}_{q^m}$ for all $m$ are packaged into finitely many Frobenius eigenvalues, organized by cohomological degree. Étale cohomology is precisely what makes this packaging exist as a theorem, not an analogy.

5. Comparison theorems: étale, singular, de Rham, crystalline

Étale cohomology earns its keep only if it reproduces what we already know when we already know it — and plugs the gap smoothly when we don't. The comparison theorems are the bridge.

Artin's comparison (Artin, Grothendieck, SGA 4). For $X$ a smooth projective variety over $\mathbb{C}$ and any prime $\ell$,

$$H^i_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n) \;\cong\; H^i_{\text{sing}}(X(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n), \qquad H^i_{\text{ét}}(X,\mathbb{Q}_\ell) \;\cong\; H^i_{\text{sing}}(X(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Q})\otimes\mathbb{Q}_\ell.$$

So the $\ell$-adic Betti numbers agree with the classical topological Betti numbers — the same integers, for every $\ell$, computed from totally different data. That independence from $\ell$ is itself a nontrivial consequence.

de Rham comparison (Grothendieck). Over a field $k$ of characteristic zero, smooth varieties carry algebraic de Rham cohomology $H^i_{\text{dR}}(X/k)$ computed from the Koszul complex of Kähler differentials. For $k=\mathbb{C}$,

$$H^i_{\text{dR}}(X/\mathbb{C}) \;\cong\; H^i_{\text{sing}}(X(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{C}),$$

so étale (after tensoring with $\mathbb{C}$ via a chosen embedding $\mathbb{Q}_\ell\hookrightarrow\mathbb{C}$), singular, and de Rham all agree in char $0$. Hodge theory refines the singular side with the $(p,q)$-decomposition.

Crystalline & p-adic Hodge. When the ground field has characteristic $p$ and we try $\ell=p$, étale cohomology breaks down. Crystalline cohomology (Berthelot, Grothendieck) $H^i_{\text{cris}}(X/W)$ is a module over the Witt vectors $W(k)$ with a semi-linear Frobenius — the right $p$-adic Betti theory. In mixed characteristic, p-adic Hodge theory (Fontaine, Faltings, Tsuji, Scholze) supplies a family of period rings $B_{\text{dR}}, B_{\text{cris}}, B_{\text{st}}$ that relate $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K},\mathbb{Q}_p)$ to $H^i_{\text{dR}}(X/K)$ and $H^i_{\text{cris}}$ after tensoring:

$$H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K},\mathbb{Q}_p)\otimes_{\mathbb{Q}_p}B_{\text{cris}} \;\cong\; H^i_{\text{cris}}(X_k/W)\otimes_{W}B_{\text{cris}}.$$

Motivic shadow. The fact that $\ell$-adic Betti numbers are the same integer $b_i$ for every $\ell$, that this integer also equals $\dim_{\mathbb{C}}H^i_{\text{dR}}$ and $\dim_{\mathbb{Q}}H^i_{\text{sing}}$, and that Frobenius eigenvalues and their characteristic polynomials are also $\ell$-independent, is evidence for a conjectural motivic cohomology sitting above all these Weil cohomologies — one realization functor for each.

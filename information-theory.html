Information theory

Shannon's calculus of uncertainty: entropy as the bits-per-symbol price of describing a random source, mutual information as the bits one variable reveals about another, KL divergence as the canonical rate function, and the source / channel / rate–distortion theorems as sharp limits on lossless compression, noisy transmission, and lossy reconstruction. Every concept comes with a toy to poke.

1. Shannon entropy

For a discrete random variable $X$ taking values in $\{x_1,\ldots,x_n\}$ with probabilities $p_i=\mathbb{P}(X=x_i)$, the Shannon entropy is $$H(X) = -\sum_{i=1}^n p_i\,\log p_i,$$ with the convention $0\log 0 = 0$. Choosing $\log = \log_2$ measures uncertainty in bits; $\log = \ln$ gives nats; $\log = \log_{10}$ gives dits. Bits is the universal currency on this page.

Three facts pin the meaning of $H$:

  • Non-negative: $H(X)\ge 0$, with equality iff $X$ is a point mass (no uncertainty).
  • Bounded: $H(X)\le \log n$, with equality iff $p$ is uniform on $n$ symbols.
  • Concave in $p$: averaging two laws can only increase entropy.

The number $H(X)$ is also the average length, in bits per symbol, of the shortest possible lossless code for $X$ — that's the content of §4. So entropy is simultaneously the uncertainty in $X$ and the compressibility of $X$. Same number, two readings.

Why $-p\log p$ and not something else? Shannon's axioms — continuity in $p$, monotonicity in $n$ for the uniform law, and additivity over independent sources — pin down $H$ uniquely up to the choice of log base. Any other "natural" definition of uncertainty either fails one of these or reduces to Shannon.

Worked example: binary entropy. For $X\in\{0,1\}$ with $\mathbb{P}(X=1)=p$, $$h(p) = -p\log_2 p - (1-p)\log_2(1-p).$$ This curve is symmetric around $p=1/2$, vanishes at $p=0$ and $p=1$, and peaks at $h(1/2)=1$ bit — a fair coin is exactly one bit of uncertainty. 2. Mutual information

Conditioning shrinks uncertainty. Define the conditional entropy $$H(X\mid Y) = \sum_y \mathbb{P}(Y=y)\,H(X\mid Y=y) = -\sum_{x,y}p_{XY}(x,y)\log\frac{p_{XY}(x,y)}{p_Y(y)}.$$ The mutual information is the entropy that observing $Y$ removes from $X$: $$I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X\mid Y) = H(Y) - H(Y\mid X) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y).$$ Symmetric in $X$ and $Y$, non-negative, and zero iff $X\perp Y$. Equivalently $$I(X;Y) = D\!\left(p_{XY}\,\big\|\,p_X p_Y\right),$$ the KL distance from the joint to the product of marginals. So mutual information is "how far from independent" measured in bits.

The Venn picture below is the standard mnemonic: $H(X)$ and $H(Y)$ are two circles, their union is $H(X,Y)$, the intersection is $I(X;Y)$, and the two crescents are $H(X\mid Y)$ and $H(Y\mid X)$.

Data-processing inequality. If $X\to Y\to Z$ form a Markov chain, then $I(X;Z)\le I(X;Y)$. Post-processing $Y$ into $Z$ cannot create information about $X$ that $Y$ did not already carry — the bottleneck always loses bits.

3. Kullback–Leibler divergence

Given two distributions $p,q$ on the same alphabet, the KL divergence (relative entropy) is $$D(p\,\|\,q) = \sum_i p_i\log\frac{p_i}{q_i},$$ with conventions $0\log 0 = 0$ and $p_i\log(p_i/0)=\infty$ when $p_i>0$ and $q_i=0$. It is the average extra cost, in bits, of describing data drawn from $p$ using a code optimised for $q$.

Gibbs' inequality: $D(p\,\|\,q)\ge 0$, with equality iff $p=q$. Proof via Jensen on the concave $\log$. KL is therefore a "distance"-like statistic — but not a metric: it is asymmetric, $D(p\,\|\,q)\ne D(q\,\|\,p)$ in general, and there is no triangle inequality.

KL is the rate function. Sanov's theorem (large deviations) says that the empirical distribution of $n$ i.i.d.\ samples from $\mu$ deviates to a set $A$ of distributions with probability $\asymp\exp(-n\inf_{\nu\in A}D(\nu\,\|\,\mu))$. KL is the canonical exponent: it is to empirical-measure deviations what variance is to mean-deviations in the CLT.

4. Source coding

A prefix-free code assigns each symbol $x_i$ a binary string $c_i$ such that no $c_i$ is a prefix of another. Prefix-free codes are uniquely decodable on the fly. Kraft's inequality says that lengths $\ell_1,\ldots,\ell_n$ are realisable as a prefix-free code iff $\sum_i 2^{-\ell_i}\le 1$.

Shannon's source-coding theorem: for any prefix-free code on symbols with law $p$, the expected length satisfies $$\mathbb{E}[\ell] = \sum_i p_i\,\ell_i \;\ge\; H(X),$$ and there exist codes with $\mathbb{E}[\ell] < H(X) + 1$. Block-coding $n$ symbols at a time pushes this gap to $H(X) + 1/n$, so $H(X)$ is the asymptotic minimum bits per symbol. Entropy is exactly the compressibility limit.

Huffman's algorithm achieves this: repeatedly merge the two least-probable symbols into a new internal node with combined weight, until one tree remains. Code lengths fall out of root-to-leaf paths. The widget below builds a Huffman tree for any source you specify.

AEP (asymptotic equipartition property). For an i.i.d.\ source, with overwhelming probability an $n$-block lies in a "typical set" of size $\approx 2^{nH(X)}$, each element of which has probability $\approx 2^{-nH(X)}$. So $\approx nH(X)$ bits suffice to index it — that's the operational meaning of entropy.

5. Channel coding

A discrete memoryless channel is a transition kernel $p(y\mid x)$: a source symbol $X$ goes in, a noisy output $Y$ comes out. The capacity is $$C = \max_{p_X} I(X;Y) \quad \text{(bits per channel use).}$$ The maximum is over input distributions; the kernel is fixed.

Shannon's noisy-channel theorem: for every rate $RC$ the error probability is bounded away from $0$. Capacity is therefore a sharp threshold between achievable and unachievable rates — not a soft scaling but a brick wall.

Binary symmetric channel. Flip each transmitted bit independently with probability $p$: $$p(y\mid x) = \begin{cases}1-p & y=x\\ p & y\ne x\end{cases}.$$ By symmetry the maximising input is uniform, so $H(Y) = 1$ bit and $H(Y\mid X) = h(p)$, giving $$C_{\text{BSC}}(p) = 1 - h(p).$$ At $p=0$ (noiseless) capacity is $1$ bit; at $p=1/2$ (output independent of input) capacity is $0$; and $C(p) = C(1-p)$ since flipping all bits is a relabelling.

6. Rate–distortion

If we are willing to tolerate some reconstruction error, how few bits per symbol suffice? Fix a distortion measure $d(x,\hat x)\ge 0$ — squared error for real-valued sources, Hamming distance for finite alphabets. The rate–distortion function is $$R(D) = \min_{p_{\hat X\mid X}\,:\,\mathbb{E}[d(X,\hat X)]\le D} I(X;\hat X).$$ $R(D)$ is the smallest mutual information — equivalently the smallest number of bits per symbol — that any encoder–decoder pair can use while keeping average distortion at most $D$. It is the lossy analogue of Shannon's lossless source-coding theorem.

Two general properties: $R$ is convex and non-increasing in $D$ (relaxing the constraint cannot raise the minimum), and $R(0) = H(X)$ in the lossless limit (when finite).

Gaussian source, squared-error distortion. If $X\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ and $d(x,\hat x)=(x-\hat x)^2$, then for $0 < D \le \sigma^2$, $$R(D) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log_2\frac{\sigma^2}{D},$$ and $R(D) = 0$ for $D \ge \sigma^2$ (the trivial $\hat X = 0$ already has distortion $\sigma^2$). Each halving of $D$ costs exactly half a bit per symbol — a clean exponential trade-off. The widget plots the curve.

Why Gaussian is the worst case. Among all sources with variance $\sigma^2$, the Gaussian maximises the rate–distortion function: $R_X(D) \le R_{\mathrm{Gauss}}(D)$ for any $X$ with $\mathrm{Var}(X)=\sigma^2$. Gaussian noise is hardest to compress because Gaussian is the maximum-entropy distribution at fixed variance — entropy and rate–distortion bound each other from the same direction.

7. Connections

Shannon's 1948 framework reduced "communication" to a precise mathematical object — entropy as the cost of information — and the same object reappeared in half a dozen other places.

Probability and large deviations

KL-divergence is a Sanov rate function; large-deviation theory gives the exponential cost of empirical-distribution deviations from a reference, which is the same cost source-coding measures.

Statistical mechanics

Shannon entropy and Boltzmann entropy coincide in the i.i.d. case; the maximum-entropy distribution at fixed mean energy is the Gibbs measure. Information-theoretic inequalities (Han, Shearer) become entropy inequalities in stat mech.

Statistics and inference

Cramér–Rao bound (lower bound on estimator variance) is a Fisher-information / KL-divergence argument; Stein's lemma in hypothesis testing has KL-divergence as the asymptotic exponent. Probability theory supplies the joint distributions.

Algorithms and complexity

Kolmogorov complexity is the algorithmic counterpart of Shannon entropy; channel coding gives the existence of error-correcting codes (Reed–Solomon, LDPC, polar codes) with rates approaching capacity.

Open frontiers: capacity-achieving structured codes and 5G/6G applications; non-asymptotic / dispersion results (Polyanskiy–Poor–Verdú); information-theoretic limits in machine learning (information bottleneck, generalisation bounds via mutual information).

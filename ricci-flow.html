Ricci flow

Take a Riemannian metric and let it evolve as if it were a temperature distribution: positively curved regions cool toward roundness, negatively curved regions stretch out. Hamilton's equation $\partial_t g_{ij} = -2 R_{ij}$ is a heat equation for the metric. Singularities form, surgery removes them, and what survives is the geometric decomposition of the manifold — the path Perelman walked to prove the Poincaré conjecture.

1. The Ricci flow equation

Fix a smooth manifold $M$ and a Riemannian metric $g_0$. Hamilton (1982) introduced the evolution equation $$\partial_t g_{ij} \;=\; -2\,R_{ij}(g),$$ where $R_{ij}$ is the Ricci tensor of the time-dependent metric $g(t)$. The minus sign is decisive: where Ricci is positive, $g$ shrinks; where Ricci is negative, $g$ grows. Heuristically, Ricci flow tries to homogenise curvature.

The clearest test case is an Einstein metric $\mathrm{Ric}=\lambda g$. Try the ansatz $g(t)=\rho(t)\,g_0$. Ricci is invariant under constant rescaling, so $R_{ij}(g(t))=\lambda\,(g_0)_{ij}$ and the equation reduces to $\rho' = -2\lambda$: $$g(t) \;=\; (1-2\lambda t)\,g_0.$$ Round spheres ($\lambda>0$) shrink homothetically and disappear at $t=1/(2\lambda)$. Hyperbolic metrics ($\lambda<0$) expand. Flat tori sit still. Already we see the three behaviours that organise the long-time theory.

Heat-equation analogy. In harmonic coordinates $\Delta x^k = 0$, Ricci has the form $$-2 R_{ij} \;=\; \Delta g_{ij} \;+\; Q(g,\partial g),$$ with $Q$ a quadratic in $g$ and $\partial g$. So the principal symbol of $\partial_t g - \Delta g$ vanishes — the equation is a quasilinear parabolic system whose linearisation is the rough Laplacian on symmetric 2-tensors.

2. Short-time existence and DeTurck

Does a smooth solution to $\partial_t g = -2\,\mathrm{Ric}(g)$ even exist for $t>0$? The answer is yes on every closed manifold, but the proof has a subtle wrinkle.

Theorem (Hamilton 1982). For every smooth metric $g_0$ on a closed manifold $M^n$ there exists $T>0$ and a unique smooth one-parameter family $\{g(t)\}_{t\in[0,T)}$ solving $\partial_t g=-2\,\mathrm{Ric}(g)$ with $g(0)=g_0$. The maximal $T$ is characterised by curvature blow-up: either $T=\infty$, or $\sup_M|\mathrm{Rm}|(\cdot,t)\to\infty$ as $t\to T$.

The wrinkle: Ricci flow is diffeomorphism invariant. If $\phi_t$ is a smooth family of diffeomorphisms and $g(t)$ is a Ricci flow, then $\phi_t^* g(t)$ is also a Ricci flow with possibly different initial data. Concretely, the Lie derivative direction $\mathcal{L}_X g$ for any vector field $X$ lies in the kernel of the linearised principal symbol. So the system is only weakly parabolic, and standard parabolic short-time existence does not apply directly.

Three boxes, three arrows: (1) modify the equation by adding $\mathcal{L}_W g$ for the DeTurck vector field $W^k = g^{ij}(\Gamma^k_{ij}-\tilde\Gamma^k_{ij})$; (2) the modified flow is strictly parabolic on a closed manifold, so standard PDE theory gives a unique solution; (3) pull back along $\phi_t^{-1}$ (the diffeomorphisms generated by $-W$) to recover an honest Ricci flow $g(t)$.

DeTurck (1983) found the fix: add a Lie-derivative gauge term. Pick a fixed background metric $\tilde g$; let $\tilde\Gamma$ be its Christoffel symbols. Define the DeTurck vector field $$W^k(g) \;=\; g^{ij}\bigl(\Gamma^k_{ij}(g) - \tilde\Gamma^k_{ij}\bigr)$$ and consider the Ricci–DeTurck flow $$\partial_t g \;=\; -2\,\mathrm{Ric}(g) + \mathcal{L}_{W(g)} g.$$ The added term cancels precisely the gauge-degeneracy of the principal symbol: Ricci–DeTurck flow is strictly parabolic. Standard theory gives a unique smooth solution on $[0,T)$. Then, integrating $-W$ to a smooth family of diffeomorphisms $\phi_t$, the pull-back $\phi_t^*\tilde g(t)$ solves the genuine Ricci flow.

3. Singularity formation

The continuation theorem says smooth solutions extend until curvature explodes. So the interesting question is how curvature blows up. Hamilton classified singularities by the rate.

Suppose $T<\infty$ is the maximal existence time. Set $K(t)=\sup_M|\mathrm{Rm}|(\cdot,t)$. Three regimes:

Plotting $K(t)$ for $t\in[0,T)$. Type I: $K(t)=C/(T-t)$ — the natural parabolic rate. Type II: $K(t)=C/(T-t)^2$ — supercritical. The vertical line marks $T$. Both diverge, but Type II is so much faster that parabolic rescaling near a Type II singularity produces an eternal (not just ancient) limit.

Why does this matter? Take a singular point $(x_0,T)$ and perform parabolic rescaling: choose a sequence $\lambda_k\to\infty$ and set $$g_k(s) \;=\; \lambda_k\,g\!\left(t_k+\tfrac{s}{\lambda_k}\right),\qquad t_k\to T.$$ Hamilton's compactness theorem (modelled on Cheeger–Gromov) says that under bounded curvature plus a noncollapsing hypothesis the family $g_k$ subconverges, in the pointed Cheeger–Gromov sense, to a Ricci flow $g_\infty$ defined on $(-\infty,0]$ — an ancient solution. The geometry of the singularity is encoded in the geometry of these ancient limits.

Perelman's $\kappa$-noncollapsing theorem supplies the missing ingredient: along any Ricci flow with bounded curvature on a closed manifold, the volume of every metric ball $B(x,r)$ with $|\mathrm{Rm}|\le r^{-2}$ on it satisfies $\mathrm{vol}\,B(x,r)\ge\kappa\,r^n$. Without this, the rescaled limits could collapse to a lower-dimensional object; with it, the limit is genuinely $n$-dimensional and noncollapsed.

4. Ricci solitons

The fixed points of Ricci flow modulo scaling and pull-back are solitons. They are the natural model geometries — "asymptotic shapes" that singular regions resemble after parabolic rescaling.

Definition. A Ricci soliton is a triple $(M,g_0,X)$ where $X$ is a smooth vector field and $\lambda\in\mathbb{R}$ is a constant satisfying $$\mathrm{Ric}(g_0) + \tfrac12\,\mathcal{L}_X g_0 \;=\; \lambda\,g_0.$$ It is shrinking, steady, or expanding for $\lambda>0$, $\lambda=0$, $\lambda<0$. If $X=\nabla f$ is a gradient, the soliton is gradient with potential $f$.

The point of the definition: solving the soliton equation is exactly equivalent to producing a self-similar Ricci flow. Set $\sigma(t)=1-2\lambda t$ and let $\phi_t$ be the flow of $X/\sigma(t)$. Then $$g(t) \;=\; \sigma(t)\,\phi_t^*\,g_0$$ is a Ricci flow. Einstein metrics are the special case $X=0$. So solitons strictly extend the Einstein zoo.

Examples to keep in hand.

  • Hamilton's cigar (1988): on $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $g=(1+x^2+y^2)^{-1}(dx^2+dy^2)$, a steady gradient soliton with potential $f=-\log(1+|x|^2)$. Curvature decays at infinity; the manifold has the topology of $\mathbb{R}^2$ but the asymptotic geometry of a cylinder.
  • Bryant soliton (1990s): the unique rotationally symmetric steady gradient soliton on $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n\ge 3$, modelling Type II singularities of 3-D Ricci flow.
  • Gaussian shrinker: $\mathbb{R}^n$ with $g=$ Euclidean and $f=|x|^2/4$ is a shrinking gradient soliton; it is the model for any compact shrinker after blow-down.
  • Kähler–Ricci soliton on $\mathbb{CP}^2\#\overline{\mathbb{CP}^2}$ (Koiso, Cao 1996): a non-Einstein shrinking soliton on a non-Kähler-Einstein toric surface — proof that solitons strictly generalise Einstein.

5. Surgery on $\epsilon$-necks

If a Ricci flow on a closed 3-manifold develops a singularity at $T<\infty$, the flow simply stops as a smooth object. To continue, Hamilton introduced and Perelman perfected a procedure: cut the manifold along high-curvature regions and glue in standard caps.

$\epsilon$-neck. A region $U\subset M$ at time $t$ is an $\epsilon$-neck centered at $x$ of scale $r$ if, after rescaling by $r^{-2}$, the ball $B(x,\epsilon^{-1}r)$ in $(U,r^{-2}g(t))$ is $\epsilon$-close in $C^{[\epsilon^{-1}]}$ to the standard round cylinder $S^2\times[-\epsilon^{-1},\epsilon^{-1}]$ of unit scalar curvature.

The Canonical Neighbourhood Theorem (Perelman, 2002–03) says: in a 3-D Ricci flow with surgery, every spacetime point of sufficiently large curvature has a small neighbourhood that is, up to rescaling, $\epsilon$-close either to an $\epsilon$-neck, an $\epsilon$-cap (a half-cylinder capped off with a 3-ball), or a (closed) component of nonnegative curvature. So singularities are locally classified.

The surgery operation. Pick a small enough surgery scale $h\ll r$. Each $\epsilon$-neck of scale $r$ has a central 2-sphere cross-section. Excise a $h$-neighbourhood of the cross-section and glue in two copies of the standard cap — a fixed metric on $D^3$ that interpolates between the round cylinder boundary and a smooth interior. The new manifold $M^+$ is smooth, possibly with more components than $M$. Restart Ricci flow on $M^+$ with the postsurgery metric.

Finiteness. Perelman shows that with carefully chosen surgery scales there are only finitely many surgery times on any bounded interval $[0,T_*]$, and they have no accumulation point. Each surgery removes a definite fraction of volume, so the total volume drops monotonically across surgeries.

Topologically, surgery on an $\epsilon$-neck removes an $S^2\times I$ and glues two $D^3$ caps — so the manifold either splits into two components or its genus drops. After finitely many surgeries the flow either terminates (the manifold becomes empty) or runs forever on a manifold whose pieces have well-controlled long-time geometry. Both outcomes feed directly into the next section.

6. Poincaré conjecture and geometrisation

Two threads come together. (1) Ricci flow with surgery exists for all time on any closed 3-manifold; (2) the limiting geometry of the long-time flow is rigid enough to recover the manifold's topology.

Theorem (Perelman, 2002–03). Let $M^3$ be a closed simply connected 3-manifold and run Ricci flow with surgery starting from any smooth Riemannian metric. Then the flow becomes extinct in finite time: there is a $T<\infty$ after which the manifold is empty.

The proof of extinction uses Perelman's $\mathcal{W}$-entropy $$\mathcal{W}(g,f,\tau) \;=\; \int_M \bigl[\tau(R+|\nabla f|^2) + f - n\bigr]\,(4\pi\tau)^{-n/2}e^{-f}\,dV,$$ which is monotone-increasing along the coupled system $(\partial_t g=-2\mathrm{Ric},\;\partial_t f= -\Delta f + |\nabla f|^2 - R + n/(2\tau))$. Combined with finite extinction time for 2-sphere homotopy classes (Colding–Minicozzi / Perelman), this rules out infinite-time existence on simply connected $M^3$. Tracing through the surgeries, one finds $M$ is a connected sum of finitely many copies of $S^3$ and $S^2\times S^1$. Simple connectivity forces all but a single $S^3$ to be trivial, so $M\cong S^3$. This is the Poincaré conjecture.

For non-simply-connected closed 3-manifolds the flow may run forever. Perelman shows the long-time limit decomposes the manifold along incompressible tori into pieces, each of which is either:

  • a thick piece, on which $g(t)/t$ converges to a finite-volume hyperbolic metric; or
  • a thin piece, which is a graph manifold (a Seifert-fibred piece glued to others along incompressible tori).
Geometrisation. Combining the thick/thin decomposition with the prime decomposition (across surgery) and the JSJ decomposition (along the incompressible tori), every closed orientable 3-manifold decomposes canonically into pieces, each carrying one of the eight Thurston model geometries: $$S^3,\quad \mathbb{E}^3,\quad \mathbb{H}^3,\quad S^2\times\mathbb{R},\quad \mathbb{H}^2\times\mathbb{R},\quad \widetilde{SL_2\mathbb{R}},\quad \mathrm{Nil},\quad \mathrm{Sol}.$$

This is Thurston's geometrisation conjecture: a structural theorem about all closed 3-manifolds that subsumes Poincaré as a corollary. Ricci flow with surgery realises the decomposition geometrically — flow regularises the metric, surgery cuts along the JSJ tori, and the long-time limit lands on the model geometries.

Connections

Ricci flow is the prototype geometric flow: a parabolic equation that smooths a geometric object using its own intrinsic invariants. Its success in dimension 3 raised hopes for a parallel program in dimension 4 — but here Ricci flow develops worse singularities, surgery is harder to control, and the full geometrisation picture is open. The active research frontiers below are the most direct continuations of the ideas on this page.

  • Riemannian geometry — Ricci curvature — the static object that Ricci flow evolves; sectional and Ricci comparison theorems are the prerequisite calculus.
  • Partial differential equations — heat equation — the linear parabolic prototype; Ricci flow inherits maximum principles, regularity, and entropy methods from heat-equation theory.
  • Morse theory — the negative gradient flow $\dot\gamma=-\nabla f$ is the small-dimensional analogue: a flow that simplifies a geometric object using its own invariants.
  • Smooth manifolds — flows of vector fields — the DeTurck trick is exactly the diffeomorphism-flow construction applied to a gauge field on the space of metrics.
  • Differential geometry — mean curvature flow, Yamabe flow, Kähler–Ricci flow are the close cousins; each runs the same playbook (parabolic rescaling, soliton classification, surgery) on a different geometric quantity.

Open frontiers: 4-D Ricci flow with surgery (singularity classification still open), Kähler–Ricci flow on Fano varieties (analytic minimal model program, Tian–Donaldson–Sun), Ricci flow on metric-measure spaces and synthetic curvature bounds (Sturm–Lott–Villani), and the conjectural relation to renormalisation group flows in physics.

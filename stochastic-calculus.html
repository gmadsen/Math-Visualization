Stochastic calculus

Brownian motion has infinite total variation but finite quadratic variation $[B]_t=t$ — classical Riemann–Stieltjes calculus breaks, and a new integral is required. The Itô integral chooses the left endpoint, gains a martingale, and pays in a chain rule with a curvature correction. Out of this come SDEs, Girsanov's drift change, Feynman–Kac's PDE bridge, and the toolbox behind Black–Scholes, the Kalman filter, and optimal stopping.

1. The Itô integral

Brownian motion's paths are continuous but nowhere differentiable, with quadratic variation $\sum (B_{t_{k+1}}-B_{t_k})^2\to t$ in $L^2$ as the partition $\{t_k\}$ refines. That blows up the total variation, so $\int H_s\,dB_s$ cannot be defined pathwise as a Riemann–Stieltjes integral. Itô's idea: build the integral in $L^2$ by approximation on simple processes, then choose an evaluation rule that keeps the integrand predictable.

Simple processes. A process is simple if it has the form $H_s=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\xi_k\mathbf{1}_{(t_k,t_{k+1}]}(s)$ with $\xi_k$ being $\mathcal{F}_{t_k}$-measurable and bounded. For such $H$, define $$\int_0^t H_s\,dB_s\;=\;\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\xi_k\,(B_{t_{k+1}\wedge t}-B_{t_k\wedge t}).$$ The key fact is the Itô isometry: $\mathbb{E}\big[(\int_0^t H_s\,dB_s)^2\big]=\mathbb{E}\int_0^t H_s^2\,ds$. This makes the simple-process map $H\mapsto\int H\,dB$ an isometry from $L^2(\Omega\times[0,T],ds\,d\mathbb{P})$ into $L^2(\mathbb{P})$, and density of simple processes in this $L^2$ extends the integral uniquely.

The result is a martingale. Predictability of $H$ is what kills drift: $\mathbb{E}\big[\xi_k(B_{t_{k+1}}-B_{t_k})\mid\mathcal{F}_{t_k}\big]=\xi_k\cdot\mathbb{E}[B_{t_{k+1}}-B_{t_k}\mid\mathcal{F}_{t_k}]=0$. So $\int_0^t H_s\,dB_s$ is a continuous $L^2$-martingale.

Itô vs. Stratonovich. Choosing the midpoint $\tfrac{1}{2}(H_{t_k}+H_{t_{k+1}})$ yields the Stratonovich integral $\int H\circ dB$. It obeys the classical chain rule, but is no longer a martingale; the right-endpoint convention ($H_{t_{k+1}}$) is even worse — it correlates with the increment and adds a drift. 2. Itô's formula

For a $C^2$ function $f\colon\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ and an Itô process $X_t$ with $dX_t=b_t\,dt+\sigma_t\,dB_t$, Itô's formula states $$df(X_t)=f'(X_t)\,dX_t+\tfrac{1}{2}f''(X_t)\,(dX_t)^2,\qquad (dX_t)^2=\sigma_t^2\,dt.$$ The chain rule of ordinary calculus is the first term; the second term is the Itô correction, a Taylor remainder $\tfrac{1}{2}f''(X)\,d\langle X\rangle$ that is invisible for smooth $X$ but unavoidable when the quadratic variation is non-zero.

The pedagogical case: $f(x)=x^2$, $X_t=B_t$. Naive calculus would say $d(B_t^2)=2B_t\,dB_t$ and conclude $B_t^2=2\int_0^t B_s\,dB_s$. Itô gives $$d(B_t^2)=2B_t\,dB_t+dt\quad\Longrightarrow\quad B_t^2 = 2\int_0^t B_s\,dB_s + t.$$ The discrepancy is exactly the quadratic variation: along any partition $\sum(B_{t_{k+1}}-B_{t_k})^2\to t$. The widget below plots $B_t^2$ in yellow against the naive prediction $2\int_0^t B_s\,dB_s$ in pink — the gap fills in to $t$ as time advances.

Time-dependent version. For $f(t,x)\in C^{1,2}$, $$df(t,X_t)=\partial_t f\,dt+\partial_x f\,dX_t+\tfrac{1}{2}\partial_{xx} f\,(dX_t)^2.$$ This is what makes the exponential martingale work: applying Itô to $f(t,B)=\exp(\theta B - \tfrac{1}{2}\theta^2 t)$ gives $df=\theta f\,dB$, i.e. zero drift. Time-dependent $f$ is also what links SDEs to PDEs in §5.

Mnemonic: $(dB)^2=dt$, $dt\,dB=0$, $(dt)^2=0$. All Itô calculations reduce to a Taylor expansion using these multiplication rules. The first is the only one that's surprising; it's the heart of stochastic calculus.

3. Stochastic differential equations

An SDE writes a process as the sum of a drift and a diffusion: $$dX_t=b(t,X_t)\,dt+\sigma(t,X_t)\,dB_t,\qquad X_0=x_0.$$ Solutions come in two flavours. A strong solution is a process $X$ adapted to the filtration of a given Brownian motion $B$ that satisfies the integrated form $X_t=x_0+\int_0^t b\,ds+\int_0^t\sigma\,dB_s$. A weak solution only requires producing some probability space carrying $(X,B)$ jointly satisfying the equation in law — the driving Brownian motion is constructed along with the solution.

Picard iteration and Lipschitz uniqueness. If $b,\sigma$ are globally Lipschitz with linear growth, the operator $T(X)_t=x_0+\int_0^t b(s,X_s)\,ds+\int_0^t\sigma(s,X_s)\,dB_s$ is a contraction in $L^2$ on $[0,T]$ (use Itô isometry on the diffusion piece, Cauchy–Schwarz on the drift). Iterating gives a unique strong solution; the existence proof is parallel to ODE Picard, with the Itô isometry replacing $|\int|\le\int|\,|$.

Geometric Brownian motion. The canonical financial example is $dX_t=\mu X_t\,dt+\sigma X_t\,dB_t$. Apply Itô to $\log X_t$: with $f(x)=\log x$, $f'=1/x$, $f''=-1/x^2$, $$d\log X_t=\frac{1}{X_t}\,dX_t-\frac{1}{2X_t^2}\,(dX_t)^2 = \mu\,dt+\sigma\,dB_t-\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\,dt = (\mu-\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\,dt+\sigma\,dB_t.$$ Integrating, $X_t=x_0\exp((\mu-\sigma^2/2)t+\sigma B_t)$. Two surprises live here: (1) $X_t$ stays positive a.s.; (2) $\mathbb{E}[X_t]=x_0 e^{\mu t}$ but the median grows like $x_0 e^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2)t}$ — the lognormal mean–median gap is a real phenomenon, "volatility drag".

4. Girsanov's theorem and change of measure

Drift is measure-relative. Given a real-valued adapted process $\theta_s$ satisfying Novikov's condition $\mathbb{E}\exp(\tfrac{1}{2}\int_0^T\theta_s^2\,ds)<\infty$, define $$Z_t=\exp\!\Big(\int_0^t\theta_s\,dB_s-\tfrac{1}{2}\int_0^t\theta_s^2\,ds\Big).$$ By Itô on $\log Z$, $dZ_t=Z_t\,\theta_t\,dB_t$ — a strict Itô integral with no drift, hence $Z$ is a positive martingale with $\mathbb{E}[Z_t]=1$. Use $Z_T$ to define a new probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$ on $\mathcal{F}_T$ by $d\mathbb{Q}=Z_T\,d\mathbb{P}$. Girsanov's theorem says that under $\mathbb{Q}$, $$\tilde B_t = B_t - \int_0^t\theta_s\,ds$$ is a standard Brownian motion. The drift $\int\theta\,ds$ has been absorbed into the change of measure.

Risk-neutral pricing. If the asset solves $dS_t=\mu S_t\,dt+\sigma S_t\,dB_t$ under the real-world measure $\mathbb{P}$, set $\theta=(\mu-r)/\sigma$ (the market price of risk). Under $\mathbb{Q}$, $$dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t(d\tilde B_t+\theta\,dt) = r S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,d\tilde B_t,$$ so the discounted asset $e^{-rt}S_t$ becomes a martingale. Volatility $\sigma$ is unchanged; only the drift shifts. This is why the Black–Scholes price doesn't depend on $\mu$.

5. Feynman–Kac formula

Itô gives a direct dictionary between SDEs and second-order parabolic PDEs. Suppose $u(t,x)\in C^{1,2}$ solves the backward heat-type equation $$\partial_t u + b(x)\partial_x u + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma(x)^2\partial_{xx}u - r(x)u = 0,\qquad u(T,x)=g(x),$$ and let $X_t$ be the diffusion with drift $b$ and volatility $\sigma$. Apply Itô to $e^{-\int_0^s r\,dr}u(s,X_s)$: the $dt$ pieces cancel by the PDE, leaving a martingale. Taking expectations gives the Feynman–Kac formula: $$u(t,x)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[e^{-\int_t^T r(X_s)\,ds}\,g(X_T)\;\Big|\;X_t=x\right].$$ The PDE evaluation problem reduces to averaging over Brownian paths.

The simplest case. With $b=0$, $\sigma=1$, $r=0$ this is just $\partial_t u+\tfrac{1}{2}\partial_{xx}u=0$, $u(T,x)=g(x)$ — the backward heat equation solved by Brownian convolution: $$u(t,x)=\mathbb{E}[g(B_T)\mid B_t=x]=\int_{-\infty}^\infty g(y)\,\frac{e^{-(y-x)^2/2(T-t)}}{\sqrt{2\pi(T-t)}}\,dy.$$ The Brownian heat kernel is the Green's function. 6. Applications: Black–Scholes, Kalman, optimal stopping

The three pieces — Itô's formula, SDE solutions, Girsanov, Feynman–Kac — assemble into the workhorses of mathematical finance, signal processing, and decision theory.

Black–Scholes

Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, $S_T = S_0\exp((r-\sigma^2/2)T+\sigma\tilde B_T)$. The price of a European call $C=e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}_\mathbb{Q}[(S_T-K)^+]$ admits a closed form $$C = S_0\,\Phi(d_1) - K e^{-rT}\,\Phi(d_2),\qquad d_{1,2} = \frac{\log(S_0/K) + (r\pm\sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt T}.$$ Equivalently, $C$ solves the Black–Scholes PDE $\partial_t C+\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\partial_{SS}C+rS\,\partial_S C-rC=0$ with terminal $C(T,S)=(S-K)^+$. Kalman filter

For the linear-Gaussian state-space SDE $dX_t=A X_t\,dt+\sigma\,dB_t$ with linear noisy observation $Y_t$, the conditional distribution of $X_t$ given $\mathcal{F}_t^Y$ remains Gaussian. The conditional mean $\hat X_t=\mathbb{E}[X_t\mid\mathcal{F}_t^Y]$ and covariance $P_t$ obey the closed Kalman–Bucy ODEs — recursive, online, and exact. This is the canonical example of conditional expectations as an orthogonal projection.

Optimal stopping

An American option pays $g(X_\tau)$ at any stopping time $\tau\in[0,T]$ chosen by the holder, so $$V(t,x)=\sup_{\tau\in[t,T]}\mathbb{E}_\mathbb{Q}\!\left[e^{-r(\tau-t)}g(X_\tau)\,\big|\,X_t=x\right].$$ $V$ solves a variational PDE: $\min(\,-\partial_t V-\mathcal{L}V+rV,\;V-g\,)=0$, with a free boundary separating the continuation region (where the PDE part is active) from the exercise region (where $V=g$). This pattern — Brownian-driven SDE, Bellman/HJB, free boundary — is the prototype for stochastic control.

7. Connections

Itô calculus is the bridge between probability and analysis — once $dB^2 = dt$ is internalised, three classes of theorems start to feel inevitable rather than mysterious.

Stochastic processes and martingales

The discrete and continuous-time martingale theory is the structural backbone — Doob decomposition, optional stopping, BDG inequalities — on which the Itô integral is built. The integrand class (predictable, locally square-integrable) is exactly the language we used there.

Partial differential equations

Feynman–Kac is one face of a deep symmetry: parabolic PDEs and continuous-time martingales are two ways to look at the same flow of probability mass. The heat equation and harmonic-function theory reappear as expectations, and conversely PDE methods (maximum principles, semigroups) attack stochastic problems.

Mathematical finance

Black–Scholes is a self-financing-replication argument plus Itô's formula plus an SDE for the underlying. The change of measure that turns the discounted price into a martingale (the risk-neutral measure) is exactly the Girsanov transform of $\S 4$. Beyond European options, jump-diffusion, stochastic volatility, and interest-rate term-structure models are all formulated this way.

Statistical mechanics and SPDEs

Fluctuation theory of interacting particle systems is the source of stochastic PDEs (KPZ, Allen–Cahn, the stochastic heat equation). Roughness theory (Hairer, Gubinelli) extends Itô calculus to drivers rougher than Brownian motion.

Open frontiers: rough-path theory and regularity structures (Hairer's Fields-medal work on KPZ); Malliavin calculus as a calculus of variations on Wiener space; stochastic optimal transport and entropic regularization, which connect Itô calculus to large deviations and the Schrödinger problem.

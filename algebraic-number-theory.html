Number fields, ideals, and units

Unique factorization fails in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$, so we repair it — by promoting elements to ideals. Along the way the ring of integers becomes a Dedekind domain, primes split in predictable ways, the class group measures the failure, and Dirichlet's theorem counts the units.

1. Number fields and rings of integers

A number field is a finite extension $K/\mathbb{Q}$; its dimension $n=[K:\mathbb{Q}]$ is the degree. By the primitive element theorem, $K=\mathbb{Q}(\alpha)$ for some algebraic $\alpha\in K$ whose minimal polynomial $m_\alpha\in\mathbb{Q}[x]$ has degree $n$. The Galois-theoretic reflex of $K$ is recorded by the $n$ embeddings $\sigma\colon K\hookrightarrow\mathbb{C}$; we split them into $r_1$ real embeddings and $r_2$ conjugate pairs of complex embeddings, with $n=r_1+2r_2$.

Inside $K$ sits a distinguished subring, the ring of integers

$\mathcal{O}_K = \{\alpha\in K : \alpha \text{ is a root of a monic polynomial in } \mathbb{Z}[x]\}$.

This is the integral closure of $\mathbb{Z}$ in $K$. It is a subring (sums and products of integral elements are integral — a slick consequence of the fact that $\alpha$ is integral iff $\mathbb{Z}[\alpha]$ is a finitely generated $\mathbb{Z}$-module).

Structure theorem. $\mathcal{O}_K$ is a free $\mathbb{Z}$-module of rank $n$. A $\mathbb{Z}$-basis $\omega_1,\dots,\omega_n$ is called an integral basis. The determinant of $(\sigma_i(\omega_j))$ is well-defined up to sign and its square is the discriminant $\Delta_K\in\mathbb{Z}$.

The discriminant is the single most important invariant of $K$. It measures (among other things) which rational primes ramify: $p$ ramifies in $K$ iff $p\mid \Delta_K$. And Minkowski showed $|\Delta_K|>1$ whenever $n\ge 2$ — there is no nontrivial unramified extension of $\mathbb{Q}$.

The discriminant in one paragraph

Fix an integral basis $\omega_1,\dots,\omega_n$ of $\mathcal{O}_K$ and label the $n$ embeddings $\sigma_1,\dots,\sigma_n\colon K\hookrightarrow\mathbb{C}$. The discriminant is the integer

$\Delta_K \;=\; \det\bigl(\sigma_i(\omega_j)\bigr)^2 \;=\; \det\bigl(\mathrm{Tr}_{K/\mathbb{Q}}(\omega_i\omega_j)\bigr)\;\in\;\mathbb{Z}$,

independent of the choice of basis (different bases change the matrix by $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$, whose determinant squares to $1$). Concretely: $\Delta_{\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})}=d$ when $d\equiv 1\pmod 4$ and $4d$ otherwise; $\Delta_{\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p)}=(-1)^{(p-1)/2}p^{p-2}$ for an odd prime $p$.

The discriminant is an arithmetic invariant that sees ramification: a rational prime $p$ ramifies in $K$ iff $p\mid\Delta_K$. And it is an analytic invariant — it governs the Minkowski bound $M_K\propto\sqrt{|\Delta_K|}$ and the leading term of $\zeta_K(s)$ at $s=1$. The Hermite–Minkowski theorem $|\Delta_K|\ge\bigl(\tfrac{n^n}{n!}\bigr)^2\bigl(\tfrac{\pi}{4}\bigr)^{n}$ in particular forces $|\Delta_K|\to\infty$ as $n\to\infty$ and rules out any unramified extension of $\mathbb{Q}$.

Two features distinguish number fields from $\mathbb{Q}$ itself:

  • Unique factorization of elements can fail. The simplest example lives in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$: $\;6=2\cdot 3=(1+\sqrt{-5})(1-\sqrt{-5})$ with all four factors irreducible and non-associate.
  • The unit group can be infinite. In $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}]$ the element $\varepsilon=1+\sqrt{2}$ is a unit of infinite order: $\varepsilon(\sqrt{2}-1)=1$, and all powers $\varepsilon^k$ are distinct units.

The rest of this page is the story of how to tame both phenomena. We will replace elements by ideals (sections 3–5) and classify the units (section 7). The geometry of the embedding $K\hookrightarrow\mathbb{R}^{r_1}\times\mathbb{C}^{r_2}$ glues the two stories together (section 6).

2. Quadratic rings of integers

Let $d\ne 0,1$ be a squarefree integer and set $K=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$; then $[K:\mathbb{Q}]=2$. The ring of integers depends on $d\bmod 4$:

$\mathcal{O}_K = \begin{cases}\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{d}] & d\equiv 2,3\pmod 4,\\ \mathbb{Z}\!\left[\tfrac{1+\sqrt{d}}{2}\right] & d\equiv 1\pmod 4.\end{cases}$

The extra element $\tfrac{1+\sqrt{d}}{2}$ only becomes integral when $d\equiv 1\pmod 4$; its minimal polynomial is $x^2-x+\tfrac{1-d}{4}$, which lives in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ precisely in that case. The discriminant is

$\Delta_K = \begin{cases} 4d & d\equiv 2,3\pmod 4,\\ d & d\equiv 1\pmod 4.\end{cases}$

Two classical examples deserve names:

  • $d=-1$: $\mathcal{O}_K=\mathbb{Z}[i]$, the Gaussian integers. Norm $N(a+bi)=a^2+b^2$. A Euclidean domain, hence a PID, hence a UFD.
  • $d=-3$: $\mathcal{O}_K=\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ with $\omega=\tfrac{-1+\sqrt{-3}}{2}$ a primitive sixth root of unity — the Eisenstein integers. Also Euclidean with norm $a^2-ab+b^2$.

Geometrically, for $d<0$ the embedding $\sqrt{d}\mapsto i\sqrt{|d|}$ realizes $\mathcal{O}_K\subset\mathbb{C}$ as a rank-2 lattice. Its fundamental domain is a parallelogram of area $\sqrt{|\Delta_K|}/2$. For $d>0$ the two real embeddings give a lattice in $\mathbb{R}^2$ via $(\sigma_1,\sigma_2)$; the natural picture is harder to eyeball because the lattice is log-periodic under multiplication by units (section 7).

3. Dedekind domains

The failure of UFD in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$ is not fatal — it is exactly the sort of failure that ideal theory was invented to repair. Rings of integers are the archetypal examples of the following:

Definition. An integral domain $R$ is a Dedekind domain if it is
  1. Noetherian,
  2. integrally closed in its fraction field, and
  3. of Krull dimension $\le 1$ (every nonzero prime ideal is maximal).

This trio of axioms looks innocuous. Its punchline is the unique factorization of ideals:

Theorem (Dedekind). In a Dedekind domain $R$, every nonzero proper ideal $I$ factors uniquely (up to order) as a product of nonzero prime ideals:

$I=\mathfrak{p}_1^{a_1}\mathfrak{p}_2^{a_2}\cdots \mathfrak{p}_k^{a_k}$.

Four equivalent characterizations are worth remembering — any is a valid definition:

flavorcondition
axiomaticNoetherian + integrally closed + Krull dim $\le 1$
ideal-theoreticevery nonzero ideal factors uniquely into primes
invertible idealsevery nonzero ideal is invertible in the monoid of fractional ideals
local$R$ is Noetherian and every localization $R_\mathfrak{p}$ at a nonzero prime is a DVR

The "local" characterization is the one you use in practice: to check something about ideals, reduce to a DVR and do the calculation there.

Let us see why $\mathcal{O}_K$ meets all three axioms. Noetherian is immediate because $\mathcal{O}_K$ is a finitely generated $\mathbb{Z}$-module (rank $n$), so every submodule — every ideal, in particular — is finitely generated. Integrally closed is by construction: $\mathcal{O}_K$ is the integral closure of $\mathbb{Z}$ in $K$, and integral closure is idempotent. Dim $=1$ follows because $\mathbb{Z}\hookrightarrow \mathcal{O}_K$ is an integral extension and $\dim\mathbb{Z}=1$, or more directly: a nonzero prime $\mathfrak{p}\subset\mathcal{O}_K$ contains some prime $p\in\mathbb{Z}$, and $\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{p}$ is a finite integral domain over $\mathbb{F}_p$, hence a field.

Takeaway. Dedekind domains recover unique factorization at the level of ideals. In a PID, every ideal is principal, so that recovers unique factorization of elements as a corollary. The gap between "every ideal" and "every principal ideal" is measured by the class group (section 5).

For $\mathcal{O}_K$, the primes $\mathfrak{p}\subset\mathcal{O}_K$ above a rational prime $p$ sit in the finite set described by the factorization of $p\mathcal{O}_K$. Section 4 makes this explicit.

Fractional ideals

A fractional ideal of $\mathcal{O}_K$ is a finitely generated $\mathcal{O}_K$-submodule $I\subset K$. Equivalently, $I=\tfrac{1}{c} J$ for some nonzero $c\in\mathcal{O}_K$ and some (integral) ideal $J$. Under multiplication the nonzero fractional ideals form a group $\mathcal{I}_K$; the principal ones $(\alpha)=\alpha\mathcal{O}_K$ (for $\alpha\in K^\times$) form a subgroup $\mathcal{P}_K$. The quotient

$\mathrm{Cl}(\mathcal{O}_K) := \mathcal{I}_K / \mathcal{P}_K$

is the ideal class group, the single most important invariant of $K$ after the discriminant. Its triviality $\mathrm{Cl}(\mathcal{O}_K)=1$ is the condition that $\mathcal{O}_K$ be a PID — equivalently, a UFD (in a Dedekind domain those two conditions coincide).

4. Ramification of primes

Take $\mathcal{O}_K$ of degree $n=[K:\mathbb{Q}]$ and a rational prime $p$. The ideal $p\mathcal{O}_K$ factors uniquely:

$p\mathcal{O}_K \;=\; \mathfrak{p}_1^{e_1}\mathfrak{p}_2^{e_2}\cdots\mathfrak{p}_g^{e_g}$,

where the $\mathfrak{p}_i$ are the distinct primes of $\mathcal{O}_K$ lying above $p$. Each $\mathfrak{p}_i$ sits in a tower $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\hookrightarrow \mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{p}_i$, and the residue extension has a degree $f_i=[\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{p}_i:\mathbb{F}_p]$ called the residue degree. The $e_i$ is the ramification index.

Fundamental equality. In any number field,

$\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^g e_i f_i = n$.

This is a counting statement: $p\mathcal{O}_K$ is an ideal of index $p^n$ (since $\mathcal{O}_K/p\mathcal{O}_K$ is a vector space of dimension $n$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$), and factoring gives $\prod(\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{p}_i)^{e_i}$ as $\mathbb{F}_p$-modules of total dimension $\sum e_i f_i$.

Classification.
  • Splits completely if $g=n$, so all $e_i=f_i=1$.
  • Inert if $g=1$, $e=1$, $f=n$. (The prime $p$ stays prime in $\mathcal{O}_K$.)
  • Totally ramified if $g=1$, $e=n$, $f=1$.
  • Ramified means any $e_i\ge 2$. This happens iff $p\mid \Delta_K$.

For quadratic $K=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$ (with $d$ squarefree) and an odd prime $p\nmid d$, the trichotomy is controlled by the Legendre symbol $(d/p)$:

conditionbehaviorfactorization in $\mathcal{O}_K$
$(d/p)=+1$splits$p\mathcal{O}_K=\mathfrak{p}_1\mathfrak{p}_2$, $e=f=1$
$(d/p)=-1$inert$p\mathcal{O}_K=\mathfrak{p}$, $e=1, f=2$
$p\mid d$ramifies$p\mathcal{O}_K=\mathfrak{p}^2$, $e=2, f=1$

The prime $p=2$ is exceptional; its behavior depends on $d\bmod 8$. (For $d\equiv 1\pmod 8$ it splits; for $d\equiv 5\pmod 8$ it is inert; for $d\equiv 2,3\pmod 4$ it ramifies.)

The concrete generators are supplied by Kummer–Dedekind: if $\mathcal{O}_K=\mathbb{Z}[\alpha]$ with minimal polynomial $m_\alpha(x)\in\mathbb{Z}[x]$, then for any prime $p$ not dividing the index $[\mathcal{O}_K:\mathbb{Z}[\alpha]]$, the factorization $\bar m_\alpha(x)=\prod \bar g_i(x)^{e_i}\in\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ mirrors the factorization of $p\mathcal{O}_K$: $\mathfrak{p}_i=(p,g_i(\alpha))$. For $K=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$ one has $m_\alpha(x)=x^2-d$ or $x^2-x+(1-d)/4$, and factoring those in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ delivers the trichotomy above.

5. The ideal class group

Recall: $\mathcal{I}_K$ is the group of nonzero fractional ideals; $\mathcal{P}_K$ is the subgroup of principal ones; the class group is the quotient $\mathrm{Cl}(\mathcal{O}_K)=\mathcal{I}_K/\mathcal{P}_K$. Two ideals are equivalent in $\mathrm{Cl}(\mathcal{O}_K)$ iff $I=(\alpha)J$ for some $\alpha\in K^\times$.

Theorem (Minkowski). The class group $\mathrm{Cl}(\mathcal{O}_K)$ is finite. Its order $h_K$ is the class number.

The proof uses the geometry of numbers (section 6). The idea: in every ideal class one can find a representative integral ideal $I$ with $N(I)\le M_K$, where $M_K$ is the Minkowski bound

$M_K \;=\; \left(\tfrac{4}{\pi}\right)^{r_2}\!\tfrac{n!}{n^n}\sqrt{|\Delta_K|}$.

Since there are finitely many ideals of bounded norm, $\mathrm{Cl}$ is finite. For the practical algorithm — enumerate primes of norm $\le M_K$, record their classes, impose the relations coming from factorizations of small elements.

The $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$ tragedy (and its resolution)

Consider $K=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$, so $\mathcal{O}_K=\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$. Here $-5\equiv 3\pmod 4$. The element $6$ factors in two ways:

$6 = 2\cdot 3 = (1+\sqrt{-5})(1-\sqrt{-5})$.

All four factors are irreducible. (Check norms: $N(2)=4$, $N(3)=9$, $N(1\pm\sqrt{-5})=6$; none factors further over $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$.) This is the cleanest example of the failure of UFD.

But consider the ideals. Let $\mathfrak{p}=(2,1+\sqrt{-5})$. One computes

$\mathfrak{p}^2 = (4, 2+2\sqrt{-5}, -4+2\sqrt{-5}) = (2)$,

so $(2)=\mathfrak{p}^2$. Similarly set $\mathfrak{q}_1=(3,1+\sqrt{-5})$, $\mathfrak{q}_2=(3,1-\sqrt{-5})$; one checks $\mathfrak{q}_1\mathfrak{q}_2=(3)$. Finally $(1+\sqrt{-5})=\mathfrak{p}\mathfrak{q}_1$ and $(1-\sqrt{-5})=\mathfrak{p}\mathfrak{q}_2$. So

$(6) \;=\; (2)(3) \;=\; \mathfrak{p}^2\mathfrak{q}_1\mathfrak{q}_2 \;=\; (\mathfrak{p}\mathfrak{q}_1)(\mathfrak{p}\mathfrak{q}_2) \;=\; (1+\sqrt{-5})(1-\sqrt{-5})$.

Unique factorization of ideals is restored; the two element factorizations are simply two different ways of grouping the prime ideals into principal products.

Class number of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$. $h=2$. Generator: the class $[\mathfrak{p}]$. It is nonprincipal (there is no $\alpha\in\mathcal{O}_K$ with $N(\alpha)=2$: no solution to $a^2+5b^2=2$), and $[\mathfrak{p}]^2=[(2)]=1$.

The class group is precisely the obstruction to unique factorization in $\mathcal{O}_K$. When $h_K=1$ we get a PID (hence a UFD); when $h_K>1$ the "rearrangements" exhibited above can occur, and they are parameterized by $\mathrm{Cl}$.

For imaginary quadratic fields the list of $d<0$ (squarefree) with class number one is remarkably short: $d\in\{-1,-2,-3,-7,-11,-19,-43,-67,-163\}$ — the Heegner numbers. For real quadratic fields it is conjectured (and supported by extensive computation) that infinitely many have class number one, but this is unproven.

6. Minkowski's theorem

Here is the geometric engine that powers both finiteness of the class group and Dirichlet's unit theorem.

Minkowski's convex body theorem. Let $\Lambda\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ be a full lattice of covolume $V_\Lambda=|\det\Lambda|$, and let $S\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ be a centrally symmetric ($S=-S$), convex, measurable body. If

$\operatorname{vol}(S) > 2^n\, V_\Lambda$,

then $S$ contains a nonzero lattice point.

The proof is a beautiful pigeonhole: translate $\tfrac12 S$ by elements of $\Lambda$; if these translates were all disjoint mod $\Lambda$ they would fit inside a fundamental domain of volume $V_\Lambda$, contradicting $\operatorname{vol}(\tfrac12 S)=\operatorname{vol}(S)/2^n>V_\Lambda$.

The application to number theory: view a number field $K$ of signature $(r_1,r_2)$ inside

$K\otimes_\mathbb{Q}\mathbb{R} \;\cong\; \mathbb{R}^{r_1}\times\mathbb{C}^{r_2} \;\cong\; \mathbb{R}^n$

via the Minkowski embedding $\alpha\mapsto(\sigma_1\alpha,\dots,\sigma_{r_1}\alpha;\sigma_{r_1+1}\alpha,\dots,\sigma_{r_1+r_2}\alpha)$. Under this map, $\mathcal{O}_K$ becomes a lattice of covolume $2^{-r_2}\sqrt{|\Delta_K|}$. Any fractional ideal $I$ becomes a sub-lattice of covolume $N(I)\cdot 2^{-r_2}\sqrt{|\Delta_K|}$.

Applying Minkowski's theorem to a cleverly chosen body (the "Arakelov simplex" $\{|x_i|\le t_i\}$ with $\prod t_i$ maximized) one extracts a nonzero $\alpha\in I$ whose norm is bounded: $|N(\alpha)|\le M_K\cdot N(I)$. Since $(\alpha)=I\cdot J$ for some integral ideal $J$ with $N(J)=|N(\alpha)|/N(I)\le M_K$, every ideal class contains an integral representative of norm $\le M_K$. Finitely many such — done.

The same geometric machine, applied to the logarithm map on units, proves Dirichlet's theorem below. Minkowski: the lattice-body theorem that handles both the multiplicative and additive sides of number theory.

7. Dirichlet's unit theorem

The unit group $\mathcal{O}_K^\times$ is always nonempty — it contains $\pm 1$ — but it can be infinite. Dirichlet pinned down its structure:

Dirichlet's unit theorem. Let $K$ be a number field of signature $(r_1,r_2)$, so $[K:\mathbb{Q}]=r_1+2r_2$. Then

$\mathcal{O}_K^\times \;\cong\; \mu_K \,\times\, \mathbb{Z}^{r_1+r_2-1}$,

where $\mu_K$ is the finite group of roots of unity in $K$. The free rank $r_1+r_2-1$ is the unit rank.

The proof goes via the logarithm map

$L: \mathcal{O}_K^\times \to \mathbb{R}^{r_1+r_2},\quad \alpha\mapsto\bigl(\log|\sigma_1\alpha|,\dots,\log|\sigma_{r_1}\alpha|,\;\log|\tau_1\alpha|^2,\dots,\log|\tau_{r_2}\alpha|^2\bigr)$.

The kernel of $L$ is $\mu_K$ (units mapped to the compact torus, and a discrete subgroup of a torus is finite). The image lies in the hyperplane $\{\sum x_i=0\}$ because $|N(\alpha)|=1$ for units, giving $\sum\log|\sigma_i\alpha|+\sum\log|\tau_j\alpha|^2=0$. Dirichlet shows the image is a full lattice in this $(r_1+r_2-1)$-dimensional subspace — again via Minkowski's theorem in an exponentially warped body.

Real quadratic: Pell's equation

For $K=\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$ with $d>0$ squarefree and $d\equiv 2,3\pmod 4$, the fundamental unit $\varepsilon=x+y\sqrt{d}$ is the smallest unit $>1$. It satisfies $x^2-dy^2=\pm 1$ — Pell's equation. The continued-fraction expansion of $\sqrt{d}$ is eventually periodic; one reads $\varepsilon$ off its period.

Imaginary quadratic: just roots of unity

For $d<0$ squarefree the signature is $(0,1)$ so the unit rank is $0$: the unit group is finite, equal to $\mu_K$. Apart from $\pm 1$ we only pick up extra roots of unity for the two exceptional fields:

  • $d=-1$: $\mathcal{O}_K=\mathbb{Z}[i]$; units $\{\pm 1, \pm i\}$, a cyclic group of order $4$.
  • $d=-3$: $\mathcal{O}_K=\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ with $\omega=e^{2\pi i/6}$; units $\{\pm 1, \pm\omega, \pm\omega^2\}$, cyclic of order $6$.
  • Every other $d<0$: units are $\{\pm 1\}$ only.

8. Connections

Algebraic number theory is a hinge between several parts of the notebook.

Galois theory

If $K/\mathbb{Q}$ is Galois (as $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$ always is, and $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$ always is), then $\mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})$ acts on the set of primes $\mathfrak{p}$ above any given rational prime $p$, and the action is transitive. So all $\mathfrak{p}_i$ above $p$ share the same ramification index $e$ and residue degree $f$: the fundamental equality simplifies to $efg=n$. To each unramified $\mathfrak{p}$ there is attached a Frobenius element $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}\in\mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})$, characterized by $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}(x)\equiv x^p\pmod{\mathfrak{p}}$. For abelian $K$ the map $p\mapsto\mathrm{Frob}_p$ is class field theory in miniature. See Galois and constructibility.

Schemes

The scheme $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathcal{O}_K$ is a Dedekind scheme of Krull dimension $1$: an "arithmetic curve". Its closed points are the nonzero primes $\mathfrak{p}$ of $\mathcal{O}_K$, each with residue field $\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{p}$ of characteristic some rational prime $p$. The map $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathcal{O}_K\to\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$ is a finite flat cover; above each prime $p\in\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$ the fibre is $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K/p\mathcal{O}_K)$, whose structure is precisely the factorization data $(e_i,f_i)$. Ramified primes are the points where the cover is not étale. See Schemes.

Elliptic curves over number fields

The Mordell–Weil theorem says $E(K)$ is a finitely generated abelian group for an elliptic curve $E/K$: a higher-dimensional avatar of Dirichlet's unit theorem. Constructively, you lift heights using the Néron–Tate pairing; abstractly, you use the finiteness of $\mathrm{Sel}(E/K)$ which relies on finiteness of the class group (and of $\mathcal{O}_K^\times$ mod $n$-th powers). See Elliptic curves.

Beyond

Next stops: $L$-functions $L(s,\chi)$ attached to Dirichlet characters and their number-field analogues $\zeta_K(s)$; the analytic class-number formula that ties $h_K\cdot R_K$ (class number times regulator) to the residue of $\zeta_K$ at $s=1$; local fields $K_\mathfrak{p}$ (completions at a prime); adeles and ideles; class field theory.

One sentence. Algebraic number theory is what happens when you try to do arithmetic in a number field, find that elements don't factor uniquely, replace them with ideals, measure the difference with the class group, and discover that all of this is controlled by the geometry of lattices in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

Recommended further reading: Neukirch, Algebraic Number Theory; Marcus, Number Fields; Milne's lecture notes on Algebraic Number Theory and Class Field Theory.

Moduli spaces: the j-line, fine vs. coarse, and $M_{g,n}$

A moduli space packages "all objects of type $X$ up to isomorphism" into a single geometric object. We start where it works cleanest — the $j$-line classifying elliptic curves over $\mathbb{C}$ — watch the classical setup fail to be fine, see how level structures rescue it, and end at the moduli of curves $M_{g,n}$ and its Deligne–Mumford compactification.

Companion pages: Elliptic curves for the $j$-invariant itself, Schemes for representability, and a forward link to Stacks for the groupoid-valued fix.

1. What is a moduli problem?

Informally, a moduli problem asks: is there a space whose points are the objects of type $X$, up to isomorphism? The word "space" is deliberately vague — we will sharpen it. The running example here is

$$\text{type } X \;=\; \text{elliptic curves over } \mathbb{C}, \qquad \text{isomorphism} \;=\; \text{isomorphism of algebraic curves with marked origin.}$$

The set of such curves, up to iso, is huge — but it admits a remarkably tidy parameter. Over $\mathbb{C}$, the $j$-invariant gives a bijection

$$j\colon \{\text{elliptic curves}/\mathbb{C}\}/\!\cong \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; \mathbb{A}^1(\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}.$$

So the set of iso classes is in bijection with the set of complex numbers. The moduli problem asks more: can we do this geometrically, so that the parameter space "feels" like a variety, and so that a family of curves over a base $B$ corresponds to a morphism $B \to \mathbb{A}^1$?

Point-of-view shift. Classical geometry starts with a space and asks about its points. Moduli theory starts with a class of objects and tries to build a space whose points are those objects. Whether such a space exists, and in what category, is the subtle part.

Formally, we attach to the moduli problem a functor $$\mathcal{M}\colon \mathrm{Sch}^{\mathrm{op}} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Set}, \qquad \mathcal{M}(S) = \{\text{families of objects of type } X \text{ over } S\} / \cong_S.$$ A family over $S$ is a flat, proper morphism $E \to S$ whose geometric fibres are objects of type $X$. The moduli problem is representable if there is a scheme $M$ with $\mathcal{M}(-) \cong h_M(-) = \operatorname{Hom}(-,M)$. This is the gold standard; we call $M$ a fine moduli space.

2. The j-line

For elliptic curves over $\mathbb{C}$, every iso class has a Weierstrass model $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$ with $\Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27b^2) \ne 0$. The j-invariant $$j(E) \;=\; 1728 \cdot \frac{4a^3}{4a^3 + 27b^2}$$ is a complete invariant over an algebraically closed field: two curves are $\mathbb{C}$-isomorphic iff they have the same $j$.

The two special values:

  • $j = 0$ corresponds to curves $y^2 = x^3 + b$ (for instance $y^2 = x^3 + 1$). The extra automorphism $(x,y) \mapsto (\omega x,\,-y)$ with $\omega = e^{2\pi i / 3}$ has order $6$: $\operatorname{Aut}(E) \cong \mathbb{Z}/6$.
  • $j = 1728$ corresponds to $y^2 = x^3 + ax$ (for instance $y^2 = x^3 + x$). The extra automorphism $(x,y) \mapsto (-x,\,i y)$ has order $4$: $\operatorname{Aut}(E) \cong \mathbb{Z}/4$.
  • At every other $j$, the only non-trivial automorphism is $[-1]\colon (x,y) \mapsto (x,-y)$, so $\operatorname{Aut}(E) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$.

3. Fine versus coarse moduli

Take the moduli functor $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}(S) = \{\text{elliptic curves } E \to S\}/\cong_S$. There are two levels of "solution."

Fine moduli

$M$ is a fine moduli space for $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ if $\mathcal{M}_{1,1} \cong h_M$ as functors. Taking $S = M$ and the identity in $\operatorname{Hom}(M,M) \cong \mathcal{M}_{1,1}(M)$ produces a universal family $\mathcal{E} \to M$ such that every family $E \to S$ is the pullback of $\mathcal{E}$ along a unique morphism $S \to M$.

Coarse moduli

$M$ is a coarse moduli space if we have a natural transformation $\varphi\colon \mathcal{M}_{1,1} \to h_M$ that is (a) a bijection on $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}(\operatorname{Spec} k) \to M(k)$ for every algebraically closed field $k$, and (b) universal among such natural transformations to a scheme. Coarse says: points match, but there is no universal family.

The $j$-line is the coarse moduli space of $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ — not fine. The bijection on geometric points is exactly the $j$-invariant. Below: an interactive diagram of the two situations.

4. Why fine moduli fails for $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$

The obstruction to representability is automorphisms. Here is the mechanism in a sentence: if an object has a non-trivial automorphism, one can twist a trivial family into a non-trivial family whose fibres are still isomorphic to the original, and a representable functor cannot see this.

Concretely, consider the two families over the affine line $\mathbb{A}^1 = \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Q}[t]$:

$$E_1: \; y^2 = x^3 + 1, \qquad E_2: \; y^2 = x^3 + t^6.$$

Over every $\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$-point $t = t_0 \ne 0$, both fibres have $j = 0$ and are isomorphic (via $x \mapsto t_0^2 x$, $y \mapsto t_0^3 y$). So they are pointwise the same family. But they are not isomorphic as families over $\mathbb{A}^1 \setminus \{0\}$: the isomorphism $x \mapsto t^2 x$, $y \mapsto t^3 y$ is not defined globally, and the obstruction to gluing lives in

$$H^1\!\left(\mathbb{A}^1 \setminus \{0\},\,\underline{\operatorname{Aut}}(E_1)\right).$$

If $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ were representable, the classifying maps of $E_1$ and $E_2$ would both be the constant map to $j = 0$, and so the families would be equal — but they are not. Contradiction.

W4 · twist widget
compare $E_1$ and $E_2$ over varying $t$

Left: $E_1 = \{y^2 = x^3+1\}$ (fibre at $t$). Right: $E_2 = \{y^2 = x^3+t^6\}$ (fibre at $t$). Both have $j = 0$; both are iso as abstract curves at every $t \ne 0$. But the iso requires the sixth-root twist $x \mapsto t^2 x$, $y \mapsto t^3 y$, which is a non-trivial class in $H^1$.

The same phenomenon happens at $j = 1728$ (quartic twist) and more mildly at every $j$ (quadratic twist from $[-1]$). The mass of automorphisms at $j = 0$ and $j = 1728$ makes $\mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ an orbifold / Deligne–Mumford stack with extra structure at those two points.

5. Rigidifying with level structure

A level-$N$ structure on an elliptic curve $E$ is an isomorphism $\alpha\colon (\mathbb{Z}/N)^2 \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} E[N]$, i.e. an ordered basis of the $N$-torsion. Equivalently: a pair of points $P, Q \in E[N]$ generating $E[N]$.

The key fact: for $N \ge 3$, no non-trivial automorphism of an elliptic curve fixes a level-$N$ structure. The functor

$$\mathcal{M}_{1,1}[N](S) \;=\; \{(E \to S,\,\alpha) : \text{level-}N \text{ structure}\}/\cong$$

is representable by a smooth affine variety $Y(N)$. The natural forgetful map $Y(N) \to \mathcal{M}_{1,1}$ is finite étale (over the locus away from $j = 0, 1728$), with Galois group $\operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/N)$.

This is the standard rigidification trick: add data that kills automorphisms, get fine moduli, then quotient to recover the original.

W5 · 2-torsion visualizer
label the four 2-torsion points
Why $N \ge 3$? For $N = 2$, every elliptic curve has automorphism $[-1]$, and $[-1]$ acts as the identity on $E[2]$ (because $-P = P$ for $2$-torsion). So a level-$2$ structure is not rigid enough — $[-1]$ still fixes every level-$2$ basis. For $N = 3$, $[-1]$ acts as $-1 \ne 1$ on $E[3]$, so it is detected.

6. Higher genus: $M_g$ and $M_{g,n}$

Let $M_g$ be the (coarse) moduli space of smooth projective curves of genus $g$, and $M_{g,n}$ the moduli of such curves with $n$ ordered distinct marked points. These exist as quasi-projective varieties (for $g \ge 2$; $M_{0,n}$ and $M_{1,n}$ require care).

Their dimensions:

$$\dim M_g \;=\; \begin{cases} 0 & g = 0,\\ 1 & g = 1,\\ 3g - 3 & g \ge 2, \end{cases} \qquad \dim M_{g,n} \;=\; \dim M_g + n.$$

The $3g-3$ comes from Riemann–Roch / deformation theory: $\dim H^1(C, T_C) = 3g - 3$ is the dimension of first-order deformations of a smooth curve of genus $g \ge 2$.

Compactification: stable curves

The open moduli $M_{g,n}$ is not compact — curves can degenerate. Deligne and Mumford (1969) enlarge to the moduli of stable curves, giving a smooth proper Deligne–Mumford stack $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g,n}$ with coarse space $\overline{M}_{g,n}$.

A stable curve $(C, p_1, \dots, p_n)$ is a connected, proper, reduced, at-worst-nodal curve of arithmetic genus $g$ such that:

  • every smooth rational component contains at least $3$ special points (nodes + markings);
  • every genus-$1$ component contains at least $1$ special point.

Equivalently: $\operatorname{Aut}(C, p_1, \dots, p_n)$ is finite. Stability is exactly what is needed to cut out a Deligne–Mumford stack.

7. Mental model summary

A moduli problem is, at heart, a functor $$\mathcal{F}\colon \mathrm{Sch}^{\mathrm{op}} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Set} \;\text{ or }\; \mathrm{Grpd}.$$ The question "does $\mathcal{F}$ come from a scheme?" has three graded answers.

levelconditionwhat you get
fine$\mathcal{F} \cong h_M$ as set-valued functorsscheme $M$ + universal family
coarse$\mathcal{F} \to h_M$ bijective on $\bar k$-points, universal into schemesscheme $M$, no universal family
stack$\mathcal{F}$ valued in groupoids, satisfies descentDeligne–Mumford or Artin stack

Automorphisms are the villain and the hero. They obstruct fine moduli (by making twists), but they are also the data that upgrades a coarse variety into a richer stack: the stack $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g,n}$ remembers, at each curve $C$, the finite group $\operatorname{Aut}(C)$. The coarse space $\overline{M}_{g,n}$ is obtained by forgetting these groups.

The forward link: stacks is exactly the framework that lets us replace "functor to Set, mod out by iso, lose information" with "functor to Groupoids, remember the isos as data." The $j$-line then becomes the coarse space of the stack $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{1,1}$, with stacky points of order $6$ at $j = 0$ and order $4$ at $j = 1728$.

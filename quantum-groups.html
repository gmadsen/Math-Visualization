Quantum groups

Deform the universal enveloping algebra $U(\mathfrak g)$ by a single parameter $q$ and the cocommutativity of group multiplication breaks: tensor products of representations no longer commute, but they almost do, witnessed by an $R$-matrix that satisfies the Yang–Baxter equation. The shadow of that single failure of symmetry runs through knot polynomials, integrable lattice models, and categorification.

1. Hopf algebras

A Hopf algebra $(H,m,\eta,\Delta,\varepsilon,S)$ over a field $k$ has five structure maps:

MapTypeRole
$m\colon H\otimes H\to H$multiplicationassociative
$\eta\colon k\to H$unit$\eta(1)=1_H$
$\Delta\colon H\to H\otimes H$comultiplicationcoassociative
$\varepsilon\colon H\to k$counit$(\varepsilon\otimes\mathrm{id})\Delta=(\mathrm{id}\otimes\varepsilon)\Delta=\mathrm{id}$
$S\colon H\to H$antipodeconvolution-inverse of $\mathrm{id}$

Bialgebra axioms force $\Delta$ and $\varepsilon$ to be algebra homomorphisms. The antipode is the unique map satisfying $m\circ(S\otimes\mathrm{id})\circ\Delta = m\circ(\mathrm{id}\otimes S)\circ\Delta = \eta\circ\varepsilon$ — by abstract nonsense this forces $S$ to be an algebra anti-homomorphism.

Two prototypes

For a finite group $G$, the group algebra $\mathbb C[G]$ is a Hopf algebra with

$$\Delta(g)=g\otimes g,\qquad \varepsilon(g)=1,\qquad S(g)=g^{-1}\quad (g\in G).$$

It is cocommutative ($\tau\circ\Delta=\Delta$, where $\tau$ is the flip). For a Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$, the universal enveloping algebra $U(\mathfrak g)$ is also Hopf with

$$\Delta(x)=x\otimes 1+1\otimes x,\qquad \varepsilon(x)=0,\qquad S(x)=-x\quad (x\in\mathfrak g),$$

extended as algebra maps. Both are cocommutative — quantization will break this.

A commutative algebra of functions on a group $G$ ($\mathbb C[G]$ if $G$ is finite) is dual to the cocommutative $\mathbb C[G]$: the Hopf structure is a single algebraic object that simultaneously sees the algebra of functions and the group operation. Quantum groups are the deformations where neither side is commutative anymore.

2. Drinfeld–Jimbo $U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)$

Fix a nonzero $q\in\mathbb C$ with $q\ne\pm 1$. The quantized enveloping algebra $U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)$ is generated by $E$, $F$, $K$, $K^{-1}$ subject to

$$KK^{-1}=K^{-1}K=1,\qquad KEK^{-1}=q^2 E,\qquad KFK^{-1}=q^{-2} F,$$

$$[E,F] \;=\; \dfrac{K-K^{-1}}{q-q^{-1}}.$$

The Hopf structure is determined by

$$\Delta(K)=K\otimes K,\qquad \Delta(E)=E\otimes 1+K\otimes E,\qquad \Delta(F)=F\otimes K^{-1}+1\otimes F,$$

$$S(K)=K^{-1},\quad S(E)=-K^{-1}E,\quad S(F)=-FK,\quad \varepsilon(K)=1,\;\varepsilon(E)=\varepsilon(F)=0.$$

Why this is a deformation

Set $K=q^H$ formally and expand in $\hbar=\log q$:

$$KEK^{-1}=q^2 E \;\Rightarrow\; (1+\hbar H+\cdots)E(1-\hbar H+\cdots) = (1+2\hbar+\cdots)E,$$

which to first order is $[H,E]=2E$. Similarly $[E,F] = (K-K^{-1})/(q-q^{-1}) \to H$ in the limit. So $U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)\rightsquigarrow U(\mathfrak{sl}_2)$ as $q\to 1$ — a smooth deformation of the classical relations.

3. The universal $R$-matrix and Yang–Baxter

$U_q(\mathfrak g)$ is quasitriangular: there is an invertible element $R\in U_q(\mathfrak g)\widehat\otimes U_q(\mathfrak g)$ — the universal $R$-matrix — that intertwines the comultiplication with its opposite,

$$\Delta^{op}(x) \;=\; R\,\Delta(x)\,R^{-1}\qquad\text{for all }x\in U_q(\mathfrak g),$$

and satisfies the Yang–Baxter equation

$$R_{12}\,R_{13}\,R_{23} \;=\; R_{23}\,R_{13}\,R_{12} \quad\text{in } U_q(\mathfrak g)^{\otimes 3}.$$

Here $R_{ij}$ is $R$ inserted in the $i$-th and $j$-th tensor slots and identity elsewhere. For $U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)$ on $V_1\otimes V_1$ (with $V_1$ the 2-dimensional standard rep) the matrix in the basis $\{v\otimes v, v\otimes w, w\otimes v, w\otimes w\}$ reads

$$R \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} q & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & q-q^{-1} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & q \end{pmatrix}.$$

Geometric meaning: the third Reidemeister move

Read each $R_{ij}$ as a positive crossing of strands $i$ and $j$. The Yang–Baxter relation is then exactly Reidemeister III, the move that allows sliding a strand across a crossing. So $R$ furnishes a representation of the braid group $B_n$ on $V^{\otimes n}$.

Yang–Baxter as Reidemeister III
flip the side: both sides equal $R_{12}R_{13}R_{23} = R_{23}R_{13}R_{12}$
When $q=1$ the formula $R = q\,P_+ + q^{-1}P_- + (q-q^{-1})\,(\text{off-diag})$ collapses to the identity flip — symmetric monoidal, no braiding. The $R$-matrix is exactly the obstruction to symmetric, and that obstruction is what makes knot invariants nontrivial.

4. Representations and crystal bases

Over generic $q$ (i.e. $q$ not a root of unity), finite-dimensional type-1 $U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)$-modules look classical: the irreducibles $V_n$ for $n\in\mathbb Z_{\ge 0}$ have dimension $n+1$ with weight basis $\{v_n, v_{n-2}, \dots, v_{-n}\}$, where

$$Kv_k = q^k v_k,\qquad Ev_k = [\,(n-k)/2 + 1\,]_q\, v_{k+2},\qquad Fv_k = [\,(n+k)/2\,]_q\, v_{k-2},$$

and $[m]_q = (q^m - q^{-m})/(q-q^{-1})$ is the $q$-integer. Tensor products decompose by a quantum Clebsch–Gordan rule

$$V_m \otimes V_n \;\cong\; \bigoplus_{k=0}^{\min(m,n)} V_{\,m+n-2k}.$$

Crystals — the $q\to 0$ shadow

Kashiwara's crystal basis $B(V_n)$ for $V_n$ is the set $\{v_n, v_{n-2}, \dots, v_{-n}\}$ regarded as the vertices of a coloured graph with one $F$-arrow between consecutive weight vectors. Tensor products of crystals are computed by an explicit combinatorial rule on pairs of arrow strings: irreducible components match irreducible factors of the quantum tensor product.

Lusztig's canonical basis

Lusztig constructed a distinguished $\mathbb Z[q,q^{-1}]$-basis $\mathbf B$ of the integral form of $U_q^-(\mathfrak g)$ that is

  • bar-invariant: $\overline q = q^{-1}$ fixes each basis element,
  • has positive integer structure constants for multiplication and the bar involution,
  • compatible with all highest-weight modules: each $V_\lambda$ has a canonical basis subset of $\mathbf B$.

The positivity of these structure constants is a deep theorem; it was an early hint that there should be a categorification — vector-space dimensions whose Euler characteristic is the basis element. KLR algebras supply exactly such a categorification.

5. Reshetikhin–Turaev knot invariants

A framed oriented tangle is a 1-manifold with boundary in $\mathbb R^2\times[0,1]$ whose endpoints lie on $\mathbb R\times\{0,1\}$. Reshetikhin and Turaev built a tensor functor

$$F\colon \mathbf{Tang}_V \;\to\; \mathrm{Rep}\bigl(U_q(\mathfrak g)\bigr)$$

from the category of $V$-labelled tangles to $U_q(\mathfrak g)$-modules. The functor is forced by three pieces of data:

  • Crossing $\mapsto$ braiding $c_{V,V} = \tau\circ R_{V,V}$,
  • Cup / cap $\mapsto$ evaluation / coevaluation on $V\otimes V^*$ (rigid duality),
  • Framing twist $\mapsto$ the ribbon element $\theta$.

For an oriented knot $K$ closed up to a 1-tangle from $\mathbb C\to\mathbb C$, the functor returns a scalar — the quantum knot invariant:

$$J_K(q) \;=\; F(K) \;\in\; \mathbb Z[q^{\pm 1/2}].$$

6. Affine quantum groups, KZ, and categorification

The Drinfeld–Jimbo construction extends to the affine Lie algebras $\widehat{\mathfrak g}$, giving $U_q(\widehat{\mathfrak g})$. Its representations sit on top of an evaluation map $V(z) = V\otimes\mathbb C[z,z^{-1}]$ and the spectral $R$-matrix $R(z/w)$ depends on the ratio of evaluation parameters.

Integrable lattice models

For the six-vertex model, the Boltzmann weights of a vertex configuration are exactly the entries of the spectral $R$-matrix of $U_q(\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}_2})$ on $V_1\otimes V_1$. Yang–Baxter $R_{12}(u)R_{13}(u+v)R_{23}(v) = R_{23}(v)R_{13}(u+v)R_{12}(u)$ implies that transfer matrices at different spectral parameters commute, which is the algebraic source of integrability.

Differential geometry on configuration space matches the algebra of quantum groups.

Categorification

Khovanov–Lauda–Rouquier (KLR) constructed a graded 2-category $\mathcal U$ whose Grothendieck group is $U_q(\mathfrak g)_{\mathbb Z[q,q^{-1}]}$. Each $q^k$ becomes a grading shift; multiplication becomes composition of 1-morphisms; canonical-basis elements lift to indecomposable 1-morphisms. Khovanov homology categorifies the Jones polynomial: $V_K(q)$ becomes the Euler characteristic of a bigraded chain complex $\mathrm{Kh}^{\bullet,\bullet}(K)$, and the latter is a strictly stronger invariant (it detects the unknot).

Connections

Quantum groups sit at the intersection of three live frontiers: the quantum geometric Langlands program (Gaitsgory–Lurie) replaces the classical Langlands sheaf-on-$\mathrm{Bun}_G$ picture with categories of $U_q(\mathfrak g)$-modules and predicts a duality interchanging $q$ and the Langlands dual; 2-Kac–Moody algebras (Rouquier) categorify the entire Drinfeld–Jimbo machinery and link knot homology to derived categories of Lie-algebra representations; and quantum cluster algebras propose that canonical bases come from cluster monomials, but this is open in general type.

  • Lie algebras — root systems and Cartan classification — quantize root data, get a quantum group.
  • Representation theory — tensor and dual reps — the braided monoidal category $\mathrm{Rep}(U_q(\mathfrak g))$ is the deformation of $\mathrm{Rep}(G)$.
  • Category theory — monoidal categories — the natural home for braidings, ribbon structures, and quantum traces.
  • Algebraic topology — knot polynomials and their categorifications connect 3-manifold topology back to algebra.
  • Monstrous moonshine — vertex operator algebras and their quantum-group cousins govern modular characters; affine $U_q(\widehat{\mathfrak g})$ is closely related.

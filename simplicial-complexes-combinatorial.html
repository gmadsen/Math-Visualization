Simplicial complexes — combinatorial side

A simplicial complex is, at heart, a hereditary family of finite sets: pick a vertex set, declare which subsets are "filled in", and require that any subset of a filled subset is itself filled. From this almost-trivial axiom flow $f$- and $h$-vectors that count faces, Dehn–Sommerville symmetries on simplicial spheres, shellings that propagate Cohen–Macaulayness, the Stanley–Reisner monomial-ideal bridge to commutative algebra, nerves that recover the topology of an open cover, and persistent homology — the noise-tolerant homology that reads barcodes off a point cloud. This page walks the combinatorial toys; the geometric/topological cousins live on algebraic topology.

1. Abstract simplicial complexes

An abstract simplicial complex on a finite vertex set $V$ is a collection $K \subseteq 2^V$ such that

  • $\sigma \in K$ and $\tau \subseteq \sigma$ imply $\tau \in K$ (closed under taking subsets), and
  • $\{v\} \in K$ for every $v \in V$ (every vertex is a $0$-face).

An element $\sigma \in K$ of cardinality $k+1$ is a $k$-face (or $k$-simplex): vertices are $0$-faces, edges are $1$-faces, triangles are $2$-faces. The dimension $\dim K = \max_{\sigma \in K}(|\sigma|-1)$. The maximal faces (faces not contained in any larger face) are the facets; $K$ is determined by listing them, since every other face is a subset.

The hereditary axiom — sometimes called the downward-closed or simplicial axiom — is the only thing distinguishing $K$ from an arbitrary set system. It is exactly what lets a $k$-simplex carry a well-defined boundary: the union of its $(k-1)$-faces also lies in $K$.

Two flavors of "simplicial complex". The combinatorial object above is a finite set system with a downward-closure axiom. Its geometric realization $|K| \subset \mathbb{R}^N$ — glue an abstract $k$-simplex for each $k$-face — is a topological space that the algebraic topologists work with. The two are equivalent for the purposes of singular and simplicial homology, but combinatorialists prefer the discrete data; topologists, the space.

Boolean intuition: $K$ vs. its face lattice

Order $K$ by inclusion. The result is a graded poset $(K, \subseteq)$ — the face lattice — sitting inside the Boolean lattice $2^V$. It is closed downward and graded by dimension. Any down-closed subset of a Boolean lattice is the face lattice of a unique simplicial complex on $V$.

2. The $f$-vector and the $h$-vector

For a $(d-1)$-dimensional simplicial complex $K$, the $f$-vector $$f(K) = (f_{-1}, f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_{d-1}), \qquad f_k = \#\{k\text{-faces of }K\}.$$ We always set $f_{-1} = 1$, counting the empty face. The Euler characteristic is the alternating sum $$\chi(K) = \sum_{k=0}^{d-1} (-1)^k f_k = f_0 - f_1 + f_2 - \cdots,$$ and equals the topological Euler characteristic of the geometric realization $|K|$.

The $h$-vector

Define $h(K) = (h_0, h_1, \ldots, h_d)$ by the polynomial identity $$\sum_{k=0}^{d} h_k\, t^{d-k} \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{d} f_{k-1}\, (t-1)^{d-k}.$$ Equivalently, expand and read off coefficients: $$h_k \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{k} (-1)^{k-i}\binom{d-i}{k-i}\, f_{i-1}.$$ The $h$-vector and $f$-vector encode the same data, but the $h$-side often has signs and integers that the $f$-side does not. The total $\sum h_k = f_{d-1}$ (the number of facets when $K$ is pure).

Dehn–Sommerville (simplicial polytopes / spheres). If $K$ is the boundary complex of a simplicial $d$-polytope (or, more generally, a simplicial $(d-1)$-sphere), then $$h_k = h_{d-k}, \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, d.$$ This is a strong constraint on $f$-vectors: out of the $d$ free numbers $f_0, \ldots, f_{d-1}$, only roughly $d/2$ are independent. Stanley's $g$-theorem (1980, with McMullen's $g$-conjecture from 1971 as the upper-bound side) classifies which $h$-vectors actually arise.

3. Geometric realization and the nerve

The geometric realization of an abstract complex $K$ is the topological space $|K|$ obtained by gluing one standard $k$-simplex $\Delta^k \subset \mathbb{R}^{k+1}$ for each $k$-face along the obvious face inclusions. Combinatorial homology of $K$ — chains generated by faces, boundary $\partial\sigma = \sum (-1)^i \sigma \setminus \{v_i\}$ — agrees with the singular homology of $|K|$. So the combinatorial side computes $H_n(|K|;\mathbb{Z})$.

The other key way simplicial complexes appear in topology is via the nerve construction. Given a finite cover $\mathcal{U} = \{U_1, \ldots, U_n\}$ of a space $X$, the nerve $\mathcal{N}(\mathcal{U})$ is the abstract complex with vertex set $\{1,\ldots,n\}$ and a face $\sigma \subseteq \{1,\ldots,n\}$ exactly when $\bigcap_{i\in\sigma} U_i \neq \emptyset$. This is downward-closed by construction (any subintersection of a non-empty intersection is non-empty).

Nerve theorem (Borsuk / Leray). If $\mathcal{U}$ is an open cover by sets each of whose finite intersections is empty or contractible — a so-called good cover — then the nerve $\mathcal{N}(\mathcal{U})$ has the homotopy type of $X$. So combinatorics on the cover recovers topology of the space, and that is what makes simplicial complexes a viable computational substitute for topological spaces.

Toy: nerve of a ball cover of points in the plane

Place a few points $x_1, \ldots, x_n \in \mathbb{R}^2$ and let $U_i$ be the disk $B(x_i, r)$ of radius $r$. The nerve $\mathcal{N}(r)$ has $\sigma$ as a face iff the disks indexed by $\sigma$ have a common point. As $r$ grows, more faces appear; at $r=0$ the nerve is just the vertices, and for $r \gg \max d(x_i, x_j)$ it is the full simplex $\Delta^{n-1}$.

4. Shellings and Cohen–Macaulay complexes

Call a pure $(d-1)$-complex $K$ shellable if its facets can be ordered $F_1, F_2, \ldots, F_m$ so that for every $i \ge 2$, the intersection $$F_i \cap \bigl(F_1 \cup F_2 \cup \cdots \cup F_{i-1}\bigr) \;=\; \text{a pure } (d-2)\text{-subcomplex of } \partial F_i.$$ Geometrically: each new facet glues onto the previous union along a contractible-piece-of-its-boundary that is itself a union of codimension-$1$ faces. Boundaries of simplicial polytopes are shellable (Bruggesser–Mani 1971); shellings power inductive proofs about $h$-vectors.

Why shellability matters

A shelling decomposes the simplicial chain complex into manageable summands and leads directly to:

  • Counting: if $K$ is shellable then $h_k$ counts facets $F_i$ whose restriction $\mathcal{R}(F_i)$ has cardinality $k$ — so $h_k \ge 0$.
  • Homotopy type: a shellable pure $(d-1)$-complex is homotopy-equivalent to a wedge of $(d-1)$-spheres, with the number of spheres equal to $h_d$.
  • Algebra: shellable $\Rightarrow$ Cohen–Macaulay (over any field).

A complex $K$ is Cohen–Macaulay (CM) over $k$ if its Stanley–Reisner ring (next section) is Cohen–Macaulay as a graded $k$-algebra. Reisner's criterion (1976) gives a purely topological characterisation:

Reisner's criterion. $K$ is Cohen–Macaulay over $k$ iff for every face $\sigma \in K$ (including $\sigma = \emptyset$), the reduced homology of the link $$\operatorname{lk}_K(\sigma) \;=\; \{\tau \in K : \sigma \cap \tau = \emptyset,\ \sigma \cup \tau \in K\}$$ satisfies $\widetilde{H}_i(\operatorname{lk}_K(\sigma); k) = 0$ for $i < \dim\operatorname{lk}_K(\sigma)$. Spheres and balls are CM; complexes with "wrong-dimensional holes in some link" are not.

5. The Stanley–Reisner ring

To a simplicial complex $K$ on vertex set $V = \{1,\ldots,n\}$ associate the polynomial ring $S = k[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ and the Stanley–Reisner ideal $$I_K \;=\; \bigl(\,x_{i_1} x_{i_2} \cdots x_{i_r} \;:\; \{i_1,\ldots,i_r\} \notin K\,\bigr) \;\subset\; S.$$ That is, $I_K$ is generated by the squarefree monomials whose support is a non-face of $K$. The Stanley–Reisner ring is the quotient $$k[K] \;=\; S / I_K.$$ A monomial $x^{a}$ survives in $k[K]$ iff its support $\{i : a_i > 0\}$ is a face of $K$. So the squarefree monomials in $k[K]$ are in bijection with the faces of $K$, and graded vector-space-dimensions of $k[K]$ refine the $f$-vector.

The bridge. Every squarefree monomial ideal is the Stanley–Reisner ideal of a unique simplicial complex, and vice versa. So combinatorics of finite set systems $\leftrightarrow$ commutative algebra of squarefree monomial ideals: every theorem proved on one side translates. Cohen–Macaulay, Gorenstein, and Buchsbaum properties of $k[K]$ become topological conditions on $K$ (Reisner, Stanley).

6. Persistent homology

Suppose $X = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ is a finite point cloud — a noisy sample from some shape. We want a topological invariant that is robust to noise. The trick: don't pick a single complex, pick a family.

The Vietoris–Rips complex at scale $r \ge 0$ is $$\mathrm{VR}_r(X) \;=\; \bigl\{\sigma \subseteq X : \mathrm{diam}(\sigma) \le 2r\bigr\},$$ i.e. a face is included whenever its vertices are pairwise within $2r$. As $r$ grows, faces are only added: $r \le r' \Rightarrow \mathrm{VR}_r(X) \subseteq \mathrm{VR}_{r'}(X)$. This nested sequence is a filtration.

Apply $H_k(-;k)$ to the filtration. You get a sequence of vector spaces with linear maps, $$H_k(\mathrm{VR}_{r_0}) \to H_k(\mathrm{VR}_{r_1}) \to \cdots \to H_k(\mathrm{VR}_{r_m}),$$ called a persistence module. By a structure theorem for finitely-generated graded modules over $k[t]$ (analog of Smith normal form), it decomposes uniquely up to isomorphism as $$\bigoplus_i k[t](-b_i)\Big/\bigl(t^{d_i - b_i}\bigr),$$ each summand a "bar" born at scale $b_i$ and dying at $d_i$. The barcode is the multiset of bars; long bars are stable features, short bars are noise.

Stability. The barcode is Lipschitz in the input under bottleneck distance: a small perturbation of $X$ moves bars by at most that perturbation (Cohen-Steiner–Edelsbrunner–Harer). This is what justifies persistence as a statistic: it is robust to sampling noise in a precise quantitative sense.

7. Connections

Combinatorial simplicial complexes are the discrete shadow of algebraic-topology objects. Below: the standard places they surface.

Algebraic topology

Algebraic topology develops the topological side: simplicial homology agrees with singular homology for combinatorial complexes, and the Mayer–Vietoris and excision tools are the standard computational devices.

Simplicial sets and higher categories

A simplicial complex embeds into the category of simplicial sets; the shift from "complex" to "set" replaces "subset of vertices" by "labelled simplex" — exactly what is needed to model nerves of categories and quasi-categories.

Matroids and shellability

Matroid theory contributes shellable complexes (the order complex of a geometric lattice is shellable); these provide the clean Cohen–Macaulay structure on Stanley–Reisner rings.

Spectral and graph-theoretic invariants

Spectral graph theory in higher dimensions becomes "high-dimensional expansion": the spectrum of the up-down random walk on a simplicial complex measures rapid mixing of high-dimensional structures (Kaufman–Mass, Alev–Lau).

Open frontiers: high-dimensional expanders (HDX) and their connections to local-to-global theorems; tropical and matroidal complexes as carriers of Hodge-theoretic invariants (Adiprasito–Huh–Katz); discrete Morse theory and persistent homology in topological data analysis.

Quadratic reciprocity — the first deep symmetry of the primes

Gauss called it the theorema aureum. A statement about which numbers are squares modulo a prime turns out to hide a perfect symmetry between pairs of primes — and that symmetry is the prototype of every reciprocity law that follows, from Artin to Langlands.

1. The question

Fix an odd prime $p$ and an integer $a$ not divisible by $p$. Ask: does the congruence $$ x^2 \equiv a \pmod p $$ have a solution? If yes, call $a$ a quadratic residue (QR) mod $p$; otherwise a non-residue (NR).

For a fixed $p$, exactly half of the nonzero residues are QRs, because the squaring map $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times \to (\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ is two-to-one onto its image — the kernel $\{\pm 1\}$ has order $2$. That half of the story is easy. The hard half is the reverse:

Fix $a$ (say $a=5$). For which primes $p$ is $a$ a QR mod $p$? Equivalently: as $p$ runs through the primes, which ones does $5$ "belong to"?

Squaring is a local operation, so there is no reason to expect the answer to be describable in terms of $p$ alone. And yet — the answer depends only on $p \bmod 4a$. This is the miracle we are here to unpack.

2. The multiplicative group $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$

Before we package squareness as a sign, take stock of the arithmetic stage. For prime $p$, the nonzero residues mod $p$ form a group under multiplication: $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times = \{1, 2, \ldots, p-1\}$. It has order $p-1$, every element is a unit (because $\gcd(a,p)=1$ for $1\le acyclic. There exists a primitive root $g\in(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ whose powers $g, g^2, g^3, \ldots, g^{p-1}\equiv 1$ run through every nonzero residue exactly once.

Cyclicity is what makes the Legendre symbol multiplicative, what makes Euler's criterion work, and what underlies every reciprocity law to follow. Visualise it on a circular dial. With $p=7$ and multiplier $a=3$ (a primitive root), the map $k \mapsto 3k \bmod 7$ is a single full-cycle permutation of the seven nonzero positions. Drag $a$ to any other unit (any $a$ with $\gcd(a,7)=1$) to see another permutation; switch $n$ to a composite like $n=6$ and pick a non-unit such as $a=2$, and watch the orbit collapse onto a proper subset — exactly the failure mode that primality rules out.

3. The Legendre symbol

Package the answer as a sign. For odd prime $p$ and $a\in\mathbb{Z}$ with $\gcd(a,p)=1$, define the Legendre symbol $$ \left(\frac{a}{p}\right) = \begin{cases} +1 & a\text{ is a QR mod }p,\\ -1 & a\text{ is a NR mod }p. \end{cases} $$ Extend by $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)=0$ when $p\mid a$.

Multiplicativity

The map $a\mapsto \left(\tfrac{a}{p}\right)$ is a homomorphism $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times \to \{\pm 1\}$. Concretely $$ \left(\frac{ab}{p}\right) = \left(\frac{a}{p}\right)\left(\frac{b}{p}\right). $$ Proof: $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ is cyclic, say with generator $g$. Squares are even powers of $g$. Write $a=g^i$, $b=g^j$; then $ab=g^{i+j}$ is a square iff $i+j$ is even, iff $i$ and $j$ have the same parity — matching the sign product.

Euler's criterion

Euler's criterion. For odd prime $p$ and $\gcd(a,p)=1$, $$ a^{(p-1)/2} \equiv \left(\frac{a}{p}\right) \pmod p. $$

Proof. By Fermat $a^{p-1}\equiv 1$, so $a^{(p-1)/2}\equiv \pm 1$. If $a=x^2$ then $a^{(p-1)/2}=x^{p-1}\equiv 1$. For the converse, the polynomial $X^{(p-1)/2}-1$ has at most $(p-1)/2$ roots in the field $\mathbb{F}_p$, and the $(p-1)/2$ QRs are already roots — so the NRs cannot be, and hit $-1$ instead.

This gives a polynomial-time test: compute $a^{(p-1)/2} \bmod p$ by repeated squaring. The widget below walks through the exponentiation.

4. The supplementary laws

Multiplicativity reduces any Legendre symbol to a product of symbols $\left(\tfrac{-1}{p}\right)$, $\left(\tfrac{2}{p}\right)$, and $\left(\tfrac{q}{p}\right)$ for odd primes $q$. Reciprocity will handle the last kind. The first two are the supplementary laws:

First supplement. $\displaystyle \left(\frac{-1}{p}\right) = (-1)^{(p-1)/2} = \begin{cases} +1 & p\equiv 1\pmod 4,\\ -1 & p\equiv 3\pmod 4. \end{cases}$

Proof: by Euler, $\left(\tfrac{-1}{p}\right) \equiv (-1)^{(p-1)/2}$; both sides are $\pm 1$, so they are equal as integers.

Second supplement. $\displaystyle \left(\frac{2}{p}\right) = (-1)^{(p^2-1)/8} = \begin{cases} +1 & p\equiv \pm 1\pmod 8,\\ -1 & p\equiv \pm 3\pmod 8. \end{cases}$

This one is proved by Gauss's lemma (next section) applied to $a=2$: count how many of $\{2,4,6,\ldots,p-1\}$ reduce to the "upper half" $\{(p+1)/2,\ldots,p-1\}$ mod $p$, and read off the sign. The count turns out to be $\lfloor p/4 \rfloor$, giving the displayed parity.

Together with multiplicativity, these let us handle $-1$ and $2$ with a single glance at $p \bmod 8$.

5. Gauss's lemma

This is the combinatorial lever that lifts Euler's criterion to a form where reciprocity becomes visible. Fix odd prime $p$ and $a$ with $\gcd(a,p)=1$. Consider the set $$ S = \{a, 2a, 3a, \ldots, \tfrac{p-1}{2}a\} \pmod p. $$ Reduce each element to its representative in $\{1,2,\ldots,p-1\}$, and let $\mu$ be the number of them that land in the upper half $\{(p+1)/2, \ldots, p-1\}$.

Gauss's lemma. $\displaystyle \left(\frac{a}{p}\right) = (-1)^\mu.$

Sketch: reflect each upper-half residue $r$ to its negative $p-r$. The resulting set is exactly $\{1,2,\ldots,(p-1)/2\}$ — so $$ \prod_{j=1}^{(p-1)/2} ja \equiv (-1)^\mu \prod_{j=1}^{(p-1)/2} j \pmod p, $$ which after cancelling factorials gives $a^{(p-1)/2} \equiv (-1)^\mu \pmod p$, and Euler finishes the job.

6. The reciprocity law

Let $p, q$ be distinct odd primes. The symbols $\left(\tfrac{p}{q}\right)$ and $\left(\tfrac{q}{p}\right)$ are, individually, properties of how $p$ sits inside $\mathbb{F}_q$ and how $q$ sits inside $\mathbb{F}_p$ — two seemingly independent facts. Gauss's theorem says they are tied:

Quadratic reciprocity (Gauss 1796). $$ \left(\frac{p}{q}\right)\left(\frac{q}{p}\right) = (-1)^{\tfrac{p-1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{q-1}{2}}. $$ Equivalently: the two symbols agree unless both $p$ and $q$ are $\equiv 3 \pmod 4$, in which case they flip signs.

This is the "golden theorem." The only way the product can be $-1$ is if $p\equiv q\equiv 3\pmod 4$. Otherwise the two symbols track each other.

Sketch via Eisenstein's lemma

One way to see reciprocity is to refine Gauss's lemma. Count the lattice points strictly inside the triangle with vertices $(0,0), (p/2, 0), (p/2, q/2)$ and under the line $y = (q/p)x$. Call this count $T(p,q)$. Eisenstein's lemma says $$ \left(\frac{q}{p}\right) = (-1)^{T(p,q)}. $$ Swapping $p$ and $q$ swaps the two legs of the rectangle and replaces $T(p,q)$ by $T(q,p)$, and $$ T(p,q) + T(q,p) = \frac{p-1}{2}\cdot\frac{q-1}{2} $$ is the lattice-point count in the whole rectangle $[1,(p-1)/2]\times[1,(q-1)/2]$, split by the diagonal (which misses all lattice points when $p\ne q$). Multiplying the two symbols yields the parity of the sum, which is the reciprocity exponent.

7. The computation engine

With multiplicativity, the two supplements, and reciprocity, every Legendre symbol reduces to $\pm 1$ by elementary operations. Two tricks make it fast:

  • Reduce the top mod the bottom — $\left(\tfrac{a}{p}\right) = \left(\tfrac{a\bmod p}{p}\right)$;
  • Peel off factors of 2 using the second supplement, and odd prime on top using reciprocity (with a sign flip iff $p\equiv q\equiv 3\pmod 4$).

This is exactly how the Jacobi-symbol algorithm works — and it runs in $O(\log p)$ arithmetic operations, no factoring required. Try it.

8. The Jacobi symbol

The Legendre symbol $\left(\tfrac{a}{p}\right)$ needs $p$ prime. But the computation engine above was perfectly happy to push the "bottom" $p$ through non-prime intermediate values once reciprocity flipped the arguments. That is no accident: the natural home for reciprocity is a multiplicative extension of the Legendre symbol to all positive odd integers.

For odd $n = p_1^{e_1}\cdots p_k^{e_k} > 1$ and any integer $a$, define the Jacobi symbol by factoring the bottom: $$ \left(\frac{a}{n}\right) = \prod_{i=1}^{k}\left(\frac{a}{p_i}\right)^{\!e_i}. $$ When $n$ is prime this is just the Legendre symbol, so no ambiguity arises. The symbol is multiplicative in both entries, vanishes iff $\gcd(a,n)>1$, and otherwise equals $\pm 1$.

Caveat. $\left(\tfrac{a}{n}\right)=+1$ does not imply $a$ is a square mod $n$. E.g. $\left(\tfrac{2}{15}\right)=\left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)\left(\tfrac{2}{5}\right)=(-1)(-1)=+1$, but $2$ is not a square mod $15$. Jacobi only tracks the parity of the number of prime factors where $a$ is a nonsquare. What survives is the reciprocity identity, not the squareness interpretation.

The miracle is that reciprocity and both supplements extend verbatim. For odd positive coprime $m,n$: $$ \left(\frac{m}{n}\right)\left(\frac{n}{m}\right) = (-1)^{\tfrac{m-1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{n-1}{2}}, \qquad \left(\frac{-1}{n}\right) = (-1)^{\tfrac{n-1}{2}}, \qquad \left(\frac{2}{n}\right) = (-1)^{\tfrac{n^2-1}{8}}. $$ Each follows from the Legendre version by multiplicativity together with the identity $\tfrac{mn-1}{2}\equiv \tfrac{m-1}{2}+\tfrac{n-1}{2}\pmod 2$ for odd $m,n$ (and its mod-8 cousin for the $(2/n)$ law). So the computation engine on section 7 was running the Jacobi algorithm — it never needed the intermediate bottoms to be prime. Dropping the primality test gives a Euclidean-style algorithm that runs in $O(\log n)$ steps without ever factoring $n$.

This matters for cryptography: Solovay–Strassen primality testing compares $\left(\tfrac{a}{n}\right)$ with $a^{(n-1)/2}\pmod n$, and the Jacobi symbol is the fast-to-compute side. More philosophically, the Jacobi symbol is the first glimpse of characters of ray class groups: the map $n\mapsto \left(\tfrac{a}{n}\right)$ is a Dirichlet character modulo $4|a|$, which is exactly the class-field-theory picture the next page develops.

9. Why it matters

Quadratic reciprocity is the first and simplest in a tower of reciprocity laws, each saying that some subtle arithmetic invariant attached to a prime can be read off a congruence. The grand generalisation, Artin reciprocity, is the main theorem of class field theory; the Langlands program posits still deeper nonabelian analogues.

The bridge: Frobenius

Why should $(a/p)$ depend only on $p \bmod 4a$? Because $(a/p) = +1$ iff $p$ splits in the quadratic number field $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt a)$. Splitting is governed by the Frobenius element $\mathrm{Frob}_p \in \mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q}) \cong \{\pm 1\}$. The ramification of $K/\mathbb{Q}$ is supported on primes dividing $4a$, so $K$ is contained in the cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_{4a})$; Frobenius in a cyclotomic field is literally raising $\zeta\mapsto \zeta^p$, which depends only on $p \bmod 4a$. Quadratic reciprocity is the compatibility of these two pictures.

The keyword is abelian extension. The Frobenius element in an abelian Galois group depends only on a congruence condition on $p$ — that is the one-line summary of class field theory, and the reason reciprocity exists.

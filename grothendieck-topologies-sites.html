1. Sieves

If you have already met presheaves on a topological space as functors $\mathrm{Open}(X)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}$, the move to a Grothendieck site replaces the poset $\mathrm{Open}(X)$ by an arbitrary small category $C$ and replaces "open cover of an open set $U$" by something more flexible. The new building block is a sieve. A sieve $S$ on an object $c \in C$ is a set of arrows with codomain $c$ that is closed under precomposition: whenever $f\colon d \to c$ lies in $S$ and $g\colon e \to d$ is any arrow, the composite $f \circ g$ also lies in $S$. The closure condition is the categorical analogue of "if $V \subseteq U$ is in your cover and $W \subseteq V$, then $W$ is too" — except now $V \to U$ need not be a subset inclusion.

The slick reformulation, which makes everything click, is this: a sieve on $c$ is the same data as a subfunctor $S \hookrightarrow h_c$ of the representable presheaf $h_c = \operatorname{Hom}_C(-, c)$. Indeed, $h_c(d) = \{\text{arrows } d \to c\}$, and a subfunctor picks out, for each $d$, a subset $S(d) \subseteq h_c(d)$ stable under reindexing along $g\colon e \to d$ — which is exactly the precomposition-closure condition. So sieves on $c$ form a complete lattice $\mathrm{Sub}(h_c)$ inside the presheaf topos $\widehat{C} = [C^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathsf{Set}]$, with $\bigcup$ and $\bigcap$ computed pointwise.

Two extreme cases anchor the picture. The maximal sieve $\mathrm{max}_c$ contains every arrow into $c$, i.e. $\mathrm{max}_c = h_c$ itself. The empty sieve $\varnothing_c$ contains no arrows. A general sieve sits strictly between these, and any family $\{f_i\colon d_i \to c\}_{i \in I}$ generates a sieve — namely the smallest sieve containing each $f_i$, obtained by closing up under precomposition. This is the technical fact that lets us say "covering family" and "covering sieve" interchangeably: a Grothendieck topology can be specified either by listing covering families (a basis) or by listing the sieves they generate.

When $C$ is a poset (every hom-set has $\leq 1$ element), sieves degenerate beautifully: a sieve on $c$ is exactly a down-set $S \subseteq \{\,d : d \leq c\,\}$ of objects below $c$. The lattice $\mathrm{Sub}(h_c)$ becomes the lattice of down-sets, and the maximal sieve is the principal down-set $\downarrow\!c$. 2. Grothendieck topology axioms

A Grothendieck topology $J$ on a small category $C$ assigns to every object $c \in C$ a collection $J(c)$ of sieves on $c$, called the covering sieves. The pair $(C, J)$ is called a site. The data $J$ must satisfy three axioms. (M) Maximality: the maximal sieve $\mathrm{max}_c = h_c$ is in $J(c)$ — every $c$ is covered by "all arrows into $c$." (S) Stability under pullback: if $S \in J(c)$ and $f\colon d \to c$ is any arrow, then the pullback sieve $f^*S = \{\,g\colon e \to d : f \circ g \in S\,\}$ lies in $J(d)$. (T) Transitivity / local character: if $S \in J(c)$ and $T$ is any sieve on $c$ such that $f^*T \in J(d)$ for every $f\colon d \to c$ in $S$, then $T \in J(c)$.

Each axiom encodes a familiar property of open covers. Maximality says "every space covers itself." Stability is the restriction property: pulling a cover back along $f$ gives a cover of the source. Transitivity is the refinement property — if a presumed cover $T$ becomes a genuine cover after restricting to each piece of an actual cover $S$, then $T$ was already a cover. Together these are the minimal data needed for the sheaf condition (limits over covers) to behave well: stability gives functoriality of restriction, transitivity gives local-to-global gluing, maximality is the trivial "self-cover."

The cleanest sanity check is the chaotic / trivial topology $J_{\mathrm{triv}}$, which declares $J(c) = \{\mathrm{max}_c\}$ for every $c$ — only the maximal sieve covers. All three axioms hold: M is given; for S, $f^*\mathrm{max}_c = \mathrm{max}_d$ since every arrow into $d$ composes into $\mathrm{max}_c$; for T, the only candidate $T$ that pulls back to maximal sieves on every leg of $\mathrm{max}_c$ is $\mathrm{max}_c$ itself. So $J_{\mathrm{triv}}$ is a Grothendieck topology — sheaves on $(C, J_{\mathrm{triv}})$ are exactly all presheaves, since there is nothing nontrivial to glue. At the other extreme, the discrete topology $J_{\mathrm{disc}}$ declares every sieve covering — and only the constant presheaf at the terminal set is a sheaf.

3. Examples: small/large Zariski, étale, fpqc

The motivating examples come from algebraic geometry. Fix a scheme $X$. The small Zariski site $X_{\mathrm{Zar}}$ has, as objects, open subschemes $U \hookrightarrow X$, with arrows the open immersions; covers are the usual Zariski open covers. This recovers ordinary sheaf theory on the underlying topological space — sheaves on $X_{\mathrm{Zar}}$ are sheaves on $|X|$. The small étale site $X_{\mathrm{ét}}$ relaxes the morphisms: objects are étale $X$-schemes $U \to X$ (formally smooth + locally of finite presentation + unramified), and a family $\{U_i \to U\}$ covers if it is jointly surjective. Étale maps include open immersions but also finite separable extensions of fields and unramified covers, so $X_{\mathrm{ét}}$ sees Galois-style data invisible to $X_{\mathrm{Zar}}$.

The fpqc site goes further still: covers are jointly surjective families $\{U_i \to U\}$ where each $U_i \to U$ is faithfully flat and quasi-compact (no smoothness or finite-type hypothesis at all). Even fpqc covers are subsumed by the much larger fppf site (faithfully flat + locally of finite presentation), but for descent of quasi-coherent sheaves the canonical choice is fpqc — this is the content of fpqc descent, the cornerstone of modern algebraic geometry. The "small" versus "large" distinction is whether the site lives over a single $X$ or ranges over all schemes; the large étale site of $X$ has all $X$-schemes as objects, with étale covers.

The point of having multiple sites is that local triviality means different things in each. A line bundle on $X$ is, by definition, a quasi-coherent sheaf that is Zariski-locally free of rank one — every line bundle is Zariski-locally trivial. But a $\mu_n$-torsor — a sheaf classifying $n$th roots of unity — need not be Zariski-locally trivial: on $\operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{F}_2$, the field has only one square root of $1$ (namely $1$), so the trivialization $\mu_2 \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathbb{Z}/2$ is not Zariski-local. You have to pass to the étale site (extracting a separable closure) before $\mu_2$ becomes constant. This is the Galois descent phenomenon: the difference between Zariski-locally trivial and étale-locally trivial is precisely the failure of a sheaf to descend along finite separable extensions.

4. Sheaves and sheafification

A presheaf $F\colon C^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}$ is a sheaf on the site $(C, J)$ if for every covering sieve $S \in J(c)$, the restriction $F(c) \to \prod_{f \in S} F(\mathrm{dom}(f))$ exhibits $F(c)$ as the limit of $F$ over the sieve $S$. Equivalently: every "matching family" $\{x_f \in F(\mathrm{dom}(f))\}_{f \in S}$ — a family compatible under further restriction $g\colon e \to \mathrm{dom}(f)$, meaning $F(g)(x_f) = x_{f \circ g}$ — has a unique amalgamation $x \in F(c)$ with $F(f)(x) = x_f$ for every $f \in S$. The "exists" half is gluing; the "unique" half is separation. When $C = \mathrm{Open}(X)$ this is precisely the sheaf axiom you've already met.

The sheafification functor $a\colon \widehat{C} \to \mathrm{Sh}(C, J)$ is the left adjoint to the inclusion $\iota\colon \mathrm{Sh}(C, J) \hookrightarrow \widehat{C}$. Concretely, it's built by Grothendieck's plus-construction, applied twice: $a(F) = (F^+)^+$, where $F^+(c) = \mathrm{colim}_{S \in J(c)} \mathrm{Match}(S, F)$ is the colimit over covering sieves of matching families on $S$. The first $(-)^+$ produces a separated presheaf (gluings unique when they exist); the second $(-)^+$ adds the missing gluings. Iterating once more is idempotent, so two passes suffice — a striking elementary fact whose closest analogue is the sheafification of a presheaf on a topological space via stalks.

Not every presheaf is a sheaf, even on familiar sites. The classic non-example is the constant presheaf $\underline{A}_{\mathrm{pre}}$ on $X_{\mathrm{Zar}}$ for $X$ disconnected: $\underline{A}_{\mathrm{pre}}(U) = A$ regardless of $U$. If $X = U_1 \sqcup U_2$ with both pieces non-empty, the matching family $(a_1, a_2) \in A \times A$ over the disjoint cover has no amalgamation in $\underline{A}_{\mathrm{pre}}(X) = A$ unless $a_1 = a_2$, so gluing fails. Sheafifying gives the locally constant sheaf $\underline{A}$, with $\underline{A}(U) = A^{\pi_0(U)}$ — one copy of $A$ per connected component.

5. $\mathrm{Sh}(C, J)$ as a topos

The category $\mathrm{Sh}(C, J)$ of sheaves on a site is a Grothendieck topos. It is automatically an elementary topos — it has all finite limits, it is cartesian closed, and it has a subobject classifier $\Omega$ — and on top of those finitary properties it has all small colimits and a small generating set (the representables, sheafified). Giraud's theorem is the converse: a category $\mathcal{E}$ is equivalent to $\mathrm{Sh}(C, J)$ for some site $(C, J)$ iff (i) $\mathcal{E}$ has all small colimits, (ii) finite limits commute with filtered colimits in $\mathcal{E}$, (iii) $\mathcal{E}$ has a small generating set, (iv) all colimits are universal (stable under pullback), (v) sums in $\mathcal{E}$ are disjoint, and (vi) every equivalence relation is effective. These six axioms, the Giraud axioms, intrinsically characterize Grothendieck toposes among elementary toposes.

Examples illustrate how broadly this captures geometry. For a topological space $X$, $\mathrm{Sh}(X) := \mathrm{Sh}(\mathrm{Open}(X), \mathrm{open\ covers})$ is the topos of sheaves on $X$ — when $X$ is sober, $\mathrm{Sh}(X)$ is essentially the same as $X$. Taking $C$ to be a one-object category, sheaves are just sets and we recover $\mathsf{Set}$ as the terminal Grothendieck topos. Taking $C = G$ a group (one object, arrows are group elements) gives the topos $G\text{-}\mathsf{Set}$ of $G$-sets. The topos $\mathrm{Sh}(\operatorname{Spec} k)$ for a field $k$ is $\mathsf{Set}$ when $k$ is separably closed, and $\operatorname{Gal}(k^{\mathrm{sep}}/k)\text{-}\mathsf{Set}_{\mathrm{cts}}$ in general — Galois theory becomes topos theory.

The presheaf topos $\widehat{C}$ is the special case of the trivial topology on $C$. Inside $\widehat{C}$ the sheaves form a reflective subcategory via the sheafification adjunction $a \dashv \iota$, and $a$ preserves finite limits — making $\iota$ a geometric embedding. Conversely, every geometric embedding $\mathcal{E} \hookrightarrow \widehat{C}$ is sheafification for a uniquely determined topology $J$ on $C$ (Lawvere–Tierney's theorem on subtoposes — equivalently, on Lawvere–Tierney topologies $j\colon \Omega \to \Omega$). So Grothendieck topologies, geometric embeddings into a presheaf topos, and Lawvere–Tierney topologies are three faces of the same coin.

6. Geometric morphisms from morphisms of sites

A geometric morphism $f\colon \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{E}$ between toposes is an adjunction $f^* \dashv f_*$ where the inverse image $f^*\colon \mathcal{E} \to \mathcal{F}$ preserves finite limits. The direct image $f_*\colon \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{E}$ then automatically preserves all limits (it has a left adjoint). The asymmetry — that $f^*$ is required to be left exact, while $f_*$ has no symmetric finiteness requirement — is what makes "geometric morphism" the right notion: for a continuous map $\varphi\colon X \to Y$ of topological spaces, the induced $\mathrm{Sh}(\varphi)\colon \mathrm{Sh}(X) \to \mathrm{Sh}(Y)$ is a geometric morphism with $f^* = \varphi^{-1}$ (pullback of sheaves) and $f_* = \varphi_*$ (direct image sheaf). The opposite asymmetry would invert which-way maps go.

Site theory provides the canonical mechanism for building geometric morphisms. A continuous map of sites $u\colon (D, K) \to (C, J)$ is a functor $u\colon C \to D$ (note the direction!) that takes covering sieves to covering sieves: if $S \in J(c)$ then $u(S)$ generates a covering sieve in $K(u(c))$. Continuity is exactly the condition that the obvious "restrict along $u$" presheaf functor $u^p\colon \widehat{D} \to \widehat{C}$ preserves the sheaf condition. After sheafifying, this gives a geometric morphism $\mathrm{Sh}(D, K) \to \mathrm{Sh}(C, J)$ where the inverse image is (left Kan extension along $u$, then sheafify) and the direct image is (precompose with $u$).

The construction is functorial: a composition $C \xrightarrow{u} D \xrightarrow{v} E$ of continuous functors of sites induces a composition of geometric morphisms in the opposite direction $\mathrm{Sh}(E) \to \mathrm{Sh}(D) \to \mathrm{Sh}(C)$. This is exactly the mechanism by which a continuous map $X \to Y$ of schemes gives a geometric morphism $X_{\mathrm{ét}} \to Y_{\mathrm{ét}}$ at the level of étale toposes — and in turn how the étale fundamental group $\pi_1^{\mathrm{ét}}(X)$ is recovered from the slice topos. Not every geometric morphism between sheaf toposes comes from a morphism of sites in this strict sense, but enough do — and this is the workhorse construction of Grothendieck-style algebraic geometry.

Naive set theory

Sets, functions, equivalence classes, and Cantor's counting of infinities — the quiet undergrowth beneath every other topic in this notebook. You can't state a continuous map, a group homomorphism, or a quotient space without it, so we spell it out first.

Before topology, algebra, or analysis, everything in this notebook speaks the language of sets and functions. This page spells the language out. We work naively — Cantor's original viewpoint, not ZFC — which is enough for everything you'll do in a graduate math course. The only cost is that you have to step carefully around paradoxes like "the set of all sets that don't contain themselves" (Russell), which show up only when you try to form collections that are too large to be sets. The things we form below — finite or countable lists, power sets of named sets, images of functions — are all safe.

Working definition (Cantor). A set is any well-defined collection of distinct objects considered as a single whole. The objects are its elements. We write $x \in X$ for "$x$ is an element of $X$" and $x \notin X$ for its negation. Two sets are equal iff they have the same elements (the axiom of extensionality): $X = Y \iff \forall x\,(x\in X \leftrightarrow x\in Y)$.

1. Sets and functions

We build everything from four operations on sets, one basic construction (functions), and three classifications of those functions (injective, surjective, bijective).

Subsets, union, intersection, complement

If every element of $A$ is also an element of $B$, we write $A \subseteq B$ and call $A$ a subset of $B$. Note the two directions: $A = B$ iff $A\subseteq B$ and $B\subseteq A$. The empty set $\emptyset$ is a subset of every set, vacuously.

Given sets $A$ and $B$ inside a common universe $U$, we define:

Two foundational counting facts we'll use over and over:

Inclusion–exclusion (two sets). For finite sets, $|A\cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A\cap B|$. Equivalently, $|A\triangle B| = |A| + |B| - 2|A\cap B|$.
Power-set size. If $|A|=n$ is finite then $|\mathcal{P}(A)| = 2^n$: each element is independently in or out of a subset, giving $2^n$ independent binary choices.

Functions

A function (or map) $f\colon A\to B$ assigns to every $a\in A$ exactly one $b\in B$, written $f(a)=b$. Formally, $f$ is a subset of $A\times B$ — its graph — with the property that for each $a\in A$ there is exactly one $b\in B$ with $(a,b)\in f$. ("Exactly one" = "at least one" (totality) + "at most one" (well-definedness).)

The set $A$ is the domain, $B$ the codomain, and the image is $f(A)=\{f(a):a\in A\}\subseteq B$. The preimage of $S\subseteq B$ is $f^{-1}(S)=\{a\in A:f(a)\in S\}$.

Three classifications.
  • Injective (one-to-one): $f(a_1)=f(a_2) \Rightarrow a_1=a_2$. No two inputs collide.
  • Surjective (onto): $f(A)=B$. Every output is hit.
  • Bijective: both. Equivalently, $f$ has a two-sided inverse $f^{-1}\colon B\to A$.
A function from a finite set to itself is injective iff surjective iff bijective — the pigeonhole principle. For infinite sets this collapse fails, and that failure is the whole point of §3.

Injections and surjections compose: if $f\colon A\to B$ and $g\colon B\to C$ are both injective then so is $g\circ f$, and similarly for surjective. So bijections compose to bijections, and the bijections $A\to A$ form a group under composition — the symmetric group $S_A$. For $|A|=n$ finite, $|S_A|=n!$.

2. Cartesian products and power sets

Two simple constructions that recur in every part of mathematics: the Cartesian product $A\times B$ and the power set $\mathcal{P}(A)$. The first packages pairs; the second packages all possible subsets.

Cartesian product. Given sets $A$ and $B$, the product is $$A\times B \;=\; \{(a,b) \;:\; a\in A,\; b\in B\}.$$ For finite sets, $|A\times B| = |A|\cdot|B|$ — the product rule of counting. Products extend to families: $A_1\times A_2\times\cdots\times A_n$ (finite) and $\prod_{i\in I} A_i$ (arbitrary index set) just as naturally.

The product is characterised by its two projection maps $\pi_1\colon A\times B\to A$ and $\pi_2\colon A\times B\to B$. They satisfy a universal property: a function $f\colon X\to A\times B$ is the same data as a pair of functions $(f_1\colon X\to A,\, f_2\colon X\to B)$, via $f(x)=(f_1(x),f_2(x))$. Equivalently, $\operatorname{Hom}(X, A\times B) = \operatorname{Hom}(X,A)\times\operatorname{Hom}(X,B)$ canonically. This is your first glimpse of limits: products are limits in the category of sets. It shows up everywhere downstream — a continuous map $X\to A\times B$ is a pair of continuous maps, a group homomorphism $G\to A\times B$ is a pair of homomorphisms, and so on.

Functions $A\to B$ themselves form a set, often written $B^A$ (the notation is deliberate: for finite $A$ and $B$, $|B^A| = |B|^{|A|}$). You can think of a function $f\colon A\to B$ as its graph, the subset $\Gamma_f = \{(a,f(a)) : a\in A\}\subseteq A\times B$. Graphs are exactly the subsets $G\subseteq A\times B$ such that for each $a\in A$ there is a unique $b\in B$ with $(a,b)\in G$. So "function" is syntactic sugar for a specific kind of relation.

Power set. The power set of $A$ is $\mathcal{P}(A) = \{S : S\subseteq A\}$. For finite $|A|=n$ we already saw $|\mathcal{P}(A)|=2^n$. The general relationship is a bijection $$\mathcal{P}(A) \;\longleftrightarrow\; \{0,1\}^A,\qquad S \;\longmapsto\; \mathbf{1}_S,$$ where $\mathbf{1}_S\colon A\to\{0,1\}$ is the indicator function sending $a$ to $1$ if $a\in S$ and $0$ otherwise. This is why $|\mathcal{P}(A)|=2^{|A|}$ even for infinite $A$: each subset is a binary choice, element by element.

Three examples worth internalising:

  • Ordered $k$-tuples from $A$: $A^k = A\times A\times\cdots\times A$ ($k$ times), with $|A^k|=|A|^k$ when finite. These are the words of length $k$ in the alphabet $A$.
  • Graphs as relations: a directed graph on a vertex set $V$ is precisely a subset $E\subseteq V\times V$ — i.e. an element of $\mathcal{P}(V\times V)$. Undirected graphs are symmetric such subsets.
  • Topologies and $\sigma$-algebras: a topology on $X$ is a specific subset of $\mathcal{P}(X)$ closed under finite intersections and arbitrary unions; a $\sigma$-algebra is one closed under countable unions and complements. The power set is the universal playground these live in.

A small sanity check that uses both constructions: the set of functions from a two-element set $\{0,1\}$ to $A$ is in bijection with $A\times A$ (send $f$ to $(f(0),f(1))$). Equivalently $A^{\{0,1\}} \cong A^2$, matching the cardinal identity $|A|^2 = |A|\cdot|A|$. Cartesian powers are the function spaces on finite index sets.

3. Equivalence relations and quotients

"Equality" is one relation on a set; there are many others. A relation on $X$ is any subset $R\subseteq X\times X$; we write $x\sim y$ for $(x,y)\in R$. Three properties make a relation well-behaved:

Equivalence relation. A relation $\sim$ on $X$ is an equivalence relation if for all $x,y,z\in X$:
  • reflexive: $x\sim x$;
  • symmetric: $x\sim y \implies y\sim x$;
  • transitive: $x\sim y$ and $y\sim z \implies x\sim z$.

The equivalence class of $x$ is $[x] = \{y\in X : y\sim x\}$. The fundamental fact is that equivalence classes partition $X$: every element belongs to exactly one class, and any two classes are either identical or disjoint. Conversely, any partition of $X$ defines an equivalence relation ($x\sim y$ iff they lie in the same block). The set of equivalence classes is the quotient set:

$X/{\sim} \;=\; \{[x] : x\in X\},\qquad \pi\colon X\to X/{\sim},\;\; x\mapsto [x].$

The map $\pi$ is always surjective. It is the canonical example of "collapse data you don't care about."

Canonical examples

  • Congruence mod $n$ on $\mathbb{Z}$: $a\sim b$ iff $n\mid a-b$. Classes are $\{[0],[1],\dots,[n-1]\}$; the quotient is $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$.
  • Rational numbers: on $\mathbb{Z}\times(\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{0\})$, declare $(a,b)\sim(c,d)$ iff $ad=bc$. The quotient is $\mathbb{Q}$ by definition.
  • Projective space: on $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}\setminus\{0\}$, $v\sim\lambda v$ for any $\lambda\ne 0$. Quotient is $\mathbb{RP}^n$.
  • Homotopy: on continuous loops at a basepoint, $\alpha\sim\beta$ iff they can be continuously deformed into each other. Quotient is the fundamental group $\pi_1$.
Universal property of the quotient. Let $f\colon X\to Y$ be a function that is constant on $\sim$-classes: $x\sim x' \implies f(x)=f(x')$. Then there is a unique function $\bar f\colon X/{\sim}\to Y$ with $\bar f\circ\pi = f$. In diagram form: $$X \xrightarrow{\;\pi\;} X/{\sim} \xrightarrow{\;\bar f\;} Y,\qquad \bar f([x]):=f(x).$$ Conversely, every function $g\colon X/{\sim}\to Y$ gives a $\sim$-invariant function $g\circ\pi\colon X\to Y$. So $\operatorname{Hom}(X/{\sim}, Y)$ is in bijection with $\{\sim\text{-invariant functions }X\to Y\}$.

Why the universal property matters

"$f$ is constant on classes" is a concrete, checkable property. The universal property turns it into a construction: given such an $f$, you get $\bar f$ for free, and it is the unique map making the triangle commute. This is how you define maps out of a quotient.

Examples of maps defined by the universal property.
  • On $\mathbb{Z}/n$: addition $\overline{+}\colon\mathbb{Z}/n\times\mathbb{Z}/n\to\mathbb{Z}/n$ is well-defined because $(a+b)\bmod n$ depends only on $a\bmod n$ and $b\bmod n$.
  • On $\mathbb{Q}=(\mathbb{Z}\times\mathbb{Z}_{\ne 0})/{\sim}$: $[a,b]+[c,d]:=[ad+bc,bd]$ is well-defined (the value is a $\sim$-invariant of its inputs).
  • On $\mathbb{RP}^n$: a well-defined map out of $\mathbb{RP}^n$ is a map on $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}\setminus 0$ that is invariant under $v\mapsto \lambda v$. Checking that is the first homework of any projective-geometry course.

4. Countability

Two sets have the same cardinality, written $|A|=|B|$, if there exists a bijection $A\to B$. This is an equivalence relation on sets. For finite sets it matches the usual count. For infinite sets it is subtler — and delivers Cantor's great surprise: there are different sizes of infinity.

Countable. A set $S$ is countable if $|S|\le|\mathbb{N}|$, i.e. there is an injection $S\hookrightarrow\mathbb{N}$ (equivalently a surjection $\mathbb{N}\twoheadrightarrow S$ if $S$ is non-empty). Equivalently, $S$ is finite or its elements can be listed as $s_0, s_1, s_2, \dots$. A set that is not countable is uncountable.

Lots of sets are countable

$\mathbb{Z}$ is countable — the list $0, 1, -1, 2, -2, 3, -3, \dots$ exhibits a bijection $\mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{Z}$. More impressive:

$\mathbb{N}\times\mathbb{N}$ is countable. Walk along the anti-diagonals $i+j=0,1,2,\dots$: $(0,0); (0,1),(1,0); (0,2),(1,1),(2,0); \dots$ This is Cantor's zigzag pairing. Concretely, $\langle i,j\rangle := \tfrac{(i+j)(i+j+1)}{2}+i$ is a bijection $\mathbb{N}^2\to\mathbb{N}$.

Consequences cascade. A countable union of countable sets is countable: lay out the sets as rows of a grid, then zigzag. Products: $\mathbb{Z}^k$ is countable for any finite $k$, by induction. Rationals: $\mathbb{Q}$ injects into $\mathbb{Z}^2$ (send $p/q$ in lowest terms to $(p,q)$), hence is countable. Algebraic numbers: the set of roots of polynomials in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is a countable union (over countably many polynomials) of finite sets, hence countable.

But $\mathbb{R}$ is not

Cantor's diagonal argument shows that no countable list exhausts the real numbers — or even the binary sequences. Suppose, for contradiction, that we have a complete enumeration $s_0, s_1, s_2, \dots$ of all infinite binary sequences $\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$. Form a new sequence $d$ by flipping the $n$-th bit of the $n$-th sequence:

$d_n := 1 - s_n(n).$

By construction $d$ differs from $s_n$ in position $n$, for every $n$. So $d$ is not on the list. Contradiction. Hence $\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$ — and therefore $\mathbb{R}$ — is uncountable. Since $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable, we conclude $|\mathbb{R}|>|\mathbb{Q}|$.

Cardinal arithmetic, briefly

Cantor proved something sharper: for any set $A$, $|\mathcal{P}(A)| > |A|$. (If $f\colon A\to\mathcal{P}(A)$ were a bijection, the set $D = \{a\in A : a\notin f(a)\}$ is an element of $\mathcal{P}(A)$ not in the image — a second diagonal argument.) So there is no largest cardinal; the ladder of infinities $|\mathbb{N}|<|\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})|<|\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N}))|<\dots$ never terminates. It's conventional to write $\aleph_0 = |\mathbb{N}|$ and $\mathfrak{c} = |\mathbb{R}| = 2^{\aleph_0}$; the continuum hypothesis asks whether there is a cardinal strictly between them, and is famously independent of ZFC.

Cheat-sheet.
  • $|\mathbb{N}|=|\mathbb{Z}|=|\mathbb{Q}|=|\mathbb{N}^k|=|$algebraic numbers$|=\aleph_0$ — all countable.
  • $|\mathbb{R}|=|\mathbb{C}|=|\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})|=|\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}|=\mathfrak{c}$ — all equinumerous with the continuum.
  • Transcendental numbers: $\mathbb{R}\setminus\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$ has cardinality $\mathfrak{c}$, since the countable set of algebraic numbers can't fill $\mathbb{R}$. In particular almost every real is transcendental, even though writing down specific examples (like $\pi$ and $e$) is delicate.

5. The axiom of choice (intuition)

Every step up to here was in principle constructive — we listed elements, defined maps by formulas, and checked bijections by hand. As soon as we consider infinite families of non-empty sets, a subtle new question appears: can we pick one element from each set at once?

Axiom of choice (AC). For any family $\{A_i\}_{i\in I}$ of non-empty sets, the product $\prod_{i\in I} A_i$ is non-empty. Equivalently, there exists a choice function $f\colon I\to\bigcup_i A_i$ with $f(i)\in A_i$ for every $i$.

For a finite $I$ this is obvious: one picks $a_1\in A_1$, then $a_2\in A_2$, and so on, $|I|$ many times. The difficulty arrives when $I$ is uncountable, because there may be no uniform rule that picks an element of each $A_i$. Bertrand Russell's cartoon: if you have infinitely many pairs of shoes, you can always pick the left one — no AC needed. But if you have infinitely many pairs of socks, indistinguishable within each pair, there is no rule. AC is the assertion that a choice function still exists.

AC is independent of the other axioms of set theory (ZF): Gödel (1938) showed it is consistent with ZF, Cohen (1963) showed its negation is also consistent. So you can work with or without it, and the standard mathematical universe ZFC takes it on board. Almost all of analysis, algebra, and topology implicitly use it:

Equivalents of AC. The following statements are all logically equivalent to AC over ZF:
  • Zorn's lemma. Every non-empty partially ordered set in which every chain has an upper bound has a maximal element.
  • Well-ordering theorem. Every set can be well-ordered (given a total order with no infinite descending chain).
  • Tukey's lemma. Every non-empty family of finite character has a maximal element.
  • Every vector space has a basis. (Even $\mathbb{R}$ as a $\mathbb{Q}$-vector space — a Hamel basis.)
These are the forms you meet in practice. Zorn's lemma in particular is the workhorse: maximal ideals in rings, prime filters in lattices, maximal orthonormal sets in Hilbert spaces, algebraic closures of fields — each existence proof is "apply Zorn to the poset of partial solutions."

Consequences that are striking enough to be worth naming:

  • Every vector space has a basis. For finite-dimensional spaces this is trivial; for $\mathbb{R}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ no basis can be explicitly written down, yet AC guarantees one.
  • Tychonoff's theorem. An arbitrary product of compact topological spaces is compact. Kelley proved this is equivalent to AC.
  • Banach–Tarski paradox. The closed unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^3$ can be decomposed into finitely many pieces and reassembled, using rigid motions, into two closed unit balls. The "pieces" are non-measurable; AC is essential for their construction.

Many mathematicians reserve the right to flag when a proof uses AC (or a weaker form like countable choice, which suffices for most of analysis). In this notebook we use AC freely — every time we pass to a maximal ideal, pick a basis, or invoke compactness of $[0,1]^{\mathbb{N}}$, we are spending AC. Knowing where it sits gives you a clean answer to "why can I do this?" when a proof seems to wave its hands over an infinite choice.

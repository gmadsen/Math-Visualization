Hecke operators and the eigenforms they crown

The space of modular forms of weight $k$ is finite-dimensional but otherwise unremarkable — just a vector space. Hecke operators endow it with a commuting family of self-adjoint maps, and the simultaneous eigenvectors are the eigenforms: the arithmetic atoms whose Fourier coefficients are Galois-theoretic.

1. Among all modular forms, which are canonical?

Fix an even integer $k \ge 4$. The space $M_k = M_k(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ of weight-$k$ holomorphic modular forms for the full modular group is a finite-dimensional $\mathbb{C}$-vector space. A basis is easy to write down (Eisenstein series $E_4, E_6$ and the cusp form $\Delta$ generate everything), but a basis is not canonical — any linear combination is still a modular form.

So the question is:

Among all weight-$k$ modular forms, which ones are distinguished by structural properties of the space itself?

The answer, discovered in the 1930s, is that $M_k$ carries a commuting family of linear operators indexed by primes: $$T_2,\; T_3,\; T_5,\; T_7,\; \ldots$$ These Hecke operators preserve $M_k$. They commute with each other. And under a natural inner product they are self-adjoint. So by the spectral theorem $M_k$ has an orthogonal basis of simultaneous eigenvectors — the Hecke eigenforms. These eigenforms, and nothing else, are the distinguished elements.

The Fourier coefficient $a_p$ of a normalized eigenform is then not just a number; it is the eigenvalue of $T_p$. It turns out that sequence of eigenvalues $(a_p)_p$ behaves as if it came from a two-dimensional Galois representation — which, thanks to work in the 1970s through 2000s, it actually does.

2. The Hecke operator $T_p$ on lattices

A weight-$k$ modular form is really a function on lattices in $\mathbb{C}$: given a lattice $\Lambda$, $f$ assigns a complex number $f(\Lambda)$ that scales as $f(c\Lambda) = c^{-k} f(\Lambda)$ for $c \in \mathbb{C}^\times$. Writing $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$ with $\tau = \omega_1/\omega_2 \in \mathcal{H}$ recovers the familiar $f(\tau)$ on the upper half-plane.

Now fix a prime $p$. A lattice $\Lambda$ has finitely many sublattices of index $p$. In fact, there are exactly $p+1$ of them: the index-$p$ sublattices of $\mathbb{Z}^2$ correspond to the lines in $\mathbb{F}_p^2$, of which there are $\frac{p^2-1}{p-1} = p+1$. Explicitly, if $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$, the $p+1$ sublattices are $$\Lambda_\infty = \mathbb{Z}(p\omega_1) + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2, \qquad \Lambda_b = \mathbb{Z}(\omega_1 + b\omega_2) + \mathbb{Z}(p\omega_2)\ \ (b=0,1,\ldots,p-1).$$

The Hecke operator $T_p$ averages $f$ over these sublattices, with a weight factor that makes the result still weight $k$: $$(T_p f)(\Lambda) \;=\; p^{k-1} \sum_{\Lambda' \subset \Lambda,\; [\Lambda:\Lambda'] = p} f(\Lambda').$$

Translating back to the $\tau$-picture: the sublattice $\Lambda_\infty$ has basis $(p\omega_1, \omega_2)$ with ratio $p\tau$; each $\Lambda_b$ has basis $(\omega_1 + b\omega_2, p\omega_2)$, which after scaling by $1/p$ has ratio $(\tau + b)/p$. Working through the weight-$k$ normalization (scaling by the second basis vector to put the lattice in $\tau$-form) yields the working formula:

$$(T_p f)(\tau) \;=\; p^{\,k-1}\, f(p\tau) \;+\; \frac{1}{p} \sum_{b=0}^{p-1} f\!\left(\frac{\tau + b}{p}\right).$$

One term (the "upstairs" direction) multiplies $\tau$ by $p$; the other $p$ terms (the "downstairs" directions) divide $\tau + b$ by $p$, averaging over the $p$ possible lifts. The total is $p+1$ sublattices, weighted by $p^{k-1}$ and $1/p = p^{-1}$ respectively.

The weight factor $p^{k-1}$ is there for exactly one reason: to make $(T_p f)(\tau)$ a modular form of the same weight $k$ — not of weight $k$ shifted by anything. Checking that $(T_p f)|_k \gamma = T_p f$ for every $\gamma \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ is a short calculation using the right coset decomposition of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})\, \mathrm{diag}(1,p)\, \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$.

3. Action on $q$-expansions

Because $T_p$ commutes with translation $\tau \mapsto \tau+1$ (that is one of the modular transformations), it preserves the Fourier expansion. Plugging the $q$-expansion $f(\tau) = \sum_{n \ge 0} a_n q^n$ with $q = e^{2\pi i \tau}$ into the formula from the previous section gives a completely explicit recipe.

The "$p\tau$" term produces $\sum_n a_n q^{pn}$, multiplied by $p^{k-1}$. The sum over $b$ produces $$\frac{1}{p}\sum_{b=0}^{p-1} f\!\left(\frac{\tau+b}{p}\right) \;=\; \frac{1}{p}\sum_{b=0}^{p-1} \sum_n a_n e^{2\pi i n (\tau+b)/p} \;=\; \sum_n a_n \,q^{n/p}\!\cdot\!\frac{1}{p}\sum_{b=0}^{p-1}\! e^{2\pi i nb/p}.$$ The inner sum is $1$ if $p \mid n$ and $0$ otherwise. So only $n$ divisible by $p$ survives; re-index $n = pm$: $$\frac{1}{p}\sum_{b=0}^{p-1} f\!\left(\frac{\tau+b}{p}\right) \;=\; \sum_m a_{pm} q^m.$$ Combining:

$$T_p f \;=\; \sum_{n \ge 0} c_n q^n, \qquad c_n \;=\; a_{pn} + p^{k-1} a_{n/p},$$

with the convention that $a_{n/p} = 0$ unless $p \mid n$. So $T_p$ acts on the coefficient sequence by mixing the $p$-th contraction $a_{pn}$ with the $p$-th dilation $p^{k-1} a_{n/p}$.

4. The Hecke algebra

Hecke operators are indexed by positive integers, not just primes: there is a $T_n$ for every $n \ge 1$. On $q$-expansions, the general formula is $$(T_n f)_m \;=\; \sum_{d \mid \gcd(m,n)} d^{\,k-1}\, a_{mn/d^2}.$$ For $n = p$ prime this specializes to the formula above. The crucial structural facts are:

  • Commutativity. $T_m T_n = T_n T_m$ for all $m,n$.
  • Multiplicative relation. $T_m T_n = T_{mn}$ whenever $\gcd(m,n) = 1$.
  • Prime-power recursion. $T_{p^{r+1}} = T_p T_{p^r} - p^{k-1}\, T_{p^{r-1}}$.

So the whole algebra $\mathbb{T}_k = \langle T_1, T_2, T_3, \ldots \rangle$ is generated — as a commutative ring of operators on $M_k$ — by the $T_p$ for $p$ prime, subject to those relations. In particular, $\mathbb{T}_k$ is commutative.

Hecke algebra commutativity
$T_m T_n = T_n T_m$

Why does $T_m T_n = T_n T_m$?

The slick proof goes through the double coset algebra. The operators $T_p$ correspond to the double coset $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})\, \mathrm{diag}(1,p)\, \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ in $\mathrm{GL}_2^+(\mathbb{Q})$, and the product of two double cosets is commutative by the Satake isomorphism (abstractly: because $\mathrm{GL}_2$ modulo its center is "almost" a symmetric space). A direct coefficient computation also works — expand $T_m T_n$ on $q$-expansions using the formula above and check the divisor sum collapses symmetrically in $m,n$.

5. Eigenforms and their arithmetic coefficients

Since $\mathbb{T}_k$ is a commutative algebra of operators on the finite-dimensional space $M_k$, and since — we will see next — each $T_p$ is diagonalizable, the $T_p$'s are simultaneously diagonalizable. A simultaneous eigenvector $f \in M_k$ is a Hecke eigenform: $$T_p f \;=\; \lambda_p f \quad \text{for every prime } p.$$

Now suppose $f = \sum a_n q^n$ is a Hecke eigenform. Comparing the $q^1$-coefficient of $T_p f = \lambda_p f$: $$c_1 = a_p + p^{k-1} a_{1/p} = a_p \quad (\text{since } a_{1/p}=0), \qquad c_1 = \lambda_p \cdot a_1.$$ So $\lambda_p = a_p / a_1$. It is conventional to normalize so that $a_1 = 1$ (possible unless $a_1 = 0$, which for cusp forms is the only degenerate case and forces $f = 0$ by the $q$-expansion principle). Then:

For a normalized Hecke eigenform, the eigenvalue of $T_p$ is exactly $a_p$: $$\boxed{\;T_p f \,=\, a_p\, f\;}, \qquad a_p = \text{$p$-th Fourier coefficient}.$$

The same comparison at $q^n$ gives $$\lambda_p \, a_n \;=\; a_{pn} + p^{k-1} a_{n/p}, \quad \text{i.e.} \quad a_{pn} \;=\; a_p a_n - p^{k-1} a_{n/p}.$$ This is a recursion: the coefficients $a_{p^r}$ for $r \ge 2$ are determined by $a_p$. Combined with the fact that $T_m T_n = T_{mn}$ for $\gcd(m,n) = 1$ applied to $f$, we get the celebrated multiplicative identity:

$$a_m \cdot a_n \;=\; a_{mn} \qquad \text{whenever } \gcd(m,n) = 1,$$ for any normalized Hecke eigenform. More generally, $a_m a_n = \sum_{d \mid \gcd(m,n)} d^{\,k-1}\, a_{mn/d^2}$.

So the entire coefficient sequence $(a_n)$ is determined by its values at primes, plus the prime-power recursion. This is a huge reduction: infinitely many integers are encoded by one integer per prime.

6. The Petersson inner product and self-adjointness

To deduce that $M_k$ has a basis of eigenforms, we need the Hecke operators to be normal — and in fact they are self-adjoint for a natural inner product on the space of cusp forms $S_k = \{\,f \in M_k : f \text{ vanishes at the cusp}\,\}$.

The Petersson inner product is defined on $S_k$ by $$\langle f, g \rangle \;=\; \int_{\mathcal{F}} f(\tau) \overline{g(\tau)} \, y^{k-2} \, dx\,dy,$$ where $\tau = x + iy$, $y = \mathrm{Im}(\tau) > 0$, and $\mathcal{F}$ is the standard fundamental domain for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ acting on $\mathcal{H}$. The integrand $y^{k-2}\,dx\,dy$ is carefully chosen: the product $f(\tau)\overline{g(\tau)}\,y^k$ is $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$-invariant (because $f$ and $g$ each transform by $(c\tau+d)^k$, and $y^k$ picks up $|c\tau+d|^{-2k}$), and $dx\,dy/y^2$ is the hyperbolic measure on $\mathcal{H}$ — so $\langle f,g \rangle$ is an integral over the finite-volume hyperbolic quotient $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) \backslash \mathcal{H}$.

Petersson integral on the fundamental domain
$\mathcal{F}$ with hyperbolic measure $y^{-2}\,dx\,dy$

Self-adjointness of $T_p$

The key fact is:

For every prime $p$, $$\langle T_p f, g \rangle \;=\; \langle f, T_p g \rangle.$$

The proof is a change-of-variables computation using the explicit formula for $T_p$: the sum over cosets that defines $T_p$ is sent under the integral-equivalence to the "transpose" coset sum, and those two coset sums are identical for the standard Hecke operators on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ (for congruence subgroups $\Gamma_0(N)$, self-adjointness holds for $T_p$ with $p \nmid N$; at the primes dividing $N$ one gets Atkin–Lehner operators).

Since the $T_p$ are self-adjoint, each is diagonalizable with real eigenvalues. Since they also commute, they are simultaneously diagonalizable. The conclusion is:

$S_k$ admits an orthogonal basis of normalized Hecke eigenforms. This basis is unique (up to reordering and sign) — it is determined by the Hecke algebra acting on $S_k$.

The Eisenstein series contribute a 1-dimensional "non-cuspidal" part of $M_k$ on which the Hecke operators also act; including them, $M_k = \mathbb{C}\,E_k \oplus S_k$ decomposes as an orthogonal direct sum of Hecke eigenspaces.

7. Where this goes: Galois, Euler, and $L$-functions

The single most astonishing property of Hecke eigenvalues is how they encode Galois-theoretic data.

Eigenvalues as Frobenius traces

For a normalized eigenform $f$ of weight $k \ge 2$ and level $N$ — a Galois-representation-theoretic object lurking in the background is an $\ell$-adic Galois representation $$\rho_f \colon \operatorname{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{GL}_2(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}_\ell)$$ attached to $f$. For every prime $p \nmid N\ell$, $$\operatorname{tr}\rho_f(\mathrm{Frob}_p) \;=\; a_p, \qquad \det\rho_f(\mathrm{Frob}_p) \;=\; p^{\,k-1}.$$ The Hecke eigenvalue $a_p$, computed from a complex-analytic $q$-expansion, equals the trace of Frobenius — a purely algebraic invariant of number fields. This is the modularity / reciprocity principle in a nutshell, and it unifies the arithmetic story with the representation-theoretic one. (Cross-links to a full account live on the Galois theory page; a dedicated page on Galois representations is forthcoming.)

The Euler product of $L(f,s)$

Define the $L$-function of a normalized eigenform by its Dirichlet series: $$L(f, s) \;=\; \sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{a_n}{n^s}.$$ Because $a_{mn} = a_m a_n$ for coprime $m,n$, the series factors over primes into an Euler product, and the prime-power recursion $a_{p^{r+1}} = a_p a_{p^r} - p^{k-1} a_{p^{r-1}}$ says each local factor is a degree-2 rational function:

$$L(f, s) \;=\; \prod_{p} \frac{1}{1 \,-\, a_p\, p^{-s} \,+\, p^{\,k-1}\, p^{-2s}}.$$

The $p$-th Euler factor is the characteristic polynomial of $\rho_f(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$, evaluated at $p^{-s}$. So the classical complex-analytic $L$-function of a modular form and the motivic / $\ell$-adic $L$-function of its Galois representation are the same object, viewed through different lenses.

From here several threads unfold:

  • Ramanujan bound (Deligne). $|a_p| \le 2 p^{(k-1)/2}$ for normalized cusp-form eigenforms. Equivalently, the roots of the Euler factor have absolute value $p^{(k-1)/2}$. Proved via the Weil conjectures for products of modular curves.
  • Modularity. For every elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$, the $L$-function $L(E,s)$ equals $L(f_E, s)$ for a unique normalized weight-2 eigenform $f_E$ of level $N = $ conductor$(E)$. See elliptic curves for the role Hecke eigenforms play here.
  • Functional equation. $L(f,s)$ extends to an entire function with a functional equation $s \leftrightarrow k-s$ — mirroring the functional equation of $\zeta(s)$ and $\zeta(1-s)$, and explaining why modular forms are the right automorphic inputs for the Langlands programme.

A dedicated page on $L$-functions will unpack the analytic continuation, functional equation, and special values.

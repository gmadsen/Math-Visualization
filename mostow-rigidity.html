Mostow rigidity

In dimension three and above, hyperbolic geometry is a topological invariant: a closed hyperbolic manifold's metric, volume, and length spectrum are all determined by its fundamental group alone. The proof transports group-theoretic data through quasi-conformal analysis on the boundary sphere, where ergodicity finishes the job.

1. Mostow's rigidity theorem

For Riemannian manifolds in general, the metric and the topology are independent: a torus admits flat, Berger-sphere-like, and many other metrics simultaneously. Even a closed hyperbolic surface of genus $g\ge 2$ has a $(6g-6)$-dimensional Teichmüller space of distinct hyperbolic metrics. Then dimension $3$ arrives, and freedom collapses.

Mostow rigidity (1968). Let $M_1, M_2$ be closed (compact, no boundary), connected, orientable hyperbolic $n$-manifolds with $n\ge 3$. If $\pi_1(M_1) \cong \pi_1(M_2)$ as abstract groups, then $M_1$ and $M_2$ are isometric, and the isometry is unique up to homotopy. Equivalently: any homotopy equivalence between such manifolds is homotopic to an isometry.

The hyperbolic metric is therefore a topological invariant in dimension $\ge 3$. Every metric quantity — volume, length spectrum, eigenvalue spectrum of the Laplacian, injectivity radius — becomes computable in principle from $\pi_1(M)$ alone. There is no continuous parameter to deform along.

Two facts make this striking:

  • Dimension $2$ fails completely. Hyperbolic structures on a closed surface $\Sigma_g$ of genus $g\ge 2$ form a real-analytic manifold $\mathcal{T}_g$ of dimension $6g-6$. The same $\pi_1$ supports infinitely many non-isometric hyperbolic metrics.
  • Higher dimension is rigid. The same theorem holds for $\mathbb{H}^n$ with $n\ge 3$, and in fact for irreducible locally symmetric spaces of non-compact type other than $\mathbb{H}^2$.

The proof strategy, sketched in the remaining sections:

  1. A homotopy equivalence $f: M_1 \to M_2$ lifts to a $\Gamma$-equivariant quasi-isometry $\widetilde f: \mathbb{H}^n \to \mathbb{H}^n$ between universal covers (§3).
  2. $\widetilde f$ extends continuously to a quasi-conformal homeomorphism $\partial \widetilde f$ of the boundary sphere $S^{n-1}$ (§3).
  3. Cocompactness of $\Gamma$ implies ergodicity of the diagonal action on $S^{n-1}\times S^{n-1}$; this forces the dilatation of $\partial \widetilde f$ to be constant a.e. (§4).
  4. For $n\ge 3$ the only quasi-conformal maps with constant dilatation are Möbius; the boundary map is therefore an element of $\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^n)$, and one reads off the bulk isometry.

2. Closed hyperbolic 3-manifolds

The model space $\mathbb{H}^3$ is the upper half-space $\{(x_1,x_2,y) : y > 0\}$ with metric $ds^2 = (dx_1^2 + dx_2^2 + dy^2)/y^2$. Its boundary at infinity is the round 2-sphere $S^2 = \widehat{\mathbb{C}}$, and the orientation-preserving isometry group is

$$ \mathrm{Isom}^+(\mathbb{H}^3) \;\cong\; \mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{C}), $$

acting on the boundary $\widehat{\mathbb{C}}$ by Möbius transformations $z \mapsto (az+b)/(cz+d)$ and extending to the bulk via the Poincaré half-space formulas.

Closed hyperbolic $3$-manifolds. A closed hyperbolic $3$-manifold is a quotient $M = \mathbb{H}^3/\Gamma$ where $\Gamma\subset \mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{C})$ is a torsion-free, discrete, cocompact subgroup. Then $\Gamma\cong\pi_1(M)$, and Mostow rigidity says $\Gamma$ as an abstract group determines its conjugacy class in $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{C})$.

Three classical constructions:

  • Arithmetic examples. Take a quaternion algebra $B$ over an imaginary quadratic field $K$ that ramifies at a controlled set of places; the unit group $B^1$ embeds discretely in $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{C})$ via a real place. The Bianchi groups $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathcal{O}_K)$ are non-cocompact lattices but illustrate the family.
  • Hyperbolic Dehn surgery. Thurston's surgery construction: a complete hyperbolic structure on a knot complement deforms continuously to closed hyperbolic manifolds obtained by gluing solid tori to all but one cusp. This produces infinitely many distinct closed hyperbolic 3-manifolds whose volumes accumulate at the original cusp volume.
  • Mapping tori. A pseudo-Anosov diffeomorphism of a closed surface produces a closed hyperbolic 3-manifold as the suspension; $\pi_1$ is an HNN extension.

3. Quasi-isometry and boundary extension

The first reduction is Švarc–Milnor. A homotopy equivalence $f: M_1 \to M_2$ lifts to a $\Gamma$-equivariant continuous map $\widetilde f: \mathbb{H}^n \to \mathbb{H}^n$. By smoothing one may assume $\widetilde f$ is Lipschitz. Cocompactness of the actions makes $\widetilde f$ a quasi-isometry: there exist $K\ge 1, C\ge 0$ with

$$ \tfrac{1}{K}\, d(x,y) - C \;\le\; d(\widetilde f(x), \widetilde f(y)) \;\le\; K\, d(x,y) + C \qquad \text{for all } x,y\in\mathbb{H}^n, $$

and $\widetilde f$ is coarsely surjective. Quasi-isometry is the right coarse equivalence: it discards the fine metric data the homotopy equivalence wasn't carrying anyway.

Boundary extension. A quasi-isometry of $\mathbb{H}^n$ extends continuously to a homeomorphism of the sphere at infinity, and the boundary map is quasi-conformal: there is a constant $K' \ge 1$ such that for every infinitesimal ball, the eccentricity of its image is bounded by $K'$. Geodesics in $\mathbb{H}^n$ have well-defined endpoints on $S^{n-1}$, and the quasi-isometry sends Hausdorff-close geodesics to Hausdorff-close geodesics — so endpoints map to endpoints.

The boundary map $\partial\widetilde f: S^{n-1} \to S^{n-1}$ is therefore quasi-conformal and intertwines the boundary actions of $\pi_1(M_1)$ and $\pi_1(M_2)$. The proof has now translated a topological problem about closed manifolds into an analytic problem on $S^{n-1}$.

4. Ergodicity of the boundary action

The boundary map $\partial\widetilde f$ has bounded distortion but is far from canonical: any quasi-conformal map deforms infinitesimal balls into infinitesimal ellipsoids, and the family of dilatation tensors

$$ \mu(x) \;=\; \frac{D\partial\widetilde f(x) \, [D\partial\widetilde f(x)]^T}{\det D\partial\widetilde f(x)}\; - \; I \qquad (x\in S^{n-1}\setminus\text{null set}) $$

encodes the obstruction to being conformal. The trick is that $\mu$ is $\Gamma$-equivariant in a precise sense: $\mu$ pulled back by every $\gamma\in\Gamma$ matches $\mu$ a.e. Then ergodicity strikes.

Hopf ergodicity. For a closed hyperbolic $n$-manifold $M = \mathbb{H}^n/\Gamma$, the geodesic flow on the unit tangent bundle $T^1 M$ is ergodic (Eberhard Hopf, 1939). Equivalently, the diagonal $\Gamma$-action on $S^{n-1}\times S^{n-1}\setminus\Delta$ is ergodic with respect to the Lebesgue product measure.

The Gromov–Margulis argument: the dilatation $\mu$ defines a $\Gamma$-invariant measurable function on $S^{n-1}$. Ergodicity of the action on pairs of boundary points (which controls $\mu$ via Egoroff–Lusin) forces $\mu$ to be a.e. constant. For $n\ge 3$, a quasi-conformal map of $S^{n-1}$ with constant dilatation is $1$-quasi-conformal, hence Möbius, hence the restriction to $S^{n-1}$ of a hyperbolic isometry. Lifting back, $\widetilde f$ is at finite distance from a hyperbolic isometry of $\mathbb{H}^n$, and $\Gamma$-equivariance descends this isometry to $M_1\to M_2$.

The dimension $\ge 3$ hypothesis enters here: for $n=2$, the boundary $S^1$ has many quasi-symmetric self-homeomorphisms with constant dilatation that are not Möbius — earthquake maps build them. That degree of freedom is exactly the Teichmüller space.

5. Margulis arithmeticity

Mostow's theorem covers the rank-one symmetric spaces — most importantly $\mathbb{H}^n$. Margulis pushed the rigidity philosophy into higher rank with a far stronger conclusion.

Margulis arithmeticity (1974). Let $G$ be a connected semisimple real Lie group of real rank $\ge 2$, with no compact factors and finite center. Every irreducible lattice $\Gamma\subset G$ is arithmetic: there exists an algebraic group $\mathbf G$ defined over $\mathbb{Q}$ and an isomorphism $\mathbf G(\mathbb{R})\to G$ (modulo a compact factor) under which $\Gamma$ becomes commensurable with $\mathbf G(\mathbb{Z})$.

The chain of upgrades:

theoremsettingconclusion about $\Gamma$
Mostow rigidity$\mathrm{rank} = 1$, $n\ge 3$determined up to conjugacy (isometry)
Margulis superrigidity$\mathrm{rank} \ge 2$finite-dim. linear reps extend to $G$
Margulis arithmeticity$\mathrm{rank} \ge 2$$\Gamma$ is the integer points of an alg. group

The hyperbolic case $\mathbb{H}^n$ has $\mathrm{rank} = 1$, just below the threshold. Non-arithmetic hyperbolic lattices exist in every dimension by the Gromov–Piatetski-Shapiro construction (gluing arithmetic pieces along totally geodesic walls). So Mostow's rigidity is a conjugacy statement, not a number-theoretic structure theorem; in higher rank, Margulis gives both.

6. Volume, simplicial volume, and geometrisation

Once the metric is a function of the topology, every metric quantity is too. The most useful instance is volume.

Volume rigidity. For a closed hyperbolic $n$-manifold $M$ ($n\ge 3$), $\mathrm{Vol}(M, g_{\mathrm{hyp}})$ depends only on the homeomorphism type of $M$. For a fixed homotopy type, the volume is an invariant.

Gromov gave this invariant a topological avatar. Define the simplicial volume (or $\ell^1$-norm) of a closed oriented $n$-manifold $M$ as

$$ \|M\| \;=\; \inf\Bigl\{ \sum |a_i| \;:\; \sum a_i\sigma_i \in C_n(M;\mathbb{R})\text{ represents } [M] \in H_n(M;\mathbb{R}) \Bigr\}, $$

where $[M]$ is the fundamental class and the chains are real (so the coefficients $a_i$ may be any reals, not just integers).

Gromov–Thurston. For a closed hyperbolic $n$-manifold $M$, $$ \|M\| \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{Vol}(M)}{v_n}, $$ where $v_n$ is the volume of a regular ideal hyperbolic $n$-simplex. So simplicial volume is a topological invariant equal up to a universal constant to the hyperbolic volume.

The corollary is striking: $\|M\| > 0$ for closed hyperbolic manifolds, but $\|S^n\| = 0$ for spheres and $\|T^n\| = 0$ for tori (every fundamental class can be represented by chains of arbitrarily small $\ell^1$-norm). Simplicial volume therefore distinguishes hyperbolic 3-manifolds from spherical and Euclidean ones at the level of $\pi_1$.

Geometrisation in dimension 3

Thurston's geometrisation conjecture (proved by Perelman via Ricci flow) decomposes every closed orientable $3$-manifold $M$ along incompressible spheres and tori into pieces, each carrying one of the eight model geometries:

The hyperbolic pieces — the most numerous and most generic — are uniquely determined as Riemannian manifolds by Mostow rigidity. The spherical and Euclidean pieces are also classified, with finite (resp. continuous-but-controlled) moduli; only the hyperbolic case has zero moduli for reasons of dimension.

Connections

Mostow rigidity sits at a nexus of geometry, group theory, and arithmetic. Open frontiers include sharper rigidity for non-cocompact lattices (rank-one geometric finiteness), the still-mysterious distribution of small-volume closed hyperbolic 3-manifolds, and quantitative versions of arithmeticity. The Lubotzky–Margulis–Zuk programme on property (T) and expander graphs draws directly on the same lattice-theoretic machinery.

  • Riemannian geometry — model spaces — the constant-curvature symmetric spaces $\mathbb{S}^n, \mathbb{R}^n, \mathbb{H}^n$ are the local geometry that Mostow globalises.
  • Upper half-plane — $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ isometries — the dimension-2 isometry group whose moduli (Teichmüller space) shows why rigidity fails here.
  • Lie groups — the group-theoretic ambient $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{C})$ inside which closed hyperbolic 3-manifolds live as discrete cocompact lattices.
  • Algebraic topology — fundamental group — the topological invariant that Mostow promotes to a complete metric invariant.
  • Modular forms — the same lattice-in-Lie-group machinery, now over $\mathrm{PSL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ acting on $\mathbb{H}^2$, where rigidity gives way to a rich moduli theory.

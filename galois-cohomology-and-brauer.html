Galois cohomology and Brauer groups

Cocycles on a Galois group know about twisted forms, central simple algebras, and the obstructions to local-to-global lifting — one cohomological machine, six concrete payoffs.

Fix a finite Galois extension $L/K$ with $G=\Gal(L/K)$, or — for the absolute version — the absolute Galois group $G_K=\Gal(\bar K/K)$, regarded as a profinite group. A Galois module is an abelian group $M$ on which $G_K$ acts continuously. Galois cohomology $H^i(K,M):=H^i_{\mathrm{cts}}(G_K,M)$ is just continuous group cohomology of $G_K$ in this module — same bar resolution, same coboundary, only with continuity.

Three theorems organise the entire subject. Hilbert 90 kills $H^1$ of the multiplicative group of the algebraic closure. $H^2$ is the Brauer group — the pool of central simple algebras over $K$. And local Tate duality turns Galois cohomology over a $p$-adic field into a perfect pairing landing in $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$, the cohomological avatar of local class field theory. Six sections, six widgets.

1. Galois cocycles and $H^i(G,M)$

For a finite Galois extension $L/K$ and a $G$-module $M$, the inhomogeneous bar complex computes

$$H^i(G,M)\;=\;\frac{\{i\text{-cocycles}\,c:G^i\to M\}}{\{i\text{-coboundaries}\}}.$$

Degree zero gives fixed points: $H^0(G,M)=M^G$. A 1-cocycle is a crossed homomorphism $\varphi:G\to M$ satisfying $\varphi(gh)=\varphi(g)+g\cdot\varphi(h)$, modulo the principal ones $g\mapsto g\cdot m-m$.

The clean miracle of Galois cohomology is Hilbert's Theorem 90: for any finite Galois extension $L/K$,

$$H^1(\Gal(L/K),L^\times)\;=\;1.$$

So 1-cocycles to $L^\times$ are always coboundaries. Consequently, taking $L=\bar K$, we get $H^1(G_K,\bar K^\times)=0$ — and the next-degree cohomology, $H^2(G_K,\bar K^\times)$, becomes the most arithmetically interesting object on the page: the Brauer group.

2. $H^1$ classifies torsors and twisted forms

Let $A$ be a $G$-group (possibly non-abelian). A $G$-torsor under $A$ is a non-empty set $X$ with commuting actions of $G$ and $A$ such that $A$ acts simply transitively. The trivial torsor is $A$ acting on itself; a torsor is trivial iff it has a $G$-fixed point.

Picking a base point $x_0\in X$ (after passing to $\bar K$ if necessary) defines a 1-cocycle $\varphi:G\to A$ by $g\cdot x_0 = \varphi(g)\cdot x_0$. Changing the base point changes $\varphi$ by a coboundary. The result:

Galois descent. Isomorphism classes of $G$-torsors under $A$ are in bijection with the pointed cohomology set $H^1(G,A)$. The trivial class is the trivial torsor.

The deep payoff is the Galois descent dictionary. Suppose $X$ is a $K$-object (vector space, algebra, quadratic form, variety, …) and $X_{\bar K}=X\otimes_K\bar K$ is its base change to the algebraic closure. The set of $K$-objects $Y$ with $Y_{\bar K}\cong X_{\bar K}$ — the $\bar K/K$-twisted forms of $X$ — is identified with

$$H^1(G_K,\,\mathrm{Aut}(X_{\bar K})).$$

Each torsor under $\mu_2=\{\pm 1\}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ corresponds to a class in $H^1(\mathbb{Q},\mu_2)=\mathbb{Q}^\times/(\mathbb{Q}^\times)^2$ — equivalently, an étale double cover $\Spec\,\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt d)\to\Spec\,\mathbb{Q}$. Hilbert 90 reappears as a special case: $H^1(K,\mathrm{GL}_n(\bar K))=1$ — the only $\bar K/K$-twisted form of $K^n$ as a vector space is $K^n$ itself. But when we mod out by scalars to get $\mathrm{PGL}_n$, the $H^1$ explodes into the central simple $K$-algebras of degree $n$ — the Brauer group, in §4.

3. $H^2$ classifies central extensions

A central extension of $G$ by an abelian group $A$ is a short exact sequence

$$1\;\to\;A\;\to\;E\;\xrightarrow{\pi}\;G\;\to\;1$$

with $A\subseteq Z(E)$. Choose a set-theoretic section $s:G\to E$ with $s(1)=1$; the failure of $s$ to be a homomorphism is recorded by

$$s(g)\,s(h)\;=\;c(g,h)\cdot s(gh),\qquad c:G\times G\to A.$$

Associativity in $E$ forces $c$ to be a 2-cocycle; changing the section by $s\mapsto a\cdot s$ for $a:G\to A$ changes $c$ by the coboundary $da$.

Schreier's theorem. Equivalence classes of central extensions $1\to A\to E\to G\to 1$ are in bijection with $H^2(G,A)$. The split extension corresponds to the zero class.

The case $A=\mathbb{C}^\times$ is special: $H^2(G,\mathbb{C}^\times)=:M(G)$ is the Schur multiplier of $G$, and it controls projective representations. Every projective rep $\rho:G\to\mathrm{PGL}(V)$ lifts to a linear rep of a central extension by some element of $M(G)$; for finite simple $G$ this is the universal cover (the Schur cover).

4. The Brauer group $\mathrm{Br}(K)=H^2(K,\bar K^\times)$

A central simple $K$-algebra (CSA) is a finite-dimensional $K$-algebra $A$ whose centre is exactly $K$ and which has no two-sided ideals besides $0$ and $A$. By Wedderburn, every CSA is $A\cong M_n(D)$ for a unique central division algebra $D/K$. Two CSAs $A,B$ are Brauer-equivalent, $A\sim B$, iff $M_m(A)\cong M_n(B)$ for some $m,n$ — iff they have isomorphic underlying division algebras.

The set of equivalence classes forms a group under $\otimes_K$: this is the Brauer group $\mathrm{Br}(K)$, with identity the class of $K$ (i.e. $M_n(K)$ for any $n$) and inverse $[A]^{-1}=[A^{\mathrm{op}}]$.

Cohomological Brauer. There is a canonical isomorphism $$\mathrm{Br}(K)\;\cong\;H^2(\Gal(\bar K/K),\bar K^\times).$$ The map sends a CSA $A$ to the cocycle that records the twisting of $A\otimes_K\bar K\cong M_n(\bar K)$ as $G_K$ acts.

The most explicit families of Brauer classes are the cyclic algebras. Suppose $\mu_n\subset K$ and $a,b\in K^\times$. The cyclic algebra $(a,b)_n$ is the $K$-algebra generated by $x,y$ subject to

$$x^n=a,\qquad y^n=b,\qquad yx=\zeta_n\,xy,$$

where $\zeta_n$ is a primitive $n$th root of unity. It has dimension $n^2$ over $K$ and represents the cup product $\bar a\cup\bar b\in H^2(K,\mu_n\otimes\mu_n)\cong H^2(K,\mu_n)$ of $\bar a,\bar b\in K^\times/(K^\times)^n=H^1(K,\mu_n)$.

Over $\mathbb{R}$: $(a,b)_2 = \mathbb{H}$ (quaternions) iff $a,b<0$, else split. Over $\mathbb{Q}_p$ for odd $p$: the Hilbert symbol $(a,b)_p$ takes values in $\{0,1/2\}\subset\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$; reciprocity globalises to $\sum_v(a,b)_v=0$.

Some sample Brauer-group computations: $\mathrm{Br}(\bar K)=0$ (algebraically closed); $\mathrm{Br}(\mathbb{F}_q)=0$ (Wedderburn — no finite division ring beyond a field); $\mathrm{Br}(\mathbb{R})=\mathbb{Z}/2$ (matrix algebras and quaternions $\mathbb{H}$); $\mathrm{Br}(\mathbb{Q}_p)=\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ (the local invariant of CFT); for a number field $K$, the Hasse–Brauer–Noether sequence

$$0\to\mathrm{Br}(K)\to\bigoplus_v\mathrm{Br}(K_v)\xrightarrow{\,\sum\mathrm{inv}_v\,}\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}\to 0$$

says: a Brauer class is determined by its local invariants, which sum to zero — reciprocity in cohomological clothing.

5. Cup product and local Tate duality

The cup product on group cohomology is a graded-commutative product

$$H^p(G,M)\;\otimes\;H^q(G,N)\;\xrightarrow{\,\cup\,}\;H^{p+q}(G,M\otimes N).$$

Concretely, on cocycles: $(\alpha\cup\beta)(g_1,\dots,g_{p+q}) = \alpha(g_1,\dots,g_p)\otimes(g_1\cdots g_p)\cdot\beta(g_{p+1},\dots,g_{p+q})$. It is graded-commutative on cohomology and natural in both modules.

For a non-archimedean local field $K$ (e.g. $K=\mathbb{Q}_p$) and a finite Galois module $M$, the dual module is $M^*=\Hom(M,\bar K^\times)$. Cup product followed by the local invariant gives

$$H^i(K,M)\;\otimes\;H^{2-i}(K,M^*)\;\xrightarrow{\,\cup\,}\;H^2(K,\bar K^\times)\;\xrightarrow{\,\mathrm{inv}\,}\;\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}.$$
Local Tate duality. For $K$ a non-archimedean local field, $M$ finite of order prime to $\mathrm{char}(K)$, and $0\le i\le 2$: the cup product pairing $$H^i(K,M)\;\times\;H^{2-i}(K,M^*)\;\to\;\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$$ is a perfect pairing of finite groups.

This is the cohomological backbone of local class field theory: taking $M=\mathbb{Z}/n$, $M^*=\mu_n$ produces the perfect pairing $K^\times/(K^\times)^n\times K^\times/(K^\times)^n\to\mu_n\subset\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ — the Hilbert symbol.

6. Hasse principle, Brauer–Manin, Selmer

The Hasse principle for a class $\mathcal{C}$ of $K$-varieties says: $X\in\mathcal{C}$ has a $K$-point iff it has a $K_v$-point for every place $v$. It holds for quadratic forms in $\geq 3$ variables (Hasse–Minkowski) and for Severi–Brauer varieties (CSAs split locally everywhere $\Rightarrow$ split globally — Albert–Brauer–Hasse–Noether). It fails for cubic surfaces and many higher-degree varieties.

The Brauer–Manin obstruction explains many failures. For a smooth proper $K$-variety $X$, every Brauer class $\alpha\in\mathrm{Br}(X)$ pairs against an adelic point $(P_v)\in X(\mathbb{A}_K)$ via

$$\langle\alpha,(P_v)\rangle\;=\;\sum_v\mathrm{inv}_v\,\alpha(P_v)\;\in\;\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}.$$

Reciprocity forces the sum to vanish on global points $X(K)\subseteq X(\mathbb{A}_K)^{\mathrm{Br}}$. Failure of equality is the obstruction.

Descent on elliptic curves. For $E/K$ an elliptic curve and $n\geq 2$, the Kummer sequence $0\to E[n]\to E\xrightarrow{n}E\to 0$ produces the connecting map $\delta:E(K)/nE(K)\hookrightarrow H^1(K,E[n])$. The $n$-Selmer group is the local-global kernel

$$\mathrm{Sel}_n(E/K)\;=\;\ker\!\left(H^1(K,E[n])\;\to\;\prod_v H^1(K_v,E)\right),$$

and it sits in the exact sequence

$$0\;\to\;E(K)/nE(K)\;\to\;\mathrm{Sel}_n(E/K)\;\to\;\mathrm{III}(E/K)[n]\;\to\;0,$$

where $\mathrm{III}(E/K)$ is the Tate–Shafarevich group. Selmer is finite and computable; the rank $\mathrm{rk}(E(K))$ is determined by Selmer modulo $\mathrm{III}[n]$ — which is why $\mathrm{III}$ is the only thing standing between cohomology and the rank, the heart of BSD.

Variational methods

Stationarity is the universal pose: every classical equation of motion, every minimal surface, every gauge field is the critical point of an energy. The calculus of variations turns "minimise" into a differential equation (Euler–Lagrange), then into an existence theorem (the direct method on a reflexive Banach space), then into the saddle-point yoga of mountain-pass and Morse theory.

1. Functional derivative

A functional $J\colon X\to\mathbb{R}$ on a Banach space $X$ assigns a scalar to each function. The classic example is the Dirichlet energy

$$J(u) \;=\; \tfrac12\int_\Omega |\nabla u|^2\,dx \qquad (u\in H^1_0(\Omega)).$$

To minimise $J$ we need a notion of "derivative". Two upgrades of the directional derivative live in infinite dimensions.

Gateaux: directional derivative

Fix $u\in X$ and $h\in X$. The Gateaux derivative in direction $h$ is

$$\langle J'(u), h\rangle \;=\; \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\frac{J(u+\varepsilon h)-J(u)}{\varepsilon}.$$

If this limit exists and is linear and continuous in $h$, we call $J'(u)\in X^*$ the Gateaux differential. Concretely for the Dirichlet energy,

$$\langle J'(u), h\rangle \;=\; \int_\Omega \nabla u\cdot \nabla h\,dx,$$

obtained by expanding $|\nabla(u+\varepsilon h)|^2 = |\nabla u|^2 + 2\varepsilon\nabla u\cdot\nabla h + \varepsilon^2|\nabla h|^2$ and reading off the linear coefficient.

Fréchet: uniform-in-direction

$J$ is Fréchet differentiable at $u$ if there exists a bounded linear $J'(u)\in X^*$ with

$$J(u+h) \;=\; J(u) + \langle J'(u),h\rangle + o(\|h\|), \qquad \|h\|\to 0.$$

The error term is uniform across all directions. Fréchet implies Gateaux; the converse fails — even on $\mathbb{R}^2$ the function with sharp ridges along radial lines can have all directional derivatives but no Fréchet differential. For the smooth functionals of physics the two coincide.

In physics literature $\delta J/\delta u(x)$ denotes the density: the function for which $\langle J'(u),h\rangle = \int (\delta J/\delta u)\,h\,dx$. For the Dirichlet energy this density is $-\Delta u$ (after one integration by parts).

2. Euler–Lagrange equations

If $u_*$ minimises $J(u)=\int_a^b L(x,u,u')\,dx$ over functions with $u(a)=\alpha$, $u(b)=\beta$, then $\langle J'(u_*),h\rangle = 0$ for every test variation $h$ vanishing at the endpoints. Compute:

$$\langle J'(u),h\rangle \;=\; \int_a^b \big[\partial_u L(x,u,u')\,h + \partial_{u'}L(x,u,u')\,h'\big]\,dx.$$

Integrating the second term by parts (boundary contributions vanish because $h(a)=h(b)=0$) gives

$$\langle J'(u),h\rangle \;=\; \int_a^b \Big[\partial_u L - \tfrac{d}{dx}\partial_{u'}L\Big]\,h\,dx.$$

By the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations, vanishing for all admissible $h$ forces the bracket to be zero pointwise:

$$\boxed{\;\frac{d}{dx}\,\partial_{u'}L(x,u,u') \;=\; \partial_u L(x,u,u').\;}$$

This is the Euler–Lagrange equation — the necessary condition for a classical extremum.

The brachistochrone

Bernoulli's 1696 challenge: find the curve $y(x)$ from $(0,0)$ to $(x_1,y_1)$ down which a bead slides under gravity in least time. With $v=\sqrt{2gy}$ from energy conservation,

$$T(y) \;=\; \int_0^{x_1}\!\! \frac{\sqrt{1+(y')^2}}{\sqrt{2gy}}\,dx.$$

The Lagrangian $L=\sqrt{1+(y')^2}/\sqrt{2gy}$ has no explicit $x$-dependence, so the Beltrami identity $L - y'\partial_{y'}L = C$ holds. Solving gives the cycloid

$$x(\theta) \;=\; r(\theta - \sin\theta), \qquad y(\theta) \;=\; r(1-\cos\theta),$$

traced by a point on a wheel of radius $r$ rolling on a horizontal line.

3. Direct method (Tonelli)

Euler–Lagrange is a necessary condition: every smooth minimiser solves it, but a solution to the EL equation might be a saddle, a local maximum, or non-existent. The direct method proves existence of a minimiser by sequential compactness, three ingredients:

  1. Coercivity: $J(u_k)\to\infty$ whenever $\|u_k\|\to\infty$. Equivalently, sublevel sets $\{J\le c\}$ are bounded.
  2. Reflexivity: $X$ is a reflexive Banach space, so by Banach–Alaoglu every bounded sequence has a weakly convergent subsequence $u_{k_j}\rightharpoonup u_*$.
  3. Weak lower-semicontinuity: $u_k\rightharpoonup u\Rightarrow J(u)\le\liminf_{k\to\infty}J(u_k)$.

The argument: take a minimising sequence $u_k$ with $J(u_k)\to\inf J$. Coercivity bounds it; reflexivity extracts $u_{k_j}\rightharpoonup u_*$; lsc gives

$$J(u_*) \;\le\; \liminf_j J(u_{k_j}) \;=\; \inf J,$$

so $u_*$ attains the infimum. Convexity of $J$ in the gradient is the standard sufficient condition for weak lsc on $W^{1,p}$ — the canonical setting where this works.

Why convexity gives lsc

For $J(u)=\int F(\nabla u)\,dx$ with $F$ convex, write $F$ as the supremum of its affine minorants: $F(\xi) = \sup_\ell\,\ell(\xi)$ where $\ell$ are continuous linear functionals. Each $\int\ell(\nabla u)\,dx$ is weakly continuous (linear), and the supremum of weakly continuous functionals is weakly lsc. The classical example is the Dirichlet integral $F(\xi)=|\xi|^2$ — convex, hence weakly lsc on $H^1$.

4. Mountain-pass theorem

Not every critical point is a minimum. The mountain-pass theorem of Ambrosetti–Rabinowitz (1973) detects saddle critical points by topology of sublevel sets.

Mountain-pass theorem. Let $X$ be a Banach space and $J\in C^1(X,\mathbb{R})$ satisfying:
  • $(MP_1)$ $J(0) = 0$ and there exist $\rho,\alpha>0$ with $J(u)\ge\alpha$ for all $\|u\|=\rho$.
  • $(MP_2)$ There exists $u_*\in X$ with $\|u_*\|>\rho$ and $J(u_*)\le 0$.
  • $(PS)$ Palais–Smale: every sequence with $J(u_k)$ bounded and $J'(u_k)\to 0$ has a strongly convergent subsequence.
Then $c = \inf_{\gamma\in\Gamma}\max_{t\in[0,1]} J(\gamma(t))$ is a critical value, where $\Gamma$ is the space of continuous paths from $0$ to $u_*$.

The picture

$J$ has a "valley" at the origin (a local min) surrounded by a ring of altitude $\ge \alpha$ — the mountain ridge. There's a far point $u_*$ at sea level. Any path from $0$ to $u_*$ must climb over the ridge. The lowest such ridge-height is a saddle critical value: there's a critical point at altitude $c$ where two valleys meet through a pass.

Why the Palais–Smale condition? It plays the role of compactness for non-coercive functionals. The deformation lemma normally produces a minimising sequence $u_k$ with $J(u_k)\to c$, $J'(u_k)\to 0$; without (PS), this sequence might escape to infinity instead of converging to a critical point.

Application: nonlinear elliptic equations

For the semilinear problem $-\Delta u = |u|^{p-2}u$ on a bounded domain (with $p<2^*$ subcritical), the energy

$$J(u) \;=\; \tfrac12\int|\nabla u|^2 - \tfrac1p\int|u|^p$$

has a local min at $0$ and a far point $u_*=\lambda v$ for $\lambda$ large where $J(u_*)<0$. Mountain-pass produces a non-trivial weak solution. The semilinear theory of bound states is built on this template.

5. Isoperimetric inequality

Among planar regions of fixed perimeter $L$, which encloses the largest area? The Greeks knew the answer empirically — the disk — but the rigorous proof took until the 19th century.

$$\boxed{\;4\pi A \;\le\; L^2\;}$$

with equality iff the region is a disk. Equivalently, fixing $L$ gives $A\le L^2/(4\pi)$, fixing $A$ gives $L\ge 2\sqrt{\pi A}$.

Calibration: the variational proof

Setting up the constrained problem: maximise $A(\gamma) = \tfrac12\oint(x\,dy - y\,dx)$ subject to $L(\gamma) = \oint|d\gamma| = L_0$. The Lagrangian is

$$J_\lambda(\gamma) \;=\; A(\gamma) - \lambda\,L(\gamma).$$

Computing the Euler–Lagrange equation along normal variations $h$:

$$\delta A = \int h\,ds, \qquad \delta L = \int \kappa\,h\,ds$$

where $\kappa$ is geodesic curvature. Stationarity for all $h$ forces $\kappa\equiv\lambda$ — constant curvature. The only closed planar curves of constant curvature are circles, and the multiplier $\lambda = 1/r$ ties radius to perimeter via $L=2\pi r$.

The Bonnesen refinement

A stronger inequality due to Bonnesen quantifies how far the region is from a disk:

$$L^2 - 4\pi A \;\ge\; \pi^2(R - r)^2$$

where $R$ is the circumradius and $r$ the inradius of the convex hull. Equality forces $R=r$ (the region is a disk).

Plateau's problem: the soap-film equation

Given a closed curve $\Gamma\subset\mathbb{R}^3$, find a surface of least area spanning it. For surfaces given as graphs $z = u(x,y)$ over a domain $\Omega$ with $u|_{\partial\Omega}$ prescribed,

$$\mathrm{Area}(u) \;=\; \int_\Omega \sqrt{1+|\nabla u|^2}\,dx\,dy.$$

The Euler–Lagrange equation is

$$\nabla\cdot \frac{\nabla u}{\sqrt{1+|\nabla u|^2}} \;=\; 0,$$

the minimal-surface equation. Solutions include the helicoid, catenoid, Enneper's surface, Costa's surface (1982). Existence in full generality (Plateau, 1931; Douglas, 1939) earned the first Fields Medal.

Yang–Mills: gauge theory as variational

For a principal $G$-bundle $P\to M$ with connection $A$ and curvature $F_A = dA + A\wedge A$, the Yang–Mills functional

$$YM(A) \;=\; \int_M |F_A|^2\,d\mathrm{vol}$$

has critical points satisfying $d_A^* F_A = 0$. The Bianchi identity $d_A F_A = 0$ holds automatically. In four dimensions, self-dual connections $F_A = *F_A$ are absolute minima — Donaldson's theory of smooth $4$-manifolds is built on the moduli space of these instantons.

Connections

Variational methods sit at the centre of analysis: they convert questions about the existence of solutions to PDEs into questions about the geometry of energy landscapes on infinite-dimensional spaces. Modern frontiers include the Allen–Cahn / Ginzburg–Landau passage from energies to mean-curvature flow, Almgren–Pitts min-max for minimal surfaces in arbitrary dimension, concentration-compactness at critical Sobolev exponents, and the calculus of variations on metric-measure spaces.

  • Sobolev spaces & distributions — variational Poisson — the linear template before nonlinearity is added.
  • Partial differential equations — Lax–Milgram & regularity — variational solutions promoted to classical ones via elliptic regularity.
  • Functional analysis — weak topology & Banach–Alaoglu — the compactness engine behind the direct method.
  • Riemannian geometry — geodesics, harmonic maps, the second variation and Jacobi fields.
  • Morse theory — saddle critical points organise into a chain complex; mountain-pass is the index-1 case.

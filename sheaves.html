Sheaves — local data, coherent globally

A bookkeeping device for "what can be said locally, and how local pieces glue." From the constant presheaf's pathology to the equivalence between $R$-modules and quasi-coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$.

1. Why sheaves?

Many natural assignments are local: continuous functions, smooth functions, holomorphic functions, sections of a vector bundle, regular functions on a variety. Each open set $U \subseteq X$ carries a set $F(U)$ of "things defined on $U$," and there are restriction maps $F(U) \to F(V)$ for $V \subseteq U$ that throw away information outside $V$.

The basic question is the local-to-global principle: given an open cover $\{U_i\}$ of $U$ and a section $s_i \in F(U_i)$ for each $i$, when do these patches assemble into a single $s \in F(U)$? Two things can fail. First, the $s_i$ might disagree on overlaps $U_i \cap U_j$ — incompatibility. Second, even if they agree everywhere they overlap, there might be no global section restricting to all of them — a non-trivial cohomological obstruction, or a defect in the assignment $F$ itself.

A sheaf is an assignment $F$ for which the first kind of failure is the only obstruction on any single cover: compatible pieces always glue, and they glue uniquely. The existence of global sections satisfying additional constraints — holomorphic logarithms, meromorphic functions with prescribed poles, sections of a line bundle with prescribed zeros — is a finer question, the content of sheaf cohomology. Sheaves are the right setting for differential geometry on manifolds, complex analysis on Riemann surfaces, and algebraic geometry on schemes — anywhere "local data" is a coherent notion.

The historical motivation came from Leray's work on partial differential equations during World War II; Cartan and Serre then showed that sheaves cleanly express the relationship between holomorphic functions on Stein manifolds and the algebraic structure of their function rings (Theorems A and B). Grothendieck reorganized the entire subject by treating $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules as the basic objects of algebraic geometry, with quasi-coherent and coherent subcategories playing the role of linear-algebraic data tied to the scheme.

A few slogans to keep in mind as the machinery accumulates:

  • "A sheaf is a presheaf that believes the equalizer diagram of §3 for every open cover." This is the economical, category-theoretic statement that makes everything else fall out.
  • "A sheaf is its stalks, plus the rule for which compatible germ-families are realized by actual sections." Sheafification (§7) is precisely enforcing this rule.
  • "For a locally ringed space $(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$, linear-algebra-over-$X$ means $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules." Quasi-coherent sheaves on a scheme are the linear-algebra flavor that is honestly algebraic.

This page assumes you are comfortable with categories, rings, modules, and topological spaces. We pass quickly to the universal-property and adjoint-functor formulations, and end with the equivalence $R\text{-mod} \simeq \mathsf{QCoh}(\mathrm{Spec}\,R)$, which is the first concrete payoff for algebraic geometry.

A slightly deeper reason to care

The passage from presheaves to sheaves to $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules to quasi-coherent sheaves is a sequence of progressively-more-algebraic restrictions. At each stage, the "right" notion of morphism and the "right" notion of exactness are fully determined by the algebra, not by extrinsic topological choices. This is why algebraic geometry can be a fully algebraic subject despite reasoning "geometrically" — every geometric picture is underwritten by a sheaf-theoretic or module-theoretic translation.

Concretely: if you know how to do commutative algebra — ideals, localization, tensor products, Ext and Tor, flatness, Nakayama's lemma — then you can do algebraic geometry on affine schemes by following the dictionary of §9. The sheaf-theoretic machinery is what lets you paste local affine calculations into global statements on a non-affine scheme, with the gluing axiom guaranteeing that the pieces fit.

2. Presheaves

Let $X$ be a topological space, and let $\mathrm{Open}(X)$ be the poset of open sets ordered by inclusion, viewed as a category: there is exactly one morphism $V \to U$ when $V \subseteq U$.

Definition. A presheaf of sets on $X$ is a contravariant functor $F\colon \mathrm{Open}(X)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}$. Concretely: for each open $U$ a set $F(U)$, and for each inclusion $V \subseteq U$ a restriction map $\rho^U_V\colon F(U) \to F(V)$, satisfying $\rho^U_U = \mathrm{id}$ and $\rho^V_W \circ \rho^U_V = \rho^U_W$ when $W \subseteq V \subseteq U$.

The same definition with $\mathsf{Set}$ replaced by $\mathsf{Ab}$, $\mathsf{Ring}$, $R\text{-Mod}$, etc., gives presheaves valued in those categories. Elements of $F(U)$ are called sections of $F$ over $U$; we often write $\Gamma(U,F)$ for $F(U)$ and $s|_V$ for $\rho^U_V(s)$. Sections over $X$ itself are global sections.

Examples

  • Continuous functions. $C^0_X(U) = \{\,f\colon U\to\mathbb{R}\text{ continuous}\,\}$, with restriction the literal restriction of functions. This is in fact a sheaf of $\mathbb{R}$-algebras.
  • Holomorphic functions. On a Riemann surface $X$, $\mathcal{O}_X(U) = \{\,f\colon U\to\mathbb{C}\text{ holomorphic}\,\}$. Again a sheaf.
  • Bounded continuous functions. $B(U) = \{\,f\colon U\to\mathbb{R}\text{ continuous and bounded}\,\}$, restriction by literal restriction. This is a presheaf but not a sheaf: a function bounded on each piece of an open cover of $\mathbb{R}$ need not be bounded globally.
  • The constant presheaf. Fix a set $S$. Define $\underline{S}^{\mathrm{pre}}(U) = S$ for every nonempty $U$, with all restriction maps the identity (and $\underline{S}^{\mathrm{pre}}(\emptyset)$ a one-element set, by convention). This is a presheaf, but it fails the gluing axiom whenever $X$ is disconnected: on $X = U_1 \sqcup U_2$ a section $a \in S = \underline{S}^{\mathrm{pre}}(U_1)$ and a different $b \in S = \underline{S}^{\mathrm{pre}}(U_2)$ are vacuously compatible — they overlap on $\emptyset$ — but they cannot be glued unless $a=b$. We will fix this in §7 by sheafifying.
  • Sections of a map. Given a continuous $\pi\colon E \to X$, the assignment $U \mapsto \{\,s\colon U \to E : \pi \circ s = \mathrm{id}_U\,\}$ is a sheaf of sets. For $E \to X$ a fiber bundle this is the sheaf of local sections, the starting point for much of obstruction theory.
  • Cohomological presheaves. For a nice space $X$ and integer $n$, $U \mapsto H^n(U; \mathbb{Z})$ is a presheaf (restriction is pullback on cohomology). It is generally not a sheaf; its sheafification is the constant sheaf $0$ for $n > 0$ when $X$ is locally contractible, but what it computes on arbitrary opens — cohomology — is a genuinely global invariant. This example is why "cohomology with coefficients in a sheaf" requires first sheafifying and then deriving, not the other way around.

The functoriality is worth pausing on. A morphism of topological spaces $f\colon X \to Y$ induces no canonical restriction direction on presheaves in general — pushforward $f_*$ goes the same way as $f$, inverse image $f^{-1}$ goes the opposite way. Restriction along $V \subseteq U$ is the special case of pullback along the open inclusion, and the contravariance is baked in.

The collection of presheaves of sets on $X$ forms a category $\mathsf{PSh}(X) = \mathsf{Fun}(\mathrm{Open}(X)^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathsf{Set})$, with morphisms the natural transformations: a morphism $\varphi\colon F \to G$ is a family $\varphi_U\colon F(U) \to G(U)$ commuting with restrictions. Limits and colimits in $\mathsf{PSh}(X)$ are computed pointwise (object-by-object on each open), so it inherits all the formal properties of the target category.

Restriction and functoriality

For $V \subseteq U$, the restriction $\rho^U_V$ is part of the functor structure; you should think of it as a "forget what you knew about $U \setminus V$" map. Two immediate consequences:

  • Restriction to a subspace. For $i\colon Y \hookrightarrow X$ a subspace inclusion, there is a pullback $i^{-1}F$ on $Y$ defined as the sheafification of the presheaf $V \mapsto \mathrm{colim}_{V \subseteq W} F(W)$ over opens $W$ of $X$ meeting $Y$ in $V$. For $Y$ open, $i^{-1}F = F|_Y$ is literally the restriction. For $Y$ closed, $i^{-1}F$ is often called the "pullback to the closed subset" and gives rise to the fiber of $F$ at a point when $Y$ is a single point.
  • The fiber at a point. Be careful — "fiber" and "stalk" are different. The stalk $F_x = \varinjlim_{x \in U} F(U)$ is a colimit over neighborhoods; the fiber $F(x) = F_x \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_{X,x}} \kappa(x)$ (for $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules $F$ with $\kappa(x) = \mathcal{O}_{X,x}/\mathfrak{m}_x$) is the stalk modulo the maximal ideal. On a scheme, the fiber at $\mathfrak{p}$ of a coherent sheaf $\widetilde{M}$ is $M \otimes_R \kappa(\mathfrak{p})$ — the vector space you think of as "$M$ evaluated at the point $\mathfrak{p}$."

Morphisms of presheaves

A morphism $\varphi\colon F \to G$ of presheaves is a natural transformation: a family $\varphi_U\colon F(U) \to G(U)$ such that for $V \subseteq U$, the square $$\begin{array}{ccc} F(U) & \xrightarrow{\varphi_U} & G(U) \\ \downarrow_{\rho^F} & & \downarrow_{\rho^G} \\ F(V) & \xrightarrow{\varphi_V} & G(V) \end{array}$$ commutes. The restrictions $\rho^F, \rho^G$ are the presheaf restrictions. For presheaves of abelian groups the $\varphi_U$ are group homomorphisms; for sheaves of rings, ring homomorphisms; and so on.

The site perspective (Grothendieck topologies)

Nothing in the definition required $\mathrm{Open}(X)$ to come from a topological space. Replacing it by an arbitrary small category $\mathcal{C}$ equipped with a notion of "covering family" (a Grothendieck topology) gives the more general notion of a sheaf on a site. In algebraic geometry the étale, fppf, and fpqc topologies on the category of schemes — finer than the Zariski topology — are essential for descent and for moduli problems. For this page we stick to the Zariski-style topology of an honest topological space, but the proofs we give translate verbatim.

The functor of points, in passing

The functor-of-points perspective inverts the picture: a scheme $X$ is determined by its functor $h_X = \mathrm{Hom}(-, X)\colon \mathsf{Sch}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}$. A natural way to package geometric data on $X$ is then as a sheaf on the site of $X$-schemes (or on a small subcategory thereof). Sheaves on $\mathrm{Open}(X)$ are the special case of taking $\mathcal{C} = \mathrm{Open}(X)$ with covers = open covers.

3. Sheaves: the gluing axioms

A presheaf is a sheaf when local data uniquely determines global data, in the following precise sense.

Definition. A presheaf $F$ on $X$ is a sheaf if for every open $U \subseteq X$ and every open cover $U = \bigcup_{i \in I} U_i$:
  1. (Locality / separation.) If $s, t \in F(U)$ satisfy $s|_{U_i} = t|_{U_i}$ for every $i$, then $s = t$.
  2. (Gluing.) If sections $s_i \in F(U_i)$ are pairwise compatible — $s_i|_{U_i \cap U_j} = s_j|_{U_i \cap U_j}$ for all $i, j$ — then there exists $s \in F(U)$ with $s|_{U_i} = s_i$ for every $i$.
By (1), the section $s$ produced in (2) is unique.

A compact way to phrase both axioms is the equalizer diagram: $F$ is a sheaf iff for every cover $\{U_i\}$ of $U$ the diagram $$F(U) \;\longrightarrow\; \prod_{i} F(U_i) \;\rightrightarrows\; \prod_{i,j} F(U_i \cap U_j)$$ is an equalizer in $\mathsf{Set}$. The two parallel arrows send $(s_i)_i$ to $(s_i|_{U_i \cap U_j})_{i,j}$ and $(s_j|_{U_i \cap U_j})_{i,j}$ respectively. The first map being injective is locality; its image being precisely the equalizer of the parallel pair is gluing.

One can push the diagram further into a cosimplicial-style object: $$F(U) \;\longrightarrow\; \prod_{i} F(U_i) \;\rightrightarrows\; \prod_{i_0, i_1} F(U_{i_0 i_1}) \;\xrightarrow{\ \ \ }\;\;\cdots \;\prod_{i_0, \dots, i_n} F(U_{i_0 \cdots i_n}) \;\xrightarrow{\ \ \ }\; \cdots$$ where $U_{i_0 \cdots i_n} := U_{i_0} \cap \cdots \cap U_{i_n}$. For a sheaf, the map from $F(U)$ is the equalizer of the first two arrows; taking the total cohomology of this complex (for sheaves of abelian groups) yields the Čech cohomology of $F$ with respect to the cover. When the cover is "good enough" (e.g., intersections acyclic for the sheaf), Čech cohomology computes sheaf cohomology.

The equalizer formulation makes it obvious how to lift the definition to other categories: $F\colon \mathrm{Open}(X)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathcal{C}$ is a sheaf iff the same diagram is an equalizer in $\mathcal{C}$. For $\mathcal{C} = \mathsf{Ab}$, equalizers exist and are computed as in $\mathsf{Set}$, so a sheaf of abelian groups is just a sheaf of sets whose sets carry compatible group operations.

Two examples to internalize

Continuous functions are a sheaf. Locality is trivial — two continuous functions on $U$ that agree on every member of an open cover agree pointwise, hence are equal. For gluing, given pairwise-compatible $f_i\colon U_i \to \mathbb{R}$, define $f\colon U \to \mathbb{R}$ by $f(x) = f_i(x)$ for any $i$ with $x \in U_i$; compatibility makes this well-defined, and continuity is local on the source.

The constant presheaf is not. On $X = \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$, take the open cover $\{(-\infty,0), (0,\infty)\}$ and let $S = \{0,1\}$. The pair $(0,1)$ on the two opens is compatible (their overlap is $\emptyset$, so the compatibility condition is vacuous), but no global section in $\underline{S}^{\mathrm{pre}}(X) = \{0,1\}$ restricts to both. Gluing fails.

Bounded continuous functions are not, for a different reason. $B(U)$ from §2 satisfies locality (functions are still pointwise determined), but gluing fails: take $X = \mathbb{R}$, the cover $\{U_n = (-n, n)\}_{n \ge 1}$, and the section $f_n(x) = x|_{U_n}$ on each piece. These are pairwise compatible and each is bounded, but the only candidate global section $f(x) = x$ is unbounded, so it does not lie in $B(\mathbb{R})$. Sheafifying $B$ recovers $C^0_X$ — boundedness is genuinely a non-local condition that cannot survive sheafification.

A presheaf failing locality. The constant presheaf is "too small" globally; here is a presheaf that is "too big" on small opens. Define $F(U) = \pi_0(U) \times \mathbb{Z}$ (assignments of integers to connected components of $U$, with no compatibility under restriction). Restriction $F(U) \to F(V)$ for $V \subseteq U$ is well-defined only if every component of $V$ lies in a component of $U$ — which is forced by inclusion — but the assignment fails locality: a section over $U$ that restricts to the same data on every member of an open refinement of the connected-component decomposition need not equal the original. The fix is to redefine $F$ by demanding sections be compatible with refinements; this is what locally-constant functions naturally do.

Operational consequences of the sheaf axiom

Several formal facts make sheaves much easier to compute with than presheaves.

  • Sheafiness is checkable on a basis. If $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for the topology of $X$ and $F$ is a presheaf on $\mathcal{B}$ (i.e., defined and restriction-respecting on basis opens) satisfying the equalizer condition for covers of basis opens by basis opens, then $F$ extends uniquely to a sheaf on $X$. This is how the structure sheaf of $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ is defined: specify on distinguished opens $D(f)$, check the sheaf axiom there, extend.
  • Sections over a directed union. If $U = \bigcup_{n} U_n$ for an increasing chain $U_1 \subseteq U_2 \subseteq \cdots$ and $F$ is a sheaf, then in many contexts (when $\mathcal{C}$ has filtered colimits agreeing with limits along the relevant diagrams) $F(U)$ is the inverse limit $\varprojlim_n F(U_n)$. This is the "topological" version of the equalizer condition.
  • Gluing of sheaves. Sheaves on $X$ themselves satisfy a gluing principle: given an open cover $\{U_i\}$, sheaves $F_i$ on $U_i$, and isomorphisms $\varphi_{ij}\colon F_i|_{U_i \cap U_j} \xrightarrow{\sim} F_j|_{U_i \cap U_j}$ satisfying the cocycle condition $\varphi_{jk} \circ \varphi_{ij} = \varphi_{ik}$ on triple overlaps, there is a (unique up to canonical isomorphism) sheaf $F$ on $U = \bigcup U_i$ restricting to each $F_i$ with the prescribed transition data. This is descent for sheaves.
  • $F(\emptyset)$ is the terminal object. The empty cover of the empty open set forces $F(\emptyset)$ to be the equalizer of the empty diagram, i.e. the terminal object of the target category. So $F(\emptyset) = \{\ast\}$ for sheaves of sets, $\{0\}$ for sheaves of abelian groups, the zero ring for sheaves of rings, etc.

4. Key examples

The examples below are the working vocabulary you will use in algebraic geometry, complex analysis, and topology. The contrasts among them sharpen what "sheaf" means.

Categorical preliminaries

Before working through specific sheaves, two general facts are worth recording.

  • $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ is complete and cocomplete. Limits in $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ are computed pointwise: $(\varprojlim_i F_i)(U) = \varprojlim_i F_i(U)$. Limits of sheaves are again sheaves automatically (limits commute with limits). Colimits, by contrast, are computed by first taking the pointwise colimit (which is a presheaf) and then sheafifying — colimits in general do not commute with the equalizer-type limit defining sheaves.
  • $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$ has enough injectives. A standard construction: embed each stalk $F_x$ into an injective $\mathbb{Z}$-module $I_x$, and form the sheaf $\prod_x i_{x*}I_x$. This is injective in $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$, and the natural map $F \to \prod_x i_{x*}I_x$ is mono. Hence sheaf cohomology — the right-derived functors of $\Gamma(X, -)$ — exists.

Sheaves of functions

On any topological space $X$, the assignment $C^0_X(U) = \{\,f\colon U \to \mathbb{R}\text{ continuous}\,\}$ is a sheaf of $\mathbb{R}$-algebras. On a smooth manifold, $C^\infty_X(U)$ is a sheaf. On a complex manifold, the holomorphic functions $\mathcal{O}_X(U)$ form a sheaf of $\mathbb{C}$-algebras. In each case locality is trivial (functions are determined pointwise) and gluing is "define pointwise, check the regularity condition is local on the domain."

Constant presheaf vs. constant sheaf

For a set $S$, the constant presheaf $\underline{S}^{\mathrm{pre}}$ assigns $S$ to every nonempty open. Its sheafification is the constant sheaf $\underline{S}$: explicitly, $\underline{S}(U)$ is the set of locally-constant functions $U \to S$, equivalently the set of functions $U \to S$ that are continuous when $S$ has the discrete topology. This distinction is the cleanest illustration that "presheaves" and "sheaves" are genuinely different.

For example, on $X = S^1$ (the circle, connected), $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}(X) = \mathbb{Z}$ — same as the presheaf. On $X = S^0$ (two points), $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}(X) = \mathbb{Z}^2$, while the presheaf still gives $\mathbb{Z}$. The constant sheaf "remembers" connected components.

Skyscraper sheaf

Fix a point $x \in X$ and a set (or abelian group, ring, ...) $A$. The skyscraper sheaf $i_{x*}A$ is defined by $$(i_{x*}A)(U) = \begin{cases} A & x \in U, \\ \{\ast\} & x \notin U. \end{cases}$$ Restriction maps are $\mathrm{id}_A$ when both opens contain $x$, and the unique map to $\{\ast\}$ otherwise. Locality and gluing are easy to verify: the value at $x$ propagates uniquely to any open containing $x$, and is irrelevant elsewhere.

The notation $i_{x*}$ is suggestive: it is the pushforward along the inclusion $i_x\colon \{x\} \hookrightarrow X$ of the constant sheaf with value $A$ on the one-point space.

Direct image (pushforward)

Let $f\colon X \to Y$ be a continuous map and $F$ a sheaf on $X$. Define a presheaf on $Y$ by $f_*F(V) = F(f^{-1}(V))$. This is automatically a sheaf — verify the equalizer diagram for a cover of $V$ by pulling back to a cover of $f^{-1}(V)$. Pushforward is a functor $f_*\colon \mathsf{Sh}(X) \to \mathsf{Sh}(Y)$ that preserves limits but generally not colimits; it is right adjoint to the inverse image $f^{-1}\colon \mathsf{Sh}(Y) \to \mathsf{Sh}(X)$, defined as the sheafification of the presheaf $V \mapsto \varinjlim_{f(V) \subseteq W} G(W)$. The skyscraper sheaf at $x \in X$ is exactly $i_{x*}A$ for $i_x\colon \{x\} \hookrightarrow X$ the inclusion, and the constant sheaf $\underline{S}$ is $p^{-1}S$ for $p\colon X \to \mathrm{pt}$ and $S$ viewed as a sheaf on the one-point space.

Sheaves from open subsets

For an open inclusion $j\colon U \hookrightarrow X$ there is a third operation $j_!$, extension by zero: for a sheaf $F$ on $U$, the presheaf $V \mapsto F(V)$ if $V \subseteq U$ and $\{0\}$ otherwise is not in general a sheaf; sheafification yields $j_!F$. It is left adjoint to $j^{-1}$, and satisfies: stalks $(j_!F)_x = F_x$ if $x \in U$ and $0$ otherwise. The triple $(j_!, j^{-1}, j_*)$ for open $j$ is the simplest example of the six-functor formalism in sheaf theory.

The "sections with support in" functor

Given a closed subset $Z \subseteq X$ with complement open inclusion $j\colon U \hookrightarrow X$, the functor $\Gamma_Z(U, F) = \ker(F(U) \to F(U \setminus Z))$ (sections vanishing outside $Z$) sheafifies to $\underline{\Gamma}_Z F$. Its derived functor is local cohomology $H^i_Z(X, F)$, the sheaf-theoretic shadow of Grothendieck's depth theory.

5. Stalks and germs

The local-to-global yoga becomes really powerful at a single point.

Definition. The stalk of a (pre)sheaf $F$ at $x \in X$ is the filtered colimit $$F_x \;:=\; \varinjlim_{x \in U} F(U)$$ over the system of open neighborhoods of $x$, ordered by reverse inclusion. An element of $F_x$ is a germ at $x$ — an equivalence class $[(U, s)]$ of pairs with $x \in U$ and $s \in F(U)$, where $(U, s) \sim (V, t)$ iff there exists an open $W \subseteq U \cap V$ containing $x$ with $s|_W = t|_W$.

The category indexing the colimit is filtered (any two neighborhoods have a common refinement, namely their intersection), so in $\mathsf{Set}$, $\mathsf{Ab}$, or $\mathsf{Ring}$ the stalk has the explicit description above. Stalks of sheaves of rings are local rings in many natural cases — the stalk of $\mathcal{O}_{X}$ at $x$ on a smooth or analytic or scheme-theoretic $X$ is a local ring whose maximal ideal is the germs vanishing at $x$.

Two facts justify the importance of stalks.

Fact 1 (separation through stalks). If $F$ is a sheaf and $s, t \in F(U)$ have the same germ at every $x \in U$, then $s = t$. Proof: if $s_x = t_x$ then there is a neighborhood $W_x \ni x$ on which $s|_{W_x} = t|_{W_x}$. The $W_x$ cover $U$, and locality applied to this cover gives $s = t$.

Fact 2 (sheaves embed into stalks). The map $F(U) \hookrightarrow \prod_{x \in U} F_x$, $s \mapsto (s_x)_x$, is injective for any sheaf. So a sheaf is determined by its stalks plus the data of which "compatible families of germs" come from honest sections — and this last clause is exactly sheafification, §7.

A note on filtered colimits

The fact that the category indexing the stalk's colimit is filtered is essential. In a filtered colimit, every finite diagram can be pushed into a cone; this is what ensures that the equivalence relation defining germs is transitive and that the stalk of a presheaf of abelian groups is itself an abelian group via "choose a common refinement." In particular, exactness of a sequence of abelian sheaves is preserved by taking stalks, because filtered colimits of abelian groups are exact. This is why "stalk-local" checks work at all.

Examples of stalks

  • Continuous functions. $C^0_{X,x}$ is the ring of germs of continuous functions near $x$. Its maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_x$ is the germs vanishing at $x$; the residue field is $C^0_{X,x}/\mathfrak{m}_x = \mathbb{R}$ via evaluation at $x$.
  • Smooth functions. $C^\infty_{X,x}$ on a smooth $n$-manifold is a local ring whose maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_x$ satisfies $\mathfrak{m}_x/\mathfrak{m}_x^2 \cong T_x^* X$ — the cotangent space. This is the sheafy origin of Kähler differentials.
  • Holomorphic functions. On $\mathbb{C}^n$ or a complex manifold, $\mathcal{O}_{X,x}$ is the ring of convergent power series $\mathbb{C}\{z_1, \dots, z_n\}$ — a Noetherian local UFD.
  • Structure sheaf of a scheme. For $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,R$ and $\mathfrak{p} \in X$, $\mathcal{O}_{X,\mathfrak{p}} = R_\mathfrak{p}$ — the localization of $R$ at the prime $\mathfrak{p}$. A local ring, by definition, with maximal ideal $\mathfrak{p} R_\mathfrak{p}$ and residue field $\kappa(\mathfrak{p}) = R_\mathfrak{p}/\mathfrak{p} R_\mathfrak{p} = \mathrm{Frac}(R/\mathfrak{p})$. This is why $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ is a locally ringed space.
  • Skyscraper. $(i_{x*}A)_y = A$ if $y$ is in the closure $\overline{\{x\}}$ (with the Zariski or analytic topology this can include generizations), but for Hausdorff $X$ reduces to $A$ if $y=x$ and the terminal object otherwise.

Worked example: the stalk of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{A}^1_k}$ at the origin

Take $X = \mathbb{A}^1_k = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[t]$ for a field $k$, and $x = (t)$ the closed point "origin." Neighborhoods of $(t)$ are distinguished opens $D(f)$ with $f \notin (t)$, i.e. $f(0) \ne 0$. The stalk is $$\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{A}^1_k, (t)} = \varinjlim_{f(0) \ne 0} k[t]_f = k[t]_{(t)},$$ the localization of $k[t]$ at the prime ideal $(t)$. An element of this stalk is a rational function $g/h$ with $h(0) \ne 0$ — a "germ of a regular function at $0$." The maximal ideal is $(t)$; the residue field is $k[t]_{(t)}/(t) = k$, and the stalk is a discrete valuation ring (a regular local ring of dimension $1$).

Stalk-wise constructions

Many operations on sheaves commute with taking stalks: for a morphism $\varphi\colon F \to G$, the kernel $\ker \varphi$ and image-inside-$G$ (in $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$) have stalks $(\ker \varphi)_x = \ker \varphi_x$ and $(\mathrm{im}\,\varphi)_x = \mathrm{im}\,\varphi_x$. Cokernels are subtler — the presheaf cokernel $U \mapsto G(U)/\varphi_U F(U)$ is not always a sheaf; the sheaf cokernel is its sheafification, whose stalks are still the cokernels of stalk maps. The moral: exactness of sheaves is stalk-local, precisely because limits and filtered colimits (hence stalks) commute with the equalizer diagram defining sheaves.

Direct limits and filtered colimits commute with stalks

More useful formal fact: if $\{F_i\}_{i \in I}$ is a filtered diagram of sheaves with colimit $F = \varinjlim F_i$ (computed in $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ as the sheafification of the pointwise colimit presheaf), then for every $x \in X$, $$F_x \;=\; \varinjlim_i (F_i)_x.$$ Stalks and filtered colimits commute. This is what lets you compute the stalk of a "big" sheaf built as a union of "small" ones by passing through the small sheaves — a routine move in Galois cohomology, pro-étale sheaves, and the theory of adic spaces.

The "espace étalé" picture

Every sheaf $F$ of sets on $X$ is represented by a topological space $\mathrm{Esp}(F) = \bigsqcup_{x \in X} F_x$ (disjoint union of stalks), equipped with the topology generated by sets of the form $\{s_x : x \in U\}$ for $s \in F(U)$. The natural projection $\pi\colon \mathrm{Esp}(F) \to X$ is a local homeomorphism, and $F(U) = \{\,\text{continuous sections of }\pi\text{ over }U\,\}$. Conversely, the sheaf of local sections of any local homeomorphism $\pi\colon E \to X$ is a sheaf, and the construction yields an equivalence between sheaves of sets on $X$ and local homeomorphisms over $X$. This geometric picture is why "germ" and "section" are the right words: a section of $F$ over $U$ is literally a section of the projection $\pi$.

6. Morphisms of sheaves

Since presheaves are functors, a morphism of (pre)sheaves $\varphi\colon F \to G$ is a natural transformation: for every open $U$ a map $\varphi_U\colon F(U) \to G(U)$, commuting with restriction. The category of sheaves $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ is the full subcategory of $\mathsf{PSh}(X)$ on the sheaves.

A morphism $\varphi$ induces a map on stalks for every $x$: $\varphi_x\colon F_x \to G_x$, $[(U,s)] \mapsto [(U, \varphi_U(s))]$. The stalk perspective gives a clean criterion.

Lemma. Let $\varphi\colon F \to G$ be a morphism of sheaves on $X$. Then:
  1. $\varphi = 0$ iff $\varphi_x = 0$ for every $x \in X$.
  2. $\varphi$ is a monomorphism (injective on each $F(U) \to G(U)$) iff $\varphi_x$ is injective for every $x$.
  3. $\varphi$ is an isomorphism iff $\varphi_x$ is an isomorphism for every $x$.

Sketch. (1) and (2) reduce to: a section $s \in F(U)$ is zero / nonzero in $F_x$ iff there is a neighborhood of $x$ on which $s$ vanishes / does not vanish, by Fact 1 of §5. For (3) the new content is surjectivity: stalkwise surjective is not the same as $\varphi_U$ being surjective for every $U$. (The exponential map $\exp\colon \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{O}_X^\times$ on $\mathbb{C}^\times$ is stalkwise surjective but not surjective on global sections — there is no global logarithm.) But "stalkwise iso" plus the equalizer characterization of sheaves, plus an inverse manufactured locally and glued, gives a global inverse.

Subsheaves and quotient sheaves

A subsheaf $F' \subseteq F$ is a sheaf $F'$ with an inclusion $F'(U) \subseteq F(U)$ for every $U$, compatible with restrictions. The quotient sheaf $F/F'$ is the sheafification of the presheaf $U \mapsto F(U)/F'(U)$. Stalks compute as $(F/F')_x = F_x / F'_x$.

Two useful criteria: (i) a subpresheaf of a sheaf is automatically separated (locality inherited), but need not satisfy gluing — so subsheaves must be verified, not assumed; (ii) for sheaves of abelian groups, kernels are always subsheaves, because the kernel of a map between sheaves is a pointwise subset and inherits locality and gluing from the ambient sheaves.

Sheaf Hom and the internal hom

Beyond the set of morphisms $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathsf{Sh}}(F, G)$ there is an internal version $\mathcal{H}\!om(F, G)$, the sheaf on $X$ defined by $$\mathcal{H}\!om(F, G)(U) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathsf{Sh}(U)}(F|_U, G|_U).$$ This is already a sheaf (no sheafification needed), and its global sections recover $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathsf{Sh}}(F, G)$. The internal hom is right adjoint to the tensor product (over $\mathcal{O}_X$, when working with $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules), which makes $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$ and $\mathsf{Mod}(\mathcal{O}_X)$ closed symmetric monoidal categories. Stalks of $\mathcal{H}\!om$ are not in general $\mathrm{Hom}(F_x, G_x)$ — the point-wise formula fails for infinite-rank modules. For coherent sheaves on a Noetherian scheme it does hold.

Caution. Note the asymmetry: monomorphism in $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ coincides with "injective on every $F(U) \to G(U)$" and equivalently with "stalkwise injective." But epimorphism in $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ is "stalkwise surjective," which is strictly weaker than "$\varphi_U$ is surjective for every $U$." This is the source of the cohomology of sheaves: surjectivity on stalks gives a short exact sequence of sheaves $0 \to \ker \to F \to G \to 0$, but on global sections the right map need not be surjective, and the failure is measured by $H^1(X, \ker)$.

Cohomology, in one breath

Given the machinery above, sheaf cohomology is now easy to define. The global-sections functor $\Gamma(X, -) \colon \mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab}) \to \mathsf{Ab}$ is left exact (preserves kernels, hence injections) but not right exact — this is the fundamental failure we have been tracking. Its right-derived functors are sheaf cohomology: $$H^i(X, F) \;:=\; R^i\Gamma(X, F).$$ These are computed using an injective resolution, or (equivalently and more practically) via Čech cohomology $\check{H}^i(\mathcal{U}, F)$ for a good cover $\mathcal{U}$, or via flasque / soft / acyclic resolutions. The first nontrivial case is $H^1$: for a short exact sequence $0 \to F' \to F \to F'' \to 0$ of sheaves, one gets a long exact sequence $$0 \to \Gamma F' \to \Gamma F \to \Gamma F'' \to H^1 F' \to H^1 F \to H^1 F'' \to H^2 F' \to \cdots$$ $H^1$ measures the defect between stalkwise surjectivity and global surjectivity — exactly the obstruction we saw with $\exp\colon \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}^\times} \to \mathcal{O}^\times_{\mathbb{C}^\times}$.

Image, kernel, cokernel

In $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$: $(\ker \varphi)(U) = \ker(\varphi_U)$, computed pointwise (kernels are automatically sheaves). The presheaf image $U \mapsto \mathrm{im}(\varphi_U) \subseteq G(U)$ is not in general a sheaf; the sheaf image $\mathrm{im}\,\varphi$ is its sheafification, identified with the subsheaf of $G$ locally generated by the images. The sheaf cokernel $\mathrm{coker}\,\varphi$ is the sheafification of $U \mapsto G(U)/\varphi_U F(U)$. A sequence $F \xrightarrow{\varphi} G \xrightarrow{\psi} H$ is exact in $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$ iff the sequence $F_x \to G_x \to H_x$ is exact for every $x \in X$ — all the abelian-category machinery for sheaves reduces to stalks.

Example: the exponential sheaf sequence

On a complex manifold $X$ one has the short exact sequence $$0 \to \underline{2\pi i \mathbb{Z}} \to \mathcal{O}_X \xrightarrow{\exp} \mathcal{O}_X^\times \to 0,$$ exact in $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$ — stalkwise, locally a logarithm exists. Taking global sections gives the long exact sequence $$0 \to \underline{2\pi i \mathbb{Z}}(X) \to \mathcal{O}_X(X) \to \mathcal{O}_X^\times(X) \to H^1(X, \underline{2\pi i \mathbb{Z}}) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to \mathrm{Pic}(X) \to H^2(X, \underline{2\pi i \mathbb{Z}}) \to \cdots$$ On $X = \mathbb{C}^\times$, the coboundary $\mathcal{O}_X^\times(X) \to H^1(X, \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}$ is the winding number, and sends the identity function $z$ to $1$ — manifesting its non-exponentialness.

7. Sheafification

We have seen that not every presheaf is a sheaf. The remedy is universal.

Theorem (sheafification). The forgetful functor $\iota\colon \mathsf{Sh}(X) \hookrightarrow \mathsf{PSh}(X)$ admits a left adjoint $$(-)^{\#}\colon \mathsf{PSh}(X) \to \mathsf{Sh}(X), \qquad \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathsf{Sh}}(F^\#, G) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathsf{PSh}}(F, \iota G).$$ Equivalently, for every presheaf $F$ there is a sheaf $F^\#$ and a morphism of presheaves $\eta\colon F \to F^\#$ such that any morphism from $F$ to a sheaf factors uniquely through $\eta$.

The construction is the natural one: $F^\#(U)$ is the set of "compatible families of germs." $$F^\#(U) \;=\; \Big\{\, (s_x)_{x \in U} \in \prod_{x \in U} F_x \;\Big|\; \forall\, x \in U\ \exists\ V \ni x,\ V \subseteq U,\ \exists\ t \in F(V) :\ t_y = s_y\ \forall\ y \in V \,\Big\}.$$ A section over $U$ is a choice of germ at each point that is locally represented by an honest section. Restriction is restriction of germ-tuples. The natural map $\eta_U\colon F(U) \to F^\#(U)$, $s \mapsto (s_x)_x$, is the unit of the adjunction.

Two clean facts to keep in mind:

  • The natural map on stalks $F_x \to (F^\#)_x$ is an isomorphism for every $x$. Sheafification does not change stalks; it only fixes the global structure that gluing demands.
  • If $F$ is already a sheaf, then $\eta\colon F \to F^\#$ is an isomorphism. Sheaves are precisely the fixed points of the monad $\iota \circ (-)^\#$.

The constant presheaf example is now transparent. The stalks of $\underline{S}^{\mathrm{pre}}$ at every point are $S$ itself; so $(\underline{S}^{\mathrm{pre}})^\#(U)$ is the set of "compatible families of elements of $S$, indexed by points of $U$, with each point having a neighborhood on which the chosen element is constant" — i.e. the locally-constant functions $U \to S$. This is the constant sheaf $\underline{S}$.

The explicit construction is rarely used

In practice one almost never writes down $F^\#$ as "compatible families of germs" — the construction is correct but unpleasant to compute with. Instead, one uses universal properties and the fact that stalks are preserved. For example: to show $(F \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X} G)_x \cong F_x \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_{X,x}} G_x$, one notes that both sides receive natural maps from $F(U) \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X(U)} G(U)$ for small $U \ni x$, and that both sides share the universal property of "turning bilinear maps into linear maps on stalks." This kind of argument is the bread and butter of sheaf theory — concrete constructions are rare, universal-property arguments dominate.

Two-step construction

Some sources split sheafification into two steps. Given a presheaf $F$, let $F^+$ be the presheaf of matching families: $F^+(U)$ is the set of equivalence classes of pairs $(\mathcal{U}, (s_i))$ where $\mathcal{U}$ is a cover of $U$ and $(s_i)$ is a compatible family on $\mathcal{U}$, under the equivalence of passing to a common refinement. The functor $(-)^+$ solves the locality axiom (makes the unit injective on sections), and $F^{++} = F^\#$ solves the gluing axiom. For a presheaf already satisfying locality, $F^+$ is a sheaf. This "plus construction" is the one that generalizes verbatim to sites with Grothendieck topologies.

Sheafification and colimits

Colimits in $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ are computed by taking the presheaf colimit and sheafifying. In particular, arbitrary direct sums of sheaves exist, computed as $\bigoplus_i F_i$ = sheafification of $U \mapsto \bigoplus_i F_i(U)$. Stalks commute: $(\bigoplus_i F_i)_x = \bigoplus_i (F_i)_x$. Quotient sheaves (cokernels) are similar. The general pattern "compute in presheaves, sheafify" is why reflective subcategories are so pleasant: one can work pointwise almost freely, as long as the final answer is sheafified when needed.

Sheafification is exact

In the abelian setting (presheaves and sheaves of abelian groups), sheafification $(-)^\#\colon \mathsf{PSh}(X, \mathsf{Ab}) \to \mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$ is exact. It is a left adjoint, so it preserves colimits; since stalks commute with it and exactness in $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$ is stalk-local, it also preserves finite limits. In particular the category $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$ is abelian, with kernels computed as in presheaves and cokernels formed by sheafifying the presheaf cokernel. (If you enjoy topoi: $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Set})$ is an elementary topos, and $\mathsf{Sh}(X, \mathsf{Ab})$ is a Grothendieck abelian category.)

The universal description in one line

Here is the clean categorical statement. The category $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ is a reflective subcategory of $\mathsf{PSh}(X)$, with reflector $(-)^\#$ left-exact. This single datum is responsible for every computational fact we will meet. For instance: stalks $F \mapsto F_x$ factor as $\mathsf{PSh}(X) \to \mathsf{Sh}(X) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathsf{Sh}(X) \to \mathsf{Set}$ with the reflector invisible on stalks, which is how we proved $F_x \to (F^\#)_x$ is an isomorphism.

Stalks, again, after sheafifying

One of the cleanest checks that sheafification "doesn't change anything pointwise" is direct from the construction. A germ in $(F^\#)_x$ is represented by a section $\sigma \in F^\#(U)$ for some neighborhood $U$ of $x$. By definition of $F^\#(U)$, $\sigma$ is locally represented by an actual section of $F$: there is $V \subseteq U$ containing $x$ and $t \in F(V)$ with $t_y = \sigma_y$ for all $y \in V$. The germ $[t]_x \in F_x$ then maps to the germ of $\sigma$ at $x$ under $\eta$, showing the natural map $F_x \to (F^\#)_x$ is surjective. Injectivity uses that $\eta$ is injective on stalks (germs, by definition, only see arbitrarily small neighborhoods). Hence isomorphism, on every stalk, every time.

Sheafification is the unit of a monad

The adjunction $(-)^\# \dashv \iota$ gives a monad $T = \iota \circ (-)^\#$ on $\mathsf{PSh}(X)$: $T$ is functorial, comes with unit $\eta\colon \mathrm{id} \to T$ and multiplication $\mu\colon T \circ T \to T$, and the category of $T$-algebras is $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ — sheaves are exactly the presheaves on which the unit is an isomorphism. This is one instance of a general principle: reflective subcategories are algebras for idempotent monads.

In practice one almost never manipulates sheafification via the explicit construction. Instead one appeals to universal properties: "define $\varphi\colon F^\# \to G$ by giving $\varphi \circ \eta\colon F \to \iota G$," and "check a map $F^\# \to G$ vanishes by checking its composition with $\eta$ vanishes." The stalk-invariance $F_x = (F^\#)_x$ does the rest.

Examples of sheafification in practice

  • Image sheaf. For a morphism $\varphi\colon F \to G$ of sheaves, the image $\mathrm{im}\,\varphi$ as a sheaf is obtained by sheafifying the presheaf image $U \mapsto \varphi_U F(U) \subseteq G(U)$. On stalks, $(\mathrm{im}\,\varphi)_x = \mathrm{im}\,\varphi_x$, consistent with exactness being stalk-local.
  • Cokernel sheaf. $\mathrm{coker}\,\varphi = (\mathrm{presheaf\ coker})^\#$; stalks are $(\mathrm{coker}\,\varphi)_x = \mathrm{coker}\,\varphi_x$.
  • Tensor product. $\mathcal{F} \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X} \mathcal{G}$ = sheafification of $U \mapsto \mathcal{F}(U) \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X(U)} \mathcal{G}(U)$. The sheafification step is necessary because tensor product does not commute with arbitrary limits, so the presheaf $U \mapsto \mathcal{F}(U) \otimes \mathcal{G}(U)$ can violate gluing.
  • Pullback $f^*$ of $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules. The definition $f^*\mathcal{G} := f^{-1}\mathcal{G} \otimes_{f^{-1}\mathcal{O}_Y} \mathcal{O}_X$ is already a sheafified expression — $f^{-1}$ itself is defined via sheafification.
  • Symmetric and exterior powers, sheaf of local rings. Any construction that is "pointwise" obvious but fails the gluing axiom on large opens requires sheafification.

8. Sheaves of modules and $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules

A ringed space is a pair $(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ where $X$ is a topological space and $\mathcal{O}_X$ is a sheaf of (commutative, unital) rings on $X$. A morphism of ringed spaces $(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to (Y, \mathcal{O}_Y)$ is a continuous map $f\colon X \to Y$ together with a morphism of sheaves of rings $f^\#\colon \mathcal{O}_Y \to f_*\mathcal{O}_X$ on $Y$. (When the stalk maps $\mathcal{O}_{Y,f(x)} \to \mathcal{O}_{X,x}$ are local — i.e. send the maximal ideal into the maximal ideal — one calls $(f,f^\#)$ a morphism of locally ringed spaces; the next page will dwell on this.)

An $\mathcal{O}_X$-module is a sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ of abelian groups on $X$, together with, for every open $U$, a structure of $\mathcal{O}_X(U)$-module on $\mathcal{F}(U)$, compatibly with restrictions: the diagram $$\begin{array}{ccc} \mathcal{O}_X(U) \times \mathcal{F}(U) & \to & \mathcal{F}(U) \\ \downarrow & & \downarrow \\ \mathcal{O}_X(V) \times \mathcal{F}(V) & \to & \mathcal{F}(V) \end{array}$$ commutes for $V \subseteq U$. The category $\mathsf{Mod}(\mathcal{O}_X)$ is abelian, has tensor products $\mathcal{F} \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X} \mathcal{G}$ and $\mathcal{H}\!om_{\mathcal{O}_X}(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{G})$ as $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules, and direct sums.

An $\mathcal{O}_X$-module $\mathcal{F}$ is free of rank $n$ if $\mathcal{F} \cong \mathcal{O}_X^n$, and locally free of rank $n$ if every $x \in X$ has an open neighborhood $U$ on which $\mathcal{F}|_U \cong \mathcal{O}_U^n$. Locally free sheaves are the algebraic incarnation of vector bundles: on a smooth manifold, the sheaf of sections of a smooth $\mathbb{R}^n$-bundle is a locally free $C^\infty_X$-module of rank $n$, and the equivalence is in fact an equivalence of categories. The next layer up — the algebraic-geometry analogue — is quasi-coherent sheaves on a scheme, to which we now turn.

Locally ringed spaces — the right setting

Among ringed spaces, the geometrically meaningful ones are locally ringed spaces: those where every stalk $\mathcal{O}_{X,x}$ is a local ring. This subsumes:

  • smooth manifolds with $C^\infty_X$ — the maximal ideal at $x$ is functions vanishing at $x$, residue field $\mathbb{R}$;
  • complex manifolds with $\mathcal{O}_X$ — maximal ideal as above, residue field $\mathbb{C}$;
  • affine schemes $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ with the structure sheaf — $\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}\,R, \mathfrak{p}} = R_\mathfrak{p}$, a local ring by construction.

A morphism of locally ringed spaces $(f, f^\#)\colon (X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to (Y, \mathcal{O}_Y)$ requires the stalk maps $f^\#_x\colon \mathcal{O}_{Y, f(x)} \to \mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ to send the maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_{f(x)}$ into $\mathfrak{m}_x$ — equivalently, to be a homomorphism of local rings. Without this hypothesis there are pathological "ring-valued" morphisms that don't correspond to anything geometric. (For instance, between schemes, one can construct a ring-only morphism $\mathrm{Spec}\,k(t) \to \mathrm{Spec}\,k[t]_{(t)}$ that lands at the closed point but whose stalk map is the inclusion $k[t]_{(t)} \hookrightarrow k(t)$ — clearly not a local-ring morphism, and not a morphism of schemes.) The next page treats this in detail.

Pullback and pushforward for $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules

For a morphism of ringed spaces $f\colon (X,\mathcal{O}_X) \to (Y,\mathcal{O}_Y)$, $f_*$ maps $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules to $\mathcal{O}_Y$-modules using the ring morphism $\mathcal{O}_Y \to f_*\mathcal{O}_X$. The corresponding pullback is $f^*\mathcal{F} = f^{-1}\mathcal{F} \otimes_{f^{-1}\mathcal{O}_Y} \mathcal{O}_X$ — the $\mathcal{O}_X$-module obtained from the set-theoretic inverse image by scalar extension. For an open inclusion $f = j$, $j^*\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{F}|_U$ is literally restriction. The adjunction $f^* \dashv f_*$ on $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules is the fundamental "change of base space" operation.

Two subtleties worth flagging. First, $f^*$ is right exact but not left exact; to compute homological invariants after pullback one uses the derived functor $Lf^*$, which commutes with $\otimes$ in a precise sense. Second, for morphisms of schemes, $f_*$ sends quasi-coherent sheaves to quasi-coherent sheaves provided $f$ is quasi-compact and quasi-separated (qcqs); without this hypothesis $f_*\mathcal{F}$ of a quasi-coherent $\mathcal{F}$ need not be quasi-coherent on the base. This is one of several places where scheme-theoretic finiteness hypotheses are genuinely needed.

Vector bundles as locally free sheaves — the dictionary

Let $E \to X$ be a real (complex, smooth) vector bundle of rank $n$. The assignment $U \mapsto \Gamma(U, E) = \{\text{sections of }E\text{ over }U\}$ is an $\mathcal{O}_X$-module — where $\mathcal{O}_X = C^0_X, C^\infty_X, \mathcal{O}_X^{\mathrm{hol}}$ according to the bundle's regularity. By local trivializations of $E$, this sheaf is locally isomorphic to $\mathcal{O}_X^n$, hence locally free of rank $n$. Conversely every locally free $\mathcal{O}_X$-module of rank $n$ arises this way — the construction is: use the transition functions $\varphi_{ij}\colon \mathcal{O}_{U_{ij}}^n \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathcal{O}_{U_{ij}}^n$ from a local-free cover to glue together open subsets of $X \times \mathbb{R}^n$ (resp. $\mathbb{C}^n$, $\mathbb{A}^n$).

Line bundles (rank $1$) play an especially clean role: the set of isomorphism classes is an abelian group under tensor product (with inverse the dual), called the Picard group $\mathrm{Pic}(X)$. By the exponential sheaf sequence, $\mathrm{Pic}(X) \cong H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^\times)$.

Tensor product and $\mathcal{H}\!om$

For $\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{G}$ $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules: $\mathcal{F} \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X} \mathcal{G}$ is the sheafification of $U \mapsto \mathcal{F}(U) \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X(U)} \mathcal{G}(U)$, and its stalks are $\mathcal{F}_x \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_{X,x}} \mathcal{G}_x$. The internal hom $\mathcal{H}\!om_{\mathcal{O}_X}(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{G})$ is the sheaf $U \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_X|_U}(\mathcal{F}|_U, \mathcal{G}|_U)$ — already a sheaf without sheafification. These satisfy the expected tensor-hom adjunction.

9. Quasi-coherent sheaves on affine schemes

Let $R$ be a commutative ring and $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,R$ with its Zariski topology and structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$. Recall: the distinguished open $D(f) = \{\mathfrak{p} \in X : f \notin \mathfrak{p}\}$ for $f \in R$ form a basis, and $\mathcal{O}_X(D(f)) = R_f$ is the localization at the multiplicative set $\{1, f, f^2, \dots\}$.

For an $R$-module $M$, define a sheaf of $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules $\widetilde{M}$ on $X$ by specifying it on the basis of distinguished opens: $$\widetilde{M}(D(f)) \;=\; M_f \;=\; M \otimes_R R_f.$$ The restriction maps for $D(g) \subseteq D(f)$ (which holds iff some power $g^n$ lies in the ideal $(f)$, equivalently $f$ becomes a unit in $R_g$) are induced by the natural localization maps $M_f \to M_g$. One checks directly that this is a sheaf on the basis (the sheaf condition reduces to a finite covering by distinguished opens, and to the algebraic fact that for $R = \prod R_{f_i}$ with $\sum (f_i) = R$, the diagram $M \to \prod M_{f_i} \rightrightarrows \prod M_{f_i f_j}$ is exact). This extends uniquely to a sheaf on all of $X$.

A sheaf of $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules $\mathcal{F}$ on $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,R$ is quasi-coherent if it is isomorphic to $\widetilde{M}$ for some $R$-module $M$. (More generally on a scheme, $\mathcal{F}$ is quasi-coherent if every point has an affine open neighborhood $U = \mathrm{Spec}\,R$ on which $\mathcal{F}|_U \cong \widetilde{M}$ for some $R$-module $M$.)

Theorem. The functor $M \mapsto \widetilde{M}$ is an equivalence of categories $$R\text{-}\mathsf{Mod} \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; \mathsf{QCoh}(\mathrm{Spec}\,R),$$ with inverse $\mathcal{F} \mapsto \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$. The functor is exact, preserves direct sums, and sends $M \otimes_R N$ to $\widetilde{M} \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X} \widetilde{N}$. The stalk at $\mathfrak{p} \in X$ is $\widetilde{M}_\mathfrak{p} = M_\mathfrak{p}$.

The theorem reduces all questions about quasi-coherent sheaves on an affine scheme to module theory over the coordinate ring. This is one of the major payoffs of the scheme-theoretic language: the local structure of $\mathcal{F}$ at $\mathfrak{p}$ is computed by localizing $M$ at $\mathfrak{p}$, which is exactly the concrete tool you reach for in commutative algebra.

Worked example

Take $R = \mathbb{Z}$ and $M = \mathbb{Z}/6$. Then on $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$:

  • The global sections are $\widetilde{M}(X) = \mathbb{Z}/6$.
  • $\widetilde{M}(D(2)) = (\mathbb{Z}/6)_2 = \mathbb{Z}/3$, since inverting $2$ in $\mathbb{Z}/6$ kills the ideal generated by $3$? — let's redo: $\mathbb{Z}/6 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/3$ by CRT; localizing at $2$ inverts $2$, which acts as $0$ on the $\mathbb{Z}/2$ summand and as a unit on $\mathbb{Z}/3$. So $(\mathbb{Z}/6)_2 = \mathbb{Z}/3$.
  • Similarly $(\mathbb{Z}/6)_3 = \mathbb{Z}/2$, and $(\mathbb{Z}/6)_5 = \mathbb{Z}/6$ (since $5$ is already a unit mod $2$ and mod $3$).
  • The stalk at $\mathfrak{p} = (5)$ is $(\mathbb{Z}/6)_{(5)} = \mathbb{Z}/6$ since neither $2$ nor $3$ is in $(5)$.
  • The stalk at $\mathfrak{p} = (2)$ is $(\mathbb{Z}/6)_{(2)} = \mathbb{Z}/2$, and at $\mathfrak{p} = (3)$ is $\mathbb{Z}/3$.

So $\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}/6}$ is supported on $V(6) = \{(2),(3)\} \subset \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$, with respective stalks $\mathbb{Z}/2$ and $\mathbb{Z}/3$. It is the direct sum of two skyscraper-like sheaves.

Why the basis is enough

It is worth pausing on the magic that $\widetilde{M}$ is a sheaf, because it reveals a pattern used repeatedly. Define $\widetilde{M}$ only on the basis $\{D(f)\}_{f \in R}$ of distinguished opens, with values $\widetilde{M}(D(f)) = M_f$. Check the sheaf axiom on this basis: for a finite cover $D(f) = \bigcup D(f_i)$ (we may reduce to finite covers because $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ is quasi-compact), the condition $\sum_i (f_i) \supseteq (f)$ translates to $1 = \sum c_i f_i^k / f^k$ for some $k$, and the Čech-like exactness of $M_f \to \prod M_{f_i} \rightrightarrows \prod M_{f_i f_j}$ is a direct algebraic calculation. Then a general theorem on bases ("a sheaf on a basis uniquely extends to a sheaf on all opens") finishes the job.

This pattern — check the sheaf axiom on a suitable basis, extend — is used to construct:

  • the structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$ on $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$;
  • the structure sheaf on $\mathrm{Proj}\,S$ for a graded ring $S$;
  • the sheaves $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on projective space;
  • every quasi-coherent sheaf in sight.

Sketch of the equivalence

The two functors are $M \mapsto \widetilde{M}$ and $\mathcal{F} \mapsto \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$. That they are mutual inverses splits into two assertions.

$\Gamma(X, \widetilde{M}) = M$. Take the cover of $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,R$ by the single distinguished open $D(1) = X$ — of course this is just $\widetilde{M}(X) = M_1 = M$. Less trivially: any cover of $X$ by distinguished opens $\{D(f_i)\}$ has $\sum (f_i) = R$ (since the $D(f_i)$ cover iff the $f_i$ generate the unit ideal, by spectrum-of-quotient considerations); and the sheaf condition $M = \mathrm{eq}(\prod M_{f_i} \rightrightarrows \prod M_{f_i f_j})$ is the standard exact sequence in commutative algebra.

$\widetilde{\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})} \cong \mathcal{F}$ for $\mathcal{F} \in \mathsf{QCoh}(X)$. This uses that $\mathcal{F}$ is locally of the form $\widetilde{N}$ on some distinguished basis, and a sheaf-theoretic patching argument to identify the $N$'s as localizations of $M = \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$.

Coherent sheaves

A quasi-coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a Noetherian scheme is coherent iff it corresponds (locally) to a finitely generated module. For $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,R$ with $R$ Noetherian, coherent sheaves are precisely $\widetilde{M}$ for $M$ finitely generated, and the category $\mathsf{Coh}(X)$ is the abelian subcategory of $\mathsf{QCoh}(X)$ consisting of finitely generated objects. Coherent = finite-dimensional linear algebra over the scheme; quasi-coherent = arbitrary (possibly infinite-dimensional) linear algebra.

Why "quasi-coherent" and not just "$\mathcal{O}_X$-module"?

On a scheme $X$, generic $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules can be wildly non-algebraic. Consider $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}$ and the skyscraper sheaf at the generic point $(0)$: the corresponding presheaf does not arise from any $\mathbb{Z}$-module. Quasi-coherence is the algebraic-finiteness condition that says "this $\mathcal{O}_X$-module is locally determined by its global sections in a way that respects localization." Equivalently, $\mathcal{F}$ is quasi-coherent on $X$ iff for every affine open $U = \mathrm{Spec}\,R \subseteq X$ and every $f \in R$, the natural map $\mathcal{F}(U)_f \to \mathcal{F}(D(f))$ is an isomorphism. This is the algebraic engine behind the equivalence with $R$-modules.

The three viewpoints, reconciled

An $R$-module can be viewed in three ways, each emphasizing a different aspect of what it "is":

  1. Algebraically. As a module in the usual sense — a set with addition and $R$-action.
  2. As a quasi-coherent sheaf. As $\widetilde{M}$ on $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$, with local data encoded in the localizations.
  3. As a family of vector spaces over the points of $X$. For each $\mathfrak{p}$, the fiber $M \otimes_R \kappa(\mathfrak{p})$ is a $\kappa(\mathfrak{p})$-vector space; the "geometric object" is this varying family of vector spaces — with the caveat that the fibers are not in general related by linear maps (they live over different residue fields).

For $M$ finitely generated projective, (3) is honest: the fibers are all the same dimension (= rank of $M$), and $\widetilde{M}$ really is a vector bundle. For general $M$ the fiber dimensions jump — torsion elements "disappear" at certain points — and $\widetilde{M}$ is a more flexible object than a vector bundle. Nakayama's lemma, upper-semicontinuity of fiber dimension, and the generic-freeness theorem all live here.

Locally free vs. quasi-coherent

A locally free $\mathcal{O}_X$-module of finite rank is quasi-coherent (locally a free module $R^n$). On an affine scheme, a quasi-coherent $\widetilde{M}$ is locally free of rank $n$ iff $M$ is a finitely generated projective $R$-module of constant rank $n$. For a Noetherian local ring $R$, projective is the same as free (if finitely generated). So on $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ for $R$ a regular Noetherian local ring, every coherent sheaf has a finite locally-free resolution — Hilbert's syzygy theorem in disguise.

Globalization to projective space

The equivalence above is purely affine. On $\mathbb{P}^n_R = \mathrm{Proj}\,R[x_0, \dots, x_n]$, quasi-coherent sheaves correspond not to graded modules tout court, but to graded modules up to the equivalence "kill homogeneous elements annihilated by some power of the irrelevant ideal $(x_0, \dots, x_n)$." The functor $M \mapsto \widetilde{M}$ on graded modules gives a sheaf with $\widetilde{M}(D_+(f)) = (M_f)_0$ — the degree-zero piece. The natural quasi-coherent sheaves $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}(d)$ correspond to the graded module $R[x_0, \dots, x_n](d)$, the same module with grading shifted by $d$. Computing $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(d))$ is a famous Čech-cohomology calculation; the answer is non-zero only in degrees $0$ and $n$, and is responsible for Serre duality.

10. Coda: where this goes

We have set up the basic machinery: presheaves, sheaves, stalks, sheafification, $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules, and the equivalence between $R$-modules and quasi-coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$. What this buys, in concert with the next page (morphisms and fiber products):

  • Geometric pullback of modules. A morphism of schemes $f\colon X \to Y$ gives functors $f^*\colon \mathsf{QCoh}(Y) \to \mathsf{QCoh}(X)$ and $f_*\colon \mathsf{QCoh}(X) \to \mathsf{QCoh}(Y)$ — for affine $Y = \mathrm{Spec}\,R$ and $X = \mathrm{Spec}\,S$ corresponding to $R \to S$, these are the $-\otimes_R S$ and "view as $R$-module" functors of commutative algebra, sheafified.
  • Fiber products. The category of schemes has fiber products $X \times_Z Y$, and $\mathsf{QCoh}$ behaves well under base change. Geometric statements ("the fiber of $f\colon X \to Y$ over $y$") become computable as $\mathrm{Spec}\,\kappa(y) \times_Y X$.
  • Cohomology. The right-derived functors of $\Gamma(X, -)$ on $\mathsf{Sh}(X)$ are sheaf cohomology $H^i(X, -)$; restricted to $\mathsf{QCoh}(X)$, they become the workhorse invariants of algebraic geometry — Serre's theorem $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(d)) = 0$ for $i \ne 0, n$, the Euler characteristic of a coherent sheaf, the Riemann–Roch theorem.
  • Vector bundles and line bundles. Locally free sheaves of finite rank are vector bundles; locally free of rank $1$ are line bundles, classified by $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^\times) = \mathrm{Pic}(X)$ — a group built directly out of the cohomology of an exact sheaf sequence.

The slogan: once you have sheaves, you have a uniform language for "local data on a space." Once you have $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules, you have a uniform language for "linear algebra over a space." Once you have $\mathsf{QCoh}$ on schemes, the algebra of commutative rings becomes the geometry of schemes — the dictionary is exact, and almost every geometric construction has a clean module-theoretic translation.

What is left out

A short list of nearby topics that the next several pages would naturally cover:

  • Čech cohomology $\check{H}^i(\mathcal{U}, F)$ for an open cover $\mathcal{U}$ — combinatorial cocycles that, on nice spaces (paracompact Hausdorff for ordinary sheaves; affine covers for quasi-coherent sheaves on a scheme), compute sheaf cohomology.
  • Derived categories of sheaves. The right home for sheaf cohomology is $D^+(X, \mathsf{Ab})$. The six-functor formalism $f^*, f_*, f^!, f_!, \otimes, \mathcal{H}\!om$ on derived categories is the modern language for "sheaves on a varying space."
  • Étale and other topologies. Replace $\mathrm{Open}(X)$ by the étale site of a scheme: étale cohomology $H^i_{\mathrm{ét}}(X, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ is the foundation for arithmetic geometry, the proof of the Weil conjectures, and modern Galois representations.
  • Coherent duality. On a proper scheme, the Serre / Grothendieck duality theorem identifies $\mathrm{Hom}(\mathcal{F}, \omega_X[\dim X])$ with $H^{\dim X}(X, \mathcal{F})^*$ — a profound duality computed entirely on quasi-coherent and coherent sheaves.
  • D-modules. Sheaves of modules over the sheaf $\mathcal{D}_X$ of differential operators; the Riemann–Hilbert correspondence relates them to perverse sheaves on the topological side. A sheaf-theoretic incarnation of partial differential equations.

A small epilogue on "locality"

The thread connecting every topic on this page is "locality": every operation we cared about was either manifestly local (computed stalk-by-stalk) or recovered from local data by gluing. That observation is the actual content of sheaf theory. When Grothendieck reorganized algebraic geometry, the first move was to replace varieties with locally ringed spaces; all of the rest — quasi-coherence, cohomology, the étale site, derived categories — is a progressively-richer recording of what "locally ringed" means and how it globalizes.

The second thread is adjoint functors. Sheafification is a left adjoint (§7). Pushforward is a right adjoint to inverse image (§4). Tensor product is left adjoint to $\mathcal{H}\!om$ (§8). The $(\iota, (-)^\#)$ and $(f^{-1}, f_*)$ and $(\otimes, \mathcal{H}\!om)$ adjunctions between them produce most of the formal tools of the subject; the remaining tools are the derived versions of these adjunctions.

A note on "coherence" across categories

Unfortunately the word "coherent" is overloaded. For a general ringed space $(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$, an $\mathcal{O}_X$-module $\mathcal{F}$ is coherent (in Serre's sense) iff it is of finite type and for every open $U$ and every morphism $\mathcal{O}_U^n \to \mathcal{F}|_U$, the kernel is also of finite type. This is automatic when $\mathcal{O}_X$ itself is coherent as a sheaf of rings — true for Noetherian schemes and for complex-analytic spaces by Oka's theorem — but fails in general for non-Noetherian rings. The equivalence between finite-type modules and coherent sheaves mentioned above is specifically the Noetherian case.

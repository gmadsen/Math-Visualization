Morse theory

Pick a smooth function $f\colon M\to\mathbb{R}$ whose critical points are non-degenerate, watch the sublevel sets $\{f\le c\}$ grow as $c$ increases, and read off the topology of $M$ from the indices of the critical points. The local picture (Hessian) controls the global picture (handles, cells, homology) — that is the whole idea.

1. Morse functions

To extract the topology of $M$ from a function $f\colon M\to\mathbb{R}$, we need $f$ to be generic enough that its critical behaviour is non-degenerate everywhere. The Morse condition is the right genericity requirement — strong enough to force a quadratic-form normal form at every critical point, weak enough to be satisfied by a dense set of smooth functions.

Let $M$ be a smooth $n$-manifold and $f\colon M\to\mathbb{R}$ smooth. A point $p\in M$ is a critical point of $f$ if $df_p=0$. At a critical point the Hessian $$H_p f \colon T_pM\times T_pM \to \mathbb{R}$$ is a well-defined symmetric bilinear form (the choice of chart drops out because $df_p=0$). The critical point is non-degenerate if $H_pf$ is non-degenerate as a bilinear form, equivalently the Hessian matrix in any chart is invertible.

A function is a Morse function if all of its critical points are non-degenerate. The Morse index $\ind(p)$ is the number of negative eigenvalues of $H_pf$ — the dimension of the maximal subspace on which the Hessian is negative-definite.

Morse lemma. Around any non-degenerate critical point $p$ of index $k$, there is a chart $(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ with $p\mapsto 0$ in which $$f = f(p) - x_1^2 - \cdots - x_k^2 + x_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + x_n^2.$$ So locally, every Morse critical point looks the same — only the index distinguishes them.

A vertically-mounted torus has four critical points of the height function $h(x,y,z)=z$: two minima and maxima at the top/bottom rims (indices $0,2$) and two saddles where the inner equator meets the level set (indices $1,1$). Tilting away from vertical perturbs the critical-point positions but keeps the count: Morse functions form an open set.

Why insist on non-degeneracy? Two reasons.

  1. Isolation. The Morse lemma quadratic form has $0$ as its only critical point, so each non-degenerate critical point is isolated. Functions like $x^4$ on $\mathbb{R}$ have a degenerate critical point at $0$ that is non-isolated under perturbation.
  2. Density. By Sard's theorem and a perturbation argument, Morse functions are open and dense in $C^\infty(M,\mathbb{R})$: every smooth $f$ can be approximated arbitrarily well by a Morse function. So nothing is lost by working only with Morse functions.

2. Handle decomposition

Fix a Morse function $f\colon M\to\mathbb{R}$ on a closed manifold and consider the family of sublevel sets $$M^c = \{p\in M : f(p)\le c\}.$$ As $c$ increases from $\min f$ to $\max f$, $M^c$ grows from empty to all of $M$. Two fundamental theorems describe how.

Theorem A (regular intervals). If $[a,b]$ contains no critical values of $f$, then $M^a$ is a deformation retract of $M^b$, and $M^b\setminus \mathrm{int}(M^a)$ is diffeomorphic to a collar $\partial M^a\times [a,b]$. (Proof: integrate the rescaled negative gradient $-\nabla f / \|\nabla f\|^2$.)
Theorem B (crossing a critical value). If $[c-\epsilon,c+\epsilon]$ contains a single critical value with one critical point $p$ of index $k$, then up to diffeomorphism $$M^{c+\epsilon} \;\cong\; M^{c-\epsilon} \cup_\varphi (D^k\times D^{n-k}),$$ where the $k$-handle $D^k\times D^{n-k}$ is glued along $\partial D^k\times D^{n-k}\subset \partial M^{c-\epsilon}$.

So topology is built handle-by-handle. The cell that "matters" — the descending disk — has dimension equal to the Morse index $k$; the transverse $D^{n-k}$ is just thickening to keep things a manifold.

3. CW structure

A $k$-handle $D^k\times D^{n-k}$ deformation-retracts onto its core disk $D^k\times\{0\}$. Iterating Theorem B and contracting each handle to its core, the manifold $M$ has the homotopy type of a CW complex with one cell of dimension $k$ for every critical point of $f$ of Morse index $k$.

CW theorem (Milnor). Every closed smooth manifold $M$ admitting a Morse function $f$ is homotopy equivalent to a CW complex with one $k$-cell for each index-$k$ critical point of $f$.

This is the bridge from differential to algebraic topology: smooth analysis (Morse theory) computes the same invariants as cellular methods (CW homology). Concrete consequence — the Euler characteristic $$\chi(M) \;=\; \sum_k (-1)^k c_k(f)$$ where $c_k(f)$ counts the index-$k$ critical points. Independent of the Morse function chosen.

4. Morse inequalities

Let $b_k(M)=\mathrm{rank}\,H_k(M;\mathbb{Q})$ be the $k$-th Betti number. Since $b_k(M)$ is bounded above by the rank of $C_k$ in any cellular chain complex, we get the weak Morse inequalities: $$b_k(M) \;\le\; c_k(f) \quad \text{for every } k.$$ The strong Morse inequalities sharpen this: $$\sum_{k\le j} (-1)^{j-k}\bigl(c_k(f) - b_k(M)\bigr) \;\ge\; 0 \quad \text{for every } j,$$ with equality at $j=n$ (Morse–Reeb / Lefschetz: the alternating sum is $\chi(M)$).

Widget · Betti vs Morse counts on $\Sigma_g$drag $g$; see how counts grow

Bar chart per index: $b_0=1$, $b_1=2g$, $b_2=1$ (Betti numbers of $\Sigma_g$), and minimal $c_k = b_k$ for the standard height function on the obvious embedding. Equality everywhere — the standard height function is perfect. The Euler characteristic $\chi(\Sigma_g)=2-2g$ falls out as $1 - 2g + 1$.

A Morse function with $c_k(f)=b_k(M)$ for all $k$ is called perfect. Perfect Morse functions exist on $\mathbb{C}P^n$, on flag varieties, and on compact Lie groups (Bott). They do not exist on every manifold: the strong inequalities can be strict.

5. Morse homology

The Morse inequalities motivate a chain complex whose generators are the critical points. Pick a Riemannian metric $g$ on $M$ so that $-\nabla f$ has integral curves, and assume $(f,g)$ is a Morse–Smale pair: stable and unstable manifolds of critical points intersect transversally.

Define $$C_k(f) \;=\; \mathbb{Z}\langle\,p \in \mathrm{Crit}(f) : \ind(p)=k\,\rangle.$$ For $\ind(p)=k$ and $\ind(q)=k-1$ the moduli space of unparameterised gradient trajectories from $p$ to $q$ is a finite set (under Morse–Smale + compactness); count signed: $$\partial p \;=\; \sum_{\ind(q)=k-1} \#\mathcal{M}(p,q) \cdot q.$$

Theorem. $\partial^2 = 0$, and the resulting homology $HM_*(M;f,g)$ is independent of the Morse–Smale pair; there is a canonical isomorphism $$HM_*(M) \;\cong\; H_*(M;\mathbb{Z}).$$

6. Applications and generalisations

Even before Morse homology, the theory has bite.

Reeb's sphere theorem

If a closed connected $M^n$ admits a Morse function with exactly two critical points, then $M$ is homeomorphic to $S^n$. (The two critical points must be a min and a max — index $0$ and $n$ — so $M$ is two disks glued along their common boundary.) This was Milnor's route to the exotic 7-spheres: certain $S^3$-bundles over $S^4$ admit such a "two-critical-point" $f$ but are not diffeomorphic to the standard $S^7$.

$S^2$ vs $\mathbb{R}P^2$

A direct contrast. The standard height function on $S^2$ has 2 critical points: indices $0,2$, so $\chi(S^2)=1-0+1=2$. The minimal Morse function on $\mathbb{R}P^2$ has 3 critical points: indices $0,1,2$, giving $\chi(\mathbb{R}P^2)=1-1+1=1$. The extra index-$1$ critical point is not optional: $\mathbb{R}P^2$ is non-orientable, $b_1(\mathbb{R}P^2;\mathbb{Q})=0$ but the $\mathbb{Z}/2$ Betti number is $1$, and Morse counts respect that.

$S^2$ as a CW complex: one 0-cell + one 2-cell, $\chi=2$. $\mathbb{RP}^2$ as a CW complex: one 0-cell + one 1-cell + one 2-cell, $\chi=1$. The 1-cell is the Möbius core that distinguishes $\mathbb{RP}^2$ from the sphere.

Bott and infinite dimensions

Bott observed that Morse theory works nearly as well when critical points are replaced by non-degenerate critical submanifolds (Morse–Bott theory). Applied to the energy functional on path/loop spaces of compact Lie groups, this gave Bott periodicity — one of the cleanest periodicity statements in topology.

The further leap: in the 1980s, Floer realised the action functional on the loop space of a symplectic manifold is "Morse" in a formal sense. Critical points are periodic orbits of a Hamiltonian; gradient flow lines are pseudo-holomorphic cylinders; the analogue of Morse homology is Floer homology, which proved the Arnold conjecture and is now ubiquitous in symplectic topology.

7. Connections

Morse theory is a hinge between three different ways of looking at a smooth manifold — analysis (gradients, flow), topology (cells, homology), and combinatorics (the index data). Each thread points to a sibling page that picks up where this one stops.

Differential geometry and gradient flow

The negative gradient flow $\dot \gamma = -\nabla f$ is the workhorse of differential geometry applications: heat-flow regularisation, Łojasiewicz inequalities, mean-curvature-flow analysis. The Morse stratification is the prototype every gradient-flow argument generalises.

Algebraic topology and CW homology

Singular homology, the Mayer–Vietoris sequence, and CW-complex homology are the algebraic targets — Morse homology is isomorphic to all three for compact $M$. The Morse inequalities are a numerical witness that this isomorphism is non-trivial: $f$ has at least the topology of $M$ baked in.

Lie groups and homogeneous spaces

Bott’s theorem on the homotopy of compact Lie groups uses Morse theory of the energy functional on the loop space of $G$. Equivariant Morse theory (Atiyah–Bott) extends to manifolds with group actions and underlies the Witten-style proofs of Atiyah–Singer.

Symplectic and Floer-theoretic generalisations

Floer’s insight: do Morse theory on the symplectic action functional on the loop space of a symplectic manifold. Characteristic classes and pseudo-holomorphic curves take the place of the Hessian; Floer homology generalises Morse homology to infinite-dimensional settings (Lagrangian, Hamiltonian, instanton, monopole, Heegaard).

Open frontiers: discrete Morse theory and topological data analysis (Forman, persistent homology); $A_\infty$- and $L_\infty$-Morse theory (Fukaya, Kontsevich); equivariant and twisted Floer theories (Seiberg–Witten, Heegaard, embedded contact); the symplectic-cobordism / Khovanov-homology bridge.
